## 1. Historical Context

### The Authors

**Penelope Brown** (b. 1944, Summit, New Jersey) is an American anthropological linguist. She earned a BA in Psychology from Carleton College (1965), an MA in Anthropology and Linguistics from the University of Iowa (1970), and a PhD in Anthropology from the University of California, Berkeley (1979), supervised by John J. Gumperz. Her doctoral dissertation was titled "Language, interaction, and sex roles in a Mayan community: a study of politeness and the position of women." She conducted extensive fieldwork with Tzeltal Maya speakers in Chiapas, Mexico, and later on Rossel Island in Papua New Guinea. Brown became an emeritus member of the Language Acquisition Department at the Max Planck Institute for Psycholinguistics in Nijmegen, the Netherlands, where she worked from 1991 to 2009. She won the Stirling Prize for Best Work in Psychological Anthropology in 1992.

**Stephen Curtis Levinson** (b. 6 December 1947, London, England) is a British social scientist. He was educated at Bedales School and King's College, Cambridge (BA in Archaeology and Social Anthropology), and the University of California, Berkeley (PhD in Linguistic Anthropology). He held posts at Cambridge, Stanford, and the Australian National University before becoming founding director of the Language and Cognition department at the Max Planck Institute for Psycholinguistics in 1991. He is currently Professor of Comparative Linguistics at Radboud University. Levinson authored the first comprehensive textbook on pragmatics (1983) and the influential *Presumptive Meanings: The Theory of Generalized Conversational Implicature* (MIT Press, 2000). He is a Fellow of the British Academy, member of the Academia Europaea, was the 2009 Hale Professor of the Linguistic Society of America, and received an honorary doctorate from Uppsala University in 2017. He served as president of the International Pragmatics Association.

Brown and Levinson met while both were pursuing graduate degrees at UC Berkeley. They married, moved to England in 1974, and worked for many years in Cambridge before both being recruited to the Max Planck Institute.

### The Publication History

The theory first appeared as an extended essay in 1978:

- **Brown, P., & Levinson, S. C. (1978).** "Universals in language usage: Politeness phenomena." In E. N. Goody (Ed.), *Questions and Politeness: Strategies in Social Interaction* (pp. 56-311). Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.

This was an essay in a collection edited by **Esther N. Goody**, a social anthropologist at Cambridge. The essay was remarkable for its ambition and length -- spanning 255 pages, it was essentially a monograph embedded within an edited volume. The analysis was grounded in the detailed study of three unrelated languages: **Tamil** (South India), **Tzeltal** (Mayan, Chiapas, Mexico), and **English** (USA and England). By examining politeness phenomena across these typologically diverse languages, Brown and Levinson sought evidence for universal principles.

In 1987, the work was republished as a standalone book with revisions:

- **Brown, P., & Levinson, S. C. (1987).** *Politeness: Some Universals in Language Usage.* Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.

The 1987 edition included a substantial new introduction (50 pages), corrections, and an updated bibliography responding to the considerable scholarly debate the 1978 version had generated. The core theoretical framework remained intact.

### Intellectual Foundations: Building on Goffman

Brown and Levinson explicitly built their theory on the sociological concept of **"face"** developed by Erving Goffman. They took Goffman's unitary concept of face and split it into two complementary aspects (positive face and negative face), creating the analytical framework that would become the most influential model in linguistic politeness research. They also drew on the work of philosopher Paul Grice, particularly his Cooperative Principle and conversational maxims, to explain how indirect speech acts function as politeness strategies. Their "Model Person" -- a hypothetical rational agent who calculates the most efficient way to achieve communicative goals while managing face -- reflects the Gricean tradition of treating communication as rational, goal-directed behavior.

## 2. Erving Goffman's Concept of FACE

### Who Was Goffman

**Erving Goffman** (1922-1982) was a Canadian-American sociologist widely regarded as the most influential sociologist of the second half of the twentieth century. His work focused on the micro-sociology of everyday interactions -- how people behave in face-to-face encounters, how they present themselves, and how social order is maintained in the smallest units of human contact.

### The Origin of "Face" as an Academic Concept

The concept of "face" in its social sense has roots in Chinese culture, where two distinct terms capture different dimensions:

- **Mianzi** (面子): prestige, status, social position -- the reputation a person has achieved through success, social standing, or displays of competence
- **Lian** (臉): moral character -- the respect of the community for a person of good moral character; the loss of lian means being condemned by the group as untrustworthy

Goffman acknowledged the Chinese source of his concept in a footnote to his seminal 1955 essay, citing works by Arthur Smith (1894), Hsien Chin Hu (1944), and Martin Yang (1947) on Chinese society. However, Goffman largely divorced the concept from its specifically Chinese cultural context, treating it instead as a universal feature of social interaction. This universalization became the foundation that Brown and Levinson later built upon.

### Goffman's Definition of Face

In his 1955 essay "On Face-Work: An Analysis of Ritual Elements of Social Interaction" (later collected in his 1967 book *Interaction Ritual: Essays on Face-to-Face Behavior*), Goffman defined face as:

> **"The positive social value a person effectively claims for himself by the line others assume he has taken during a particular contact."**

Key components of this definition:

**"Line":** A person's line is the pattern of verbal and nonverbal acts through which they express their view of a situation and their evaluation of themselves and others. Every time you speak, gesture, dress, or carry yourself in a particular way, you are taking a "line." Your line communicates: *This is who I am, this is how I see things, this is how I regard you and myself.*

**"Face":** Face is the self-image that is consistent with the line you have taken. It is not an intrinsic property of the person -- Goffman was explicit that face "is not lodged in or on [the person's] body, but rather something that is diffusely located in the flow of events in the encounter." Face is a social construct, created and maintained through interaction.

### States of Face

Goffman identified several states a person can be in regarding face:

- **"In face":** When the line a person takes presents a consistent, positive self-image that is confirmed by the evidence and supported by the judgments of others. When you are "in face," things are going smoothly -- your self-presentation matches what others expect and accept.

- **"Wrong face":** When information emerges that is incompatible with the line you have taken. You claimed to be an expert on wine, and someone catches you making an elementary mistake about grape varieties. The face you presented does not match reality.

- **"Out of face":** When you are in a situation where you have no ready line to present -- you are unprepared, caught without a viable self-image for the occasion. You arrive at a formal event in casual clothes and have no way to reframe the situation.

- **"Shamefaced":** When you experience the emotional sting of having your face damaged. This is the felt experience of losing face -- embarrassment, shame, humiliation.

### Emotional Attachment to Face

Goffman emphasized that people develop a strong **emotional attachment** to their face. If the encounter sustains an image of the person that they find comfortable and consistent, they "feel good." If their face is threatened, they experience anxiety, shame, or anger. This emotional investment is what makes face-work so consequential:

- A person whose face is sustained feels confident, secure, and can interact fluidly
- A person whose face is threatened feels anxious, defensive, and may become hostile or withdrawn
- A person who loses face experiences shame, embarrassment, or humiliation

This emotional dimension is why Goffman described participants as having a **moral commitment** to maintaining the expressive order. People feel obligated -- not just strategically motivated, but genuinely morally compelled -- to help maintain each other's faces.

### The Dramaturgical Metaphor

Goffman is best known for his **dramaturgical approach** to social life, developed most fully in *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life* (1956/1959). This metaphor treats social interaction as theatrical performance:

- **Front stage:** The visible performance -- what you present to your audience. Your clothing, speech, posture, facial expressions -- everything managed for public consumption.
- **Back stage:** The private realm where the performer prepares, rehearses, relaxes, and drops the performance. Where you can complain about the audience, fix your costume, plan your next scene.
- **Audience:** The other participants who observe and evaluate the performance.
- **Impression management:** The deliberate (and sometimes automatic) control of what information you convey to your audience.

The fundamental insight: in social interaction, people are always performing. They are always managing the impressions others form of them. This is not deception -- it is the basic mechanism by which social life operates. The performance IS the social reality.

The connection to face is direct: your "face" is the self-image you project in your front-stage performance. "Face-work" is the specific subset of impression management concerned with maintaining that self-image.

### Face-Work: The Actions People Take to Maintain Face

**Face-work** is the term Goffman used for all the actions a person takes to make their behavior consistent with face -- both their own face and the faces of others. Face-work is the operational core of Goffman's theory: it describes *what people actually do* to manage face in real-time interaction.

Goffman identified two main types of face-work processes:

#### The Avoidance Process

The most common form of face-work is simply **avoiding situations** where face might be threatened. This includes:

- **Avoiding topics** that might embarrass oneself or others ("Let's not talk about that")
- **Avoiding people** with whom face-threatening encounters are likely
- **Steering conversations** away from sensitive territory
- **Using tact and diplomacy** to prevent face-threats from arising
- **Changing the subject** when conversation drifts toward dangerous ground
- **Pretending not to notice** minor face-threats (the "studied nonobservance")

The avoidance process is *preventive*: it stops face-threats before they occur. Most of what we think of as "politeness" in everyday life falls into this category -- the thousands of small decisions we make to not say things that might cause embarrassment.

#### The Corrective Process

When face-threat cannot be avoided -- when someone *has* said or done something that damages face -- the **corrective process** kicks in. This is a ritualized sequence of moves that Goffman called an **interchange**:

1. **Challenge:** The face-threatening event is acknowledged (it can be pointed out by any participant, or simply become mutually evident)
2. **Offering:** The offender is given (or takes) the opportunity to correct the offense -- typically through an apology, explanation, or compensation
3. **Acceptance:** The offended party accepts the offering, signaling that the face-threat has been adequately addressed
4. **Gratitude:** The offender expresses thanks for being forgiven

This sequence restores what Goffman called **"ritual equilibrium"** -- the social state in which everyone's face is being adequately maintained. The length and intensity of the corrective effort matches the severity of the face-threat. A minor slip might need only a quick "sorry" and a dismissive "no problem." A serious offense might require elaborate apology, explanation, and extended reconciliation.

#### Poise

Goffman also identified **poise** as a crucial aspect of face-work. Poise is the ability to control your own embarrassment -- and therefore the embarrassment that others might feel on your behalf. A person with poise can recover smoothly from a face-threatening incident, minimizing the disruption to the interaction. A person who lacks poise amplifies the disruption -- their visible embarrassment makes everyone else uncomfortable, cascading the face-threat through the entire group.

### How Brown & Levinson Adapted Goffman's Face

Brown and Levinson took Goffman's concept and made three crucial moves:

1. **They split face into two types** (positive and negative face), where Goffman had treated it as a single, undifferentiated concept. This analytical division became the engine of the entire theory.

2. **They treated face as consisting of specific "wants"** rather than a general self-image. This made the concept more precise and more amenable to systematic analysis.

3. **They linked face directly to linguistic strategies**, showing how specific language choices correspond to specific face-management goals. Goffman had discussed face at the level of social behavior generally; Brown and Levinson showed how it works at the level of individual utterances.

## 3. Positive Face and Negative Face

### Brown and Levinson's Central Innovation

Brown and Levinson's most important theoretical move was splitting Goffman's unitary concept of face into two distinct but complementary aspects: **positive face** and **negative face**. This division is what generates the entire architecture of the theory -- different face-types yield different threats, which in turn require different strategies to mitigate.

### Positive Face: The Desire to Be Valued

**Definition:** Brown and Levinson defined positive face as:

> "The positive consistent self-image or 'personality' (crucially including the desire that this self-image be appreciated and approved of) claimed by interactants."

More specifically, positive face is:

> "The want of every member that his wants be desirable to at least some others."

**In plain language:** Positive face is your desire to be liked, approved of, appreciated, and valued by others. It is the part of your self-image that craves connection, recognition, and social belonging. When someone validates your ideas, compliments your work, laughs at your joke, or signals that they share your values -- they are nurturing your positive face.

**Positive face encompasses:**
- The desire to be liked and admired
- The desire for your goals, values, and personality to be appreciated
- The desire to be seen as a competent, worthy member of the group
- The desire for your possessions, achievements, and attributes to be positively evaluated
- The desire to feel included and connected rather than excluded and isolated
- The desire for your wants and preferences to be treated as desirable

**Examples of positive face being maintained:**
- A colleague says "That's a brilliant idea!" after your suggestion in a meeting
- A friend remembers your birthday and sends you a thoughtful message
- Someone asks for your opinion on a topic you know well, signaling they respect your expertise
- A group uses "we" language that includes you: "We should all go together"
- Someone notices your new haircut and compliments it
- A teacher writes encouraging comments on your essay

**Examples of positive face being threatened:**
- Someone ignores your suggestion in a meeting and moves on to the next topic
- A friend forgets an important event in your life
- Someone corrects your pronunciation in front of others
- A colleague rolls their eyes when you start talking
- Someone says "I don't think that's a very good idea"
- Being excluded from a group activity that others are invited to
- Receiving no response when you share something you're proud of

### Negative Face: The Desire for Autonomy

**Definition:** Brown and Levinson defined negative face as:

> "The want of every 'competent adult member' that his actions be unimpeded by others."

And more fully:

> "The basic claim to territories, personal preserves, rights to non-distraction -- i.e., to freedom of action and freedom from imposition."

**In plain language:** Negative face is your desire to be autonomous, independent, and free from interference. It is the part of your self-image that wants to make your own choices without being pressured, interrupted, burdened, or constrained by others. When someone respects your time, doesn't impose on you, and gives you space to decide freely -- they are respecting your negative face.

**Note on the word "negative":** The word "negative" here does NOT mean "bad." It means "freedom from" -- the absence of imposition, the absence of constraint. Think of it as negative liberty (freedom from interference) versus positive liberty (freedom to achieve your goals). Negative face is about what you do NOT want others to do to you.

**Negative face encompasses:**
- The desire not to be imposed upon
- The desire to have your time, space, and resources respected
- The desire to make your own decisions without pressure
- The desire not to be obligated to do things you haven't chosen
- The desire for your personal territory (physical, emotional, cognitive) to be respected
- The desire not to be watched, monitored, or evaluated without consent

**Examples of negative face being maintained:**
- A colleague says "No rush, whenever you get a chance" when asking for something
- Someone prefaces a request with "I know you're busy, so please say no if this doesn't work"
- A friend offers to help but says "Only if you want -- I don't want to intrude"
- An email says "I wanted to flag this for your awareness -- no action needed on your part"
- Someone asks permission before sitting next to you on a bench
- A boss says "Take your time deciding" rather than demanding an immediate answer

**Examples of negative face being threatened:**
- Someone asks you to do something that will take hours of your time
- A colleague assigns you a task without asking if you have capacity
- A stranger sits too close to you on public transit
- Someone insists you try a food you've said you don't want
- Receiving a phone call that demands an immediate response
- Being told "You have to come to this event" with no opt-out
- Someone looking over your shoulder while you type

### Why Brown and Levinson Split Face Into Two Types

The split is motivated by the observation that two fundamentally different kinds of social need are in play during interaction, and they generate **different kinds of politeness:**

1. **Positive face generates solidarity-based politeness:** When you attend to someone's positive face, you're saying "I like you, I value you, we're connected." This produces warmth, friendliness, in-group bonding, compliments, shared humor, and expressions of common ground. This is the politeness of closeness.

2. **Negative face generates deference-based politeness:** When you attend to someone's negative face, you're saying "I respect your autonomy, I won't impose, you're free to refuse." This produces formality, hedging, apologizing for impositions, indirectness, and respect for personal space. This is the politeness of distance.

These two orientations can **conflict**. Being very friendly (positive politeness) can feel intrusive (threatening negative face). Being very formal and distant (negative politeness) can feel cold and rejecting (threatening positive face). Much of the complexity of real-world politeness comes from navigating this tension.

### The Two Types Generate Different Strategies

The positive/negative face distinction directly generates the two main redressive strategies in Brown and Levinson's framework:

| Positive Face (desire to be valued) | Negative Face (desire for autonomy) |
|---|---|
| Threatened by: criticism, disapproval, rejection, ignoring | Threatened by: requests, orders, suggestions, impositions |
| Maintained by: compliments, inclusion, agreement, attention | Maintained by: deference, hedging, apologizing, giving options |
| Produces: Positive Politeness strategies | Produces: Negative Politeness strategies |
| Tone: warm, friendly, inclusive | Tone: formal, respectful, deferential |
| Message: "I like you and value you" | Message: "I respect your freedom and won't impose" |

## 4. Face-Threatening Acts (FTAs)

### What Is a Face-Threatening Act?

A **face-threatening act (FTA)** is any communicative act -- verbal or nonverbal -- that potentially damages someone's face. Brown and Levinson's central claim is that many ordinary speech acts inherently threaten face, and that politeness consists of the strategies speakers use to mitigate those threats.

The theory holds three universal assumptions:
1. **Everyone has face** -- every competent adult member of a society has and knows the others have positive and negative face wants
2. **Many speech acts inherently threaten face** -- certain types of communicative acts are intrinsically face-threatening
3. **Rational agents seek to minimize face-threats** -- speakers will employ strategies to reduce the damage

Brown and Levinson organized FTAs along two dimensions: **whose face is threatened** (speaker's or hearer's) and **which type of face** (positive or negative). This creates a four-cell matrix:

### 4.1 FTAs That Threaten the HEARER'S POSITIVE FACE

These are acts in which the speaker indicates (directly or indirectly) a negative evaluation of the hearer's self-image, or signals indifference to the hearer's desire to be liked and valued.

**Category A: Acts that directly evaluate the hearer negatively**

- **Criticisms:** "Your report has several serious errors." / "That's not how you do it." / "You've been performing below expectations."
- **Disapproval:** "I don't approve of your decision to quit school." / "I think that was a poor choice."
- **Complaints:** "You always leave the kitchen a mess." / "You were supposed to be here an hour ago."
- **Reprimands:** "You should know better than that." / "How many times do I have to tell you?"
- **Accusations:** "You took my parking spot deliberately." / "You're the one who broke it."
- **Insults:** "That's the stupidest thing I've ever heard." / "You're completely useless at this."
- **Contempt or ridicule:** "Oh, you think you're so clever." / Mocking someone's accent or mannerisms.
- **Contradictions or disagreements:** "No, that's wrong." / "I completely disagree." / "Actually, that's not true at all."
- **Challenges:** "Prove it." / "I don't believe you." / "What evidence do you have?"

**Category B: Acts showing the speaker doesn't care about the hearer's positive face**

- **Expressions of violent or uncontrolled emotions:** Losing your temper at someone, crying in a way that makes others uncomfortable, displaying extreme anger.
- **Raising taboo or controversial topics:** Bringing up topics the hearer finds distressing (death, finances, religion in the wrong context, personal failures).
- **Bringing bad news about the hearer:** "I have to tell you -- you didn't get the job." / "Your ex is dating someone new."
- **Raising emotionally dangerous topics:** Mentioning a topic connected to the hearer's emotional vulnerabilities.
- **Being irreverent or disrespectful:** Making light of something the hearer takes seriously.
- **Mentioning things the hearer doesn't want mentioned:** "How's the diet going?" (to someone who is self-conscious about weight).
- **Non-cooperation in conversation:** Not paying attention, looking at your phone while they talk, changing the subject abruptly, interrupting.
- **Using inappropriate forms of address:** Using someone's first name when formality is expected, or being overly formal when intimacy has been established. Calling an adult by a diminutive without permission.
- **Ignoring or snubbing:** Failing to acknowledge someone's presence. Not responding to a message. Walking past someone without greeting them.

### 4.2 FTAs That Threaten the HEARER'S NEGATIVE FACE

These are acts that impinge on the hearer's freedom of action, territory, time, or resources -- acts that impose on the hearer in some way.

**Category A: Acts that predicate a future act of the hearer (putting pressure on the hearer to do or not do something)**

- **Orders and commands:** "Finish this by Friday." / "Clean your room." / "Send me that file."
- **Requests:** "Can you help me move this weekend?" / "Would you mind proofreading my paper?"
- **Suggestions and advice:** "You should see a doctor about that." / "I think you ought to apologize."
- **Reminding:** "Don't forget you promised to call your mother." / "Remember, the deadline is tomorrow."
- **Threats:** "If you don't finish this, there will be consequences." / "I'll have to report this to your supervisor."
- **Warnings:** "Be careful with that." / "You might want to reconsider that decision."
- **Dares:** "I bet you can't do it." / "I dare you to say that to her face."

**Category B: Acts that predicate a future act of the speaker toward the hearer (creating obligations the hearer must respond to)**

- **Offers:** "Let me carry that for you." / "I'd love to help you paint your house this weekend." (The hearer is now under social pressure to accept or gracefully decline -- either way, their autonomy is constrained.)
- **Promises:** "I'll definitely be there." / "I promise I'll pay you back." (The hearer is now burdened with the obligation of being the beneficiary -- they must acknowledge, appreciate, and potentially reciprocate.)

**Category C: Acts expressing the speaker's desire toward the hearer or the hearer's possessions**

- **Compliments:** "That's a beautiful necklace." / "Your garden is gorgeous." (Paradoxically, compliments threaten negative face because they indicate the speaker has been *noticing and evaluating* the hearer's possessions or attributes, which implies monitoring and potential envy.)
- **Expressions of envy or admiration:** "I wish I had your talent." / "You're so lucky to have that house." (These imply the speaker wants what the hearer has.)
- **Expressions of strong negative emotions toward the hearer:** Expressing anger, hatred, or lust -- all of which impose on the hearer by demanding an emotional response.

### 4.3 FTAs That Threaten the SPEAKER'S POSITIVE FACE

These are acts that damage the speaker's own self-image -- things a speaker says or does that make themselves look bad.

- **Apologies:** "I'm so sorry I forgot your birthday." / "I apologize for the mistake in my calculations." (By apologizing, the speaker admits fault, which damages their image as competent and reliable.)
- **Self-criticisms and self-deprecation:** "I'm terrible at math." / "I can never remember names." / "I made a fool of myself." (The speaker directly attacks their own positive face.)
- **Confessions:** "I have to admit, I lied about my qualifications." / "I actually don't know anything about this topic." (Revealing information that damages the speaker's own image.)
- **Accepting a compliment (in certain cultures):** "Oh, this old thing? It's nothing special." (In cultures where modesty is expected, accepting a compliment too readily threatens the speaker's positive face by making them seem vain; deflecting the compliment also implicitly criticizes the speaker's own possessions.)
- **Breakdown of physical or emotional control:** Crying, trembling, stumbling -- losing composure in a way that undermines one's self-presentation.
- **Self-contradictions:** Being caught saying something that contradicts what you said earlier.
- **Self-humiliation:** Performing actions that are beneath the speaker's perceived station or dignity.
- **Admitting ignorance or inability:** "I don't know." / "I can't do this."

### 4.4 FTAs That Threaten the SPEAKER'S NEGATIVE FACE

These are acts that constrain the speaker's own freedom of action -- things the speaker feels obligated to do that impinge on their own autonomy.

- **Expressing thanks:** "Thank you so much for your help." (The speaker feels *obligated* to express gratitude; their freedom of action is constrained by the social requirement to acknowledge a debt.)
- **Accepting offers:** "Oh, that would be great, thank you." (By accepting, the speaker incurs a social debt and is now obligated to reciprocate in some way. Their future actions are constrained.)
- **Accepting thanks or apologies:** "Don't mention it." / "It's fine, really." (The speaker is socially obligated to graciously accept, constraining their freedom to express their true feelings about the matter.)
- **Making excuses:** "I'm sorry, I can't come because I have a prior commitment." (The speaker feels obligated to justify their actions to the hearer, which is an imposition on their own autonomy -- why should they have to explain themselves?)
- **Making unwilling promises:** "I suppose I can try to be there." (The speaker is pressured into committing to a future action, restricting their own freedom.)
- **Making unwilling offers:** "I guess I could help you move..." (Social pressure compels the speaker to offer something they'd prefer not to offer.)
- **Responding to faux pas:** When someone says something embarrassing to you and you are obligated to respond in a way that manages the situation -- you are constrained by the social requirement to perform face-saving work.

### The Four-Cell Matrix of FTAs (Summary)

|  | **Positive Face** (desire to be valued) | **Negative Face** (desire for autonomy) |
|---|---|---|
| **Hearer's Face** | Criticisms, insults, disagreements, disapproval, complaints, bringing bad news, ignoring, taboo topics | Requests, orders, advice, suggestions, offers, promises, reminders, threats, compliments, expressions of envy |
| **Speaker's Face** | Apologies, confessions, self-criticism, self-humiliation, admitting ignorance, loss of emotional control | Expressing thanks, accepting offers, accepting thanks/apologies, making excuses, unwilling promises |

## 5. The Five Super-Strategies for Performing FTAs

When a speaker needs to perform a face-threatening act, Brown and Levinson argue that they must make a strategic choice about HOW to do it. The theory identifies five "super-strategies" arranged in a hierarchy from most direct/least polite to most indirect/most polite:

```
Most Direct / Least Polite
  |
  |  1. Bald on-record (no mitigation)
  |  2. Positive politeness (solidarity-based redress)
  |  3. Negative politeness (deference-based redress)
  |  4. Off-record / indirect (hints and implications)
  |  5. Don't perform the FTA (stay silent)
  |
Most Indirect / Most Polite
```

The higher the perceived weight of the FTA (see Section 6: The Weight Formula), the further down this hierarchy the speaker will go. A low-weight FTA can be done bald on-record; a very high-weight FTA might not be done at all.

### Strategy 1: Bald On-Record

**Definition:** Performing the FTA directly, clearly, unambiguously, and concisely, with no attempt to mitigate the face-threat. The speaker says exactly what they mean, with maximum efficiency and no hedging.

**The basic formula:** "Do X."

**When it is used:**

1. **Emergencies and urgency:** When the situation is too urgent for politeness. "Watch out!" / "Fire!" / "Call an ambulance!" / "Get down!" In these cases, the need for speed overrides the need for face-management. Everyone understands that politeness is irrelevant when safety is at stake.

2. **Task-oriented efficiency:** When the focus is on getting a job done and both parties understand that face is not at issue. A surgeon saying "Scalpel" during an operation. A director saying "Cut!" on a film set. Air traffic control: "Descend to flight level 230."

3. **Minimal face-threat situations:** When the FTA is so minor that no mitigation is needed. "Pass the salt." / "Come in." / "Sit down." (These requests are so routine and low-cost that no one feels imposed upon.)

4. **Power asymmetry favoring the speaker:** When the speaker has clear authority over the hearer. A military officer to a subordinate: "Report to my office at 0800." A parent to a small child: "Put your shoes on." / "Stop hitting your sister."

5. **Close intimate relationships:** Among very close friends or family, the pretense of face-management can be dropped because the relationship is secure enough to absorb face-threats. "Give me a bite of that." / "Move over." / "Don't forget to take your medication."

6. **When the speaker is doing something in the hearer's interest:** "Your headlights are on!" / "Don't forget to lock the door!" / "Watch your step!" Here, the speaker is helping the hearer, so the face-threat of the directive is offset by its benevolent purpose.

7. **Sympathetic advice or warnings:** "Be careful." / "Take care of yourself."

8. **Granting permission:** When the hearer has asked for something and the speaker grants it. "Yes, go ahead." / "Sure, help yourself."

**What it communicates socially:** Directness, urgency, authority, intimacy, or indifference to social niceties. It can signal: "I have the authority to speak to you this way," or "We are so close that we don't need to bother with politeness," or "This situation is too urgent for politeness."

**Examples:**
- "Close the door." (No hedging, no softening)
- "Don't touch that." (Direct prohibition)
- "Give me your phone number." (Direct request)
- "You're wrong." (Direct disagreement)
- "Finish this by Tuesday." (Direct order)
- "Lend me your pen." (Direct request with no mitigation)

### Strategy 2: Positive Politeness (Appeal to Solidarity)

**Definition:** Performing the FTA while simultaneously attending to the hearer's positive face -- their desire to be liked, valued, and included. The speaker signals: "I recognize and appreciate you; we share common ground; I care about your wants."

Positive politeness is approach-based. It moves toward the hearer, seeking connection and closeness. It treats the hearer as an in-group member, a friend, a valued person whose desires are shared by the speaker.

**When it is used:**

- When the speaker and hearer have a relatively close relationship
- When the speaker wants to minimize the social distance between themselves and the hearer
- When the FTA is moderate and can be softened by warmth and friendliness
- When the cultural context favors solidarity over deference
- In informal settings among peers

**Brown and Levinson's 15 Positive Politeness Sub-Strategies:**

**Group A: Claim Common Ground**

1. **Notice, attend to the hearer** (their interests, wants, needs, goods): Pay attention to what the hearer cares about.
   - "Goodness, you cut your hair! By the way, I came to borrow some flour."
   - "I noticed you've been working really hard on that project."
   - "That's a great shirt -- is it new?"

2. **Exaggerate** (interest, approval, sympathy with the hearer): Use intensifiers and emphatic language.
   - "What a *fantastic* idea!"
   - "You *absolutely must* tell me about your trip!"
   - "That is *so* incredibly kind of you."

3. **Intensify interest to the hearer**: Make the conversation vivid and engaging; use narrative, direct quotation, tag questions.
   - "You'll never guess what happened next..."
   - "So there I was, right, and he says to me..."
   - "It was the most amazing thing, wasn't it?"

4. **Use in-group identity markers**: Use language that signals shared group membership -- slang, jargon, dialect, nicknames, in-group terms of address.
   - "Hey bro, can you help me out?"
   - "Mate, could you pass me that?"
   - Using technical jargon shared by the group: "Can you push the latest commit to staging?"

5. **Seek agreement**: Raise "safe" topics where agreement is likely; repeat or rephrase what the hearer said to show alignment.
   - "I know, right? The weather has been terrible."
   - "Isn't it? I thought the same thing."
   - Repeating: Hearer: "I went to Paris." Speaker: "Paris! How wonderful!"

6. **Avoid disagreement**: Use token agreement, hedged opinions, or white lies to avoid direct contradiction.
   - "Well, yes, in a way... but..."
   - "I can see what you mean, although..."
   - "Sort of, yes."
   - (Instead of "No, you're wrong": "Well, I see it a little differently...")

7. **Presuppose, raise, or assert common ground**: Use gossip, small talk, or shared knowledge to establish that both parties share a perspective.
   - "Isn't it always the way?" (presupposing shared experience)
   - "You know how hard it is to find good coffee around here."
   - "As we both know..."

8. **Joke**: Use humor to put the hearer at ease and signal that the FTA is not serious.
   - "How about lending me your car for the weekend? (laughs) No but seriously, could I borrow your drill?"
   - "Well, *somebody* forgot to buy milk again! (winks)"
   - Using humor to soften a criticism: "I see you've gone for the 'creative' approach to spelling again."

**Group B: Convey that Speaker and Hearer Are Cooperators**

9. **Assert or presuppose the speaker's knowledge of and concern for the hearer's wants**: Show that you know what the hearer needs and that you care.
   - "I know you're really busy right now, so I'll keep this quick."
   - "Look, I know you want to finish that on time, so let me help."
   - "I understand this is important to you."

10. **Offer, promise**: Demonstrate good intentions by offering to help or promising positive outcomes.
    - "I'll help you with that report tomorrow."
    - "If you need anything, just let me know."
    - "I promise I'll have it done by Monday."

11. **Be optimistic**: Assume the hearer will want to cooperate; presuppose shared goals.
    - "I'm sure you won't mind lending me your notes."
    - "You'll help me with this, won't you?"
    - "I figured we could work on this together."

12. **Include both speaker and hearer in the activity**: Use inclusive "we" and "let's" forms.
    - "Let's get this done together."
    - "Shall we go for a coffee?"
    - "Let's see what we can do about this."

13. **Give (or ask for) reasons**: Explain why you are making the request, treating the hearer as a rational agent.
    - "I need to borrow your car because mine is in the shop."
    - "Could you help me move? I'm having surgery next week and can't lift anything."
    - "The reason I'm asking is..."

14. **Assume or assert reciprocity**: Remind the hearer that cooperation goes both ways.
    - "I helped you last time, so..."
    - "I'll do the same for you next time."
    - "You'd do the same for me, right?"

15. **Give gifts to the hearer**: Not just physical gifts, but gifts of sympathy, understanding, and cooperation.
    - Listening to the hearer's problems with genuine attention
    - Offering food or drink
    - Giving compliments, showing care: "You look like you could use a break. Let me get you some tea."

**What positive politeness communicates socially:** "We are friends. We are in the same group. I value you. I see you. We share the same wants and goals. I am not a threat to your self-image."

### Strategy 3: Negative Politeness (Appeal to Autonomy)

**Definition:** Performing the FTA while simultaneously attending to the hearer's negative face -- their desire for autonomy, freedom from imposition, and respect for their time and choices. The speaker signals: "I recognize this is an imposition; I respect your right to refuse; I am minimizing my intrusion."

Negative politeness is avoidance-based. It creates distance between the speaker and hearer, showing deference and self-effacement. It acknowledges that the speaker IS imposing and tries to minimize the damage.

**When it is used:**

- When the speaker and hearer are socially distant or the relationship is formal
- When the speaker has less power than the hearer
- When the imposition is significant
- When the cultural context favors deference over solidarity (e.g., many Western and East Asian contexts for formal interactions)
- In professional settings, with strangers, or with social superiors

**Brown and Levinson's 10 Negative Politeness Sub-Strategies:**

1. **Be conventionally indirect**: Use indirect speech acts that are nonetheless understood as having a specific communicative purpose.
   - "Could you pass the salt?" (indirect request understood as a request, not a question about ability)
   - "I was wondering if you might be free on Saturday?"
   - "Would it be possible to extend the deadline?"

2. **Question, hedge**: Use hedges (words or phrases that soften a statement) and questions rather than assertions.
   - "I think perhaps it might be better if we..." (multiple hedges)
   - "Sort of" / "kind of" / "in a way" / "more or less"
   - "It seems like there could be an issue, possibly."
   - "If I'm not mistaken..." / "Correct me if I'm wrong..."

3. **Be pessimistic**: Assume the hearer is unlikely to comply, giving them an easy out.
   - "I don't suppose you'd be willing to help me move?"
   - "You couldn't possibly lend me your car, could you?"
   - "I imagine you're probably too busy, but..."
   - "There's no chance you could...?"

4. **Minimize the imposition**: Downplay the size of the request.
   - "Could I borrow just a tiny bit of sugar?"
   - "I just need a second of your time."
   - "It's a small thing, really."
   - "If you could just take a quick look..."

5. **Give deference**: Humble yourself and/or elevate the hearer.
   - "You know so much more about this than I do -- could you possibly advise me?"
   - "I'd really value your expert opinion on this."
   - Using formal titles: "Professor Smith, I was wondering..."
   - "With all due respect, sir..."

6. **Apologize**: Express reluctance, acknowledge the imposition, or beg forgiveness.
   - "I'm terribly sorry to bother you, but..."
   - "I hate to ask this, but..."
   - "Forgive me for intruding, but..."
   - "I know this is a terrible imposition, but I really need your help."
   - Admitting the impingement: "I know this is a big ask..."
   - Indicating reluctance: "I wouldn't normally ask, but..."
   - Giving overwhelming reasons: "I've tried everything else and you're the only person who can help."

7. **Impersonalize speaker and hearer**: Avoid the pronouns "I" and "you" to dissociate both parties from the FTA.
   - "It is necessary that this form be completed." (instead of "You need to fill out this form")
   - "One does not simply walk into Mordor." (instead of "You can't just walk in")
   - "Passengers are requested to remain seated." (instead of "Sit down")
   - Using passive voice: "It has been decided that..." (instead of "I have decided that...")
   - Using performatives: "Visitors are reminded to sign the guest book."

8. **State the FTA as a general rule**: Frame the request not as your personal demand but as an impersonal requirement.
   - "Regulations require that all visitors sign in."
   - "Company policy states that receipts must be submitted within 30 days."
   - "It's generally expected that one RSVP's before the event."
   - "The rules say no food in the library."

9. **Nominalize**: Use noun forms rather than verb forms to increase formality and distance.
   - "Your prompt completion of this task would be appreciated." (instead of "Please complete this quickly")
   - "An inspection of the premises is required." (instead of "We need to inspect the premises")
   - "Your consideration of this matter is requested." (instead of "Please think about this")

10. **Go on record as incurring a debt, or as not indebting the hearer**: Acknowledge the obligation you are creating.
    - "I'd be eternally grateful if you could..."
    - "I'll owe you one."
    - "I'll make it up to you, I promise."
    - "Don't worry about repaying me -- it's a gift."

**What negative politeness communicates socially:** "I know I'm imposing. I respect your right to refuse. I'm minimizing my intrusion. I don't presume you will comply. You are a free agent whose autonomy I respect."

**Note:** Negative politeness is the most culturally familiar form of "politeness" in English and many Western cultures. When English speakers think of "being polite," they typically think of negative politeness strategies -- hedging, apologizing, being indirect, showing deference. This is the politeness of strangers, formal letters, and professional interactions.

### Strategy 4: Off-Record / Indirect

**Definition:** Performing the FTA in such a way that the speaker cannot be held to have committed themselves to any particular interpretation. The speaker hints, implies, or suggests, leaving it to the hearer to figure out the intended meaning. If challenged, the speaker can plausibly deny having made the face-threatening request at all.

**The key feature:** **Plausible deniability.** The speaker wants something but expresses it in a way that allows them to deny it. "I never *asked* you to do that -- I just made an observation."

**When it is used:**

- When the FTA is very serious and the speaker wants to avoid all responsibility
- When the relationship is such that a direct request would be too presumptuous
- When the speaker wants to test the waters without committing
- When the speaker wants to preserve the illusion that no imposition is being made
- When there is great uncertainty about how the request would be received

**Brown and Levinson's 15 Off-Record Sub-Strategies:**

**Group 1: Invite conversational implicatures (violate Gricean maxims)**

1. **Give hints:** State a reason that implies the desired action.
   - "It's cold in here." (hint: close the window / turn on the heat)
   - "I haven't eaten since breakfast." (hint: offer me food / let's go eat)
   - "That looks heavy." (hint: offer to help carry it)

2. **Give association clues:** Mention something associated with the desired action.
   - "Oh, there's that new Italian place around the corner." (hint: let's go eat there)
   - "My car is in the shop again." (hint: I need a ride)

3. **Presuppose:** State something that logically presupposes the desired action.
   - "I washed the car again today." (implication: someone else should have done it)
   - "When are you going to clean up?" (presupposes: you need to clean up)

4. **Understate:** Say less than is needed, violating the Gricean maxim of quantity.
   - "He's not exactly the friendliest person." (meaning: he's quite rude)
   - "That's not a bad idea." (meaning: that's a good idea)
   - "There seems to be a small problem." (meaning: there's a significant problem)

5. **Overstate:** Exaggerate to the point where the literal meaning is clearly not intended.
   - "I've told you a million times." (meaning: I've told you several times)
   - "Everyone is talking about it." (meaning: some people mentioned it)
   - "There were tons of people there." (meaning: it was fairly crowded)

6. **Use tautologies:** Say something obviously true that conveys an implicature.
   - "Boys will be boys." (meaning: don't expect them to change / excuse their behavior)
   - "War is war." (meaning: terrible things happen in war; accept it)
   - "If he does it, he does it." (meaning: we can't control him)

7. **Use contradictions:** Say something self-contradictory to generate an implicature.
   - "Well, I do and I don't." (meaning: it's complicated)
   - "He is and he isn't my friend." (meaning: the relationship is ambiguous)

8. **Be ironic:** Say the opposite of what you mean.
   - "What lovely weather!" (said during a downpour -- meaning: the weather is terrible)
   - "Oh, brilliant, just brilliant." (meaning: this is terrible)
   - "Well, that went well." (after a disaster)

9. **Use metaphors:** Use figurative language whose literal meaning does not match the intended meaning.
   - "He's a real piece of work." (meaning: he's difficult to deal with)
   - "She bit my head off." (meaning: she responded angrily)
   - "We're drowning in paperwork." (meaning: we have too much paperwork)

10. **Use rhetorical questions:** Ask a question whose answer is obvious, implying the answer as a criticism or request.
    - "How many times do I have to ask you?" (meaning: stop making me repeat myself)
    - "Who cares?" (meaning: it doesn't matter)
    - "What can I do?" (meaning: there's nothing I can do)
    - "Is that really necessary?" (meaning: I don't think it's necessary)

**Group 2: Be vague or ambiguous (violate the Gricean maxim of manner)**

11. **Be ambiguous:** Use language with multiple possible interpretations.
    - "Nice shoes." (compliment or sarcasm? The hearer must decide)
    - "That's interesting." (genuinely interesting or dismissive? Unclear)

12. **Be vague:** Use imprecise language.
    - "Perhaps someone should do something about that."
    - "A certain person has been causing trouble."
    - "I heard something about it."

13. **Over-generalize:** Make general statements that can apply to the specific situation.
    - "People who live in glass houses shouldn't throw stones." (meaning: you shouldn't criticize others given your own faults)
    - "Mature people sometimes have to do things they don't want to do."
    - "Good employees are always willing to help their colleagues."

14. **Displace the hearer:** Address the FTA to someone who is NOT the intended target, in the presence of the intended target.
    - Telling one child to behave while looking pointedly at the misbehaving child
    - Loudly telling a friend "I hate it when people don't clean up after themselves" while a messy roommate is within earshot

15. **Be incomplete:** Use ellipsis -- start to say something but leave it unfinished.
    - "Well, if you had studied harder..." (trailing off, letting the implication speak for itself)
    - "I mean, it's not like I don't have other things to do..." (trailing off)
    - "Oh, I thought you were..." (not finishing the sentence)

**What off-record strategies communicate socially:** "I haven't actually asked you for anything. I just made an observation / told a joke / stated a fact. If you happen to infer that I want something and choose to act on that inference, that's your decision."

**The risk:** The hearer might not pick up on the hint at all. If you say "It's cold in here," the hearer might respond "Yeah, it is" and do nothing about the window. Off-record strategies sacrifice clarity for face-protection.

### Strategy 5: Don't Perform the FTA

**Definition:** The speaker decides not to perform the face-threatening act at all. They stay silent, change the subject, or find an alternative way to achieve their goal that does not involve threatening face.

**When it is used:**

- When the FTA is very serious and no mitigation strategy seems adequate
- When the social risk is too high
- When the relationship cannot absorb the face-threat
- When the speaker decides the cost of performing the FTA exceeds the benefit
- When there is extreme power differential against the speaker

**Examples:**

- Not telling your boss their idea is terrible, because the professional risk is too high
- Not asking a new acquaintance for a large favor, because the relationship can't support it yet
- Choosing not to criticize a friend's partner, because it could damage the friendship
- Biting your tongue when someone says something you disagree with, because starting an argument isn't worth it
- Not requesting a raise during a period of company layoffs

**What it communicates socially:** Nothing -- literally. The FTA is never performed, so no social signal is sent. But the speaker may pay an internal cost: frustration, resentment, unmet needs, or the sense that they have been unable to express themselves authentically. The avoidance itself can create problems over time (accumulated resentment, misunderstandings, unresolved issues).

### The Hierarchy in Practice

The five strategies form a continuum of increasing face-protection:

| Strategy | Face-protection | Clarity | Risk to relationship |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Bald on-record | None | Maximum | Highest |
| 2. Positive politeness | Moderate (positive face) | High | Moderate |
| 3. Negative politeness | Moderate (negative face) | Moderate-High | Low-Moderate |
| 4. Off-record | High | Low | Low |
| 5. Don't do FTA | Maximum | Zero | None (but unmet needs) |

There is a fundamental **trade-off between clarity and face-protection**. The more you protect face, the less clear your message becomes. The clearer your message, the more face-risk you take. This trade-off is central to the entire theory.

## 6. The Weight Formula: W = D + P + R

### The Formula

Brown and Levinson proposed a formula for calculating the **weightiness** (seriousness) of a face-threatening act:

> **Wx = D(S,H) + P(H,S) + Rx**

Where:
- **Wx** = the weightiness (seriousness) of the FTA x
- **D(S,H)** = the social distance between the speaker (S) and the hearer (H)
- **P(H,S)** = the relative power of the hearer (H) over the speaker (S)
- **Rx** = the absolute ranking of the imposition of the act x in the particular culture

The formula predicts: **The greater the total weight, the more polite (more indirect, more mitigating) the speaker's chosen strategy will be.**

### The Three Variables in Detail

#### D: Social Distance

**What it measures:** The degree of familiarity, closeness, and social connection between the speaker and hearer.

**Low D (close relationship):**
- Best friends
- Spouses and partners
- Close family members
- Long-term colleagues who socialize outside work
- Members of a tight-knit community

**High D (distant relationship):**
- Complete strangers
- New acquaintances
- Someone you've met once at a party
- A person from a very different social world
- Someone you interact with only through institutional roles (bank teller, government official)

**How D affects politeness:** When D is low (close relationship), less politeness is needed because the relationship can absorb face-threats. Close friends can be blunt with each other. When D is high (distant relationship), more politeness is needed because there is no relational cushion to absorb face-threats.

**Example:**
- To your best friend: "Hand me that wrench." (bald on-record; D is low)
- To a stranger in a hardware store: "Excuse me, I'm terribly sorry to bother you, but would you mind reaching that wrench on the top shelf for me?" (negative politeness; D is high)

#### P: Relative Power

**What it measures:** The degree to which the hearer has power, authority, or social status over the speaker. This is specifically the power of the **hearer over the speaker** -- P(H,S).

**Low P (speaker and hearer are equals or speaker has more power):**
- Two colleagues at the same level
- Two friends with similar social standing
- A boss speaking to a subordinate (boss has the power)
- A teacher speaking to a student (teacher has the power)

**High P (hearer has more power than speaker):**
- An employee speaking to their boss
- A student speaking to a professor
- A private speaking to a general
- A citizen speaking to a judge
- A junior doctor speaking to a senior consultant

**How P affects politeness:** When P(H,S) is high (the hearer has power over the speaker), the speaker will use more polite strategies. When P(H,S) is low (the speaker has equal or greater power), less politeness is needed.

**Example:**
- Employee to boss: "Dr. Martinez, I was wondering if there might be any possibility of discussing my workload at some point that's convenient for you?" (heavy negative politeness; P is high)
- Boss to employee: "Come see me about your workload." (bald on-record; P is reversed -- speaker has the power)

#### R: Absolute Ranking of the Imposition

**What it measures:** How big the imposition is -- how much the act costs the hearer in terms of time, resources, effort, emotional labor, or face-damage. This ranking is culture-specific and situation-specific.

**Low R (small imposition):**
- Asking someone what time it is
- Asking someone to pass the salt
- Asking someone to hold the elevator
- Telling someone they have spinach in their teeth

**High R (large imposition):**
- Asking someone to lend you a large sum of money
- Asking someone to help you move (entire day commitment)
- Telling someone their spouse is cheating on them
- Asking someone to change their deeply held religious or political views
- Asking a stranger for a ride to the airport
- Telling someone they are being fired

**How R affects politeness:** The greater the imposition, the more politeness is needed. Small requests need minimal mitigation. Enormous requests require elaborate face-work.

**Example:**
- Low R: "What time is it?" (bald on-record; the imposition is trivial)
- High R: "I know this is an enormous ask, and I completely understand if you can't, but I've found myself in a terrible situation and I was wondering if there's any possibility that you might be able to lend me five thousand dollars? I would pay you back within six months, and I'd put it in writing." (heavy negative politeness with multiple mitigation strategies; R is very high)

### Worked Examples: How Changing D, P, and R Changes the Strategy

#### Example 1: Asking someone to close a window

**Scenario A: Low D, Low P, Low R** (asking your roommate)
- D = low (you live together, close relationship)
- P = low (equals)
- R = low (closing a window takes 2 seconds)
- **Total weight: LOW**
- **Strategy: Bald on-record**
- **Utterance:** "Close the window, would you?"

**Scenario B: High D, Low P, Low R** (asking a stranger on a bus)
- D = high (complete stranger)
- P = low (equals)
- R = low (closing a window takes 2 seconds)
- **Total weight: MODERATE**
- **Strategy: Negative politeness**
- **Utterance:** "Excuse me, would you mind closing that window?"

**Scenario C: High D, High P, Low R** (asking a senior executive during a meeting)
- D = high (different organizational levels)
- P = high (they outrank you significantly)
- R = low (closing a window is still a small act)
- **Total weight: HIGH**
- **Strategy: Off-record**
- **Utterance:** "It's gotten quite cold in here, hasn't it?"

#### Example 2: Asking to borrow a car

**Scenario A: Low D, Low P, Low R** (asking your spouse to use the car)
- D = low (married partners)
- P = low (equals)
- R = low (you share the car regularly)
- **Total weight: LOW**
- **Strategy: Bald on-record**
- **Utterance:** "I'm taking the car today."

**Scenario B: Low D, Low P, High R** (asking your best friend who only has one car and needs it)
- D = low (best friends)
- P = low (equals)
- R = high (significant imposition -- they'd be without transportation)
- **Total weight: MODERATE-HIGH**
- **Strategy: Positive politeness with some negative politeness**
- **Utterance:** "Hey, I know this is a big ask, and I totally understand if you can't, but my car just died and I've got that job interview tomorrow. Any chance I could borrow yours? I'd fill up the tank and have it back by noon."

**Scenario C: High D, Low P, High R** (asking an acquaintance you barely know)
- D = high (barely know each other)
- P = low (equals)
- R = high (significant imposition)
- **Total weight: VERY HIGH**
- **Strategy: Don't perform the FTA / Off-record at most**
- **Utterance:** You probably don't ask at all. Or at most: "I'm in a real bind with transportation tomorrow. I've tried everything -- rental places are sold out..." (off-record hint, leaving it to the acquaintance to offer)

**Scenario D: High D, High P, High R** (asking a CEO you've just met at a conference)
- D = high (just met)
- P = high (they're a powerful person)
- R = high (a car is a major possession)
- **Total weight: MAXIMUM**
- **Strategy: Don't perform the FTA**
- **Utterance:** You would never ask this. The combined weight of distance, power, and imposition makes the request virtually unthinkable.

#### Example 3: Telling someone their work has errors

**Scenario A: Low D, Speaker has power** (a mentor reviewing their mentee's work)
- D = low (established relationship)
- P = low (speaker has the power; P measures hearer's power over speaker)
- R = moderate (criticism stings, but this is the mentor's role)
- **Total weight: LOW-MODERATE**
- **Strategy: Positive politeness**
- **Utterance:** "Hey, this is really solid work overall. I noticed a couple of things we should look at together -- nothing major, but let's make it even stronger."

**Scenario B: High D, Hearer has power** (a new employee pointing out errors in the boss's report)
- D = high (new employee, not yet established)
- P = high (the boss has power over the employee)
- R = high (criticizing a superior's work is face-threatening for both parties)
- **Total weight: VERY HIGH**
- **Strategy: Off-record or heavy negative politeness**
- **Utterance:** "I may be completely wrong about this, and please correct me if I'm misunderstanding, but I was looking at the figures on page seven and I noticed some numbers that seemed like they might not quite match up with the Q3 data. I thought you'd want to know, just in case."

### Important Notes About the Formula

1. **It is additive, not multiplicative.** Brown and Levinson presented it as a simple sum. This means any single variable can drive up the weight: even between close friends (D = low), asking to borrow $10,000 (R = very high) produces a high-weight FTA.

2. **The variables are not numerical scales with fixed units.** Brown and Levinson did not assign specific numbers. The formula is a *conceptual tool* for thinking about what factors influence politeness choices, not a literal mathematical calculation. You don't compute "D = 3, P = 2, R = 5, therefore W = 10." Rather, you assess: "We're pretty close, they don't have much power over me, but this is a huge ask, so I need to be quite polite."

3. **R is culture-specific.** What counts as a big imposition varies by culture. In some cultures, asking to stay at someone's house is a trivial request (low R); in others, it's a major imposition (high R). Asking about someone's salary is low R in some cultures and high R in others.

4. **The formula predicts strategy level, not specific words.** It tells you WHETHER you'll be bald on-record, positive polite, negative polite, off-record, or silent -- but it doesn't tell you which specific sub-strategy or which exact words to use.

5. **Critics note that the formula may be too simple.** Some scholars argue that the variables interact rather than simply adding up -- for instance, that very high power differentials might amplify the effect of high R, rather than just adding to it. Others argue that additional variables are needed, such as the emotional state of the participants, the specific cultural context, or the nature of the relationship (e.g., competitive vs. cooperative). Research has shown that power and imposition tend to be more predictive than social distance.
