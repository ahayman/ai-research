# Hedging and Autism: Research Synthesis

## Purpose
Research synthesis for a knowledge base teaching English pragmatics to autistic individuals. This document covers the intersection of hedging (epistemic, approximative, and performative) and autism spectrum conditions, drawing on empirical research, pragmatic theory, conversation analysis, cross-cultural linguistics, and the neurodiversity paradigm.

---

## 1. Autistic Production of Hedges

### 1.1 The General Finding: Fewer Hedges, Particularly Negative Politeness Hedges

Multiple lines of evidence converge on the finding that autistic individuals produce fewer hedging devices in their speech compared to non-autistic peers. However, as with discourse markers (Category 5), this finding is not uniform across all hedge types and the underlying reasons are more complex than a simple deficit account suggests.

**The Tilburg University Study (Butterbrod) — [PRELIMINARY, methodological caveat]:**
Tim Butterbrod's Bachelor's thesis at Tilburg University, "The Effect of Autistic Traits on the Use of Politeness Strategies," is often cited for the finding that higher autistic-trait scores predict lower use of negative politeness markers. A methodological caveat is essential: the study used typically-developing university students scoring on the Autism-Spectrum Quotient (Baron-Cohen et al. 2001), not clinically diagnosed autistic participants, and the data came from fictive elicited scenarios rather than natural interaction. The finding should therefore be read as a preliminary signal about autistic-trait variation in a non-clinical population, not as a finding about clinical autism.

With that caveat in place, the reported pattern: participants higher on autistic traits produced "significantly lower overall politeness markers compared to individuals lower on autistic traits, with differences primarily driven by lower use of negative politeness markers." If this signal replicates in clinical samples and natural interaction, it would suggest that the deficit is not across all politeness but concentrated in *negative* politeness strategies — the hedges, softeners, downtoners, indirect forms, and imposition-minimizers that Brown & Levinson (1987) categorize as tools for respecting the hearer's autonomy and freedom from imposition. Positive politeness markers (warmth, solidarity, in-group markers) were less affected.

- Source: Butterbrod, Tilburg University Bachelor's thesis (arno.uvt.nl/show.cgi?fid=133361). Evidence level: [PRELIMINARY], non-clinical sample, elicited task.

**The Voicemail Elicitation Task Study — [CITATION UNCERTAIN]:**
A small study (ASD n=22, controls n=10) published in the *Journal of the International Neuropsychological Society* reportedly found that autistic young adults produced fewer social-modal hedges ("would," "could," "may," "might") in employment voicemail role-play scenarios than neurotypical controls, with the difference concentrated in modals that serve a social rather than informational function. **The full bibliographic details for this study could not be verified during Stage 3 Research Validation** and should be re-located or removed before this section is published. If the direction of the finding holds, it supports the same pattern documented elsewhere: autistic speakers show reduced social-politeness hedging in task-specific workplace contexts.
- Source: *Journal of the International Neuropsychological Society* (full citation pending verification).

**Computational Pragmatic Analysis (Lake, Bhullar & Bhattacharyya, 2022):**
A computational linguistics study collected transcribed natural conversations where ASD and non-ASD participants completed collaborative tasks with neurotypical peers, investigating three pragmatic features: politeness, uncertainty, and informativeness. Neural models trained with BERT embeddings could predict these pragmatic features with F1 scores exceeding 0.82, confirming that measurable, systematic differences exist in how autistic adults deploy hedging and uncertainty markers in conversation.
- Source: Lake, Bhullar & Bhattacharyya (2022), PMC9633181.

**The Tikiz-Erturk (2025) Case Study:**
This qualitative study of a 15-year-old ASD language learner found that despite "persistent challenges in comprehending abstract concepts, conversational implicature, and social role recognition," the participant "employed several adaptive strategies, including politeness strategies, off-record communication, and explicit verbal markers." Over time, the participant showed progress in using both direct and indirect politeness techniques, though gains were "context-dependent and most evident in structured interactions." This suggests hedging and politeness strategies are learnable for autistic individuals, especially in structured or scaffolded environments.
- Source: Tikiz-Erturk, 2025, International Journal of Applied Linguistics (onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ijal.12783)

### 1.2 Which Types of Hedges Are Reduced?

Drawing on Prince et al.'s (1982) taxonomy of hedges, we can map the autistic hedging profile onto specific hedge types:

**Prince et al.'s (1982) Taxonomy:**
- **Approximators** (change the truth value of the proposition):
  - *Adaptors*: "sort of," "kind of," "to some extent" -- make nearly-true assertions seem closer to reality
  - *Rounders*: "approximately," "about," "around" -- provide imprecise but adequate values
- **Shields** (describe the speaker's relationship to the proposition without changing truth value):
  - *Plausibility shields*: "I think," "it seems," "probably," "maybe" -- signal speaker uncertainty
  - *Attribution shields*: "according to X," "research suggests," "they say" -- attribute claims to other sources

**Predicted autistic reduction pattern (synthesized from available evidence):**

1. **Plausibility shields -- likely most reduced.** These are the core negative politeness hedges. Saying "I think" or "it seems" when you actually *know* conflicts with the autistic value of epistemic honesty (see Section 2.2). The Tilburg finding of reduced negative politeness markers strongly implicates this category, since plausibility shields are the primary mechanism for performing epistemic deference.

2. **Approximators (adaptors) -- likely reduced for different reasons.** "Sort of" and "kind of" introduce imprecision that may feel actively misleading to someone who values precision. An autistic speaker might reject "sort of blue" in favor of "cerulean" or "blue-grey" -- not because they lack the hedging device, but because they prefer the precise term.

3. **Rounders -- likely less reduced.** "About five minutes" or "approximately 200" serve informational rather than social functions. They convey genuine imprecision in the speaker's knowledge. Because they are *accurate* (signaling that the speaker genuinely lacks a precise figure), they align with epistemic honesty rather than conflicting with it. Autistic speakers comfortable saying "I don't know the exact number" should find rounders relatively natural.

4. **Attribution shields -- likely less reduced.** "According to the study" or "the report says" serve an information-management function that may align with autistic cognitive styles, especially systemizing. Attributing claims to sources is an accuracy practice, not a social face-saving one. (However, performative attribution shields used purely for social distancing -- "Some people might say..." when you mean "I think..." -- may be reduced.)

5. **Hedged performatives -- likely reduced.** "I would suggest," "I might say," "could I ask" use modal verbs to attenuate illocutionary force. These are quintessentially negative politeness devices and the VET study specifically found reduced use of "would," "could," "may," "might" in workplace-relevant scenarios.

### 1.3 Connection to the "Um" vs. "Uh" Research (Category 5)

The hedging reduction profile parallels the discourse marker findings in Category 5, particularly the "um" vs. "uh" dissociation.

**The parallel:**
- "Um" is listener-oriented -- it manages the listener's expectations. It is a hedge against conversational disruption. Autistic speakers produce "um" significantly less than non-autistic peers (Lake et al., 2011; Gorman et al., 2016).
- "Uh" is speaker-oriented -- it reflects the speaker's own processing difficulty. Autistic speakers produce "uh" at comparable rates to non-autistic peers.
- Similarly, hedges that serve *social/listener-oriented* functions (plausibility shields, hedged performatives) appear more reduced than hedges that serve *informational/speaker-oriented* functions (rounders, attribution shields).

**The unifying principle:** The specific hedging devices that are reduced in autistic speech are those that require the speaker to model the listener's expectations and adjust their assertion accordingly -- the same listener-modeling that underlies "um" production. Hedging devices that simply encode the speaker's own epistemic state (genuine uncertainty markers) appear less affected.

**Recent nuance (2023):** A study by researchers including those at the University of Edinburgh found that autistic adults produced filled pauses at the same rate as non-autistic adults and showed an equivalent preference for "uhm" over "uh." The difference was in *prosodic realization*: non-autistic speakers produced filled pauses with a canonical level pitch contour more often, while autistic speakers showed more intonational variation. This suggests that even when hedging devices are present at similar rates, their *delivery* may differ in ways that affect how listeners interpret them.
- Source: PMC (pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11286642/)

### 1.4 How Autistic Hedging Differs from L2 English Learner Hedging

Both autistic English speakers and L2 English learners may produce fewer hedges than native neurotypical speakers, but the underlying reasons are fundamentally different. This comparison is instructive for understanding what hedging difficulty means in each case.

**L2 English learner under-hedging:**
- L2 learners at elementary and intermediate levels significantly underuse hedges compared to native speakers and advanced L2 learners.
- L2 speakers of English used only 20 out of 102 potential hedging expressions examined in one study, whereas native English-speaking teachers used about half.
- L2 learners "become so direct in their English academic prose that it is deemed inappropriate by native speakers" -- a failure attributed to not yet acquiring L2 pragmatic fluency.
- The cause is primarily *competence-based*: L2 learners have not yet acquired the hedging repertoire or the sociopragmatic knowledge of when to deploy it.
- Sources: University of Hawaii SLS (hawaii.edu/sls/wp-content/uploads/2014/09/Wishnoff.pdf); Norwegian Journal of Language Teaching and Linguistics (journal.uia.no)

**Autistic English speaker under-hedging:**
- Autistic L1 English speakers have full linguistic access to hedging forms -- they can produce "I think," "maybe," "sort of" when asked to.
- The reduction is driven by different mechanisms: epistemic honesty (reluctance to hedge what is known), precision preference (resistance to introducing imprecision), processing load (hedging requires simultaneous social monitoring), and values (directness as an authentic communication style).
- The cause is primarily *performance-based* (in the pragmatic sense) and *values-based*: the hedging repertoire is available but is either deprioritized, filtered by different values, or dropped under cognitive load.

**Key difference:** L2 learners typically *want* to hedge but lack the forms; autistic speakers typically *have* the forms but have principled reasons for not using them, or find the social-monitoring cost too high. Teaching hedging to these two groups requires fundamentally different approaches: L2 learners need repertoire building; autistic speakers need context-mapping and explicit decision frameworks.

## 2. Why Autistic Speakers May Hedge Less

### 2.1 Preference for Precision and Accuracy

The most commonly cited and most intuitive explanation is the autistic preference for precision over vagueness. This maps onto Grice's Maxim of Quantity and Quality simultaneously -- saying exactly what you mean, with exactly the certainty you have.

**How precision conflicts with hedging:**
- Approximators ("sort of," "kind of") introduce imprecision that an autistic speaker may experience as actively misleading. If you know the meeting is at 3:00 PM, saying "around 3" feels wrong -- it is less accurate than the information you possess.
- Plausibility shields ("I think," "maybe," "probably") can feel dishonest when the speaker *does* know. Saying "I think the answer is X" when you are confident the answer is X violates the commitment to honest communication.
- This is not a deficit in understanding hedging -- it is a principled objection to using language that misrepresents one's epistemic state.

**Cross-reference to autistic values research:**
Research on autistic communication values (Crompton et al., Scandinavian Journal of Disability Research) found that autistic people "prioritize honesty, directness, clarity, and authenticity." More bluntly: "Neurodivergent people seem more preoccupied with information, accuracy, solutions, truth, and correctness, whereas neurotypical people seem more preoccupied with status, power, appearance, gathering allies, and defining groups."
- Source: Scandinavian Journal of Disability Research (sjdr.se/articles/10.16993/sjdr.1184)

### 2.2 Epistemic Honesty: Reluctance to Claim Less Certainty Than Felt

This is a more specific version of the precision argument and deserves its own treatment because it represents a genuine values conflict, not merely a processing difference.

**The epistemic honesty principle:**
Many autistic individuals report that hedging feels dishonest when it does not reflect genuine uncertainty. Consider:

- "I think the deadline is Friday" -- If you KNOW the deadline is Friday, adding "I think" is a lie. It represents an epistemic state (uncertainty) that you do not hold.
- "Maybe we should try a different approach" -- If you are quite sure the current approach is failing, "maybe" misrepresents your confidence level.
- "It seems like the data shows X" -- If the data clearly shows X, "it seems like" implies doubt you do not feel.

For many autistic speakers, this is not a subtle or academic point. The experience is: "You're asking me to say something that isn't true about my own mental state in order to make other people feel more comfortable." This feels fundamentally wrong to someone for whom "a need to be authentic is inherent" and for whom inauthentic social performance creates significant psychological distress.
- Source: Relational Psych (relationalpsych.group); Embrace Autism (embrace-autism.com)

**Connection to Intolerance of Uncertainty (IU):**
Research on autism and IU adds nuance. Wilson & Bishop's (2022) Stage 2 registered report in *PeerJ* investigating preference for certainty in autistic adults found that IU was correlated with pragmatic processing difficulty, supporting the idea that "discomfort with uncertainty plays a role in pragmatic language and communication challenges in autism." However, the study found that both accuracy and confidence were reduced on pragmatic inference tasks -- not confidence alone. Autistic adults did not show a selective *preference* for certainty so much as general difficulty with the entire implied-meaning system.

Additionally, research on IU and dichotomic (black-and-white) thinking in autism suggests that "to circumvent the discomfort of uncertainty, autistic individuals may tend to attribute a binary outcome to uncertain states of truth." This cognitive style is incompatible with the gradient nature of hedging, which operates on a continuum from full assertion to full doubt.
- Sources: PMC (pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8919847/); Nature Scientific Reports (nature.com/articles/s41598-023-41164-8)

### 2.3 Processing Load: The Dual-Task Problem

Hedging is not just choosing different words -- it requires simultaneously monitoring the social situation and constructing an utterance. This is functionally a dual-task problem.

**What hedging requires in real time:**
1. **Content formulation**: What do I want to say?
2. **Epistemic assessment**: How certain am I?
3. **Social calibration**: How will this land? How much authority do I have here? Is this a face-threatening act? How much softening does this context require?
4. **Form selection**: Which hedge fits? "I think"? "Maybe"? "Sort of"? "Would it be possible"?
5. **Prosodic delivery**: The hedge needs appropriate intonation to signal genuine tentativeness rather than performative or sarcastic hedging.

Steps 1-2 are content-oriented and relatively preserved in autistic speakers. Steps 3-5 require rapid social monitoring and multi-channel coordination that research consistently identifies as more costly for autistic individuals.

**The processing cost evidence:**
- Slower processing speeds in adults with autism "may be contributing to impairments in social communication skills" (PMC, 2018).
- "The effective use of uh and um requires a speaker to consider the listener's perspective while constructing an utterance, and perspective-taking is an area of particular difficulty for people with ASD" (Lake et al., 2011).
- Autistic individuals report that text-based communication is preferred precisely because it provides "more control, thinking time, clarity and fewer sensory issues and streams of information that must be processed and interpreted" (Howard & Sedgewick, 2021). The real-time processing demands of spoken interaction -- including hedging calibration -- are genuinely taxing.

**The shedding hypothesis:** Under processing load, speakers drop the elements that are most computationally expensive and least essential for conveying content. Hedges are prime candidates: they do not change propositional meaning, but they require social monitoring. A speaker under load preserves content (what they want to say) and sheds social packaging (how they frame it). This is functionally identical to what happens to any speaker under cognitive overload -- autistic speakers simply reach the overload threshold sooner in socially complex situations.

### 2.4 Values: Authenticity and Directness as Communication Ideals

Research consistently shows that autistic communication is characterized by different values, not absent values.

**Autistic communication values (from community research):**
- "Being honest, direct, clear, and authentic" (Neurodivergent Insights)
- "The freedom of speaking frankly within my family (both my teenagers are autistic also) without all the silly window dressing" (community member quoted in Crompton et al.)
- Autistic people "expect blunt language and honest answers, as that's how they communicate" (Autistic Nick)
- "Many autistic people feel that being inauthentic during interactions -- even for the sake of politeness -- is a form of deception, and therefore negative" (Autistic Science Person)

**Directness as respect:**
This parallels cross-cultural communication norms where directness is the valued default (see Section 7.2). Just as German Direktheit "represents the commitment to honest, efficient communication that cuts through ambiguity to reach clear understanding," autistic directness embodies a belief that clarity and honesty are more respectful than social lubrication.

### 2.5 Reduced Awareness of Expected Hedge Level

A separate factor from the values-based explanations: some autistic individuals may genuinely not register what level of hedging a particular context expects.

**The awareness challenge:**
- Neurotypical speakers intuitively calibrate hedge level based on power dynamics, social distance, and imposition weight -- the three variables in Brown & Levinson's Weight Formula (see Category 3). This calibration happens largely below conscious awareness.
- Autistic individuals "don't view social hierarchy the way neurotypical people do -- the intuitive concept of social hierarchy that neurotypical people seem to learn as children doesn't appear to happen the same way for autistic folks" (The Articulate Autistic). If you don't automatically register the power differential in a conversation, you can't calibrate your hedging accordingly.
- "For many autistic people, everyone is equal until told otherwise" (Specialisterne USA). This egalitarian default means the power variable (P) in the Weight Formula is effectively zeroed out, producing less hedging than the social situation expects.

## 3. Social Consequences of Under-Hedging

### 3.1 How Unhedged Assertions Are Perceived

The social consequences of producing fewer hedges are well-documented, both in autism research and in broader pragmatics research.

**Common neurotypical perceptions of unhedged autistic speech:**
- "Arrogant" -- an unhedged assertion of knowledge can be read as claiming epistemic authority the speaker doesn't have (or shouldn't display in context)
- "Rude" -- the absence of softeners before disagreement or criticism reads as interpersonal aggression
- "Blunt" -- the most common description; factually accurate information without social packaging
- "Aggressive" or "confrontational" -- especially in professional contexts where hedged criticism is the norm
- "Condescending" -- telling someone something directly can read as assuming they don't already know

**The core asymmetry (double empathy problem):**
- Autistic speakers are often *aware* that their style is perceived differently. Research found that "autistic people acknowledged that other people could find their own directness and honesty rude or upsetting, and that this could cause issues when interacting with others (especially non-autistic people)" (Crompton et al., SJDR).
- Neurotypical listeners typically *fail* to recognize the communicative intent behind directness. They interpret the absence of hedges as a deliberate interpersonal choice (choosing to be rude) rather than a communication style difference.
- This is the double empathy problem (Milton, 2012) applied to hedging: the autistic speaker doesn't produce the expected hedges; the neurotypical listener doesn't have the framework to interpret the absence charitably.
- Source: Milton, 2012; Crompton et al., 2020 (on information transfer between matched neurotypes)

### 3.2 Specific Workplace Consequences

The workplace is where under-hedging produces the most acute consequences, because professional communication has particularly rigid hedging expectations.

**Workplace hedging norms:**
- "Individuals with ASD show deficits in politeness marker use which could impact their interpersonal relationships within the workplace environment" (VET Study).
- Hedged language is expected in: giving feedback to peers ("You might want to consider..."), making suggestions to superiors ("I was wondering if maybe..."), declining requests ("I'm not sure I can do that right now"), disagreeing in meetings ("I see your point, but I wonder if...").
- Unhedged versions of the same acts -- "That's wrong," "We should do it this way," "No," "You're mistaken" -- can be career-damaging even when factually accurate.

**The "soft skills" framing problem:**
The workplace communication challenges autistic employees face are often framed as "soft skills deficits," implying the autistic person needs to be fixed. However, as SkillPath's analysis argues, this framing misattributes a communication style difference as an individual problem: "Autistic communication in the workplace: It's not a 'soft skills' problem to be solved."
- Source: SkillPath (skillpath.com/blog/autistic-communication-in-workplace-not-soft-skills-problem)

**Hierarchy blindness and hedging:**
The workplace intensifies the hedging problem because hedging expectations scale with power differentials (the P variable in the Weight Formula):
- "Autistic people don't view social hierarchy the way neurotypical people do" and "for many autistic people, everyone is equal until told otherwise" (The Articulate Autistic; Specialisterne USA).
- If an autistic employee treats a conversation with their CEO with the same hedge level as a conversation with a peer, the CEO may perceive the interaction as disrespectful or insubordinate.
- "The fact that some people are expected to follow rules to the letter while others can flout them with no consequences is confusing to someone whose brain doesn't automatically conform to neurotypical social hierarchy norms" (Specialisterne USA).
- Autistic individuals "question why someone should have unwritten authority over them" -- this justice orientation, while principled, can produce interactions that read as insubordinate when combined with unhedged speech.

### 3.3 The Gendered Dimension: Double Penalties for Autistic Women

Autistic women face compounded penalties for under-hedging because of the intersection of autism-related communication patterns with gendered communication expectations.

**The double bind:**
- Women in most English-speaking cultures are expected to hedge MORE than men. Research on gender and language consistently shows that women are expected to use more softeners, qualifiers, and indirect forms -- and are penalized when they don't.
- Autistic women, who may hedge less than neurotypical expectations, thus violate both autism-related and gender-related communication norms simultaneously.
- Research found that "preferences for direct communication [in autistic women] are associated with masculine traits, creating a gendered conflict" (Autism Spectrum News).
- Autistic women report that "their social communication difficulties make it difficult to join female peer groups, which they perceive as more subtle and less forgiving of social mistakes than male groups" (Frontiers in Psychology, 2021).
- Sources: Frontiers in Psychology (frontiersin.org/fpsyg.2021.621742); Autism Spectrum News; University of New Hampshire thesis on social-pragmatic communication in women with ASD

**The masking intensification:**
- Women are more likely to mask (camouflage) autistic traits, and the research suggests "gendered behaviors are carefully taught through reinforcement and penalties, and assigned female individuals are not taught to feel shame for deviating from the norm to the same degree as assigned male individuals, leading to greater efforts to disguise autistic behaviors."
- The hedging dimension of masking is particularly taxing: autistic women who learn the hedging system must maintain this learned performance constantly, contributing to the exhaustion and burnout associated with camouflaging.

**Over-apologizing as a gendered masking strategy:**
Research documents a pattern of over-apologizing that is particularly common among autistic women. "Sorry for Everything: Over-Apologizing to Survive with Autism" describes a pattern where autistic individuals engage in "apologetic survivalism -- saving face and preserving grace by being quick to apologize." The intersection with gender makes this pattern especially prevalent in autistic women, who receive both autism-related and gender-related social pressure to preemptively smooth interactions.
- Source: The Life Autistic (thelifeautistic.com); Salon (salon.com/2023/11/29/sorry-not-sorry-the-link-between-gender-autism-and-over-apologizing/)

### 3.4 Interaction with Social Hierarchy (Connection to Category 22)

The consequences of under-hedging are amplified in hierarchical contexts:
- Speaking up-hierarchy (to someone with more power) requires MORE hedging in neurotypical norms.
- Speaking down-hierarchy (to someone with less power) allows LESS hedging.
- Speaking across-hierarchy (to peers) requires moderate hedging calibrated to relationship closeness.

Because autistic individuals may not automatically register hierarchy or may apply a default egalitarian framework, they may produce the same hedge level regardless of the listener's relative power -- a flat hedging profile where a graduated one is expected. This can result in being perceived as:
- Disrespectful when speaking to superiors (not enough hedging)
- Patronizing when speaking to subordinates (the same amount of hedging that's insufficient for superiors may be excessive for subordinates)
- Appropriately calibrated only with close peers (where the default egalitarian level happens to match expectations)

## 4. Social Consequences of Over-Hedging

### 4.1 The Overcorrection Pattern

Some autistic individuals who explicitly learn the hedging system may overcorrect, producing more hedges than the context warrants. This is a predictable consequence of learning a social rule without the intuitive calibration that neurotypical speakers develop through years of implicit social feedback.

**How overcorrection develops:**
1. The autistic person receives feedback (social, professional, therapeutic) that their speech is "too direct" or "blunt."
2. They learn the rule: "Add hedges to soften your speech."
3. Without the implicit sense of *how much* hedging is appropriate, they apply the rule broadly: "Maybe I was sort of thinking that perhaps we could possibly consider..." when "I was thinking we could try..." would be sufficient.
4. The resulting speech sounds not rude but insecure, uncertain, or lacking confidence.

**The masking connection:**
This pattern is a specific instance of camouflaging. Autistic adults describe masking as "hyper-vigilance for and constant adaptation to the preferences and expectations (whether expressed, implied or anticipated) of the people around you, and tightly controlling and adjusting how you express yourself." When the specific behavior being controlled is hedging level, the result is often calibration error in the over-hedging direction.

### 4.2 How Over-Hedging Is Perceived

Where under-hedging is perceived as arrogant or rude, over-hedging is perceived as:
- "Insecure" or "lacking confidence" -- excessive hedging signals that the speaker doubts their own claims
- "Wishy-washy" or "uncommitted" -- too many hedges make it unclear what the speaker actually believes
- "Not leadership material" -- in professional contexts, over-hedging can be career-limiting, particularly for people being evaluated for promotion or leadership roles
- "Passive" or "not assertive enough" -- the hedges may be read as inability or unwillingness to take a position
- "Seeking permission to think" -- excessive hedging on factual claims ("I sort of think maybe the data suggests...") can undermine credibility

**The ironic consequence:**
An autistic person who learns to hedge as a masking strategy to avoid seeming rude may create a different but equally negative impression. They have traded "arrogant and blunt" for "insecure and wishy-washy" -- neither impression reflects their actual epistemic state or communicative intent.

### 4.3 The Calibration Challenge

The fundamental challenge is that hedging is not binary (hedge/don't hedge) but scalar, and the appropriate level depends on multiple simultaneous contextual variables:

**Variables that determine appropriate hedge level:**
1. **Power differential (P)**: How much more/less power does the listener have? (More power = more hedging)
2. **Social distance (D)**: How close is the relationship? (More distance = more hedging)
3. **Imposition weight (Rx)**: How face-threatening is the act? (Bigger imposition = more hedging)
4. **Domain expertise**: Are you the acknowledged expert? (More expertise = less hedging needed)
5. **Cultural context**: What is the baseline hedging norm? (Varies by culture, institution, and subculture)
6. **Speech act type**: Is this a request, criticism, disagreement, assertion, suggestion? (Each has different hedging expectations)
7. **Channel**: Written or spoken? Formal or informal? Public or private? (Each has different norms)

Neurotypical speakers calibrate these variables implicitly and simultaneously. Autistic speakers who learn to hedge may need to process them explicitly and sequentially -- a much more cognitively demanding task that may still produce miscalibrated output. This connects to the processing load argument in Section 2.3: even when the knowledge is present, the real-time calculation is taxing.

## 5. Hedging, Prosody, and Stress Placement (Connection to Category 15)

### 5.1 The Stress-Hedge Interaction

Hedges do not operate in isolation -- their pragmatic meaning is profoundly shaped by prosodic delivery, particularly stress placement. This creates an additional layer of complexity for autistic speakers and listeners, given the well-documented differences in prosodic production and perception in autism.

**The critical examples:**
- "I THINK you might be wrong" -- Stress on "I" makes this LESS hedged (emphasizes the speaker's position: "I, personally, think...")
- "I think you MIGHT be wrong" -- Stress on "might" makes this MORE hedged (emphasizes the uncertainty: "it's only a possibility")
- "I think you might be WRONG" -- Stress on "wrong" makes this a direct criticism barely softened by the hedges
- "I think YOU might be wrong" -- Stress on "you" makes this confrontational (emphasizes the target)

The same words, the same hedges, but four different meanings depending on which word carries primary stress. The hedging devices ("I think," "might") are present in all four, but their hedging function is modulated by prosody.

### 5.2 Prosodic Inversion of Hedge Meaning

More dramatically, prosody can completely *invert* the meaning of a hedged statement:

**Genuine hedge vs. sarcastic use:**
- "Oh, I'm SURE that will work" (with sarcastic intonation) -- the hedge word "sure" conveys the opposite of certainty
- "Well, MAYBE you're right" (with dismissive falling tone) -- "maybe" conveys certainty that the person is NOT right
- "I SUPPOSE we could try that" (with heavy stress and falling tone) -- "suppose" conveys reluctance, not genuine openness

**The autism-prosody literature:**
- "Impairments in prosody (e.g., intonation, stress) are among the most notable communication characteristics of individuals with autism spectrum disorder" (Grice, 2023 review).
- "The formal functions of linguistic prosody are assumed to be more learnable for individuals on the autism spectrum, as they are rule-based, while pragmatic and emotional functions are assumed to be more intuitive and context-dependent" (Language and Linguistics Compass).
- This means: autistic speakers may produce hedges with atypical prosody, causing the hedge to be interpreted differently than intended. Autistic listeners may not register the prosodic cues that invert or modulate hedge meaning.

### 5.3 The Sarcasm Problem

The interaction between hedging and sarcasm is particularly challenging because sarcasm uses hedge words to convey the *opposite* of their literal meaning:

**How sarcasm interacts with hedges:**
- Heavily hedged statements delivered with confident, mocking prosody are sarcastic: "Oh, I really THINK that's a GREAT idea" (meaning: it is a terrible idea).
- The prosodic cues that distinguish genuine hedging from sarcastic hedging include: exaggerated stress patterns, flattened or exaggerated pitch contours, slower delivery on key words, and sustained vowels.
- Research confirms autistic individuals have more difficulty detecting sarcasm, particularly when it relies on prosodic rather than contextual cues: "Children with ASD had difficulty in taking advantage of contextual information available to interpret the speaker's intent" and "sarcasm is frequently cued by subtle prosody -- intonation, timing, stress -- and differences in auditory processing or sensitivity to prosodic cues make those signals less salient for some autistic listeners" (PMC, 2013; Neurolaunch).

**Practical implication:**
An autistic listener may hear "I think that's a GREAT idea" and process the hedge ("I think") and the positive adjective ("great") literally, missing the sarcastic prosodic overlay that inverts both. This is not a failure of intelligence -- it is a processing difference in which channel (words vs. prosody) receives priority when the two channels convey conflicting messages.

## 6. Practical Frameworks for Autistic Individuals

### 6.1 Deciding How Much Hedging a Situation Requires

The Weight Formula from Brown & Levinson (1987), discussed in Category 3, provides a systematic framework for calculating hedge level:

**Wx = D(S,H) + P(H,S) + Rx**

Where:
- **D** = social distance between speaker and hearer
- **P** = relative power of the hearer over the speaker
- **Rx** = absolute ranking of the imposition of the speech act

The higher the Weight (Wx), the more hedging the situation requires. This formula can be taught as an explicit decision tool:

**Simplified hedge-level calculator:**

| Factor | Low hedge | Medium hedge | High hedge |
|--------|-----------|--------------|------------|
| **Power** | You have equal or more power | Slightly less power | Much less power (boss, authority) |
| **Distance** | Close friend/family | Colleague/acquaintance | Stranger/formal contact |
| **Imposition** | Small ask, minor opinion | Moderate request, disagreement | Major criticism, big request |

**Scoring:** If most factors are "low," minimal hedging is needed. If most are "high," heavy hedging is expected. Mixed profiles require judgment, but the formula provides a starting framework.

### 6.2 Common Contexts and Their Expected Hedge Levels

**Low hedging expected (directness is valued):**
- Close friends and family (especially if they know you're autistic)
- Autistic-autistic interactions (shared directness norms)
- Technical/engineering discussions (precision valued over social softening)
- Emergency or safety situations (clarity more important than politeness)
- Written technical documentation
- Certain professional cultures (startups, some STEM environments)

**Medium hedging expected:**
- Workplace conversations with peers
- Email to colleagues you know
- Social media discussions
- Giving opinions in meetings (as an equal participant)
- Everyday customer interactions

**High hedging expected:**
- Speaking to someone with significantly more power (CEO, professor, judge)
- Giving negative feedback or criticism (especially upward)
- Declining requests (especially from someone with more power)
- First meetings and formal introductions
- Job interviews
- Cross-cultural communication with high-context cultures
- Public-facing professional communication

### 6.3 Scripts and Templates for Common Hedged Speech Acts

**Disagreeing (graduated hedge levels):**
- Low: "I see it differently -- I think X."
- Medium: "I see your point, but I wonder if X might also be worth considering."
- High: "That's an interesting perspective. I was wondering whether it might be worth exploring X as well -- what do you think?"

**Giving negative feedback (graduated):**
- Low: "This part needs work. The data doesn't support the conclusion."
- Medium: "I think the data section could be strengthened. The conclusion might not fully follow from what's presented."
- High: "I really appreciate the effort here. I was wondering if it might be worth revisiting the data section -- I think there could be an opportunity to make the connection to the conclusion a bit stronger."

**Making requests (graduated):**
- Low: "Can you send me that file?"
- Medium: "Would you be able to send me that file when you get a chance?"
- High: "I was wondering if it would be possible for you to send me that file? I'd really appreciate it."

**Expressing an opinion (graduated):**
- Low: "I think we should go with option A."
- Medium: "I'd lean toward option A -- it seems like the stronger choice."
- High: "I might be wrong, but my sense is that option A could be worth exploring further. What does everyone think?"

### 6.4 When Directness Is Valued and Hedging Is Unnecessary

It is equally important to know when NOT to hedge:

**Contexts where hedging is counterproductive:**
- Stating facts: "The deadline is Friday" (not "I think the deadline might be Friday" if you know it's Friday)
- Emergency communication: "There's a fire, evacuate now" (not "I was wondering if maybe we should consider leaving")
- Setting personal boundaries: "I need to leave at 5:00" (not "I was sort of hoping I might be able to leave around 5")
- Answering direct questions with known answers: "The report has 12 pages" (not "I think it might have around 12 pages or so")
- Communicating with other autistic people who prefer directness
- Writing technical documentation where precision is paramount

**The danger of over-hedging facts:**
Hedging factual statements you're certain about damages credibility. If you know the answer and hedge it, listeners may think you *don't* know the answer. The rule of thumb: hedge your opinions and assessments, not your facts.

## 7. The Neurodiversity Perspective

### 7.1 Autistic Directness as a Valid Communication Style

The neurodiversity paradigm reframes autistic directness not as a deficit but as a legitimate communication style with its own norms and values.

**Key principles:**
- "Autistic ways of communicating are different, not wrong" (neurodiversity-affirming practice literature).
- "Both verbal and nonverbal communication styles, and autistic ways of socializing and self-expression should be valued and accepted" (Play Learn Chat).
- The double empathy problem (Milton, 2012) demonstrates that communication breakdowns between autistic and non-autistic people are *mutual* -- not one-sided failures by the autistic person.
- Crompton et al. (2020) showed that "autistic individuals communicate more successfully and experience higher levels of rapport when interacting with other autistic individuals" -- autistic communication is effective *within its own community*.

**What this means for hedging:**
- Hedging norms are not universal truths but cultural conventions that vary across communities.
- The degree of hedging expected in mainstream neurotypical English-speaking culture is one set of norms, not the objectively correct amount.
- Teaching hedging to autistic individuals should be framed as acquiring a tool for navigating specific social contexts -- not as correcting a deficiency.

### 7.2 Cross-Cultural Parallels: Directness as Cultural Norm

Autistic directness has striking parallels with communication styles valued in several world cultures, demonstrating that the neurotypical Anglophone hedging norm is culturally specific, not universal:

**German Direktheit:**
- "Direktheit represents the German commitment to honest, efficient communication that cuts through ambiguity to reach clear understanding" (Everything About Germany).
- "Germans believe direct communication prevents misunderstandings, saves time, and demonstrates genuine care by providing authentic feedback rather than empty pleasantries."
- "German directness is often a form of respect. Unlike cultures where indirect speech is used to protect feelings, Germans believe that honesty shows consideration for others' time and intelligence."
- Source: germanculture.com.ua; everythingaboutgermany.de

**Israeli Dugriut (dugri = "straight"):**
- "The direct communication style of Israelis is known as 'dugriut,' which refers to a forthright and unapologetic directness in communicating" (Cultural Atlas).
- "Interests and desires are often expressed through direct phrases such as 'I want...' or 'I need...' as opposed to indirect phrases such as 'would it be possible...'"
- "In professional settings, Israeli managers and colleagues may offer candid feedback or critique without the cushioning often found in other cultures. This directness is not intended to be rude but rather to foster transparency."
- Source: Cultural Atlas (culturalatlas.sbs.com.au); Progressive Israel

**Dutch Bespreekbaarheid ("the ability to discuss anything"):**
- "Bespreekbaarheid has no direct English translation but fundamentally means 'the ability to discuss anything' and is deeply ingrained in Dutch society" (GConnection).
- "Dutch professionals are famously direct in their communication style, giving frank feedback or sharing opinions without sugarcoating, rooted in a cultural belief that honesty and clarity show respect."
- Historical roots: "Much of Dutch land is reclaimed from the sea, requiring centuries of cooperation to build and maintain dikes -- clear communication was essential; ambiguity could literally mean disaster."
- Source: GConnection (gconnection.nl); Cultural Atlas; Undutchables (undutchables.nl)

**The cross-cultural insight for autistic learners:**
These cultural examples demonstrate that:
1. Directness is valued in many thriving cultures -- it is not inherently rude or dysfunctional.
2. What counts as "appropriate hedging" is culturally variable, not universally fixed.
3. Autistic directness aligns with communication norms that hundreds of millions of people follow successfully.
4. The Anglophone neurotypical hedging norm is the *most* hedging-heavy end of the global spectrum, not the default.

### 7.3 When Hedging Requirements Are Excessive or Unnecessary

A neurodiversity-affirming approach recognizes that some hedging expectations are genuinely excessive:

**Institutional over-hedging:**
- Academic writing requires extensive hedging that many researchers (autistic and non-autistic) find gratuitous: "The data appear to suggest that it may be the case that..." when "The data show X" would be clearer and more honest.
- Corporate communication often requires hedging that obscures meaning: "We might want to consider the possibility of exploring..." when "Let's do X" would be more effective.
- These are not universal social necessities -- they are cultural artifacts of specific institutional contexts.

**The cost of imposed hedging:**
- "Masking is both physically and mentally exhausting" and is associated with "higher depression and anxiety symptoms, lower self-esteem, and less personal authenticity" (PMC, 2022).
- Forcing an autistic person to hedge at neurotypical levels at all times is a form of imposed masking with real mental health costs.
- A neurodiversity-affirming approach teaches hedging as a *tool to be deployed strategically* rather than a *constant performance requirement*.

### 7.4 Teaching Hedging as a Tool, Not a Rule

**The framing principle:**
"You don't have to hedge. But it helps to know how, and when, and why others expect it -- so you can choose."

**The toolbox approach:**
- Hedging is a communication tool, like a wrench. You don't carry a wrench everywhere; you use it when you need to loosen a bolt.
- Understanding hedging gives you the ability to deploy it *when the consequences of not hedging are significant* -- in job interviews, when speaking to people with power over your life, when delivering bad news.
- Outside those high-stakes contexts, direct communication is perfectly valid and often more efficient.

**Explicit context-mapping over implicit socialization:**
- Rather than trying to develop the implicit "feel" for hedge level that neurotypical speakers acquire through years of social feedback, autistic learners can develop explicit context maps: "In this type of situation, with this type of person, this much hedging is expected."
- The Weight Formula (Category 3) provides the systematic framework for this mapping.
- Scripts and templates (Section 6.3) provide starting points that can be customized.

**Choice and agency:**
- The ultimate goal is not that autistic speakers always hedge "correctly" but that they have the knowledge to make informed choices.
- Sometimes the choice will be: "I know this context expects hedging, and I'm choosing to hedge because the consequences of not hedging are significant."
- Sometimes the choice will be: "I know hedging is expected here, but I'm choosing directness because clarity is more important right now."
- Both choices are valid. The knowledge base provides the information; the individual retains the agency.

## 8. Cross-References to Other Categories

### 8.1 Category 3 (Politeness Theory)
- The Weight Formula provides the quantitative framework for calculating hedge level.
- Brown & Levinson's positive/negative politeness distinction maps directly onto the Tilburg finding: negative politeness markers (hedges, softeners, deference markers) are specifically reduced in autistic speech.
- Face-threatening acts (FTAs) are the triggers for hedging: the greater the face threat, the more hedging expected.

### 8.2 Category 5 (Discourse Markers)
- The "um" vs. "uh" dissociation parallels the hedge-type specificity: listener-oriented markers are reduced more than speaker-oriented ones.
- Discourse markers like "well," "sort of," "I mean" serve double duty as both discourse organizers and hedging devices.
- The computational finding (BERT-based pragmatic feature detection) applies to both discourse markers and hedges.

### 8.3 Category 15 (Prosodic Pragmatics)
- Stress placement modulates hedge meaning (Section 5.1).
- Prosodic differences in autism affect both hedge production and hedge interpretation.
- Sarcasm detection requires integration of hedge words with prosodic contour.

### 8.4 Category 22 (Register and Social Context)
- Hedging expectations scale with formality and power distance.
- Register-shifting includes hedge-level adjustment.
- Autistic hierarchy blindness interacts with hedge-level calibration.
