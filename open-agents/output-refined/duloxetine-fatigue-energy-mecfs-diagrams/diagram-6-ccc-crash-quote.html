Written Down in 2003, In His Own Word

He suspects sensory overwhelm drives his crashes. That is not a hunch he constructed to explain himself. It is a documented clinical phenomenon in a primary ME/CFS consensus document, and he rediscovered it independently, twenty-three years after it was published.

"There may be overload1 phenomena: cognitive, sensory (e.g. photophobia and hypersensitivity to noise) and/or emotional overload, which may lead to “crash”2 periods and/or anxiety."

"Patients are often hypersensitive to sensory stimulation including noise, bright lights, temperature extremes, and odours... Emotional overload may be unduly stressful. Overload phenomena may trigger a “crash” where the patient becomes temporarily immobilized by physical and/or mental fatigue and recovery is slow."

Criterion 6b, Neuroendocrine Manifestations: "loss of adaptability and worsening of symptoms with stress."
Sensory overload → crash → anxiety. That is his exact sequence, written down in 2003, in a primary ME/CFS clinical consensus document, using his own word. He arrived at it independently. His phenomenology is a recognised pattern in his illness. It is not an idiosyncrasy, and it is not something he constructed after the fact to explain himself. That validates the phenomenology completely, and it is worth saying before anything else. reasoning, not evidence — the quotations are primary and verified

It asserts that the phenomenon exists and that clinicians should recognise it. It does not demonstrate that the overload→crash link is causal, and, this being the part that matters for the dose question, it says nothing whatsoever about crashes causing lasting damage. Nothing here is a psychological model of his illness. The claim under examination is pharmacological: whether a drug preventing a documented ME/CFS phenomenon would slowly reduce his pain. He raised it. A criteria note he is entitled to

The IOM 2015 / SEID fatigue criterion requires onset "of new or definite onset (not lifelong)". He describes lifelong ME/CFS. That is a criteria-fit tension worth being aware of. The CCC and the ICC do not impose that requirement. primary, verified This is a note about criteria, not about whether he is ill.

His crash duration fits the data

Chu 2018 (PMID 29856774), N=150: 99% exacerbation with physical or cognitive effort; 90% with emotional distress; duration 84% ≥24h, most common longest 3–7 days (38%), and 24% up to more than a week, up to several months. primary, verified His 1–2 week crash sits comfortably inside the observed range.

⚠ The correction most sources get wrong

Chu's trigger questions asked about physical/mental activity and emotional distress. Sensory exposure was NEVER ASKED ABOUT as a trigger. "Sensory overload" appears in Chu as a write-in symptom of PEM (N=12 after exertion, N=7 after distress), not as a trigger. primary, verified Chu establishes emotional distress → PEM. It does NOT establish sensory exposure → PEM. Secondary sources blur this routinely.

Where each criteria set stands on triggers

ICC 2011 (PENE): names "physical and/or cognitive" exertion; does not name sensory exposure; "A relapse can last days, weeks or longer." IOM 2015 / SEID: the PEM criterion is literally the two words "Post-exertional malaise" plus a frequency footnote; it enumerates no triggers. NICE NG206 adds "cognitive, physical, emotional and, social": that specification is NICE's addition, not IOM's. primary, verified
