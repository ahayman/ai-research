Gotts 2015: You Are Common, and Your Arrow May Run Backwards

There is exactly one number on nap-failure in ME/CFS, and it says he is not unusual. The same study contains a finding that runs against his own theory, and it is handed over here rather than filed somewhere he will not look.

Figure 3: The prevalence number, and the reversal Both halves come from the same paper, verbatim. Verbatim on prevalence: "21 (20.8%) patients in the sample did not report any daytime napping throughout the 14-day period." Verbatim on outcome: "Napping, particularly in the afternoon is associated with poorer cognitive functioning and more daytime sleepiness in CFS." primary, verified In the only dataset on napping in his illness, napping is associated with worse outcomes, not better ones, and the sign is the opposite of his assumption. It converges with ordinary sleep medicine, where daytime napping lowers nocturnal sleep pressure and degrades night sleep. reasoning, not evidence
The half that is good news, and it should not be lost under the reversal. After a lifetime in which not being able to nap has probably looked like a personal defect, the number says about one in five people with his illness shares it. That is common. It is not a sign that something extra is wrong with him. primary, verified
The limit, and it belongs at the claim rather than below it. Gotts measured reported napping, not nap ABILITY. It cannot separate "cannot nap" from "chooses not to nap". His claim is about ability. This study cannot speak to it. It is the closest number that exists and it is not quite the number he needs. And the study is cross-sectional, so the direction of the association is unresolvable and it is confounded by severity: sicker people may nap more and have worse cognition, with no causal arrow between the two. reasoning, not evidence

What the reversal is, and is not

It is not a claim that he is wrong. It is a claim that the one relevant dataset points the other way, that nobody has looked properly, and that not napping may be protecting his night sleep rather than damaging it. That is live and untested, and worth knowing before he treats "learning to nap" as a goal. reasoning, not evidence

The killer confound on any nap test

Duloxetine improved his sleep, which reduces sleep drive, which makes napping HARDER. Bonnet & Arand measured it: sleep deprivation drove nap latency from 13.4 to 3.8 min. Sleep drive dominates arousal. primary, verified The drug pushes the outcome both ways at once, so a null is uninterpretable.

He has been running an aborted test

The MSLT protocol allows 20 minutes and scores a no-sleep trial as 20. He quits at 10 to 20. His lifelong "hard zero" may be a lifelong latency of 18 minutes he has never let run. Running the full 20 converts a lifelong binary into a number, and a number can move. A "no" cannot. reasoning, not evidence

The verdict before he starts

A positive is informative. A negative is not. A clean, repeatable nap on a lifelong hard-zero channel would be the first thing anyone has ever recorded about duloxetine and daytime sleep propensity, in any population. A null teaches nothing, because the improved-sleep confound predicts a null under both hypotheses. He should know that before he runs it, so a null does not read to him as a refutation. reasoning, not evidence
