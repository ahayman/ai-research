His Hypothesis, Scored Honestly

One claim is established and it is a genuine find. One is unstudied and it is the one everything rests on. One is speculation. Conflating them is the main risk, and almost every discussion of this idea conflates them.

"I suspect they [the post-overwhelm crashes] aggravate the whole CFS and fibromyalgia."
Figure 7: Three claims, three statuses, and a verdict with three parts The verdict is descriptively supported, causally unstudied, adjacent evidence unfavourable, and no part of it cancels the others. What that adds up to is a hypothesis that should not bear weight in a dose decision, that is nevertheless not a crank idea, and that nobody has tested. The information-value argument (below) is a much better reason to hold 20mg than the pain argument is.

That is the noise floor against which any n-of-1 claim must be read, and it means a substantial drift down at a fixed 20mg would be genuinely surprising and therefore informative.

⚠ The separating observation, and the trap underneath it

The separating observation, and it is free. His model must run through the fatigue limb, because Okifuji says the only surviving prospective path from distress to pain in fibromyalgia is distress → fatigue → pain. So his model predicts fatigue improves first or concurrently, and pain follows weeks later. The standard model (pain needs the ~40–60mg noradrenergic threshold) predicts pain moves without any change in fatigue, and only after a dose increase.

→ Track fatigue and pain as SEPARATE numbers. If pain improves while fatigue does not, his model is dead, whatever is helping. If fatigue improves and pain follows with a lag, his model gains real support. This is the sharpest and cheapest test available, it rests on the one high-quality within-person dataset that exists, and it uses the same journal the somnolence tracker already needs. One instrument, two questions. reasoning, not evidence — Okifuji is primary

And the trap, which is the actual decision content. His model and the standard model make different predictions now and IDENTICAL predictions at 60mg, because 60mg crosses the noradrenergic threshold and carries on preventing crashes. If pain improves at 60mg, both models are satisfied and he learns nothing about his own hypothesis, permanently.

The 20mg window is the only clean test of his hypothesis that will ever exist for him. It is open now, it is free, and a dose increase closes it irreversibly. Conversely, if he holds 20mg for 3 to 6 months and pain is unchanged while crashes stay prevented, his model's pain limb is cleanly falsified and the dose decision becomes trivial.

This is not an argument for staying at 20mg. The cost of waiting is months of unrelieved pain, and pain matters to him; he has said so explicitly and has corrected the framing that treats his pain as something he would trade away. The real trade is not "which model is right". It is "how much is the answer worth, against months of pain?" That question is his and his doctor's. This page's only job is to make sure he knows the window exists before it shuts.

Pre-commit, or the test is worthless. His model must specify how long and how much, in advance. Without a pre-registered threshold, any outcome reads as confirmation ("it's working, just slowly"), which is precisely how an unfalsifiable belief forms. Given Bergenheim's base rate, something like: "≥30% pain reduction by month 4, with ≥2 survived exposures and 0 crashes." Failing it must count. reasoning, not evidence

A second, sharper test he gets for free: if a crash does break through, does pain step up and STAY up for weeks? If crashes truly aggravate, a breakthrough crash should leave a durable pain signature. If pain returns to baseline within days, the aggravation limb is falsified even though crash-prevention is real, which would mean the crashes are unpleasant but not damaging. That is a completely different thing, and it changes what the crash benefit is worth.
