The Retraction: His Nap-Failure Is Not the ME/CFS Finding

An earlier pass of this project told him his lifelong nap-failure was the ME/CFS sleep-latency finding experienced from the inside. That is wrong. It is retracted here rather than quietly dropped, because a retraction is worth more to him than the claim was.

Figure 5: The whole literature, enumerated This is not a selection. PubMed ("multiple sleep latency"[tiab]) AND ("chronic fatigue"[tiab] OR "myalgic encephalomyelitis"[tiab]) returns 15 records, and that is the entire literature. primary, verified — search-level, reproducible It points three ways. Nothing in it describes a group that cannot nap. Where it is directional at all, it points toward more sleep propensity. So his nap-failure is closer to the opposite of the ME/CFS group finding, and it needs its own explanation. reasoning, not evidence
Two claims retracted

1. "His nap-failure is the ME/CFS MSLT finding lived." Watson's twins fell asleep in 8.9 minutes. That is not nap failure; that is normal-to-brisk sleep onset. primary, verified

2. "The ESS-high/MSLT-normal dissociation is a replicated ME/CFS finding." It is not merely unreplicated. It is contradicted, in both directions. primary, verified ×11

Two methodological catches

Neu 2014 cannot count as a replication. Its cohort was selected for the absence of objective sleepiness; a normal MSLT was an inclusion criterion. Counting it toward "MSLT is normal in CFS" is circular. primary, verified

Spitzer's 80% is probably a contaminated population: HLA DQB1*0602 in 43% is a narcolepsy marker, and the sample is a neurology referral practice. But that cuts both ways, because it means "MSLT is normal in ME/CFS" is a claim about well-characterised research cohorts, not about people who show up in clinics. reasoning, not evidence

What his nap-failure DOES resemble

The insomnia hyperarousal phenotype, where the objectively-verified inability to nap is the phenotype. Bonnet & Arand (PMID 10737337), 12 psychophysiological insomnia patients, verbatim: "Patients with insomnia have been shown to have increased physiological arousal and to also have longer MSLT latencies." Baseline nap latency 13.4 min. primary, verified

But the fit is imperfect and it is not being oversold: he does not report insomnia. He reports his sleep improved and that it is the best thing about the drug. Daytime nap-failure with decent night sleep is not a described phenotype in either literature.

The two literatures do not agree they describe one thing

Dressle & Riemann 2023 (PMID 37183177), verbatim: "there is no conclusive evidence for the involvement of autonomous variables such as heart rate and heart rate variability"; the literature supports "cortical arousal"; and "future studies should also focus on similarities and differences in hyperarousal across different diagnostic entities." primary, verified The field's own 2023 review names the cross-diagnostic comparison as an open question, not a settled overlap.

The fifth evidence-absence

No MSLT study of duloxetine exists. No MSLT study of any SNRI exists. no evidence exists — checked three ways duloxetine[tiab] AND (tonic[tiab] AND phasic[tiab]) returns exactly one record, and its "tonic/phasic" refers to chin EMG. Nobody has measured what duloxetine does to daytime sleep propensity, at any dose, in any population.

What survives the retraction, and it is the useful part. The instrument advice was right and the mechanism story under it was oversold. "Count events, do not rate feelings" now rests on Olson 1998 (PMID 9844851), verbatim: "The ESS cannot be used to demonstrate or exclude sleepiness as it is measured by MSLT." primary, verified That is a statement about the instrument itself, which holds regardless of what the ME/CFS group data does. He sits between two literatures and the between-space is unstudied.
