The Dose Step in the Currency That Matters

Milligrams are the wrong unit for this question, because serotonergic engagement saturates. Converted to occupancy, the same four steps produce both the sharpest argument against going up and the sharpest argument that the fear is overdrawn. Both are below. Neither wins.

First, the framing that has to go

The tempting sentence is “a 1.5× step flipped the sensory filter in Oranje's lab, and you are contemplating 3×.” It conflates a dose ratio with a drive ratio, and a saturating curve does not permit it. 20mg is already at roughly 72% transporter occupancy; 60mg is roughly 88%. That is a step of about sixteen points, not a tripling. The concern is real. The multiplier is not, and the fix is not to drop the arithmetic but to redo it in the right unit.

Read it both ways, because it reads both ways

The argument against going up
4.5×
Your contemplated step is 4.5 times the step that flipped the sensory filter in a blind crossover, and 2.9 times the step that cost you a week of your life. This is the sharpest form of the sensory-gating warning anyone has produced, and it is arithmetic on this project's own numbers rather than a vibe.
Its weakness: both comparators are escitalopram and the contemplated step is duloxetine. It crosses drugs at exactly the point where crossing drugs is the whole question.
The argument that the fear is overdrawn
4.3×
Your duloxetine initiation was 4.3 times larger than the step you are contemplating, in the same drug and the same person, two weeks ago, and it produced mild floatiness. If 71.7 points of duloxetine did that, there is no arithmetic reason to expect 16.7 points of the same drug in the same person to produce a catastrophe.
Its strength, and it is worth naming: unlike the warning, this one does not cross drugs. It is within-drug, within-person, and two weeks old. Its weakness: it is still n=1, and “no arithmetic reason to expect a catastrophe” is not evidence of safety. It removes a reason for alarm. It supplies no reassurance.
Two arguments, opposite directions, both partly on your own data, and neither settles it. The fair summary is the one Oranje's own paper reaches, and you are precisely the patient in the sentence: since people treated with these drugs “might already suffer from disrupted sensory gating and habituation, the current results call for caution in the determination of a proper dose.” Caution in dose determination. Not a prohibition, and not a green light.
The one thing neither column can supply: nobody has measured where on that curve the filter flips, or whether it flips at all with chronic dosing. Every study in the gating literature is a single dose in a drug-naive volunteer.

Provenance: the gating outcomes are primary, verified ×2 (Jensen 2008, PMID 18000656, 10mg, P50 intact; Oranje 2011, PMID 20971512, 15mg, P50 disrupted — same lab, same paradigm, an explicitly planned dose extension). The occupancy values are modelled and are reasoning, not evidence: escitalopram fitted from Klein 2006 (PMID 16955282), duloxetine from Takano 2006's published ED50 of 7.9mg (PMID 16506079), which independently reproduces this project's standing 16.7 and 71.7 figures. Your outcomes are your own report. Nothing here has been measured in your brain.
