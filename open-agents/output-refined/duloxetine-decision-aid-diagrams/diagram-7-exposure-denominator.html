The Denominator Is Exposures, Not Time

Your episodes are triggered, not spontaneous. That single fact about yourself changes what counts as evidence, and it makes a faster, better-powered test available than waiting.

Two quiet months are worth almost nothing. One theme-park day is worth more.
Two months with no episodes while working from home, avoiding crowds, having a quiet winter: the drug was never tested. One significant sensory overload with no episode afterwards is a test. [reasoning, not evidence — this is a study-design argument, not a finding]

Faster, better-powered, and more specific than waiting. And it has a property the calendar lacks: regression to the mean cannot fake it. Regression explains why a bad stretch is followed by a better one. It does not explain why a specific, repeated, previously reliable trigger stopped producing its effect.
Limits, at the claim: it requires characterising p₀ retrospectively, which is recall-biased; exposures must be genuinely comparable, which is a judgement call; and deliberately seeking out overload to test a drug is a real cost with a real risk, and is not being recommended. The point is that when overloads happen naturally, those are the informative data points — record them as such, prospectively, starting now.

At two weeks, 13–37% of people for whom this drug does absolutely nothing would be seeing exactly what you are seeing. Roughly a one-in-three to one-in-seven chance of a pure fluke. That is not a reason for pessimism. It is a reason not to treat two weeks as informative.
Chance dies at ~6 weeks and is dead by 3 months. You do not need six months. You need about eight to twelve weeks.

⚠ The qualifier where most n-of-1 reasoning goes wrong

The time math kills "random fluctuation." It does NOT touch placebo, regression to the mean, or natural course.

Those are not chance. They are systematic effects, and they produce exactly the same zero-episode observation. A three-month clean run proves the improvement is real. It says nothing about whether the drug caused it. These are different questions, and the passage of time only answers the first. [reasoning, not evidence]

Your appointment lands at roughly four weeks. At four weeks you will have 2–14% odds of a pure fluke and, unless chance supplies a trigger, no exposure-conditional data at all. The data arrives after the decision. Say that out loud in the room — it is more useful to a doctor than confidence would be.

⚠ An inconsistency in the input, flagged rather than smoothed over

"Several times a month" combined with "episodes last 1–2 weeks" does not arithmetically fit: 3 episodes/month × 1.5 weeks each = 4.5 weeks of episode per 4.3-week month, i.e. continuous illness.

So either the episodes usually run much shorter than the theme-park example, or "several times a month" is an overestimate, or they overlap. The theme-park→2-week episode may be an unusually severe instance rather than the modal one.

This ambiguity propagates into every number above, and you are the only person who can resolve it. Worth resolving before the appointment, because the whole n-of-1 rests on it. [reasoning, not evidence]

The three sharpest markers, ranked

  1. The trigger test. The only marker that separates drug from regression to the mean. The highest-value observation available to you.
  2. The RLS, vivid dreams and floaty feeling — and this is underrated. These are pharmacological signatures, and they are not plausible placebo responses, because nobody's expectation produces restless legs. They do not prove the drug caused the episode benefit, since two real effects of one drug need not be the same effect. But they remove "the drug is inert at 20mg" from the table entirely, and they supply a covariate: if the dreams and RLS fade while the episode benefit persists, that dissociation is informative. If they fade together, that is a coherent drug story.
  3. The escitalopram contrast. You have already run a within-subject control, and it came out negative. A pure placebo account has to explain why the first serotonergic drug produced dissociation and non-function while the second produced resolution. Expectancy does not usually reverse sign between two drugs in the same class. Not conclusive, because the drugs differ pharmacologically, your circumstances differ, and time has passed. But it is a genuine asymmetry that the deflationary account has to absorb, and it long predates duloxetine.
