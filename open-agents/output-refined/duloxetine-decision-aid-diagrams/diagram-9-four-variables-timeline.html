Four Variables Already in Motion

Decide what to measure and how to tell causes apart BEFORE changing anything. The reason is specific and it is on the timeline below: iron's onset lands precisely on top of the window in which a dose change would be evaluated.

VariableStartedOnsetStatus at the appointment (~4 weeks)
Duloxetine 20mg ~2 weeks ago Anxiolysis days–weeks; SERT plateau immediate Fully expressed. This is the signal. At ~75% striatal SERT at steady state [primary, verified — Abanades 2011].
Melatonin Nightly, ongoing Immediate Steady background. Not a new variable, so it cannot explain a change. See the note below on the RLS evidence, which points both ways, weakly.
Oral iron This week ~12 weeks CANNOT explain anything at the appointment. It is not a confound yet. It becomes one in the spring — which is exactly when you would otherwise be reading your own data for signs the dose decision worked.
A possible dose change Undecided Steady state ~3 days at a ~12h half-life; somnolence early; pain at 2–4 weeks if it comes The variable under discussion. Collect ≥2 weeks at 20mg before any change; then ≥2 weeks at the new dose, discarding the first 3–5 days as transition.

⚠ The consequence, and it is the point of this diagram

Iron's twelve-week onset lands directly on top of the window in which a dose change would be evaluated. If both move, they will move together and be inseparable.

That is not an argument against either. It is an argument for writing down, in advance, what each is predicted to do and by when — because after the fact, any improvement will have two candidate causes and no way to tell them apart. [reasoning, not evidence]

There is one clean separation available: iron acts on RLS and on fatigue-via-iron-status. It has no reason to touch pain or the episode pattern. So if pain or episodes move at week 12, iron is not a plausible explanation. If RLS moves at week 12, it very much is. [reasoning, not evidence]

The rule this buys — and the time-critical item

  • The 20mg baseline window is NOW, and it closes the moment the dose changes. Collect at least two weeks of the measures you intend to use, at the dose you are currently on, before anything moves. This is the time-critical item in the whole project.
  • Pre-register the prediction in writing before any change. This substitutes for the blinding you cannot have. Expectancy will contaminate the ratings and not the event counts, which is a second reason counts are primary.
  • Do NOT attempt an ABAB / withdrawal-reintroduction design. Duloxetine's discontinuation syndrome makes self-directed withdrawal blocks unsafe. The canonical n-of-1 crossover is not available here, and that is a real methodological loss rather than a formality.
  • Log daily: sleep timing and duration · crash/PEM day (y/n) · pain · caffeine (dose and time of last) · alcohol · iron · melatonin (dose and time) · other meds · illness · heat · previous-day exertion.
  • Caffeine deserves special flagging: it is the one covariate that ACTIVELY MASKS the outcome being measured. If caffeine intake drifts upward in response to emerging somnolence, it suppresses the very signal being hunted — a hidden compensatory loop that makes a real effect look absent. Log it, or the whole exercise is unfalsifiable. [reasoning, not evidence]

On melatonin and RLS: all three earlier passes of this project were wrong

The claim that melatonin is "clearly aggravating" your restless legs does not survive its sources. Whittom 2010 (PMID 20226733): n=8, 3mg at 19:00, NO placebo condition, PLM increased. Kunz 2001 (PMID 11247054): melatonin 3mg REDUCED PLM (n=9) — and that paper was missed by every prior pass. The evidence points both ways, weakly. It is hypothesis-generating only. [primary, verified ×2] Related: "duloxetine raises PLMS" is also dead — it was extrapolated from venlafaxine with no duloxetine primary, and duloxetine has never been measured for PLMS at any dose. In 340,099 inpatients, duloxetine severe-RLS was 0.01% vs mirtazapine 0.07%. Monitor; don't predict.
