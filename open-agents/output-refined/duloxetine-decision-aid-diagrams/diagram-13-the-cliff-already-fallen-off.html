The Cliff You Have Already Fallen Off

This project has been arguing for the compounded-liquid question from a systematic review. It turns out you do not need one. You ran the experiment on yourself, and you can describe the result to a prescriber in a single sentence.

“Every 2 weeks I reduce the dose by 5mg… Each dosage reduction did nothing as far as I could tell. When I finally quit, though, (from 2 weeks at 5mg → 0mg), I lost 2 weeks of functionality.”

The last step is thirty times the first, sixteen times the second, and six and a half times the largest intermediate one. Same five milligrams. Thirty times the pharmacology. This is the hyperbolic taper model's central prediction, and you lived it without knowing the model existed.

Now stand where you are standing

The cliff you fell off: escitalopram
60 points
  • The drop from the lowest tablet to zero.
  • Escitalopram at least has a 5mg tablet, and it can be halved, so there was a 2.5mg rung below the one you stepped off.
  • You did the sensible things: even 5mg steps, and two weeks at each rather than one.
  • It cost you two weeks anyway.
The cliff in front of you: duloxetine
71.7 points
  • The drop from 20mg, your current dose, to zero.
  • There is no capsule below 20mg. Duloxetine's smallest manufactured dose is the cliff edge. There is no rung to step down to.
  • It is bigger than the one that cost you two weeks, and it has less below it.
  • Going up to 60mg does not touch this. 60 back down to 20 costs about 16.7 points with capsules at every step. The cliff is below you either way.
Which turns a systematic review into one sentence you can say out loud

“When the time comes, will you write a compounded liquid?”

Ask it now, while it is entirely hypothetical and the conversation costs nothing. A yes removes the largest obstacle from a decision you may never make. A no, or a “we'll just do every other day,” is important information at the cheapest possible moment. And you are no longer arguing from a model: you are arguing from two weeks of your own life.

One further reason to raise it now rather than later: Horowitz's own risk model, which is the source of the hyperbolic-tapering recommendation in the first place, states that “past experience of withdrawal effects is known to predict future risk” and that longer duration of use raises both incidence and severity. You now have a documented past experience of severe withdrawal. By that rubric you are a predicted case, which is a fact worth your prescriber having before it is urgent.

⚠ What this does NOT license, and it is the trap this whole project keeps walking into

“You already fell off this cliff once, so you know what duloxetine's will feel like” is not available. It is satisfying, it is emotionally compelling, and your own history refutes it: your two drug initiations delivered matched transporter occupancy and produced opposite outcomes, which means your response to a given occupancy change is drug-specific. The arithmetic transfers. The felt experience does not.

What survives is worth more than what fails. The taper-cliff argument never needed your escitalopram history at all: duloxetine's 71.7-point drop stands on duloxetine's own dose-response data, independently of anything about escitalopram. Your experience adds a vivid demonstration that the arithmetic is real, and adds nothing about what duloxetine's version will feel like.

And one honest gap underneath all of it: the link from occupancy loss to withdrawal symptoms is assumed rather than validated. The systematic review that established the curve says occupancy change “might be related” to withdrawal symptoms, and no study has measured occupancy change against withdrawal severity in the same people. The recommendation may well be right, and your history is consistent with it, but n=1 cannot separate “the occupancy fell 60 points” from “you stopped a drug you had taken for six months.”

Provenance: the taper and its outcomes are your own report, retrospective and unblinded. The occupancy figures are reasoning, not evidence, modelled and shown here so they can be checked or rejected: escitalopram fitted from Klein 2006 (PMID 16955282, single dose, midbrain, 5 per arm), duloxetine computed from Takano 2006's published ED50 of 7.9mg (PMID 16506079). Sørensen 2022 (PMID 34548628, 17 studies, 294 participants, 309 occupancy measures) establishes that the hyperbolic shape holds across ten antidepressants including duloxetine, and predicts this pattern verbatim; Horowitz 2023 (PMID 36513909) is the risk model, primary, abstract only. One mismatch, reported rather than smoothed: the model says 10→5mg should have been your second-worst step at 9.2 points and you report it as nothing, which means either your detection threshold sits above ~9 points or the real curve is even more terminal-loaded than this model.
