The Nap Protocol — And Why a Null Means Nothing

Worth running, but not for the reason you would guess, and the asymmetry has to come first because it is the verdict.

✓ Sound for a POSITIVE

A channel that has been at zero for decades has no false-positive noise to speak of. If P(nap) ≈ 0 across your whole life, one unambiguous success is genuinely surprising and therefore informative.

It would also be the first thing anyone has ever recorded about duloxetine and daytime sleep propensity, in any population. [no evidence exists — no MSLT study of duloxetine, or of any SNRI, exists at any dose]

✗ Near-worthless for a NEGATIVE

A null is uninterpretable, because the improved-sleep confound predicts a null under both hypotheses.

"Still can't nap" is fully compatible with "tonic arousal reduced, but I'm also less sleep-deprived than I've been in thirty years."

You should know this before you start, so that a null does not read to you as a refutation.

The asymmetry is the verdict. This is not a test that can tell you "no."

⚠ The killer confound: the drug pushes both ways at once

If it lowered your arousal → latency should FALL. You would nap.
but at the
same time
It improved your sleep → less sleep drive → latency should RISE. Napping gets harder.

These cancel, and nobody knows by how much. And the direction of dominance is measured: sleep drive wins.

Bonnet & Arand 2000 (PMID 10737337), 12 patients with psychophysiological insomnia, measured nap latency directly:

ConditionNap sleep latencyReading
Baseline13.4 minThe starting point
After sleep deprivation3.8 minSleep drive collapses latency by 3.5×. It dominates arousal.
After a 5-minute walk11.9 minA five-minute walk is a bigger effect than any plausible drug effect
After 15 min watching TV6.9 minSame person, same day, nearly double the difference

Their conclusion, verbatim: "measured sleep tendency is a combination of sleep drive and level of central nervous system arousal, where arousal has both state and trait components." [primary, verified]

So why run it at all? Because you have been running an ABORTED version your whole life.

The Multiple Sleep Latency Test protocol allows 20 minutes and scores a no-sleep trial as 20. You lie there for 10 to 20 minutes and give up.

Your lifelong "hard zero" may be a lifelong latency of 18 minutes that you have never once let run to completion. A 20-minute latency is long, but it is a number, and a number can move. A "no" cannot. Running the full 20 converts a lifelong binary into a measurement. [reasoning, not evidence]

Four more things that weaken it, stated because selling you this test would be dishonest

1. The effort paradox is your specific risk. Trying to sleep prevents sleep, and that is the defining mechanism of psychophysiological insomnia — the very phenotype you most resemble. A deliberate test with a hypothesis attached maximises exactly the performance anxiety that raises latency. [reasoning, not evidence]

2. The denominator is unknown. "I don't nap, ever" plus "I haven't tried since the duloxetine" means your attempts are rare, self-selected and uncontrolled. A lifelong zero over an unknown and possibly small number of badly-run attempts is a weaker prior than it feels.

3. One nap is an event, not a rate. A single success does not establish nap ability.

4. You have no comparison dose. The dose-dependence discriminator — the sharpest one — only exists after the decision it would inform.

⚠ And one finding that runs against your own theory

You wondered whether not napping might be causing your poor sleep. Gotts 2015 (PMID 25575044) followed 101 ME/CFS patients with 14-day sleep diaries. Two findings:

21 of 101 (20.8%) did not nap at all across the fortnight. You are in a common fifth, not an anomalous one. [primary, verified]

And, verbatim: "Napping, particularly in the afternoon is associated with poorer cognitive functioning and more daytime sleepiness in CFS." Cross-sectional, so the direction is unresolvable, and confounded by severity. But the sign is the opposite of your assumption, and it converges with standard sleep medicine: daytime napping reduces nocturnal sleep pressure.

Your causal arrow may run backwards. Not napping may be PROTECTING your night sleep rather than damaging it. Untested, live, and worth knowing before you treat learning to nap as a goal. [reasoning, not evidence] One limit: Gotts measured reported napping, not nap ability. It cannot separate "cannot nap" from "chooses not to." Your claim is about ability. It is the closest number that exists and it is not the number you need.

A retraction carried from supplement 7

The claim that "his nap-failure IS the ME/CFS MSLT finding lived" is DEAD. Watson's twins fell asleep in 8.9 minutes. That is not nap failure. And where the ME/CFS MSLT literature is directional at all, it runs the other way: Neu 2008 found CFS patients fall asleep significantly FASTER than controls; Spitzer 2010 found 80% abnormal in the sleepy direction [primary, verified ×2]. Maksoud's systematic review calls MSLT findings "inconsistent" and "non-replicated"; CDC's Decker found "no reproducible perturbations." Your phenotype resembles the insomnia hyperarousal profile (increased MSLT latency), not the ME/CFS one — and even that fit is imperfect, because you do not report insomnia; you report that your sleep improved. You sit between two literatures, and the field's own 2023 review (Dressle & Riemann) names the between-space as an open question rather than a settled overlap.
