ESS 10.9 crosses the clinical threshold for excessive daytime sleepiness — in people whose objective test says they are not sleepy at all. [primary, verified — PMID 15453557]

When you introspect "am I sleepy?", your fatigue answers. The needle is already pinned. Meanwhile the objective channel is clean, normal, and has room to move. So route around introspection. Ask "did I fall asleep?", never "do I feel sleepy?" [reasoning, not evidence — the premises are primary]

Mechanistically supported: Olson 1998 (PMID 9844851, n=225 including 12% with CFS) found ESS correlated with every SCL-90 psychological subscale except psychoticism; mean sleep latency correlated with none of them. Verbatim: "The ESS cannot be used to demonstrate or exclude sleepiness as it is measured by MSLT." [primary, verified]

The discriminator that works: volitional vs intrusive

Two counters. Never merge them. Requires no introspection at all, because "did I choose this?" is a question your fatigue cannot answer for you.

Counter 1 — the benefit channel

Deliberate nap attempts that succeeded count
"I decided to try to sleep, and I did."
Benefit looks like: this rises, and Counter 2 stays at zero.
Maps onto the MSLT: can you sleep when you try?

Counter 2 — the harm channel

Unintended dozes count
"I fell asleep without deciding to" — reading, in conversation, as a passenger.
Head-nods / microsleeps count
Plus: re-read the same paragraph, rewound the same scene.
Drowsy driving or near-miss binary
Safety-critical. Any occurrence is a finding.
Somnolence looks like: this rises, whatever Counter 1 does.
Maps onto the MWT: can you stay awake when you must?

The honest limit, stated because it matters more than the taxonomy

If you can nap, function better, and nothing intrudes, then whether a physiologist would call it "reduced tonic arousal" or "mild somnolence" does not change what you should do.

The question that actually bears on the dose decision is not "which is it?" but "does it cost me anything?" — and that is answered by the unintended-doze count, the night-sleep log, and whether hours disappear. Those are countable. The mechanism label is not. [reasoning, not evidence]

⚠ Two honesty notes. (1) [no evidence exists]: no study validates a behavioural dozing-event diary against drug somnolence in any population. This design is mechanistic reasoning from the MSLT/ESS dissociation, not a validated instrument. Said plainly rather than dressed up. (2) Do not over-cite Watson. Its "may mistake" line is an inference from a group-level dissociation, not a measurement of any individual's discrimination. No study has ever tested, as a psychophysical question, whether an ME/CFS patient can distinguish sleepiness from fatigue. Your exact problem is real and scientifically unaddressed.

Safety note that travels with this design: if sleep-propensity events do rise, duloxetine is not the only candidate. Mariman 2013 (PMID 24182640): of 279 patients referred with presumed CFS, only 23.3% had unequivocal CFS and 49.8% had a sleep disorder, mostly OSA [primary, verified]. Undiagnosed sleep apnoea is the main competing explanation for any new objective sleepiness signal, and that is a clinician conversation.
