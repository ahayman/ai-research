The Mismatch — Therapist-Side vs Patient-Side

The same therapy session, viewed from two angles, produces very different reports. Both reports are real. The locus of the problem is contested — and the framing has consequences.

Anchor. Ogrodniczuk JS, Kealy D, Hadjipavlou GA, Cameron K. (2020) Alexithymia and the therapeutic alliance: A multiple case study. PMC7451330. Documents the therapist-side experience without forcing resolution toward either side.
Therapist-side

What clinicians describe

  • The patient seems bored or disengaged
  • Hard to engage emotionally
  • Closed off / stiff
  • Intellectualizing / defended
  • I find it hard to maintain my own engagement
  • Sessions feel flat or stuck
What's real here: a real interpersonal phenomenon. Working with alexithymic patients can be experientially flat for therapists trained on emotionally expressive clients. The reports are not invented; they are felt.
Mismatch
Patient-side

What the same patients describe

  • I'm confused about what's expected of me
  • I'm anxious about not doing therapy right
  • I'm ashamed I can't produce what they seem to want
  • I feel like a failed therapy patient
  • The therapy isn't fitting my actual problem
  • I'm inside something — sometimes intense — and they read it as emptiness
What's real here: intense effort, often shame, often confusion. The patient is doing something — usually trying hard — even when from outside it looks like little is happening.

The locus question — where is the problem?

Traditional framing Locus is in the patient. The alexithymia is a defence; the work is to break through it. The patient becomes responsible for the alliance friction.
Parsing-deficit / autistic-affirming framing Locus is in the architecture mismatch. The patient's emotional access is genuinely different. What looks like "closed off" is the surface texture of an underlying capacity difference. The therapy framework matters more than the patient's character.
For the user: if past therapy experiences produced reports of being "closed off" or "intellectualizing," that is worth holding alongside what the experience felt like to you. Both are data. Neither is automatically right. The mismatch itself is a documented phenomenon — not a personal pathology. The cumulative shame this dynamic produces is the bridge into the next diagram.
