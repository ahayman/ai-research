Slow Breathing & RSA Mechanism

The single highest evidence-to-cost intervention in this chapter — and a mechanism that does not require polyvagal theory to be correct.

Tier A. Multiple independent RCTs, meta-analyses, established cardiovascular physiology, mechanism predates polyvagal theory by decades, zero conflict-of-interest concerns. The Lehrer resonance-frequency biofeedback protocol (Lehrer et al. 2000) and Russo et al. 2017 review (Breathe journal) anchor the evidence base.
The resonance phenomenon
Breath cycle ≈ 0.1 Hz ≈ Baroreflex feedback delay → Resonance · large RSA amplitude · 4–10× baseline
At a breathing rate of ~6 breaths per minute (cycle ≈ 10 seconds = 0.1 Hz), the breathing cycle matches the natural feedback delay of the baroreflex loop. The cardiovascular system enters resonance. Heart rate oscillates with breathing at 4–10× the amplitude seen at rest. The component of HRV that is vagally mediated (high-frequency HRV) increases dramatically.
Inhale (≈5 sec)
1. Diaphragm descends, lungs expand
2. Vagal brake releases — parasympathetic input to sinoatrial node decreases
3. Heart rate rises
4. Baroreceptors detect change, prep contralateral response
Exhale (≈5 sec)
1. Diaphragm rises, lungs deflate
2. Vagal brake reasserts — parasympathetic input to SA node increases
3. Heart rate falls
4. Baroreceptors detect change, complete the loop
Repeat at ~6 breaths/min · vagally mediated HRV climbs 4–10× baseline · sympathovagal balance shifts parasympathetic

Why this intervention sits at Tier A

  • Independent replication: Lehrer et al. 2000, 2003; Russo et al. 2017; Laborde et al. 2022; multiple meta-analyses 2017–2024.
  • Mechanism well-characterized: baroreflex sensitivity, cardiorespiratory coupling, sympathovagal balance — all established cardiovascular physiology.
  • Effects replicated outside polyvagal theory: HRV biofeedback literature predates Porges's 1994 SPR address and would persist if polyvagal theory were entirely refuted.
  • Cost: free.
  • Risk: essentially zero for moderate practice in healthy adults.
  • Autism-specific endpoints: pilot evidence is positive (PMC9463620, PMC11208699 mini-review) but adult RCTs are sparse. The autism-specific claim sits at Tier B; the general claim sits at Tier A.
