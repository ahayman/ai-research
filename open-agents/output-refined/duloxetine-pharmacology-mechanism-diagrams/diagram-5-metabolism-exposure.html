Metabolism and exposure: why "only 20mg" is not a fixed quantity of anything

Duloxetine is cleared mainly by CYP1A2, with CYP2D6 second. Anything that touches those enzymes moves the dose without moving the prescription.

The direction here is easy to garble, so it is stated the way the study measured it. Augustin compared smokers against non-smokers and found smokers 38.4% and 53.6% lower. Turning that around does not preserve the percentages, because percentage differences are not symmetric when reversed: a smoker sitting 38.4% below a non-smoker means the non-smoker sits about 62% above the smoker, and the dose-adjusted figure inverts to about 116%, not 53.6%. So "the non-smoker runs 38–54% higher" is not what was measured, and it understates the gap by roughly half. The chart above avoids the inversion entirely.
And be careful what the finding does and does not tell you. It does not locate you within the 8.5-fold individual spread, because smoking is one contributor among several, alongside CYP2D6 genotype and individual transporter affinity. What it does tell you is that the population averages quoted at patients are drawn from mixed smoking populations, so they come from people who on average clear this drug faster than you do. The narrow but real use: if the reasoning "you are only on 20mg, that is barely a dose" ever appears in a conversation, the honest reply is that 20mg is not a fixed quantity of anything.
Duloxetine is also itself a moderate CYP2D6 inhibitor, so it acts on other drugs in the opposite direction: tramadol, codeine, metoprolol, some antipsychotics and TCAs. The tramadol case is the one that matters clinically for a fibromyalgia patient, and Category 10 handles it. [primary, verified]
