Severity Continuum vs Cluster Subtypes — Why the Continuum Wins

The Chen four-class scheme (2011/2017) became widely cited because the labels match recognizable presentations. Recent latent-profile-analysis work largely fails to replicate it. The current best evidence supports a severity continuum.

Status: Has not consistently replicated in LPA / FMM work. Cluster analysis (the original method) is methodologically inferior to LPA / FMM for this question.

Better-supported (2021-2025 evidence)

Recent LPA / FMM solutions typically yield 2-3 latent classes distinguished primarily by severity rather than by qualitatively different facet profiles.

What recent LPA/FMM work actually finds:
  • Schaeuffele et al. 2021 (PubMed 33667948, N=612 students). LPA on BVAQ: solutions correspond to "various degrees of severity."
  • Hogrefe FMM 2025 (Chinese sample): TWO-class solution — High-EOT alexithymia 18.2% / Non-alexithymia 81.8%.
  • 2025 Chinese nursing students (BMC Med Educ): three categories distinguished by severity; "high-risk group" most distressed.
  • 2025 elderly LPA (Frontiers Psychology): three profiles — Pervasive Alexithymia 21.15%, Adaptive 72.81%, Affective-Cognitive Dissociation 6.04%.
How to use the cluster labels anyway. The Chen labels (general-high, introvert-high, extrovert-high, non-alexithymic) remain useful as descriptive language people sometimes find helpful for self-recognition. You can identify with one. Just don't treat it as a validated discrete entity. The dimensional question — "where on each axis am I?" — fits the data better than the categorical question — "which of four boxes is mine?" The smallest cluster across LPA studies (~5-10%) consistently shows an affective-cognitive dissociation pattern that maps onto Bermond's Type II — a real but rare profile.

Sources: Chen et al. (2011, 2017) PubMed 28606490; Schaeuffele et al. (2021) PubMed 33667948; Hogrefe FMM 2025 DOI 10.1027/1015-5759/a000828; 2025 Chinese nursing students LPA (BMC Med Educ); 2025 elderly LPA (Frontiers Psychology).
