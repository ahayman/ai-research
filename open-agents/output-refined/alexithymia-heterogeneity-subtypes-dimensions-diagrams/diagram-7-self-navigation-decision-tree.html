Self-Navigation Decision Tree — Which Axes Apply to You?

This is not a diagnostic instrument. It's a navigation tool: each question identifies which heterogeneity axes are most relevant to attend to for your particular profile. Answers can be "yes," "no," "sometimes," or "I'm not sure" — partial information is still useful.

Read first: The whole point of the dimensional reframe in this category is that boxes mislead. Use this tree to identify which axes to attend to, not which category to belong to. "Mostly toward Type II with some hypervigilance overlay" is a more accurate self-description than "I am Type II."
Question 1 — Detection

Can you reliably register body signals?

"When you check in with your body — heart rate, stomach, muscle tension, breath — can you reliably register what's happening? Or do body signals seem absent or unclear?"
"Yes, I register them" Axis 6Detection-route deficit ruled out. Move to parsing question. The deficit is likely upstream at parsing or labeling, not at registration.
"Body signals seem absent / unclear" Axis 6Detection-route concern. Brewer/Bird-style multidomain detection failure may be a factor. Gentler interoception scaffolding warranted; ADIE-style training has shown some effect.
Question 2 — Parsing

When you do detect signals, can you tell what they are?

"When you DO detect body signals, can you tell whether they're emotional (anxiety, anger, sadness) versus non-emotional (hunger, fatigue, illness)? Or do they all feel similar / hard to distinguish?"
"I can usually tell the difference" Axis 6Parsing intact. Alexithymia may be language-route (vocabulary), affective (low arousability), or a different mechanism entirely.
"They all feel similar / blurred" Axis 6Parsing-route deficit likely. Granularity-building / labeling work is the theoretically-motivated intervention. Standard mindfulness contraindicated for the hypervigilance subset (Q6).
Question 3 — Presence vs Absence

Are feelings "there but illegible," or simply absent?

"Do you have feelings that are 'there but illegible' — you sense something is happening internally but can't name it? Or do you experience an absence — not 'I can't name it' but 'there's nothing to name'?"
"There but illegible" Axis 2Closer to Type II — affective dimension intact, cognitive impaired. Granularity-building / labeling work most appropriate. The user's central concern is "what is this thing I'm feeling?"
"Absence — there's nothing to name" Axis 2Closer to Type I — affective dimension impaired. Different therapeutic landscape. Pure introspection may not yield much; psychoeducation and scaffolded affect-induction may be more useful.
Question 4 — Context Dependence

Are your feelings context-dependent?

"Are your feelings absent or unclear in some contexts but present and clear in others? Or is the pattern consistent across all situations?"
"Yes, context-dependent" Axis 5State component is significant; not pure trait alexithymia. Investigate which contexts produce access. Often suggests secondary alexithymia or substantial state overlay.
"Pretty consistent across contexts" Axis 5Trait component dominant. The Tolmunen long-follow-up data (11-year stability) makes this profile look closer to constitutional baseline.
Question 5 — Etiology

How long has this been the case?

"Has this been the case as long as you can remember? Or did it emerge after a specific event, illness, or period of prolonged stress?"
"As long as I can remember" Axis 3Primary alexithymia. Constitutional component likely. Heritability ~30-42%; often co-occurs with autism. Externalized / cognitive-first interventions tend to fit better.
"Post-event / period" Axis 3Secondary alexithymia. More state-changeable; trauma-informed work often appropriate. Cameron 2024 review: medium effects with therapy.
"Post-stroke / TBI / brain event" Axis 3Organic alexithymia. Anterior insula involvement likely. Neurorehabilitation framework, not insight-oriented therapy.
Question 6 — Hypervigilance

Are you hypervigilant about body signals?

"Are you hypervigilant about body signals AND prone to interpreting them somatically (medical concerns, physical symptoms) rather than emotionally? Have you been told you have somatic symptom disorder, fibromyalgia, IBS, FND, or chronic pain?"
"Yes, hypervigilant + somatic interpretation" Axis 6Hypervigilance-with-mislabeling profile. Interoception-first work specifically contraindicated. Defusion (ACT) / reattribution work indicated. This is the user's flagged risk.
"No, body attention is not heightened" Axis 6Rules out the hypervigilance complication. Standard interoception-graded interventions are not specifically contraindicated on this axis.
Question 7 — Emotional Intensity

How intense are your emotions when they happen?

"Setting aside whether you can identify them, how intense are your emotions when they happen? Do you experience strong reactions, vivid fantasy life, dreams with emotional content? Or is your inner emotional life relatively flat?"
"Intense / vivid" Axes 2, 7Affective dimension intact. The cognitive dimension (identifying, verbalizing, analyzing) is the locus. Maps to Bermond Type II profile.
"Flat / muted" Axes 2, 7Affective dimension impaired. Closer to Type I. The deficit reaches into emotional intensity itself, not just identification.

What to do with your answers

Each question identifies which heterogeneity axis is most relevant for you to attend to. The pattern matters more than any single answer. A common autistic-adult profile is: detection or language-route (Q1, Q2) + feelings present but illegible (Q3) + relatively trait-stable (Q4, Q5) + emotionally intense underneath (Q7) — which clusters loosely toward Type II / cognitive alexithymia.

A common trauma-derived profile is: detection variable (Q1) + parsing variable (Q2) + feelings sometimes present, sometimes overwhelmed (Q3) + context-dependent (Q4) + post-event onset (Q5) + may include hypervigilance (Q6) — which clusters loosely toward secondary alexithymia with possible state overlay.

The point is not to assign yourself to a category. The point is to know which axes are most active for you, so you can read therapy claims, intervention recommendations, and clinical advice with the right filter.

Caveats — limits of self-classification
  • The recursion problem. People with alexithymia are by definition less able to introspect on emotional/internal states. Self-report on emotion-introspection has known reliability limits. Triangulate with: behavioral evidence (what do you do?), others' reports, observable patterns over time.
  • State contamination. Acute distress, depression, or trauma flare-ups can distort answers. The tree is most reliable when answered during a relatively stable state.
  • Comorbidity confounds. Autism, ADHD, complex trauma, somatic symptom disorder, depression — each shapes how the questions land. The same answer can mean different things in different comorbidity contexts.
  • Self-classification ≠ diagnosis. The tree helps you know which axes to attend to and which interventions are theoretically appropriate. It does not substitute for clinical assessment using validated instruments.
  • Categorical thinking creep. Even after using the tree, hold your location on each axis as continuous and probabilistic, not categorical. The dimensional reading fits the data better than the box-assignment.
