The Polyvagal Ladder for Autistic Experience — Neutral Map

Deb Dana's ladder metaphor is the most widely-used didactic version of polyvagal theory. This diagram presents it neutrally — so a reader who finds it useful for self-narrative can see it clearly — alongside an explicit "what this map is / what this map is not" panel. Scaffolding can be valuable even when the mechanism claims are contested.

TOP — VENTRAL VAGAL

Social engagement / safety / connection

Felt sense: calm, present, curious, available to other people, "felt safety." Capable of dual focus on internal state and external interaction.

For autistic adults: a state of regulated alone-time can be ventral; autistic ventral may look quieter and less smiley than the popular ladder assumes. Solo focus, special interest engagement, regulated co-presence — all can be ventral if internal cost is low.

MIDDLE — SYMPATHETIC

Mobilisation / fight-flight / meltdown maps here

Felt sense: activation, agitation, "too much," urgency, fight or flight, panic, anger, motor restlessness, sensory amplification.

For autistic adults: meltdown sits here. The Cheng 2020 HRV evidence (RSA reactivity g = −0.72 under social stress) is consistent with autistic systems entering this state with less parasympathetic withdrawal — i.e., more raw sympathetic dominance, faster.

BOTTOM — DORSAL VAGAL

Immobilisation / collapse / shutdown maps here

Felt sense: withdrawal, numbness, disconnect, "checked out," frozen, dissociated, low energy, low engagement, mute, distant.

For autistic adults: shutdown sits here as a description. Neuhuber & Berthoud (2022) note the proposed DMV-mediated mechanism may be wrong — the experience is real, the neural pathway claim is contested.

What This Map IS / What This Map IS NOT

IS — useful as scaffolding

  • A recognisable map of internal states for self-noticing
  • A shared language with practitioners who use it
  • A way to communicate state to other people ("I dropped into dorsal in that meeting")
  • A noticing tool for the transition between states (often the most useful intervention point)
  • A framework that connects internal experience to behavioural patterns over time
  • A reminder that "not regulated" is not "broken" — states cycle

IS NOT — claims with weak/no evidence

  • A measured neural state. No one is currently measuring "you are in dorsal vagal" in vivo.
  • An empirically validated mechanism. The DMV-mediated freeze claim is contested (Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022).
  • A causal hierarchy. Ventral being "best" frames anything-but-ventral as worse — this can pathologise normal nervous-system fluctuation.
  • A diagnostic substitute. "I'm in dorsal" doesn't tell you whether you have ME/CFS, depression, sleep loss, or autistic burnout.
  • An autism-specific framework. It's a general PVT didactic, not built from autistic phenomenology.
  • A treatment plan. Naming the state isn't the same as addressing what's loading it.
How to use this diagram honestly If the ladder helps you notice your state, name it, and respond — keep using it. That's real value. Just hold the framing as scaffolding rather than as confirmed neural mechanism. "Felt sense map" is the most accurate way to describe what it is. Trauma Geek's neurodiversity-affirming reading is consistent with this stance: adopt the felt-sense pattern, push back on the deficit framing and the ventral-supremacy claims.
