### Diagram 1: Timeline of Constructed Languages
- **Type**: Custom HTML/CSS timeline (horizontal, era-grouped)
- **Elements**: 6 eras, 15+ individual language events
- **Key Insight**: Nine centuries of linguistic engineering, from Hildegard von Bingen's mystical Lingua Ignota through the Scientific Revolution's philosophical languages, 19th-century international auxiliary movement, mid-20th-century logical experiments, Tolkien's artistic conlangs, and into the modern community-driven era. Each era reflects a different motivation -- divine revelation, scientific taxonomy, international peace, logical precision, artistic expression, creative play.

### Diagram 2: Wilkins' Taxonomic Real Character
- **Type**: SVG tree diagram with annotation panels
- **Elements**: 4-level hierarchy tree, encoding example panel, structure summary, problem annotation
- **Key Insight**: Wilkins attempted to classify all reality into 40 genera, each subdivided by differences and species. Each concept's symbol encodes its taxonomic position: "Zi" = Beast genus, "Zit" = Dog. The fatal flaw was assuming nature divides into clean, non-overlapping categories -- one misclassification cascades through the entire system.
- **Accessibility**: Top: "All of Reality" root node. Second level: three example genera (Element, Beast, Motion) from 40 total. Under Beast: three differences (Viviparous, Oviparous, Insect). Under Viviparous: three species (Dog-kind, Cat, Horse). Encoding panel shows Zi (genus) + t (difference) = Zit (dog), plus Zita (male dog) and Ziti (female dog). Problem panel notes that nature does not divide into neat categories.

### Diagram 3: Esperanto Morphology Decomposition
- **Type**: SVG morphological decomposition (block composition)
- **Elements**: Part-of-speech endings table, root derivation example, affix combination chain, common affixes reference
- **Key Insight**: Esperanto's agglutinative morphology builds words from invariant components with zero irregularity. ~900 roots + ~50 affixes + 4 endings = tens of thousands of words. The word "malsanulejo" (hospital) decomposes transparently: mal- (opposite) + san- (health) + -ul- (person) + -ej- (place) + -o (noun).
- **Accessibility**: Top section: four mandatory part-of-speech endings (-o noun, -a adjective, -e adverb, -i/-as/-is/-os/-us verb). Example 1: root "san-" (health) branches to sano (noun), sana (adj), sane (adv), sani (verb). Example 2: five colored blocks chained: mal- (prefix: opposite) + san- (root: health) + -ul- (suffix: person) + -ej- (suffix: place) + -o (ending: noun) = malsanulejo (hospital). Bottom: tables of ~10 common prefixes and ~40 common suffixes with examples.

### Diagram 4: Language Type Feature Comparison Matrix
- **Type**: HTML table with styled cells
- **Elements**: 5 language types compared across 12 features in 3 categories
- **Key Insight**: Natural languages score highest on the features conlang designers most want to eliminate (ambiguity, irregularity, redundancy, polysemy) but also highest on usability, expressiveness, and community adoption. The inverse correlation between formal precision and practical expressiveness reveals the perfection paradox: Lojban eliminates ambiguity but struggles with emotion; Esperanto succeeds because it retained natural-language features.
- **Accessibility**: Five columns: Natural Language (English, Mandarin), Philosophical (Wilkins, Dalgarno), Auxiliary (Esperanto, Ido), Logical (Lojban, Loglan), Artistic (Quenya, Na'vi). Twelve rows in three categories. Structural Properties: ambiguity, irregularity, redundancy, polysemy. Usability: learnability, expressiveness, emotional range. Social/Practical: speaker community, cultural evolution, error tolerance, primary goal. Cells color-coded: green (high), amber (medium), red (low/none), purple (varies).

### Diagram 5: The Perfection Paradox Cycle
- **Type**: SVG cyclical flowchart with examples
- **Elements**: 4-step cycle, 3 historical example boxes, lesson summary
- **Key Insight**: Every constructed language encounters the same cycle: identify a natural-language "flaw," engineer it away, discover the "flaw" served an essential function, watch the language fail or evolve to reintroduce the feature. Wilkins' taxonomy made errors catastrophic, Lojban's precision made casual conversation impossible, Volapuk's rigid control drove speakers to Esperanto.
- **Accessibility**: Four-step cycle: (1) Identify a "Flaw" in natural language (blue, top) -> (2) Design "Perfect" Fix to eliminate the flaw (green, right) -> (3) Discover Hidden Cost -- the "flaw" was serving a purpose (amber, right-bottom) -> (4) Language Fails or Adapts -- speakers abandon or reintroduce the feature (red, bottom). Dashed arrow loops from step 4 back to step 1. Three historical examples below: Wilkins (targeted arbitrary words, made errors cascade), Lojban (targeted ambiguity, lost casual expressiveness), Volapuk (targeted irregularity, lost community to Esperanto).

### Diagram 6: Conlang Family Tree

- **Elements**: 18 language nodes, 5 tradition branches, chronological axis
- **Key Insight**: Constructed languages form branching intellectual lineages. Philosophical languages (Lodwick, Dalgarno, Wilkins, Leibniz) influenced each other directly. Leibniz's unfinished algebra of thought anticipates logical languages by three centuries. The auxiliary tradition runs from Volapuk through Esperanto and its forks. Tolkien launched the artistic tradition leading to Hollywood conlangs. All streams converge in the modern community.

Auxiliary (teal): Volapuk 1879, Esperanto 1887, Ido 1907, Occidental 1922, Novial 1928, Interlingua 1951. Logical (green): Loglan 1955, Lojban 1987. Solid lines show direct derivation; dashed lines show intellectual influence.

### Diagram 7: Why Natural Language "Flaws" Are Necessary
- **Type**: SVG mind map (radial, 4 branches)
- **Elements**: Central node, 4 "flaw" nodes, 4 "function" nodes with detail lists, conlang evidence for each
- **Key Insight**: Four features conlang designers repeatedly try to eliminate -- ambiguity, irregularity, redundancy, polysemy -- each serve essential communicative functions. Ambiguity enables efficiency (Zipf's Law). Irregularity aids rapid processing of high-frequency words. Redundancy provides error correction (listeners miss ~25% of speech signal). Polysemy enables metaphorical extension (nearly all abstract vocabulary originates from concrete metaphors).
- **Accessibility**: Central node "Natural Language 'Imperfections'" with four radiating branches. Top-left: Ambiguity (red) -> Communicative Efficiency (green): fewer words needed, Zipf's Law, enables humor/diplomacy, allows strategic vagueness. Without it: Wilkins required 2000+ symbols. Top-right: Irregularity (purple) -> Rapid Pattern Recognition (green): high-frequency words stay irregular for faster retrieval, distinct forms prevent confusion, gives language "flavor." Without it: Volapuk felt artificial. Bottom-left: Redundancy (blue) -> Error Correction (green): noisy environments, like telecom error-correcting codes, listeners miss 25% of signal. Without it: Wilkins one-mark errors. Bottom-right: Polysemy (amber) -> Metaphorical Extension (green): new concepts via existing words, conceptual metaphor, organic vocabulary growth. Without it: Lojban struggles creatively. Bottom summary: Every "flaw" is a feature optimized by millennia of human use.
