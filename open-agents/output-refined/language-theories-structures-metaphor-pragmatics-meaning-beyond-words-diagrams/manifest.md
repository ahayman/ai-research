## Diagrams

### Diagram 1: Grice's Cooperative Principle and Maxims
- **File:** `diagram-1-mindmap.html`
- **Type:** Mermaid mind map
- **Description:** Cooperative Principle at center radiating to the four maxims -- Quantity (be informative), Quality (be truthful), Relation (be relevant), and Manner (be clear) -- each with sub-requirements and flouting examples that generate conversational implicature. Shows how blatant violation of each maxim produces inferred meaning beyond literal content.
- **Key concepts:** Cooperative Principle, maxims, flouting, conversational implicature, Grice (1975)

### Diagram 2: Speech Act Theory -- Three Simultaneous Layers
- **File:** `diagram-2-flowchart.html`
- **Type:** Custom SVG/CSS layered diagram
- **Description:** Three stacked layers showing that every utterance simultaneously performs a locutionary act (what is said), an illocutionary act (what is meant/done), and a perlocutionary act (what effect is achieved). Uses "Can you pass the salt?" as the running example to demonstrate how a grammatical question functions as a request (indirect speech act).
- **Key concepts:** Austin's three-act model, locutionary/illocutionary/perlocutionary, indirect speech acts, Searle's taxonomy, felicity conditions

### Diagram 3: Relevance Theory Processing Model
- **File:** `diagram-3-flowchart.html`
- **Type:** Mermaid flowchart
- **Description:** Step-by-step comprehension procedure: utterance received, decode linguistic meaning, enrich to explicature (disambiguate, assign reference, saturate, free-enrich), derive implicatures, test for optimal relevance (sufficient cognitive effects for minimal processing effort), accept or revise. Includes the effort/effect trade-off and the Communicative Principle of Relevance.
- **Key concepts:** Relevance Theory, Sperber & Wilson, explicature vs. implicature, ostensive-inferential communication, cognitive effects, processing effort

### Diagram 4: Politeness Strategy Selection
- **File:** `diagram-4-flowchart.html`
- **Type:** Mermaid decision tree/flowchart
- **Description:** Decision tree from face-threatening act assessment through weightiness calculation (W = D + P + R) to five strategies ranked by increasing indirectness: bald on record, positive politeness, negative politeness, off record, and don't do the FTA. Each strategy includes concrete example utterances. Includes the two dimensions of face (positive and negative).
- **Key concepts:** Brown & Levinson, face, positive/negative face, FTA weightiness, politeness strategies, indirectness

### Diagram 5: Presupposition Triggers and Projection
- **File:** `diagram-5-flowchart.html`
- **Type:** Mermaid flowchart
- **Description:** Eight categories of presupposition triggers (factive verbs, definite descriptions, change-of-state verbs, cleft sentences, iteratives, temporal clauses, counterfactuals, implicative verbs) feeding into the negation test. Shows how presuppositions project (survive negation) unlike entailments. Addresses the projection problem: how presuppositions behave when embedded in conditionals, disjunctions, and attitude reports. Includes accommodation.
- **Key concepts:** Presupposition, presupposition triggers, projection problem, filtering, accommodation, common ground

### Diagram 6: Types of Deixis
- **File:** `diagram-6-flowchart.html`
- **Type:** Mermaid mind map (radial)
- **Description:** Five types of deixis radiating from center: Person deixis (I/you/he/she/they), Spatial deixis (here/there, proximal/distal), Temporal deixis (now/then, tense as deictic), Discourse deixis (this/that in text), and Social deixis (tu/vous, honorifics). Each branch includes examples and cross-linguistic notes (Japanese three-way spatial distinction, T-V systems).
- **Key concepts:** Deixis, indexicality, person/spatial/temporal/discourse/social deixis, context-dependence

### Diagram 7: Pragmatic Inference Chain -- "I have to work"
- **File:** `diagram-7-flowchart.html`
- **Type:** Mermaid flowchart
- **Description:** Traces the complete pragmatic inference process for the exchange "Are you going to the party tonight?" / "I have to work." Four stages: linguistic decoding (logical form), pragmatic enrichment (reference resolution, temporal enrichment, saturation yielding the explicature), implicature derivation (refusal, explanation, regret, illocutionary force assignment), and full speaker meaning recovery. Demonstrates the underdetermination thesis: the literal words say nothing about parties, yet competent speakers process all of this instantly.
- **Key concepts:** Underdetermination, explicature, implicature, reference resolution, enrichment, illocutionary force, the architecture of pragmatic inference
