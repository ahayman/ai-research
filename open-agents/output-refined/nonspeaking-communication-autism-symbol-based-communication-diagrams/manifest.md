## Diagram Descriptions

### Diagram 1: PECS Six Phases Progression
- **Type**: Flowchart (stepped progression)
- **Elements**: 6 phase nodes, 1 stall warning, communicative function labels
- **Key Insight**: PECS phases build from basic requesting (mand) to spontaneous commenting (tact); many programs stall at Phase III, never reaching the higher communicative functions
- **Accessibility**: Six sequential phases. Phase I: physical exchange (basic mand). Phase II: distance and persistence. Phase III: picture discrimination. Warning: many programs stall here. Phase IV: sentence structure. Phase V: responding to questions. Phase VI: commenting (tact).

### Diagram 2: Symbol System Comparison Matrix
- **Type**: Flowchart (comparison matrix)
- **Elements**: 4 system subgraphs with 6 dimensions each
- **Key Insight**: PECS has strongest autism evidence; Makaton is most widely adopted in UK; Blissymbols has highest compositionality; ARASAAC has best iconicity and is free/open-source
- **Accessibility**: Four systems compared across iconicity, compositionality, modality, evidence base, primary population, and cost.

### Diagram 3: Symbol Transparency Spectrum
- **Type**: Flowchart (horizontal spectrum)
- **Elements**: 4 transparency levels, learnability/expressiveness indicators, trade-off note
- **Key Insight**: Transparent symbols are easier to learn but less flexible; opaque compositional systems offer more expressive power at the cost of a steeper learning curve
- **Accessibility**: Four levels from left to right. Transparent: photograph immediately guessable. Translucent: line drawing requires inference. Opaque: Blissymbol requires teaching. Abstract: modifier symbols from rules only.

### Diagram 4: Communicative Functions Hierarchy
- **Type**: Flowchart (inverted pyramid)
- **Elements**: 6 levels from requesting to narrating, intervention gap warning
- **Key Insight**: Most symbol-based programs stall at requesting; the full range of human communication extends far beyond getting needs met
- **Accessibility**: Six levels. Level 1 (base): requesting/mand. Level 2: rejecting. Level 3: social routines. Level 4: commenting/tact. Level 5: asking questions. Level 6: narrating, emotions, humor. Warning about gap between intervention focus and communication range.

### Diagram 5: Development Pathways
- **Type**: Flowchart (branching paths)
- **Elements**: 1 entry point, 4 path subgraphs with 3 steps each, validation node
- **Key Insight**: Symbol-based communication leads to four equally valid outcomes -- bridge to speech, bridge to literacy, lifelong primary mode, or bridge to high-tech AAC
- **Accessibility**: Entry: symbol-based communication. Four paths diverge. Path A: bridge to speech (frustration reduced, speech emerges, symbols fade). Path B: bridge to literacy (symbols to written words to text communication). Path C: lifelong primary mode (vocabulary expands, sophistication increases). Path D: bridge to high-tech AAC (low-tech skills transfer to SGD/app). All paths converge on validation: all paths are valid.
