Why downgraded: dropouts"Substantial dropouts from all trials." "Nearly all the studies had a dropout rate of more than 20%"; only four came in under 20%.
Why downgraded: narrow populationsOverwhelmingly female trial populations. Nobody in these trials was recruited as an autistic adult with lifelong ME/CFS.
Why downgraded: publication biasFlagged explicitly in CD010292, alongside indirectness and imprecision.
Why downgraded: sponsorshipAlmost every study performed or sponsored by the manufacturer, which Cochrane names as a bias domain.

What sponsorship does and does not license you to conclude

It does not mean the trials are wrong. Lilly's trials are the reason we know 60mg beats placebo, and they are also the reason we know 20mg does not, which is not a result a sponsor would choose to produce. Goldstein 2005 is a Lilly trial and it is the single most decision-relevant finding on this page, pointing against a larger market. reasoning, not evidence

What it does mean is concrete and specific: the interpretations layered on top of the data are not neutral. The most-cited reading of Russell's 20mg null, that it was a power artifact "and not due to lack of efficacy in pain relief," is an opinion authored by someone paid to speak for the manufacturer. It is not a finding. And the claim from the same source that Russell's 20mg effect was "similar to that seen in the 60 mg/day group" does not survive arithmetic:

Arnold 2012's adequately powered 30mg arm produced a drug–placebo difference of 0.34 on BPI average pain. Russell's 120mg arm produced 0.92. A 20mg effect of 0.75 to 0.92 is not plausible when 30mg, a higher dose, adequately powered, produces 0.34. reasoning, not evidence — a cross-trial inference, not a within-trial comparison

The rule this suggests: trust the sponsored trials' results, and read the sponsored interpretations of those results with a much colder eye.
