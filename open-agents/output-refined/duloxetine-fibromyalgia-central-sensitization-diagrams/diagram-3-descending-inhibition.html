Why Norepinephrine, Specifically

Your brainstem runs descending pain-control pathways down to the spinal dorsal horn, and they filter nociceptive traffic before it becomes pain. Two transmitter systems run those pathways. Only one of them is reliably a brake. This is the mechanism that predicts the threshold rather than being retrofitted to it.

The noradrenergic limb: three brakes, one transmitter

This is why norepinephrine matters to pain specifically, and it is why "more serotonin" is not a substitute. Three anatomically distinct inhibitory mechanisms all run on the same transmitter, which gives descending noradrenergic fibres unusually effective control over incoming nociceptive traffic.

Pertovaara also notes the state-dependence that matters for a chronic condition, verbatim:

"While in baseline conditions the noradrenergic system may have little effect, sustained pain induces noradrenergic feedback inhibition of pain." primary, verified

A drug that raises norepinephrine availability in this limb is aimed at the right target. Whether it hits it hard enough at any tolerable dose is what the trials answer, and the answer is "somewhat, in a minority."

The serotonergic limb: not simply additive

Descending serotonergic control is bidirectional, which is the complication that usually gets left out of the SNRI story.

In healthy states Broadly antinociceptive. Serotonin damps the signal.
After tissue / nerve injury Drives facilitation, not inhibition. Serotonin amplifies the signal.

The best-supported mechanism for the pro-nociceptive limb is spinal 5-HT3 receptor signalling from the RVM, acting on primary afferent terminals, excitatory interneurons and projection neurons. secondary — unverified on the taxonomy; the individual findings are primary

The consequence: more serotonin is not straightforwardly more analgesia, and in a sensitised state it can point the wrong way.

Nociplastic pain: why this is the right target for fibromyalgia

In 2017 the IASP adopted "nociplastic pain" as a third mechanistic descriptor alongside nociceptive and neuropathic pain, defined as:

"pain that arises from altered nociception despite no clear evidence of actual or threatened tissue damage causing the activation of peripheral nociceptors or evidence for disease or lesion of the somatosensory system causing the pain." primary, verified

Peripheral and central sensitisation, presenting as allodynia and hyperalgesia, are typically present though not specific to it. Fibromyalgia is the archetype: hyperalgesia, allodynia, expanded receptive fields, and impaired endogenous inhibition.

That last item is the point of contact with everything above. If the descending brakes are part of what is failing, then raising transmitter availability in the descending noradrenergic limb is aimed squarely at the defect. reasoning, not evidence

A citation correction found while building this page

The original research for this category cited "Noradrenergic pain modulation" to PMID 16766858. That PMID resolves to Yoshimura & Furue, a different paper. "Noradrenergic pain modulation" is the title of Pertovaara's paper, PMID 17030082, and the three-receptor formulation used above is Pertovaara's.

Both papers are real and both support the mechanism, so nothing collapses. But the content and the identifier came from different sources, which is this project's standing failure mode in miniature: a real paper attached to the wrong identifier. A resolving PMID is not verification.

A second correction: the original research attributed the serotonergic switch to "spinal chloride imbalance switches inhibition to facilitation." That conflates two mechanisms. The chloride-gradient story (KCC2 collapse) concerns GABA-A and glycine signalling becoming excitatory; it is not the serotonergic facilitation mechanism. This page does not reproduce the chloride attribution.
