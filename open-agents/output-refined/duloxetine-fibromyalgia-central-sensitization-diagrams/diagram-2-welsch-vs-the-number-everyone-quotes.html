NNTB 11, Not the NNTB 8 Everyone Quotes

This project spent eight passes arguing over a number from a mixed-indication review while a fibromyalgia-specific review, larger, newer and far more precise, sat unused. Here is the comparison, and the nuance that has to travel with it.

Why the newer number wins even though it is less flattering

The point estimate moves down and the precision improves substantially. The intervals overlap, so these are not contradictory findings; the fibromyalgia-specific review is simply a better match for a fibromyalgia patient.

Lower NNTB is better. The vertical mark is the point estimate. Duloxetine's fibromyalgia effect is smaller than almost anyone quotes.

The nuance that must travel with the headline

Reporting "NNTB 11, no clinically relevant benefit" on its own would be accurate and would still misrepresent the review. Three things belong with it.

1 "No clinically relevant benefit" is a threshold judgment, not a measurement. Welsch pre-specified a cut-off of NNTB 10. NNTB 11 missed it by one point. The effect itself is 31% versus 21%, and it is statistically solid (RD 0.09, 95% CI 0.07 to 0.11). primary, verified

3. Welsch pools duloxetine with milnacipran. It is fibromyalgia-specific but not duloxetine-specific: duloxetine supplies only 7 of the 18 studies. So the two reviews are not perfectly comparable. They differ on which axis they match: CD007115 matches the drug, CD010292 matches the disease.

Cochrane's own bottom line keeps both halves, and so should any honest summary:

"A minority of people with fibromyalgia might experience substantial symptom relief without clinically relevant adverse events with duloxetine or milnacipran." primary, verified

The honest summary: duloxetine's fibromyalgia effect is real, is small, and sits close enough to the line that where you put the line decides the verdict.
