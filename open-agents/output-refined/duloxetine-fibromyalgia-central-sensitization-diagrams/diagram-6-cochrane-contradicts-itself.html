Cochrane Contradicts Itself on Russell 2008

Two passes of this project reached opposite conclusions about the 20mg arm while both citing real sources correctly. This diagram shows why. The source is internally inconsistent, and the primary paper is paywalled everywhere.

The single source

Cochrane CD007115.pub3 — one review, one document, two incompatible statements about the same trial arm.

⇩       ⇩
Location 1 — the fibromyalgia narrative

"Significant on nothing"

"The 20 mg dose of duloxetine in Russell 2008 (223 participants) did not show significant differences in any of the reported measures."

Reading: 20mg separated on NOTHING.
Who followed this: the original research pass, which concluded 20mg "failed."
Location 2 — Analysis 3.8

"Significant at 20mg"

"Four studies reported the PGI‑I ..., which was significantly in favour of duloxetine at the 20 mg, 30 mg, 60 mg and 120 mg doses... The magnitude of change at each dose was very similar, with no dose effect. However, the magnitude of change failed to reach a level considered to be clinically significant."

Reading: 20mg DID separate on PGI-I.
Who followed this: the validation pass, which concluded 20mg was "under-studied, not disproven."

Both are Cochrane's own words, in the same review.

Neither pass fabricated anything. One followed the narrative prose, the other followed the quantitative analysis. Russell 2008's full text is paywalled everywhere, and three separate passes have now failed to get past it. This page reports the contradiction as UNRESOLVED and will not pick a side.

No dose effect. Never clinically significant at any dose.

A measure that separates at every dose tested, does not move with dose, and never reaches clinical significance is not evidence that 20mg works. It is evidence that PGI-I is a weak, dose-insensitive instrument. The validation pass built its rehabilitation of 20mg on it. That rehabilitation does not hold. Both earlier passes need correcting here, in opposite directions.

Design details that change what this trial can say

Randomisation1:2:2:2. The 20mg arm held 79 patients against 144 on placebo, 150 on 60mg, 147 on 120mg. Total 520.
PowerRoughly 70–90% depending on the true effect size. A null here is weak evidence of absence, not no evidence. (This is an estimate from published arm sizes, not a retrieved power calculation.)
The titration20mg patients were titrated up to 60mg at week 15. So the arm labelled "20mg" at 6 months is not a 20mg arm: those patients had been on 60mg for 13 weeks. No 6-month conclusion about 20mg can be drawn from this trial in either direction.
Lilly's design intentVerbatim from Lilly's own pooled analysis: "these data were not included in this analysis because this dose was used as a subtherapeutic control." That establishes Lilly did not consider 20mg a dose under test. It does not establish that 20mg is inert, because that is a design decision made in advance, not a result. primary, verified
Who interpreted itThe most-cited reading of the 20mg null, that it was a power artifact "and not due to lack of efficacy in pain relief," comes from a review whose senior author is on Eli Lilly's speaker's bureau. That is an interested party's opinion, not a finding.

Why this trial is not the load-bearing one anyway

Goldstein 2005 settles the dose question without needing Russell at all. It randomised 1:1:1:1, so the 20mg arm and the 60mg arm had the same number of patients (≈114 each). 60mg separated on pain. 20mg did not. The power argument that makes Russell ambiguous is simply unavailable in an equal-arm within-trial comparison.

And Arnold 2012 tested 30mg with adequate power (n=155 vs 153) and the pain primary came back P=0.202. Russell can stay unresolved forever and the threshold still stands.
