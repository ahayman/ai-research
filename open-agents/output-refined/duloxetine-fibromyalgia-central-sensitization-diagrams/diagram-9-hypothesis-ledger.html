The Crash-Aggravation Hypothesis, Audited

"I suspect they aggravate the whole CFS and fibromyalgia." That is three separate claims, and they have wildly different evidential status. Conflating them is the main risk, and almost every discussion of this idea conflates them.

Claim 1

Sensory / emotional overload triggers crashes in ME/CFS

DOCUMENTED

Described in a primary diagnostic consensus document, using your literal word "crash." Not experimentally demonstrated, but formally recognised.

Claim 2

Crashes aggravate fibromyalgia / ME-CFS through cumulative damage

UNSTUDIED, AND ADJACENT EVIDENCE LEANS AGAINST

This is where the model is load-bearing and unsupported.

Claim 3

Therefore preventing crashes improves pain at 20mg over months

NEVER TESTED BY ANYONE

The nearest proxies are unfavourable.

The load-bearing link is Claim 2, and it is the one nothing supports. Claim 1 is a real find and it is genuinely validating. Claim 3 is the one that would change a dose decision. Claim 1 does not carry Claim 2, and without Claim 2 there is no Claim 3.

What genuinely favours the hypothesis

The CCC describes your exact sequence, in your own word REAL FIND
Canadian Consensus Criteria 2003, Criterion 5, verbatim: "There may be overload phenomena: cognitive, sensory... and/or emotional overload, which may lead to "crash" periods and/or anxiety." The document defines "crash" as "a temporary period of immobilizing physical and/or cognitive fatigue." Sensory overload → crash → anxiety, written down in 2003.
Carruthers & van de Sande 2003. Primary, verified. But: expert consensus and clinical description, not experimental evidence. It says crashes happen. It says nothing about crashes causing lasting damage.
Emotional distress → post-exertional malaise has survey support PARTIAL
Chu 2018, N=150: 90% (130/144) reported symptom exacerbation with emotional distress.
Critical correction: Chu never asked about sensory exposure as a trigger. "Sensory overload" appears in that paper as a write-in symptom of PEM, not a trigger. The 90% is real and it is about a different trigger than yours.
Week-long post-exertional states are documented FITS
Chu 2018: 84% reported ≥24h; 24% reported up to more than a week, in some cases several months. Your 1–2 week crash sits comfortably inside the documented range.
One allostatic-load datapoint points your way CANNOT SHOW DIRECTION
Goertzel 2006: within ME/CFS, high allostatic load associated with worse bodily pain and physical functioning.
Cross-sectional, so direction is unresolvable. This is the one datapoint that favours you, and it cannot establish causation.
Your intersection has essentially no literature ABSENCE OF RESEARCH
"autistic burnout" AND ("chronic fatigue" OR "ME/CFS" OR "post-exertional") returns 1 result, and it mentions chronic fatigue only as something to rule out. That is an absence of research, not a demonstrated null.

What the adjacent evidence says

Allostatic load has never been measured in fibromyalgia 0 PAPERS
"allostatic load"[ti] AND fibromyalgia[ti] = 0 results. [tiab] = 9 results, and not one measured an allostatic load index in fibromyalgia patients. The word is borrowed.
The one FM-specific item (Leombruni 2019) is a 2-page letter with no abstract, and it studies allostatic overload, a different construct.
In ME/CFS it is non-significant or marginal WEAK
Maloney 2006: OR 1.9, 95% CI 0.75–4.75, not significant, and the abstract's confident conclusion overstates its own data. Maloney 2009: OR 1.26, CI 1.00–1.59, lower bound exactly 1.00; and could not distinguish ME/CFS from subsyndromal fatigue (OR 1.10, 0.93–1.31).
The direction is backwards CONTRADICTS
Maloney 2009, verbatim: "Among persons with CFS, the duration of fatigue was inversely correlated with ALI (r = −.26, p = .047)." Longer illness, lower allostatic load. A cumulative-damage model predicts the opposite.
The limb that predicts pain is the wrong limb WRONG TARGET
Liang & Booker 2024, N=781, ~6.7 years, 27 biomarkers: metabolic dysregulation predicted high-interference pain (RRR 2.00) and ≥3 pain sites (RRR 2.03). Parasympathetic dysregulation predicted nothing. No AL phenotype predicted chronic pain onset at all.
Your model runs entirely through autonomic and HPA activation. Where allostatic load predicts pain, it is the cardiometabolic limb: BMI, waist-to-hip ratio, lipids.
The best within-person data is null on the direct path NULL
Okifuji 2011, N=81, 30 days, 3×/day: fatigue→pain p<.001; distress→fatigue p<.001; pain→distress p<.001; distress→pain NULL. Authors: "The failure of emotional distress to predict subsequent pain may seem counterintuitive."
An indirect path survives: distress → fatigue → pain. That is your model's best scaffold, and it is what makes the fatigue limb the test.
The timescale gap is ~3 orders of magnitude FATAL TO A NAIVE READ
Stress-induced hyperalgesia is real (Crettaz 2013; Löffler 2023). But every human experiment measures pain sensitivity minutes to ~90 minutes after a stressor. Not one measures weeks or months later. Your model lives on the timescale of a season; the experiments live on the timescale of a lab visit.
Nobody has ever tested the actual claim NO EVIDENCE EXISTS
No prospective study has ever asked whether crash frequency predicts later pain or illness severity. Not one. And "allostatic load" AND (intervention OR reduc*) AND randomized[pt] = 0 results: no RCT has ever tested whether reducing allostatic load improves any outcome.

The verdict is an awkward shape, and the awkwardness is the honest part

Descriptively supportedThe phenomenology is real and formally recognised.
Causally unstudiedThe load-bearing link has never been tested by anyone.
Adjacent evidence unfavourableThree places where nearby evidence speaks, it leans the same way.

And that is not a refutation. A null average is compatible with a large individual effect, and much of this "absence of evidence" is absence of research: your intersection is exactly the population these studies exclude or never assemble. The individual-variation evidence here is unusually strong. When Abanades fitted individual concentration-response values across just ten people, the spread was 8.5-fold. The population curve does not describe anybody in it. primary, verified

But three separate places where adjacent evidence does speak, the inverse duration correlation, Okifuji's null direct path, and the metabolic-not-autonomic limb, all lean the same way. Intellectual honesty means saying so out loud rather than filing them under "unstudied." reasoning, not evidence

What this does and does not license

It does not license dismissal. You independently rediscovered a documented clinical phenomenon and rediscovered it in the exact words the literature uses. That is a legitimate find and it validates the phenomenology completely.

It does not license the hypothesis carrying weight in a dose decision either. The route from "crashes are real" to "preventing them will relieve my pain at 20mg" runs through a link that nobody has ever tested and that nearby evidence does not favour.

What it does license is the test. The 20mg window is the only clean test of this that will ever exist for you, and the separating observation is free: track fatigue and pain as two separate lines. If pain improves while fatigue does not, the hypothesis is dead. See the trap diagram.

One caveat about the framing itself, because it must not be misread

Nothing here is a psychological model of your illness. NICE NG206 (2021) withdrew graded exercise therapy and the deconditioning model, verbatim: "Do not offer people with ME/CFS... any programme that... uses fixed incremental increases in physical activity or exercise, for example, graded exercise therapy", and states that CBT "is not curative." primary, verified

The question examined here is pharmacological: whether a drug preventing a documented ME/CFS phenomenon would slowly reduce pain. You raised it. The evidence answers it unfavourably. Neither the question nor the answer implies the illness is psychogenic, and the allostatic-load evidence in fact points away from a stress-reactivity account and toward a metabolic one.
