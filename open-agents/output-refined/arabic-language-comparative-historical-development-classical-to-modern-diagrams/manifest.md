## Diagram Descriptions

### Diagram 1: Arabic vs. English vs. French Historical Development
- **Type**: Triple parallel timeline
- **Elements**: Arabic track (pre-Islamic poetry -> Quran -> Golden Age -> Nahda -> MSA), English track (Anglo-Saxon -> Norman Conquest -> Middle English -> standardization -> global), French track (Gallo-Romance -> Old French -> Academie -> Revolution -> modern). Contact points between languages highlighted. Grammatical continuity comparison.
- **Key Insight**: Arabic has the MOST continuous grammatical tradition (1,200+ years); English has the MOST disrupted one; French is in between.
- **English Comparison**: Old English is unintelligible to modern speakers. Pre-Islamic Arabic poetry is accessible to educated modern Arabic speakers. The contrast in structural stability is stark.

### Diagram 2: The Translation Movement -- Knowledge Flow
- **Type**: Multi-stage flow diagram
- **Elements**: Source languages (Greek, Persian, Sanskrit, Syriac) -> Arabic processing (8th-13th century, original contributions added) -> Latin transmission (12th-13th century) -> European vernaculars -> modern science. Key figures and domains at each stage.
- **Key Insight**: Arabic was not merely a conduit -- Arabic-language scholars made original contributions transmitted along with the Greek legacy.
- **English Comparison**: English became a world language of science only in the 20th century. Arabic held that role 800 years earlier.

### Diagram 3: The Semitic Language Family Tree
- **Type**: Phylogenetic tree
- **Elements**: Proto-Afroasiatic -> Proto-Semitic -> branches (East: Akkadian, Northwest: Hebrew/Aramaic/Phoenician, Central: Arabic, South: Ge'ez/Amharic/Tigrinya). Living vs. extinct marked. Cognate examples. Timeline markers.
- **Key Insight**: Arabic is the LARGEST Semitic language by far -- more speakers than all other Semitic languages combined.
- **English Comparison**: English is Germanic but so heavily mixed that its morphological core is less stable than Arabic's Semitic core.

### Diagram 4: Arabic Vocabulary Modernization Strategies
- **Type**: Four-strategy taxonomy with examples
- **Elements**: Root-based coinage (ta'ira = airplane from t-y-r), Arabization (tilifun), semantic extension (sayyara = car), calquing (mahattat al-qitar). Which strategy "won" for each concept. Academy vs. popular adoption.
- **Key Insight**: Root-based coinage preserves the generative system; borrowing breaks it. The tension between these strategies is the central dynamic of Arabic modernization.
- **English Comparison**: English just borrows without gatekeeping. Arabic TRIES to coin from its own roots.

### Diagram 5: The Succession of Semitic World Languages
- **Type**: Overlapping historical bars
- **Elements**: Akkadian (~2400 BCE-500 CE), Aramaic (~800 BCE-700 CE), Arabic (~600 CE-present). Political drivers labeled. Modern survival status.
- **Key Insight**: Each succession was driven by POLITICAL power, not linguistic evolution. Each language was fully functional when replaced.
