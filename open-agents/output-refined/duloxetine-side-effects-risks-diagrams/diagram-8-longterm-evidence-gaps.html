Long-Term Use: Where the Evidence Stops

Duloxetine has been on the market for two decades, which makes the evidence base feel thicker than it is. The gap is not that the long-term data is thin. It is that beyond six months the data changes shape: it stops being controlled at all.

"The efficacy of Cymbalta in the management of fibromyalgia has been demonstrated in placebo-controlled studies up to 3 months. The efficacy of Cymbalta was not demonstrated in longer studies."

FDA Cymbalta prescribing information — verbatim, and consistent across the 2008, 2010 and 2014 labels [primary, verified]

What that sentence actually describes

It is not bureaucratic hedging, and it is not a gap in the record. It is a description of a specific failed trial. There are two six-month placebo-controlled fibromyalgia trials of duloxetine, and they disagree:

Russell 2008

6 months, randomised 1:2:2:2, duloxetine 20 / 60 / 120mg vs placebo. 120mg separated on both co-primaries. 60mg on BPI only.

Chappell AS, et al. 2008

Int J Gen Med 1:91–102 · PMID 20428412 · 27 weeks, duloxetine 60/120mg (n=162) vs placebo (n=168). Sponsored by Eli Lilly and Boehringer Ingelheim; three authors are Lilly employees and stockholders. FAILED both co-primaries: BPI −1.62 vs −1.13, P=0.053; PGI-I 3.43 vs 3.72, P=0.073.

The dose commonly proposed as the target, 60mg, failed both co-primary endpoints in a 330-patient, six-month fibromyalgia trial. Both were near-misses rather than flat nulls, and the secondaries were positive. But the FDA sentence above is describing this. [primary, verified]

Citation note: Chappell 2008 and Chappell 2009 are different papers. 2008 is the 6-month placebo-controlled trial. 2009 (PMID 19454869) is the 1-year study below. Do not merge them.

The evidence horizon, by what is actually being compared

Efficacy — fibromyalgia
0–3 months
Placebo-controlled, demonstratedThis is the whole of what the FDA says is established. Best fibromyalgia-specific estimate: NNTB 11 (95% CI 9–14), which the review authors called "no clinically relevant benefit."
3–6 months
Placebo-controlled, and splitTwo trials: one positive (Russell), one failed both co-primaries (Chappell 2008). Cochrane records ≥50% pain response sustained at 28 weeks, RR 1.58 (95% CI 1.10–2.27) — the best long-term controlled signal, and it comes from these same split trials.
6–12 months
Duloxetine vs duloxetine — NO placebo armThe 1-year study (PMID 19454869). See below.
> 12 months
Nothing controlled, in any indicationThere is no placebo-controlled duloxetine efficacy data past 12 months anywhere.
Safety — all indications
0–12 months
Large and controlledLilly's five-trial pooled safety analysis (PMC2721139): duloxetine 20–120mg/day showed "a similar safety and tolerability profile in the treatment of fibromyalgia to that seen in the treatment of other indications."
> 12 months
Open-label and sponsor-runAn open-label 52-week Japanese phase-III extension exists (PMID 27796152). Follow-up beyond a year is uncontrolled.

The one-year study randomises duloxetine against duloxetine

8 weeks open-label duloxetine (30mg × 1wk → 60mg × 7wk), then randomised 1:2 for 52 weeks, double-blind. n=350, 95.7% female.

Result: pain reduction from the open-label phase continued through the 52-week phase in both duloxetine groups.

What it can establish: that 60mg and 120mg behave similarly over a year.

What it cannot establish:

  • That either dose beats placebo.
  • Drug effect as distinct from natural history or regression to the mean.
  • Drug effect as distinct from the 30–40% placebo response that fibromyalgia trials reliably produce.

And everyone enrolled had already responded well enough to continue through eight open-label weeks, so the population is selected and enriched before the randomisation even begins. [primary, verified]

The asymmetry is the point, and it is routinely conflated

The safety database

Large and long. Tens of thousands of patients, pooled across indications, with follow-up out past a year. This is a genuinely well-characterised drug on the harms side.

The efficacy database

Small and short. Controlled evidence stops at six months, and the two trials at that horizon disagree. Past twelve months, in any indication, there is nothing controlled at all.

"Well tolerated long-term" is much better established than "still working long-term."
Those are different claims, resting on different evidence, and they get treated as one.

Does the benefit persist? Unknown, and not answerable from the current evidence base. That is not a reason to stop or to continue. It is a reason to keep the question open rather than assume it has been settled.

Nothing here directs a dose. It is what to understand and what to raise.
