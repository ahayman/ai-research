# Avaz AAC: India's Voice for the Voiceless

In a world where approximately 97% of people who need augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) tools live in developing countries, access to affordable speech technology is not merely a convenience -- it is a fundamental human right. When Ajit Narayanan, a young engineer from IIT Madras, walked into his first special education classroom in India, he encountered children with autism and cerebral palsy who had no way to express their thoughts, needs, or emotions. The AAC devices available at the time cost between $5,000 and $10,000 and supported only English. For Indian families, these tools were simply out of reach. Narayanan set out to change that, and the result was Avaz -- a word meaning "Voice" in Hindi -- which would become India's first AAC intervention and one of the most innovative communication apps in the world.

Today, Avaz AAC stands as a unique force in the AAC landscape: the only major communication app available on both iOS and Android, supporting over 13 languages including 8 Indian languages, packed with 40,000+ SymbolStix pictures, and priced at a fraction of its competitors. Now backed by PRC-Saltillo, the world's leading AAC company, Avaz is poised to bring communication to millions of people who have never had a voice.

## The Origin Story: From Chennai Classrooms to MIT Recognition

### The Problem That Sparked a Revolution

The story of Avaz begins in 2009 in the special education classrooms of Chennai, India. Ajit Narayanan, an electrical engineering graduate from the prestigious Indian Institute of Technology Madras, had been working with schools serving children with autism, cerebral palsy, and other complex communication needs. What he found was a vast, unmet need.

In India alone, an estimated 2-3 million children have autism spectrum disorder, and millions more have conditions that affect speech. Yet the AAC devices available in the market were designed for Western users, priced for Western incomes, and programmed in Western languages. A single dedicated AAC device from companies like DynaVox (now Tobii Dynavox) cost upwards of $8,000. For a country where the average annual household income at the time was around $1,500, these devices existed in a parallel universe.

Narayanan's insight was both simple and radical: what if AAC technology could be built on affordable consumer hardware, using languages that Indian children actually spoke, with vocabulary that reflected Indian culture -- the foods, festivals, family structures, and daily routines of South Asian life?

### Building India's First AAC Device

Working closely with speech therapists, special educators, and families across India, Narayanan's team developed the first Avaz device in 2010. By partnering with Asian hardware manufacturers, they produced a dedicated tablet device that cost approximately $800 -- still significant, but a fraction of the $5,000-$10,000 price tag of American equivalents. More importantly, it supported Indian languages with culturally appropriate vocabulary.

The impact was immediate. Special educators and therapists reported that children who had never communicated independently were suddenly able to express wants, make choices, and participate in classroom activities. The device saw incredible adoption across India's network of special education schools.

### MIT Technology Review Recognition

The innovation did not go unnoticed. In 2010, Avaz won the National Award for Empowerment of People with Disabilities from the President of India -- the country's highest honor in the disability sector. Then, in 2011, came the recognition that would put Avaz on the global map: Ajit Narayanan was named "Innovator of the Year" by MIT Technology Review as part of their prestigious TR35 list of innovators under 35. Avaz was the first product in the disability category to ever receive this recognition.

In 2013, Narayanan took the TED stage in Long Beach, California, delivering a talk that would accumulate over one million views. He demonstrated not just Avaz but also FreeSpeech, a revolutionary visual language tool that maps relationships between words spatially rather than linearly -- an approach that resonated deeply with how many people with autism process information.

## How Avaz Works: Two Modes, One Voice

At its core, Avaz AAC operates through two complementary communication modes that allow users to grow with the app as their skills develop.

### Picture Mode: Visual Communication

Picture Mode is Avaz's symbol-based communication system, designed for users who communicate primarily through images. The app comes pre-loaded with over 15,000 SymbolStix pictures from a library of more than 40,000, covering an enormous range of words, concepts, and ideas.

Symbols are organized in a grid layout using the **Fitzgerald Key color coding system**, a decades-old educational convention that color-codes words by their grammatical function:

- **Yellow**: People and pronouns
- **Green**: Verbs and actions
- **Orange**: Adjectives and descriptors
- **Blue**: Nouns and objects
- **Pink**: Social phrases and interjections
- **White/Gray**: Miscellaneous and function words

This color coding is consistent with materials used across special education classrooms worldwide, allowing children to transfer their understanding between the app and other learning environments.

Users build sentences by tapping symbols in sequence. A sentence strip at the top of the screen shows the constructed message, which can then be spoken aloud using high-quality text-to-speech voices. The system uses **consistent motor planning**, meaning that core words appear in the same position across all vocabulary levels.

### Keyboard Mode: Text-Based Communication

For users who can read and type, Keyboard Mode provides a full QWERTY keyboard with predictive text. This mode is essential for older users, adults with acquired communication disorders (such as aphasia from stroke), and children who have progressed beyond symbol-based communication.

The seamless **Quick Toggle** feature allows users to switch between Picture and Keyboard modes instantly within the same conversation.

### The Core Word Philosophy

Avaz's vocabulary is organized around a research-backed principle: core words -- the small set of words that make up approximately 80% of everything we say -- should be the foundation of AAC communication.

Avaz organizes these core words into three graded vocabulary levels based on generative language development principles. The **Frozen Row** feature ensures that the most critical core words remain visible and accessible at all times, regardless of which category folder the user is browsing.

## What Makes Avaz Unique

### Cross-Platform: iOS AND Android

Perhaps Avaz's single most distinctive feature in the AAC market is its availability on both iOS and Android. The dominant AAC apps -- Proloquo2Go ($249.99), TouchChat HD ($299.99), and LAMP Words for Life ($299.99) -- are all available exclusively on Apple's iOS platform. In India, where Android commands over 95% of the smartphone market, iOS exclusivity effectively locks out the vast majority of families. Avaz's Android support is a core design principle rooted in the company's mission to make AAC accessible in developing countries.

### 8+ Indian Language Support

Avaz supports more Indian languages than any other AAC app:

- **Hindi** -- Over 500 million speakers
- **Tamil** -- Primary language of Tamil Nadu
- **Kannada** -- Official language of Karnataka
- **Malayalam** -- Language of Kerala
- **Telugu** -- Over 80 million speakers
- **Marathi** -- Official language of Maharashtra
- **Gujarati** -- Personalizable vocabulary
- **Bengali** -- Over 230 million speakers, personalizable vocabulary

Each language version includes culturally relevant vocabulary specific to regional foods, festivals, currencies, clothing, and family structures.

### AI-Powered Expressive Tones

Expressive Tones uses AI technology to infuse emotional nuance into synthesized speech. Users can select from tones including excitement, frustration, sarcasm, sadness, and curiosity.

### Media-Rich Communication

- **YouTube Integration**: Built-in search and player with custom start/stop times, no ads
- **GIFs**: Animated GIFs with adjustable loop count for sensory management
- **Animation**: Visual reinforcement when words are selected

### Built-In Caregiver Training: The Avaz Dashboard

The Avaz Dashboard provides hands-on guidance for AAC strategies across 10+ real-world scenarios: mealtimes, circle time, reading, playground interactions, and more.

### Progress Tracking and Analytics

Comprehensive analytics tracking communication development through graphs, charts, word usage patterns, and email notifications.

## Strengths and Limitations

### Strengths
1. True cross-platform support (iOS + Android)
2. Unmatched multilingual coverage (8+ Indian languages)
3. Most affordable premium AAC ($199.99 lifetime)
4. Built-in caregiver training (Avaz Dashboard)
5. Research-based vocabulary design
6. Modern media features (YouTube, GIFs, animation)
7. AI expressive tones
8. Comprehensive analytics
9. Low barrier to entry (14-day free trial)
10. PRC-Saltillo backing

### Limitations
1. Speech speed/pitch controls could be more granular
2. Smaller US market presence
3. Subscription model frustrates some users
4. Prediction engine less sophisticated than some competitors
5. Grid navigation can overwhelm users with apraxia
6. Smaller third-party ecosystem
