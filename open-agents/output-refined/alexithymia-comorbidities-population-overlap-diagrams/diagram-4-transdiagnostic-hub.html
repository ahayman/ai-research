The transdiagnostic-mechanism reading. Brewer, Murphy & Bird (2021, PMC8522807, Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews 130:470–508) argue that variation in interoception — substantially overlapping with alexithymia — is a transdiagnostic risk factor for psychopathology. The argument: a common substrate (atypical interoceptive labelling, emotion-concept access, autonomic-state perception) underlies emotion-processing differences across autism, PTSD, eating disorders, anxiety, depression, somatization, and SUD. ICD-11 CPTSD network analyses (Frontiers 2024) treat emotional processing and dissociative experiences as central nodes in trauma networks, with alexithymia clustering at the centroid.
The honest hedge. Transdiagnostic mechanism is evidence-supported but not closed. Alternative readings:
· Negative-affectivity confound. Alexithymia, depression, anxiety, and most affective psychopathology share substantial general-distress variance. Some apparent transdiagnostic positioning may reflect this shared general factor.
· Population-specific causal structures. Mechanisms may differ enough across populations that "transdiagnostic mechanism" obscures more than it illuminates.
· Construct stability. "Alexithymia" in autism and "alexithymia" in PTSD may not be the same thing even with similar TAS-20 scores. The construct-non-equivalence concern is real.

Defensible position: alexithymia behaves like a transdiagnostic mechanism in much of the available evidence, but "transdiagnostic mechanism" should be read as "frequently-implicated common feature with mechanisms that may vary across populations" rather than "single underlying cause."
