The Denominator Is Exposures, Not Weeks

This is the one place where something you already know about yourself converts directly into a study design. Your episodes are not spontaneous. They follow sensory overwhelm. That single fact changes what counts as evidence.

Two months with no episodes while working from home, avoiding crowds, and having a quiet winter is worth almost nothing, because the drug was never tested. One festival day with no crash afterwards is worth more than two uneventful months.

At two weeks: 13–37% chance of a pure fluke. Between one in three and one in seven people whose drug does absolutely nothing would be sitting exactly where you are, seeing exactly what you see, and drawing exactly your conclusion. This is not pessimism. It is the reason not to treat two weeks as informative. Chance dies somewhere between six weeks and three months.

Three to five comparable overloads survived cleanly ≈ p<0.05. Faster, better-powered, more specific, and it is a result about the thing that actually matters.
The distinction that decides which test you need, and it is where most n-of-1 reasoning goes wrong
Random fluctuation
Time kills this
Wait long enough and the odds of a chance-driven quiet run collapse. By three months it is under 1 in 400.
Placebo response
Time does NOT kill this
Not chance. A systematic effect that produces the identical zero-episode observation. It should decay, which is its own signature, but it survives the waiting.
Regression to the mean
Time does NOT kill this
Also not chance. Explains why a bad stretch is followed by a better stretch, no matter how long you watch.
Natural course
Time does NOT kill this
Same. An illness running its own trajectory produces quiet runs on its own schedule.
Why the trigger test is the good one, in one sentence: regression to the mean explains why a bad stretch is followed by a better stretch, and it does not explain why a specific, repeated, previously reliable trigger stopped producing its effect. That is the only marker available to you that separates the drug from regression. A three-month clean run proves the improvement is real. It says nothing about whether the drug caused it. Those are different questions, and waiting only answers the first.
The limits, stated at the claim. This requires you to characterise p₀ from memory, which is recall-biased. It requires exposures to be genuinely comparable, which is a judgement call. And deliberately seeking out overload to test a drug carries a real cost and a real risk, so nothing here suggests you go looking. The point is that when overloads happen anyway, those are the informative days, and they are worth recording prospectively rather than reconstructing later. One further wrinkle worth resolving yourself: “several episodes a month” and “episodes last one to two weeks” do not arithmetically fit, because three episodes a month at 1.5 weeks each is continuous illness. Either they usually run much shorter than the theme-park example, or the rate is an overestimate, or they overlap. That ambiguity propagates into every number in the left-hand table, and you are the only person who can resolve it.

Provenance: reasoning, not evidence throughout. Both tables are standard arithmetic (a Poisson process for recurrent events; a binomial for repeated trigger exposures), computed for this page and reproducible. They are models, not measurements. The one plotted data point is your own report.
