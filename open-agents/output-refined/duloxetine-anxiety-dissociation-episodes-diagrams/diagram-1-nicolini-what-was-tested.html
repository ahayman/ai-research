Non-inferiority of 20mg to 60–120mg
NEVER ASKED
Not tested badly. Not underpowered. Not inconclusive. The question was never put. The formal non-inferiority analysis (Allgulander 2008, PMID 18635722) pooled only the 60–120mg arms and excluded the 84 people on 20mg entirely. See diagram 2 for the arithmetic that proves it.

Did the higher dose do better or worse?

HAM-A change, 20mg−14.7
HAM-A change, 60–120mg−15.3
Response, 20mg → 60–120mg60% → 65%
Remission, 20mg → 60–120mg42% → 44%
Slightly better, not worse. All three comparisons point the same way. So the story is not “20mg is the sweet spot and more would spoil it.” It is “20mg cleared the bar, and 60mg cleared it by a little more.” These point estimates are secondary-source; the widely-quoted “45% vs 55%” percentages are internally inconsistent with them and are not reproduced here.

The placebo arm, which does not collapse

HAM-A change, placebo−11.6
Response, placebo42%
Remission, placebo20%
Drug–placebo gap at 20mg≈ 3.1 pts
The drug adds roughly three HAM-A points and eighteen percentage points on top of a large placebo effect. An earlier pass of this project claimed the 20mg evidence made the placebo explanation collapse. It does not. What collapses is only the narrower claim that a 20mg anxiolytic benefit is pharmacologically unsupported.
The claim that survives, in full: 20mg beat placebo on HAM-A total and the psychic factor, in one 84-patient fixed-dose arm of one Lilly trial, over ten weeks, never replicated. It was never compared to 60mg. It failed on the somatic factor. That is simultaneously the best dose-matched evidence in this entire deep dive and a thin foundation, and both halves of that sentence are load-bearing.

Provenance: significance results are primary, verified (abstract verbatim; the full text is paywalled and was not retrieved). Point estimates and response/remission rates are secondary — unverified (from a review, not the primary paper). The drug–placebo gap and NNTs are reasoning, not evidence (derived arithmetic).
