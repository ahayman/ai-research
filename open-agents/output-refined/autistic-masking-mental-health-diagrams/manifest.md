## Diagram Descriptions

### Diagram 1: Components of Autistic Masking
- **Type**: Mind Map
- **Elements**: 3 main branches, 18 leaf nodes
- **Key Insight**: Masking involves three distinct processes: compensating (learning rules), masking (suppressing behaviors), and assimilating (blending in)
- **Accessibility**: Three-branch mind map. Center: "Autistic Masking/Camouflaging." Branch 1 "Compensation": learning social rules, scripting conversations, watching/imitating, intellectual strategies, preparing topics, monitoring behavior. Branch 2 "Masking": suppressing stimming, hiding sensory distress, forcing eye contact, controlling expressions, concealing interests, suppressing responses. Branch 3 "Assimilation": fitting in, adjusting personality, adopting mannerisms, performing social role, mirroring body language, managing identity.

### Diagram 2: Masking Process & Associated Outcomes
- **Type**: Flowchart
- **Elements**: 4 driver nodes, 4 masking nodes, 4 cost nodes, 4 outcome nodes, 1 causal caveat
- **Key Insight**: Social pressures drive masking, which is associated with cognitive/emotional costs and mental health outcomes — but causal direction is not established
- **Accessibility**: Four-stage flow. Social pressures (stigma, workplace expectations, desire for connection, safety) lead to masking behaviors (suppress stimming, force eye contact, script conversations, imitate expressions), associated with costs (cognitive depletion, identity confusion, emotional suppression, chronic stress), associated with outcomes (depression r=0.36, anxiety r=0.27, burnout, suicidal ideation). Warning box notes evidence is cross-sectional and causal direction unestablished.

### Diagram 3: Competing Causal Models
- **Type**: Flowchart (4 parallel models)
- **Elements**: 4 models, each with 2-3 nodes
- **Key Insight**: Cross-sectional evidence cannot distinguish between direct harm, reverse causation, common cause, or bidirectional models
- **Accessibility**: Four side-by-side models. Model A (Direct Harm): masking causes poor mental health. Model B (Reverse Causation): poor mental health drives more masking. Model C (Common Cause): shared factor causes both masking and poor mental health. Model D (Bidirectional): masking and poor mental health form a feedback loop.

### Diagram 4: The Double Empathy Problem
- **Type**: Flowchart
- **Elements**: 2 models (deficit vs. double empathy), 4 evidence citations
- **Key Insight**: Communication difficulties are bidirectional — both autistic and non-autistic people struggle to understand each other
- **Accessibility**: Left side shows deficit model (autistic person fails to understand non-autistic person, one-sided). Right side shows double empathy model (mutual misunderstanding between autistic and non-autistic people, bidirectional). Supporting evidence from Crompton 2020/2025, Sasson 2017, Edey 2016, Keating 2026.

### Diagram 5: ABA Evidence Landscape
- **Type**: Flowchart
- **Elements**: 5 measured outcomes, 6 unmeasured outcomes, measurement gap, consequence
- **Key Insight**: ABA studies measure behavioral outcomes but systematically fail to measure mental health, wellbeing, or adverse events
- **Accessibility**: Three columns. Left (green): outcomes measured by ABA (cognition, adaptive behavior, language, target behaviors, social skills). Center (yellow): measurement gap (93% don't mention adverse outcomes, only 7% of trials report results). Right (red): outcomes not measured (mental health, quality of life, identity, autonomy, long-term outcomes, adverse events). Leads to consequence: cannot conclude safety because safety never measured.

### Diagram 6: Intervention Approaches Spectrum
- **Type**: Flowchart
- **Elements**: 3 approach categories, 12 example interventions, evidence note
- **Key Insight**: Interventions range from person-modification (teaches masking) to environmental accommodation (reduces need to mask)
- **Accessibility**: Three-column spectrum. Left (red, Person-Modification): train normal expressions, eliminate stimming, force eye contact, compliance-based skills. Center (yellow, Balanced/Adaptive): collaborative goals, teach understanding not mimicry, build communication with choice, support preferred methods. Right (green, Environmental Accommodation): modify sensory environments, accept diverse communication, educate NT peers, strength-based support.

### Diagram 7: Comorbidity and Masking Relationships
- **Type**: Flowchart
- **Elements**: 1 central node, 4 category groups, 10 comorbidity nodes
- **Key Insight**: Masking is associated with a wide web of comorbid conditions, but these are associations, not established causal links
- **Accessibility**: Central node "Autistic Masking" connects via dashed arrows to four groups. Cognitive/Emotional: alexithymia (50%), executive function depletion. Mental Health: depression, anxiety, burnout, suicidal ideation. Trauma-Related: PTSD, identity loss. Behavioral: eating disorders, substance use. Note clarifies all arrows are associations, not causal.

### Diagram 8: AAC Design Decision Framework
- **Type**: Flowchart (decision tree)
- **Elements**: 5 decision points, 4 outcome categories, 1 redirect loop
- **Key Insight**: The key question for AAC developers: are you changing the person to fit the environment, or the environment to fit the person?
- **Accessibility**: Decision tree. Start with "Proposed AAC Feature." Question 1: Does it aim to change how the user expresses themselves? If yes → Does it match NT norms? If yes → High masking risk (red). If no → Is it user-initiated and optional? If yes → Acceptable (green). Question 2: Does it help communicate what they want to say? If yes → Communication support (green). Question 3: Does it modify the environment? If yes → Ideal approach (green). Redirect loop from masking risk to "Can you achieve the same goal by modifying the environment?"
