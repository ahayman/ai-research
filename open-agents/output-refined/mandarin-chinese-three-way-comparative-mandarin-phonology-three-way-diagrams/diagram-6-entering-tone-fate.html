Fate of the Middle Chinese Entering Tone (入声)

Lost in Mandarin, preserved in Cantonese — a key divergence

Middle Chinese: Four Tone Categories

平Level (ping)smooth, level pitch
上Rising (shang)rises upward
去Departing (qu)falls away
入Entering (ru)ends in -p, -t, -k

The entering tone was defined by its abrupt stop-consonant endings, making syllables short and clipped. It was a phonotactic category as much as a tonal one.

Mandarin: Entering Tone Dissolved

Stop codas (-p, -t, -k) were completely lost. Former entering-tone syllables were scattered across all four modern tones — unpredictably for voiceless-initial syllables.

Same Middle Chinese syllable /tsjek/ --> three different modern tones:
Tone 1积ji (T1)
Tone 2--(not this one)
Tone 3脊ji (T3)
Tone 4迹ji (T4)

Cantonese: Entering Tone Preserved

All three stop codas maintained as unreleased finals. Three entering-tone registers correspond to pitch levels, giving the traditional "9 tones" count.

-pbilabial stop十 sap6"ten"
-talveolar stop一 jat1"one"
-kvelar stop国 gwok3"country"

Other Chinese Varieties:
Cantonese: -p -t -k preserved
Hakka: -p -t -k preserved
Min (Hokkien): -p -t -k preserved
Wu: glottal stop reflex
Mandarin: completely lost
Jin: partially merged
Why This Matters: The loss of entering tones in Mandarin means that classical Chinese poetry, which relied on the ping/ze (level/oblique) tonal distinction for its regulated verse forms, cannot be properly recited in Mandarin because speakers cannot reliably identify which modern-tone syllables were originally entering tone. Cantonese speakers can read Tang Dynasty poetry with the original tonal patterns intact because their language preserves this distinction.
