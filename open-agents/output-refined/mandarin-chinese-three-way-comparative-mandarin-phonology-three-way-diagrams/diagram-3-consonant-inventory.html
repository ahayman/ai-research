Primary Contrast

Mandarin & Cantonese: Aspiration is the primary distinctive feature. Pinyin "b" and "p" are both voiceless — the difference is aspiration (puff of air).

English: Voicing is primary. "b" is voiced, "p" is voiceless. Aspiration exists but is allophonic, not phonemic.

Retroflex Series (zh, ch, sh, r)

Mandarin: Full retroflex series unique to northern varieties. Tongue curls back to post-alveolar region.

Cantonese: No retroflexes at all. Uses dental sibilants instead.

English: Has postalveolar sounds (sh, ch, j) but they are articulated differently from Mandarin retroflexes.

Final Consonants

Mandarin: Only -n and -ng in coda position. Extremely restricted.

Cantonese: Six codas: -m, -n, -ng, -p, -t, -k. Preserves Middle Chinese finals.

English: Almost any consonant can appear in coda, including clusters of up to 4 consonants.

Voiced Obstruents

Mandarin: None. All stops, affricates, and fricatives are voiceless.

Cantonese: None (or very marginal).

English: Full set of voiced counterparts: b, d, g, v, z, etc. Voicing is a primary phonemic distinction.

Dental Fricatives (th)

The English sounds /th/ (as in "thin") and /dh/ (as in "this") are absent from both Mandarin and Cantonese. These sounds are cross-linguistically rare, appearing in only about 7-8% of the world's languages.

Labiovelar Stops (gw, kw)

Cantonese uniquely preserves labiovelar initials /kw/ and /kwh/ (written gw- and kw- in Jyutping), e.g., gwok "country" (国). These are absent from both Mandarin and English.
