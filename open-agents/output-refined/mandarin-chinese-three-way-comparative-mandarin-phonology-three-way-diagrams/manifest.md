# Diagrams: Mandarin Phonology — Three-Way Structural Comparison

## Diagram 1: Tone Pitch Contours
**File:** `diagram-1-tone-pitch-contours.html`
**Description:** Side-by-side canvas-rendered pitch contour visualizations comparing Mandarin's 4 tones + neutral tone, Cantonese's 6 tones, and English intonation patterns (falling, rising, fall-rise). Uses a 1-5 pitch scale with color-coded tone lines. Highlights the fundamental difference: Mandarin and Cantonese use pitch at the syllable level to distinguish word meaning, while English uses pitch only at the phrase level for intonation.

## Diagram 2: Syllable Structure Comparison
**File:** `diagram-2-syllable-structure.html`
**Description:** Visual block diagrams showing the maximum syllable template for each language: Mandarin (C)(G)V(N)+Tone, Cantonese (C)V(C)+Tone, and English (C)(C)(C)V(C)(C)(C)(C). Includes syllable count statistics (~410/1,300 for Mandarin, ~630/1,760 for Cantonese, ~10,000+ for English) and key constraints for each system.

## Diagram 3: Consonant Inventory Three-Way Comparison
**File:** `diagram-3-consonant-inventory.html`
**Description:** Tabbed interface with three views: Stops & Affricates table (showing aspiration vs. voicing contrast), Fricatives & Others table, and Key Differences feature cards. Color-codes consonants by which languages share them. Highlights: aspiration as primary contrast in Chinese vs. voicing in English, Mandarin-unique retroflexes, Cantonese-unique labiovelars, and English-unique dental fricatives.

## Diagram 4: Erhua Distribution Map
**File:** `diagram-4-erhua-distribution.html`
**Description:** Gradient spectrum showing erhua (rhotacization) usage from heavy (Beijing) through moderate (standard Putonghua) to absent (Taiwan Mandarin). Includes individual variety cards with 5-bar intensity meters, usage examples with pinyin, and a section on the phonological effects of erhua on different syllable finals (-n absorption, -ng nasalization, vowel modification).

## Diagram 5: Tone Sandhi Rules Flowchart
**File:** `diagram-5-tone-sandhi-flowchart.html`
**Description:** Decision flowcharts for the three major Mandarin tone sandhi rules: Third-Tone Sandhi (T3+T3 --> T2+T3, with multi-syllable grouping patterns), Yi (一) Sandhi (changes before different tones), and Bu (不) Sandhi (changes before Tone 4). Each rule shows conditions, results, and real-word examples. Includes a special section on three consecutive Tone 3s with structure-dependent grouping.

## Diagram 6: Fate of the Entering Tone
**File:** `diagram-6-entering-tone-fate.html`
**Description:** Historical divergence diagram showing how Middle Chinese's four tone categories evolved differently in Mandarin (entering tone dissolved, syllables scattered randomly across four modern tones) vs. Cantonese (entering tone preserved with -p, -t, -k finals). Includes a comparison table of specific characters showing their Middle Chinese, Mandarin, and Cantonese pronunciations, plus a map of which Chinese varieties preserve vs. lost the entering tone.
