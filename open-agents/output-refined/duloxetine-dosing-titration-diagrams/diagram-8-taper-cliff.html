The Cliff Is Below You, Not Above You

"Going up will make coming down harder" sounds obviously true. It is the weakest argument in this whole area, and the same curve that makes duloxetine awkward to taper is the reason why.

The taper problem exists right now, at your current dose. It was true before this appointment was scheduled, and it does not depend on the outcome. If you ever want to stop duloxetine, this year or in five years, you face the same wall: no dose form below your current dose, an acid-labile drug that cannot be liquefied at home, and roughly 72 occupancy points to cross with nothing commercial to stand on. Going to 60mg does not create that problem, and staying at 20mg does not avoid it. This is why "going up makes coming down harder" is true at the margin and misleading as a headline, and why this page does not offer it as a reason to stay.
What this diagram does not settle. Grade the curve honestly. The ED50 of 7.9mg comes from Takano 2006 (PMID 16506079), which used roughly 3 subjects per dose, single-dose administration rather than steady state, and thalamic measurement only primary, verified. Everything below 20mg on this chart is modelled, not measured: the model happens to track the measured values well at 20mg and 60mg, which is reassuring but not independent confirmation, because the model was fitted to those measurements. The precise occupancy figures below 20mg have never been measured in anyone, because nobody has ever given a person 5mg of duloxetine in a PET scanner no evidence exists. The shape of the curve is established pharmacology and is not in dispute; the numbers on the left half of this chart are a model reasoning, not evidence. There is also a real counterweight: more drug does mean more to remove, and the withdrawal signal at 120mg is genuine, so going up adds a modest increment of withdrawal risk. The dominant fact is that you are already at duloxetine's taper floor. One further practical warning: alternate-day dosing is precisely the wrong strategy at a roughly 12-hour half-life, and it is the most likely piece of bad advice you will be offered. The modelling is explicit: increasing the dose interval at minimum therapeutic doses "likely increases the risk of withdrawal symptoms and cannot be recommended as a prudent strategy for tapering."
