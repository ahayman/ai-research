The Trap

The only irreversible thing in this decision, and it has nothing to do with milligrams. Going to 60mg is a decision you can undo. Closing the 20mg window is not.

What this is not. This is not an argument for staying at 20mg, and it would be easy to smuggle one in here. Information is not the only thing worth having. If the pain is bad enough, buying relief at the cost of an experiment is an entirely rational trade, and it is your trade to make. What the trap is: a thing you should know before you close the door, because the door does not re-open.

Three places adjacent evidence leans AGAINST your hypothesis, stated because honesty requires it. (a) Allostatic load in CFS: duration of fatigue correlates inversely with the allostatic load index (r = −.26, p = .047), so cumulative damage predicts the opposite of what your model needs primary abstract only. (b) Prospectively, the allostatic limb that predicts pain is metabolic; parasympathetic dysregulation predicted nothing — and your model runs entirely through the limb that fails primary, verified. (c) Okifuji's direct distress→pain path was null primary, verified. On current evidence, your hypothesis should not be the reason you stay at 20mg. The information-value argument is a much better reason than the pain argument.
Why it survives anyway, and why it is not a crank idea. It is killed by absence of research, not by logic, and those are different deaths. A model that predicts the opposite of your report is dead no matter how unusual you are. A model nobody has tested is simply untested. Your exact intersection — autistic, sensory-triggered crashes, ME/CFS, fibromyalgia — is the population these studies exclude or never assemble. A PubMed search for "autistic burnout" AND ("chronic fatigue" OR "ME/CFS" OR "post-exertional") returns one result primary, verified — search-level. That is the whole literature at your intersection. A second free falsifier: under the crash-protection account, a breakthrough crash should leave a durable multi-week pain signature. A breakthrough crash whose pain resolves in days falsifies it, and it costs nothing to watch for.
And four variables are already in motion. Duloxetine started about two weeks ago and its early effects characteristically attenuate over weeks, so the drug is still moving. Melatonin is a constant nightly background. Iron started this week and acts at roughly 12 weeks, which means it cannot explain anything you observe at the appointment but will be quietly working underneath everything you observe after it. A dose change would make four. This is not an argument against changing the dose. It is an argument for deciding what you are measuring before you change anything, because with four variables moving and one observer, most results will not be interpretable.
