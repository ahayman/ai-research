Why two similar drugs diverging wildly in one person is the expected observation rather than the anomalous one.

The population curve does not describe any individual in it.

Look at the red lines. Every one of them is a real, published, correctly calculated number, and not one of them describes a person. On the top row, the man at 0.54 and the man at 4.61 both contributed to a mean of 2.29, and the mean is a fact about the group rather than a fact about either of them.

This is the quantitative backbone of the argument he made himself: every measured effect has a subpopulation that does not show it and a subpopulation that shows its opposite, so hunting for the single explanation that cannot be disproved is the wrong activity, and compiling a list of possibly valid explanations is the right one.

What follows from this, and it is more than it looks

His divergence is ordinary, not anomalous

If individual pharmacology varies this much on one drug, then two chemically similar drugs producing opposite outcomes in one person is exactly what the distribution predicts. The thing that needs explaining may not need explaining.

"Try it and see" is correct practice

It is not clinical laziness and it is not a failure of the science. When the between-person spread is 8.5-fold, the individual trial is the measurement. There is no substitute available.

Nobody can tell him his own number

Striatal occupancy on the identical 20mg ranged 59% to 86%. "He is at X% occupancy" is not a statement anyone can make without scanning him, and no one has. Every occupancy figure on every page of this deep dive is a group fact.

It explains the forums

The wild variance in what people report on this drug is not mostly noise, bias, or unreliable reporting. A large part of it is the actual distribution, showing through.

The honest cost of this candidate, stated because it has one

If individual variation is the answer, then there is no mechanism here to find, only a distribution to act on. That is a real loss. It means the satisfying causal story he is looking for may not exist for his case, and the useful move is to treat his own tracking as the measurement rather than as a poor substitute for the literature.

And it is very hard to falsify, which is worth noticing as a weakness rather than a strength. A hypothesis that cannot lose is not doing much work. Its value here is practical rather than explanatory: it licenses acting on his own data instead of the population's.
