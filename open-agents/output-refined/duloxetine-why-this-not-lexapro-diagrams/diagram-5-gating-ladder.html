More gets in, less gets screened out, and nothing switches off. That is not a metaphor for sensory overwhelm. It is an electrophysiological description of one. reasoning, not evidence — the synthesis across the three rows is an interpretation; every individual row is primary and verified.

And the left-hand column is milder in exactly one specific way: the filter still works.

Why this is a candidate for him. His episodes are triggered by sensory overwhelm. Escitalopram gave him dissociation, non-functionality, and zero anxiolysis. A drug that measurably degrades the sensory filter is a candidate explanation for why that drug made things worse rather than better.

The caveats, stated at the claim and not below it

  1. This is a cross-study comparison presented as a dose-response. Two studies laid side by side, not one dose-response experiment. This project's single most repeated error is exactly that, and this candidate commits it. The mitigation is real but partial: same lab, paradigm, population, design, consecutive, author-framed as an extension. That is about as clean as cross-study gets, and it is still not within-study.
  2. No independent replication, and here that claim is true. All the escitalopram studies come from one group. Only three papers on serotonin and P50 gating in healthy volunteers exist in total: Jensen 2008 (raised serotonin), Oranje 2011 (raised serotonin), Mann 2008 (depleted it). Two labs, one of which never raised serotonin. primary, verified by enumeration
  3. The one independent lab got a mirror image, not a replication. See below.
  4. Every study is a single acute dose in a drug-naive healthy volunteer. A search specifically for chronic-dosing gating studies found none. So whether an acute gating cost persists is not weakly answered. It is unanswered. no evidence exists
  5. Escitalopram is not duloxetine, and n≈20 healthy males in an EEG lab is not him at a festival. The leap is enormous and nobody has bridged it. reasoning, not evidence
Caveat 3 in full, because it matters. Mann 2008 (PMID 17895917, Monash, n=16, double-blind placebo-controlled crossover): "selective serotonin depletion significantly disrupted PPI, but not P50 suppression." primary, verified

So lowering serotonin breaks PPI and spares P50. Raising serotonin breaks P50 and spares PPI. These are mirror images, and they do not describe a single dial. Mann does not replicate Oranje, because it never raised serotonin. It corroborates that serotonin modulates gating, and it refutes any simple "more serotonin equals worse gating" monotonic story. An inverted-U with a different measure on each limb is a more honest reading. reasoning, not evidence
