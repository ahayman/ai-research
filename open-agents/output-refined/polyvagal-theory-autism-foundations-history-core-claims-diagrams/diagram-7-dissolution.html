Jacksonian Dissolution — Under Threat & in Recovery

Polyvagal theory borrows the "dissolution" principle from 19th-century neurologist John Hughlings Jackson: under threat, the autonomic hierarchy disengages in reverse phylogenetic order — newest tier first, oldest last. Recovery reverses the sequence.

Jackson's original principle (1884): "When the higher are suddenly rendered functionless, the lower rise in activity." Jackson used "dissolution" to describe what brain damage and disease produce — the loss of newer, higher-order neural functions revealing older, lower-order substrates. Porges generalizes this to adaptive autonomic state shifts under perceived threat, with the explicit claim that recovery is possible by re-establishing cues of safety.

Recovery through safety cues

Dorsal Vagal (oldest) Stuck in shutdown or collapse

↑

safety cues, gentle activation, co-regulation

Sympathetic (transitional) Movement, trembling, tears, breath — the system mobilizes

↑
