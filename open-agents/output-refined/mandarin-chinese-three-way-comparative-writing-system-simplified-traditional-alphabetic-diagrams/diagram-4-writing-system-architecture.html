Three Writing System Architectures

How logographic (Traditional & Simplified) and alphabetic (English) systems encode meaning differently

Traditional Chinese
Logographic / Morphosyllabic
Unit: Character = Morpheme
電 話
"electric" + "speech" = telephone
Each character = 1 syllable = 1 meaning unit
Internal Structure
Semantic radical (meaning hint)
Phonetic component (sound hint)
214 Kangxi radicals classify characters
語 = 言 (speech) + 吾 (sound: wu)
Phonetic Information
Partial: phonetic component gives approximate pronunciation in ~20-40% of characters. No direct sound encoding -- you must know the character to pronounce it.
Inventory
~4,800 characters for full literacy
~47,000 total characters exist
Avg. 12.2 strokes per character
Simplified Chinese
Logographic / Morphosyllabic (Reformed)
Unit: Character = Morpheme
电 话
"electric" + "speech" = telephone
Same principle as traditional -- only forms differ
Internal Structure
Simplified radicals (meaning hint, reduced strokes)
Phonetic component (sometimes substituted)
Component-based analysis replaces strict 214 radicals
语 = 讠(speech) + 吾 (sound: wu)
Phonetic Information
Partial: similar reliability to traditional. Component substitution occasionally improves or degrades sound hints. Pinyin layer adds full phonetic representation.
Inventory
~3,500 characters for newspaper literacy
2,236 characters officially simplified
Avg. 9.7 strokes per character
English
Alphabetic / Deep Orthography
Unit: Letter ≈ Phoneme
t-e-l-e-p-h-o-n-e
Letters map to sounds, not meanings
Words built from sequences of sound-units
Internal Structure
Morphological spelling preserves roots
Etymology visible in spelling layers
Germanic roots + Latin/Greek borrowings
tele + phone (Greek: far + sound)
Phonetic Information
Primary but highly irregular: ~44 phonemes mapped by 26 letters. "Deep" orthography: through, though, tough, thought share "ough" but differ in pronunciation.
Inventory
26 letters (all you need!)
~170,000 words in active use
Avg. 2.5 strokes per letter
Reading Path: Meaning ↔ Symbol ↔ Sound
