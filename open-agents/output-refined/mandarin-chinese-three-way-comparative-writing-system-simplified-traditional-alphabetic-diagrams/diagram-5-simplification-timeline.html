Chinese Character Simplification Timeline

From first proposals to the current standard -- a century of script reform

1909-1935 -- Early Reform Movement
Intellectual Foundations
Lu Feikui publishes first simplification proposals (1909). May Fourth Movement (1919) calls for script reform as part of modernization. Qian Xuantong advocates for character simplification or even complete alphabetization. The ROC Ministry of Education publishes a table of 324 simplified characters in 1935 but retracts it due to conservative opposition.
324 chars proposed (1935)
Retracted same year
1949 -- PRC Founded
New Government, New Language Policy
Mao Zedong states character simplification as a national priority. Chinese Committee for Script Reform established (1952). Initial debates between complete romanization vs. simplification -- Mao reportedly favored eventual phonetic writing but accepted simplification as an intermediate step.
China's illiteracy rate: ~80% (over 400 million of 500 million people)
January 28, 1956 -- First Simplification Scheme
The Chinese Character Simplification Scheme Implemented
The State Council's 23rd Plenary Meeting passes the resolution. Three parts: 230 characters for immediate use, 285 for phased implementation, and 54 simplified radicals. Uses six methods: cursive adoption, stroke reduction, component substitution, radical simplification, character merger, and new creations.
515 simplified characters
54 simplified radicals
6 simplification methods
1958 -- Pinyin Adopted
Hanyu Pinyin Scheme Approved
The National People's Congress approves the Pinyin romanization system. Designed by Zhou Youguang and team as a pronunciation guide -- NOT a replacement for characters. Creates a "parallel alphabetic layer" that Traditional Chinese lacks.
26 Latin letters + diacritics
4 tone marks
May 1964 -- General List Published
General List of Simplified Chinese Characters Standard
The Language Reform Committee publishes the comprehensive list, addressing defects in the 1956 scheme. Adds the 28 deferred characters from 1956 and systematically applies radical simplifications across the character set. This becomes the definitive standard.
2,236 total simplified characters
352 standalone simplifications
1,754 via radical substitution
~20% avg. stroke reduction
December 20, 1977 -- Second Round Published
Second Round Simplification Failed
Published in the shadow of the Cultural Revolution. Too many characters changed too radically. Newspapers and textbooks adopted the forms before the "discussion" period ended, causing widespread confusion. Public resistance was immediate. The distinction between "use now" and "discuss" categories was not maintained.
248 chars "immediate"
605 chars "for discussion"
~4,500 total chars affected
Mid-1978: Ministry of Education asks publishers to stop using second-round forms
June 24, 1986 -- Second Round Rescinded
Official Rescission by State Council
The State Council formally rescinds the second round of simplification. The 1964 General List (with minor corrections) is reaffirmed as the sole standard. This effectively ended further simplification -- no third round has ever been proposed.
First round reaffirmed as standard
2000 -- Language Law Enacted
Law of the PRC on the Standard Spoken and Written Language
Codifies Putonghua as the national common language and simplified characters as the standard written form. Provides legal framework for language standardization while allowing traditional characters for specific uses (calligraphy, classical texts, etc.).
2013-Present -- Normalization Table
Table of General Standard Chinese Characters
Published June 2013 with 8,105 characters for general use: 3,500 common (Level 1), 3,000 secondary (Level 2), 1,605 tertiary. This is the current definitive standard. No new simplifications -- focus shifted to standardization and digital encoding.
8,105 standardized characters
3,500 common use (Level 1)
0 new simplifications
