Arguments FOR Simplification Impact
  • Reduced stroke counts make characters faster to write and potentially easier to memorize
  • Lower barrier to entry for initial character learning in rural areas
  • Faster handwriting speed reduces fatigue in lengthy writing tasks
  • Simplified forms were already in common handwritten use -- formalization reduced confusion
Arguments AGAINST Simplification as Key Factor
  • Taiwan achieved 96%+ literacy with traditional characters -- no simplification needed
  • Japan uses a mix of simplified and traditional kanji; literacy rates match China's
  • Scholars attribute gains primarily to expanded compulsory education and rural campaigns
  • Character mergers created new ambiguities that complicate learning
  • The Second Round (1977) failed despite even more simplification -- suggesting a limit
The Scholarly Consensus
Character simplification was one component of a multi-pronged literacy campaign, but expanded access to compulsory education was the primary driver of literacy improvements. Taiwan's success with traditional characters demonstrates that simplification is neither necessary nor sufficient for mass literacy. The correlation between simplification timing and literacy gains is confounded by simultaneous massive investments in rural schooling.
