## Diagram Descriptions

### Diagram 1: The Message-Passing Test Paradigm
- **Type**: Flowchart (experimental design)
- **Elements**: 3 experimental conditions, results for each, evidence summary
- **Key Insight**: The facilitator-matched-output finding (Condition 3) is a positive demonstration of facilitator influence, not merely an absence of communication. Stress cannot explain it.
- **Accessibility**: Condition 1 (same info): ambiguous correct output. Condition 2 (facilitator blind): 0-1.8% correct, no meaningful output. Condition 3 (different info): 66-80% matches facilitator's (incorrect) stimulus. ~40-50 studies, 4 systematic reviews, consistent finding.

### Diagram 2: The Ecological Validity Debate
- **Type**: Flowchart (argument/counterargument)
- **Elements**: Proponent argument chain (4 steps), skeptic counterargument chain (6 steps), verdict
- **Key Insight**: Proponents are correct that lab settings may reduce output. Skeptics are correct that stress cannot explain facilitator-matched output. The facilitator-matched finding is the strongest skeptic evidence.
- **Accessibility**: Proponent: lab stress impairs communication, therefore tests are invalid. Skeptic: tests show not just low performance but facilitator-matched output, which is a positive finding of influence that stress cannot explain. Verdict: where each is stronger marked.

### Diagram 3: The Informed Consent Paradox
- **Type**: Flowchart (circular/recursive)
- **Elements**: 5-step circular loop, 5 escape routes, consequence section
- **Key Insight**: The consent paradox contributes to the evidence vacuum by stalling research, perpetuating the debate, and leaving non-speaking individuals caught in the middle
- **Accessibility**: Circular: want to study method X, need consent, consent only through X, if X invalid consent invalid, can't know if X valid without study. Escape routes: proxy consent, behavioral assent, ethical listening framework, accessible materials, multi-modal consent.

### Diagram 4: The ASHA-Community Conflict Map
- **Type**: Flowchart (stakeholder positions)
- **Elements**: 3 stakeholder groups (evidence priority, access priority, bridging), 9 organizations, key insight
- **Key Insight**: Positions reflect different value hierarchies (evidence vs. access), not disagreement about whether non-speaking people deserve communication
- **Accessibility**: Evidence priority: ASHA, NCSA, ASAT. Access priority: ASAN, I-ASC, United for Communication Choice. Bridging: CommunicationFIRST (cross-disability, 5M+ all speech disabilities), non-speaking AAC users (Zimmerman, Blasko).

### Diagram 5: The "Nothing About Us Without Us" Recursion
- **Type**: Flowchart (recursive paradox)
- **Elements**: Principle, problem, 2 branches (uncontested vs. contested methods), 4 attempted resolutions
- **Key Insight**: The paradox is genuinely unresolvable in the abstract. Prioritizing verified communicators while remaining open to new evidence is the least-bad approach.
- **Accessibility**: Principle: include non-speaking voices. Problem: requires communication. If AAC: include (but may not represent all). If FC: including may include facilitator; excluding reproduces gatekeeping. Resolutions: prioritize uncontested, accept all, verify then include, acknowledge paradox.

### Diagram 6: Evidence-Based Practice vs. Communication Access
- **Type**: Flowchart (tension/spectrum)
- **Elements**: 3 positions (strict EBP, middle, max access), both-sides analysis, common ground
- **Key Insight**: Evidence-based AAC is the common ground where all sides agree. The debate is specifically about which methods reliably transmit the person's own thoughts.
- **Accessibility**: Left: strict EBP, only validated methods, risk of denying access. Center: evidence-based AAC first-line, contested methods under informed consent. Right: maximum access, accept all, risk of facilitator authorship. Common ground: evidence-based AAC universally supported.

### Diagram 7: Legal Framework for Communication Rights
- **Type**: Flowchart (hierarchical)
- **Elements**: 3 levels (federal, state, local), implementation gap section
- **Key Insight**: The right to communication is legally established. The gap between mandate and implementation is where rights are lost.
- **Accessibility**: Federal: ADA (effective communication), IDEA (FAPE, IEP, LRE, assistive technology), Section 504. State: education codes, Medicaid AAC coverage. Local: IEP teams, insurance, courts. Gap: expensive devices, variable coverage, untrained staff, assessment-placement loop, legal system suspicion.
