## Diagram Descriptions

### Diagram 1: Timeline of Major Linguistic Theories
- **Type**: Timeline (Mermaid timeline)
- **Elements**: 5 sections, 25+ events spanning 1916-2003
- **Key Insight**: Linguistic theory has undergone a series of paradigm shifts, each partly a reaction against the perceived limitations of its predecessor -- from Saussure's structuralism through behaviorism, generative grammar, and the cognitive/connectionist turns
- **Accessibility**: Five-section timeline. Structuralism (1916-1933): Saussure's Course, Prague School, Bloomfield. Behaviorism & Revolution (1957-1959): Skinner's Verbal Behavior, Chomsky's Syntactic Structures, Chomsky's review. Generative Grammar (1965-1981): Standard Theory, Extended Standard Theory, Linguistics Wars, Government and Binding. Cognitive Turn (1980-1987): Lakoff and Johnson, Competition Model, connectionist past tense model, Langacker. Modern Era (1989-2003): Cognitive Linguistics Conference, Minimalist Program, Construction Grammar, Talmy, Hauser-Chomsky-Fitch, Tomasello.

### Diagram 2: Saussure's Linguistic Sign
- **Type**: Flowchart (component diagram)
- **Elements**: 4 subgraphs, 20+ nodes
- **Key Insight**: Saussure's four foundational concepts -- the bilateral sign (signifier/signified), the two axes of relation (paradigmatic/syntagmatic), the langue/parole distinction, and language as a system of differences -- form the basis of all subsequent structural linguistics
- **Accessibility**: Four-part diagram. Top: The Linguistic Sign -- signifier (sound-image) and signified (concept) linked by arbitrary relationship. Middle-left: Paradigmatic axis showing vertical substitution (cat/dog/bird/child). Middle-right: Syntagmatic axis showing horizontal combination (The-cat-sat-on-the-mat). Bottom-left: Langue (abstract system, shared, social, object of linguistics). Bottom-right: Parole (individual utterances, variable, concrete, subject to performance). Bottom: System of differences showing hot-cold-warm-lukewarm opposition network.

### Diagram 3: Chomsky's Grammar Architecture Evolution
- **Type**: Flowchart (layered evolution)
- **Elements**: 5 era subgraphs, 1 trend summary, 25+ nodes
- **Key Insight**: Chomsky's framework progressively simplified over 40 years: from many specific phrase structure and transformational rules to a single operation (Merge), and from multiple representational levels to two interface levels (PF and LF)
- **Accessibility**: Five sequential era boxes connected by arrows. 1957 Syntactic Structures: phrase structure rules to kernel sentences to transformations to surface sentences. 1965 Standard Theory: phrase structure + lexicon to deep structure, branching to semantic interpretation and transformations, then surface structure to phonological interpretation. 1970s Extended Standard: X-bar theory to D-structure, Move-alpha (with traces) to S-structure, branching to PF and LF. 1981 Government & Binding: Principles and Parameters to D-structure, Move-alpha to S-structure, branching to PF and LF, with modular subsystems. 1993+ Minimalist: Lexicon to External Merge to Internal Merge to Spell-Out, branching to PF and LF. Trend box: many specific rules to one operation, multiple levels to two interfaces, language-specific rules to universal principles.

### Diagram 4: Chomsky vs. Skinner Debate
- **Type**: Flowchart (comparison)
- **Elements**: 2 main theory subgraphs, 1 clash subgraph, 6 nested subgraphs, 30+ nodes
- **Key Insight**: The 1959 clash between Skinner's behaviorist account and Chomsky's nativist account catalyzed the cognitive revolution, but the debate was more nuanced than the standard narrative suggests
- **Accessibility**: Left side -- Skinner: Environmental Stimulus to Verbal Operant to Reinforcement to Habit Strengthening (cycle). Verbal operant types: mand, tact, echoic, intraverbal, autoclitic. Key claims: language is behavior, no mental representations, environment shapes all. Cannot explain: novel sentences, hierarchical structure, rapid acquisition. Center -- The 1959 Review: Chomsky's 33-page review, catalyst for cognitive revolution, shifted psychology to cognitivism. Right side -- Chomsky: Innate UG + Linguistic Input to Parameter Setting to Internalized Grammar to Infinite Output. Key arguments: poverty of stimulus, creativity, hierarchy, rapid acquisition. Key claims: language is innate knowledge, mental rules real, biology constrains grammar. Cannot explain: performance errors, gradual item-based acquisition, crosslinguistic statistical learning.

### Diagram 5: Cognitive Linguistics Core Architecture
- **Type**: Mind Map
- **Elements**: 6 main branches, 35+ leaf nodes
- **Key Insight**: Cognitive linguistics unifies five major strands under shared commitments to embodiment, meaning-driven grammar, usage-based learning, and the rejection of an innate language-specific module
- **Accessibility**: Central node "Cognitive Linguistics" with six branches. Branch 1 "Conceptual Metaphor (Lakoff)": source-target mappings, ARGUMENT IS WAR, TIME IS MONEY, IDEAS ARE OBJECTS, LIFE IS A JOURNEY, metaphor structures thought. Branch 2 "Cognitive Grammar (Langacker)": symbolic units, profile/base, schema/instance, construal, no lexicon-grammar boundary. Branch 3 "Cognitive Semantics (Talmy)": force dynamics, figure/ground, motion events, fictive motion, conceptual structuring. Branch 4 "Construction Grammar (Goldberg)": form-meaning pairings, morphemes to abstract patterns, ditransitive means transfer, no derivation, constructions are learned. Branch 5 "Usage-Based (Tomasello)": intention-reading, pattern-finding, item-based early language, no UG module, language constructed through use. Branch 6 "Shared Commitments": embodied, no autonomous syntax, meaning drives grammar, no innate module, usage-based, general cognition.

### Diagram 6: Language Acquisition Models Compared
- **Type**: Flowchart (4-column comparison)
- **Elements**: 4 theory subgraphs, 1 key question subgraph, 20+ nodes
- **Key Insight**: The four models differ fundamentally on what the child brings to language acquisition: language-specific innate grammar (nativist) vs. domain-general cognitive capacities (all others)
- **Accessibility**: Four side-by-side columns. Nativist/UG (Chomsky, Pinker): child brings innate UG with principles and parameters; environment provides positive evidence; mechanism is parameter setting; result is internalized grammar by age 4-5. Usage-Based (Tomasello, Goldberg): child brings general cognition, intention-reading, pattern-finding; environment provides rich social interaction; mechanism is abstraction from use; result is inventory of constructions. Connectionist (Rumelhart, Elman): child brings learning algorithms, neural architecture; environment provides distributional statistics; mechanism is weight adjustment; result is emergent rule-like patterns without explicit rules. Competition Model (Bates & MacWhinney): child brings cue sensitivity, competition mechanisms; environment provides multiple cues (word order, case, agreement, animacy); mechanism is cue competition weighted by reliability; result is language-specific cue weightings. Key question: language-specific grammar vs. domain-general cognition.

### Diagram 7: Theoretical Landscape -- Key Oppositions
- **Type**: Flowchart (network diagram)
- **Elements**: 8 theory nodes, 5 dimension subgraphs, 20+ connecting edges
- **Key Insight**: Linguistic theories cluster into two broad camps along five interrelated dimensions, with Chomsky's generative grammar on one pole (innate, formal, modular, rule-based, competence-focused) and cognitive/usage-based/connectionist approaches on the other (learned, functional, holistic, statistical, use-focused)
- **Accessibility**: Eight theory nodes: Saussure, Bloomfield, Skinner, Chomsky, Cognitive Linguistics, Construction Grammar, Connectionism, Usage-Based. Five dimension spectrums: Innate-Learned, Formal-Functional, Modular-Holistic, Rules-Statistics, Competence-Performance. Solid arrows connect theories to their position on each dimension. Dashed arrows show intellectual lineage: Saussure influenced Bloomfield, Bloomfield influenced Skinner, Bloomfield-Harris-Chomsky lineage, Chomsky reacted against by Cognitive Linguistics, which feeds into Construction Grammar and aligns with Usage-Based, which aligns with Connectionism. Color coding: blue for structuralist/generativist, green for cognitive/functional, purple for connectionist, red for behaviorist, amber for dimension labels.
