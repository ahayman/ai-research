# English Pragmatics -- Diagram Manifest

## Diagrams

### diagram-1-request-strategy-hierarchy.html
- **Title**: English Request Strategy Hierarchy
- **Type**: Vertical spectrum with frequency bars (SVG/CSS)
- **Description**: 10-level hierarchy from most direct (imperative) to most indirect (mild hint), showing example formulations and relative frequency in everyday English conversation. Highlights the massive clustering at levels 7-8 (query preparatory) that distinguishes English from more direct languages.
- **Key Pattern**: English defaults to conventionalized indirect requests (can/could you), never interpreted literally.

### diagram-2-discourse-markers-functional-map.html
- **Title**: English Discourse Markers -- Functional Map
- **Type**: Matrix/grid (CSS table)
- **Description**: 10 major discourse markers mapped against 8 pragmatic functions. Dots indicate primary, secondary, and occasional functions. Each marker has a unique "functional fingerprint."
- **Key Pattern**: No two discourse markers are interchangeable; each has a distinct functional profile. Function, not etymology, determines use.

### diagram-3-negative-politeness-strategies.html
- **Title**: English Negative Politeness Strategies
- **Type**: Strategy card layout with formula (CSS)
- **Description**: Brown & Levinson's 10 negative politeness sub-strategies with English examples, positioned within the five super-strategy framework. Includes the W = D + P + R weight formula.
- **Key Pattern**: English speakers stack strategies proportionally to D + P + R; the number of simultaneous strategies is a direct readout of perceived social weight.

### diagram-4-hedging-compositionality.html
- **Title**: Hedging Compositionality in English
- **Type**: Stacking demonstration with force meter (CSS)
- **Description**: Shows 0-4 hedges stacked on the same base request, with a force meter showing decreasing illocutionary force. Includes taxonomy of five hedge categories (modal, approximator, shield, downtoner, discourse).
- **Key Pattern**: Hedge stacking is compositional and approximately logarithmic -- the first hedge has the largest effect, each additional hedge adds less. The count of hedges reads as an assessment of face-threat weight.

### diagram-5-register-continuum.html
- **Title**: English Register Continuum
- **Type**: Five-column comparison layout (CSS)
- **Description**: Five registers (frozen, formal, consultative, casual, intimate) compared across vocabulary, syntax, hedging, and discourse markers. The same request is shown at each register level.
- **Key Pattern**: Register is four correlated dimensions moving in parallel; the same speech act transforms predictably across all five levels.
