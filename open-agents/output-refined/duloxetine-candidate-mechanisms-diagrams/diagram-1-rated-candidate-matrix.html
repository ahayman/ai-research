Four things to read off the matrix

NO ROW IS COMPLETE

Not one candidate scores above ○ on more than five of the ten. The best account is a composite, and the composite is the honest answer.

ONE COLUMN IS EMPTY

Nothing explains the restless legs. Ten features, eight candidates, and that column has no hit at all. The list is not being stretched to cover everything.

THE ACUTE COLUMN IS WHERE IT BREAKS

One candidate predicts acute-worse. Four predict its opposite. That single column is why no unified theory survives. Adding candidate 8 did not change it.

THE ESCITALOPRAM COLUMN IS THE THINNEST

Two candidates were built for it — 4 and 8 — and both earn their ✓✓ by construction rather than by predicting anything. One of them has now been tested and failed. (Candidate 2's tick is a different thing: its evidence base happens to be escitalopram.) The hole that column marks is still open.

Why the order is what it is. Rank is fit-to-him × evidence quality. The dull candidate ranks first because it is the only one with a positive randomised trial at his actual dose, and it explains the three features he values most. The elegant candidate ranks third because elegance loses to a positive RCT. The candidate that was ranked first by the orchestrator now ranks fifth, because its central claim turned out to be a misreading of its own source.
