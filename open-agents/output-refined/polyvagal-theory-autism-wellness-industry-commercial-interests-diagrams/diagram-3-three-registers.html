The Three Registers — The Analytical Anchor

The same words ("ventral vagal", "neuroception", "polyvagal-informed") mean very different things depending on the register. Knowing which one a claim comes from is the user's most useful filter.

The filter question: When you encounter a polyvagal-branded claim, ask — is this coming from the academic, clinical, or wellness/influencer register? The answer changes how much weight the claim deserves.
Register 1

Academic

Where you find it

Peer-reviewed journals (psychophysiology, neuroscience, autonomic medicine).

Evidence status (2026)

Actively contested. Grossman 2023 critique; Grossman et al. 2026 multi-author consensus ("Why The Polyvagal Theory Is Untenable", 38 international co-authors).

Example claims
  • "In our sample, RSA correlated with X under conditions Y."
  • "Myelinated cardiac vagal fibers originate from the nucleus ambiguus."
What's claimed

Specific, narrow, falsifiable propositions.

What's NOT claimed

Sweeping life applications. Cures. Diagnoses of autonomic states without measurement.

Reading load: technical. Hedging language: pervasive. Reach: small — specialist journals.
Register 2

Clinical

Where you find it

Norton Professional Books, EMDRIA continuing education, evidence-mixed clinical practice within licensed therapy, PVI clinician courses, Dana's Rhythm of Regulation, Badenoch's interpersonal-neurobiology integration.

Evidence status (2026)

Mixed evidence; some real clinical utility as orientation. Journal of Psychiatry Reform 2023 summary: "scientifically questionable but useful in practice."

Example claims
  • "The polyvagal framework offers useful orientation for therapists working with autonomic dysregulation."
  • "SSP may help reduce auditory hypersensitivity in some clients (Tier B–C evidence)."
What's claimed

Framework as orientation; specific interventions at evidence Tier B–C (per Cat 6).

What's NOT claimed (in careful practice)

Settled mechanism. Cures for autism. Treatment by non-licensed practitioners.

Reading load: medium. Hedging language: present in careful work, absent in marketing. Reach: hundreds of thousands of practitioners.
Register 3

Wellness / Influencer

Where you find it

Instagram, TikTok, podcast wellness, supplement marketing, consumer device ads, coaching certifications without clinical scope.

Evidence status (2026)

Often pseudoscientific. Explicit warnings from Northwell, MGH, McGill, The Conversation.

Example claims
  • "Reset your nervous system in 5 minutes."
  • "You're stuck in dorsal vagal."
  • "This supplement optimizes your vagal tone."
  • "This gua sha tool stimulates the vagus nerve."
  • "Rewire your nervous system in 8 weeks."
  • Autism "cure" via polyvagal interventions.
What's actually supported

Some underlying practices (slow breathing, cold face splash, humming) have real physiological effects through mechanisms that predate and do not depend on polyvagal theory. The branding is borrowed credibility.

Red flag

Strong cure / timeline / mechanism claims with no evidence base.

Reading load: low. Hedging language: absent. Reach: hundreds of millions of social media views.
How to use this: When you encounter a polyvagal-branded claim, locate it on the three-column map. A Register 1 claim is hedged and narrow; trust it within its scope. A Register 2 claim may be useful even if the underlying theory is contested; evaluate against the Cat 6 evidence-tier framework. A Register 3 claim is presumptively suspect until specific evidence is presented. The same vocabulary appears across all three registers; only the epistemic load differs.
The same theory exists in three different registers. The user's most useful filter is to ask which one a given claim comes from. This is the chapter's central analytical contribution.
