## Diagram Descriptions

### Diagram 1: Saussure's Dyadic Sign Model
- **Type**: Flowchart / concept diagram
- **Elements**: Signifier and signified as two halves of the sign, arbitrariness annotation, excluded referent, system of differences with 5 example signs
- **Key Insight**: The sign is a purely internal psychological unit (signifier + signified), not a label for a thing. The referent is excluded from the model entirely. Meaning arises from differences between signs, not from positive content.
- **Accessibility**: Two boxes (Signifier: sound-image; Signified: concept) connected by double arrow labeled "bound together like recto and verso of paper." Arbitrariness box connects to both with dashed lines. Excluded referent shown with dashed border and "EXCLUDED from model" label. System of differences shows five signs (dark, dim, gloomy, light, murky) connected by "not" relations.

### Diagram 2: Peirce's Triadic Sign Model and Unlimited Semiosis
- **Type**: Flowchart with subgraphs
- **Elements**: 3 triadic elements, 3 sign-object types (icon/index/symbol), 4-step semiosis chain, habit constraint
- **Key Insight**: The sign relation is irreducibly triadic (representamen-object-interpretant). Each interpretant becomes a new sign, creating unlimited semiosis. Habit temporarily arrests the chain in practice.
- **Accessibility**: Three main nodes (Representamen, Object, Interpretant) connected by labeled arrows (stands for, determines, grounds). Sign-Object types: Icon (resemblance), Index (causal connection), Symbol (convention). Semiosis chain: see smoke, think fire, think call 911, think evacuate, chain continues. Habit box arrests the chain.

### Diagram 3: Saussure vs. Peirce -- Two Founding Traditions
- **Type**: Side-by-side comparison flowchart
- **Elements**: 2 model subgraphs, 6 key difference items
- **Key Insight**: The two founders diverge on every fundamental question: number of elements (2 vs. 3), inclusion of referent, closed vs. open system, structure vs. process, linguistic vs. all signs, synchronic vs. dynamic.
- **Accessibility**: Left panel: Saussure's dyadic model with signifier, signified, excluded referent, and closed system of langue. Right panel: Peirce's triadic model with representamen, object, interpretant, and open process of unlimited semiosis. Center panel: six labeled contrasts (2 vs. 3 elements, referent excluded vs. included, structure vs. process, linguistic vs. all signs, synchronic vs. dynamic, fixed vs. converging meaning).

### Diagram 4: Frege's Sense and Reference (Sinn und Bedeutung)
- **Type**: Convergent path diagram
- **Elements**: 2 expressions, 2 senses, 1 shared reference, Frege's puzzle, sentence extension, compositionality
- **Key Insight**: "The Morning Star" and "The Evening Star" refer to the same object (Venus) but present it under different modes of presentation (senses). This proves meaning cannot be just reference -- it has two dimensions.
- **Accessibility**: Two expressions ("The Morning Star" and "The Evening Star") each connect to their respective senses (visible at dawn / visible at dusk), which both converge on the single reference: the planet Venus. Frege's Puzzle box explains why "morning star = evening star" is informative. Extension to sentences: sense = thought, reference = truth value. Compositionality box at bottom.

### Diagram 5: Wittgenstein's Two Phases -- From Picture to Practice
- **Type**: Before/after dual-panel flowchart
- **Elements**: Tractatus panel (3 main nodes, 3 detail nodes), radical break marker, Investigations panel (5 key concepts)
- **Key Insight**: The early Wittgenstein held that language is a logical picture of the world with single deterministic structure. The later Wittgenstein dismantled this: language has no essence, only language games embedded in forms of life, where meaning is use and categories are family resemblances.
- **Accessibility**: Left panel (Tractatus 1921): Language mirrors World through shared Logical Form. Three details: logical atomism, single structure, limits of language. Red break marker: "Wittgenstein dismantles his own earlier work." Right panel (Investigations 1953): Forms of Life lead to Language Games, which establish Meaning = Use, branching into Family Resemblance and No Private Language.

### Diagram 6: Barthes's Orders of Signification
- **Type**: Layered stacking flowchart
- **Elements**: 3 orders (denotation, connotation, myth), each with signifier + signified = sign, Paris Match example, myth's power
- **Key Insight**: Saussure's sign model operates at multiple levels. The complete first-order sign becomes the signifier for a second-order connotation, which becomes the signifier for myth -- a system that transforms culture into nature, making the arbitrary seem inevitable.
- **Accessibility**: Three stacked layers. First order (blue): signifier (photo of rose) + signified (the flower) = sign (denotation: this is a rose). Second order (purple): first-order sign becomes new signifier + new signified (romance, love) = sign (connotation: rose means love). Third order (red): connotative sign becomes new signifier + new signified (naturalized ideology) = myth. Paris Match example: denotation (photo of soldier), connotation (patriotism), myth (colonialism is natural). Myth's Power: transforms culture into nature.

### Diagram 7: The Grand Arc -- From Fixed to Fluid Meaning
- **Type**: Horizontal timeline / progression flowchart
- **Elements**: 3 stages (Foundations, Pivot, Dissolution), 7 thinkers, 2 conceptual shift markers, living tensions
- **Key Insight**: The philosophy of signs traces a trajectory from stability (Frege, Saussure, early Wittgenstein) through a pivot (later Wittgenstein) to dissolution (Barthes, Derrida) and partial synthesis (Eco). Five fundamental tensions remain unresolved.
- **Accessibility**: Stage 1 (Foundations, blue): Frege 1892, Saussure 1916, Tractatus 1921, Peirce (dashed, dynamism within foundations). Shift: "Sign as System to Sign as Process." Stage 2 (Pivot, amber): Wittgenstein Investigations 1953. Shift: "Sign as Practice to Sign as Play." Stage 3 (Dissolution, red): Barthes Mythologies 1957, Derrida 1967, Barthes S/Z 1970, Eco 1976 (green, synthesis). Living Tensions: structure vs. process, stability vs. instability, system vs. context, author vs. reader, limited vs. unlimited interpretation.

### Diagram 8: Derrida's Differance and the Chain of Signification
- **Type**: Network / concept diagram
- **Elements**: Differance with two dimensions, chain of 5 signifiers, traces subgraph, logocentrism critique, arche-writing, deconstruction
- **Key Insight**: Differance names a double movement: to differ (spatial) and to defer (temporal). Meaning is an endless chain of signifiers with no transcendental signified. Every sign carries traces of absent signs. Logocentrism (speech over writing) is inverted through arche-writing. Deconstruction reveals how texts undermine their own premises.
- **Accessibility**: Central node "DIFFERANCE" branches to "To Differ" (spatial, synchronic) and "To Defer" (temporal, diachronic). Chain of Signification: cat, feline animal, domesticated predator, carnivorous mammal, warm-blooded creature, each defined by the next. Traces: cat bears traces of bat/cut/car/hat and dog/mouse/bird; identity contaminated by alterity. Logocentrism inverted by Arche-writing. No Transcendental Signified. Both lead to Deconstruction.
