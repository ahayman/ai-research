The Vivid-Dreams Graveyard

He has vivid dreams. Three explanations circulate for why. All three are dead, and the honest answer underneath them is that nobody knows.

A fourth one, which belongs in the same graveyard. "REM suppression explains the reduced morning fatigue" traces to Vogel's 1970s REM-deprivation work (PMID 165796). Do not use it. It was never independently replicated; modern reviews report a wide range of inconsistent effects in animal models; and the confound is fatal and elegant, because REM deprivation is followed by REM rebound, and "it might be the rebound sleep, and not REM sleep deprivation itself, that has an antidepressant effect." If that is right, the mechanism runs backwards from the version used to explain it. [secondary — unverified]
What actually survives, and it is worth having. The dreams are a real, common, expected serotonergic effect. In the only ME/CFS trial of duloxetine, "vivid dreams" is a literal registry adverse-event term, reported at 13.79% versus 3.33% on placebo: his exact symptom, in his exact condition, at roughly four times the placebo rate. He is not unusual. He is in the 14%. And his report matches the treatment phase of Pace-Schott precisely: fewer dreams recalled, more intense when they come. [primary, verified]
Why this matters practically. The tidy REM story is available everywhere and it is wrong. If he is told the dreams mean REM pressure is building, or that they are a warning of rebound, or that losing them at a higher dose would signal something about REM, none of those inferences is supported. What the dream literature actually identifies as the operative variable is awakenings. That matters here, because fewer awakenings would explain his dreams and his easier mornings with a single mechanism, and it is the same variable the sleep-continuity story runs on.
