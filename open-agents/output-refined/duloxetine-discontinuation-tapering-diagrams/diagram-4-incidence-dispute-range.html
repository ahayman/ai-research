How Often Does It Happen? Three Numbers, Three Methods, One Dispute

These are not three attempts at the same measurement that happen to disagree. They are three different questions. The shaded portion of each bar is the part that happened to people who stopped a placebo.

Perahia 2005 anchor

PMID 16266753 • pooled from 6 short-term MDD trials
What it is
The only figure here that is duloxetine-specific, placebo-controlled, and trial-derived.
The catch
Abrupt stopping, which is a worst case rather than a taper. Short trial exposure.

Henssler 2024

Lancet Psychiatry • 79 studies, antidepressants generally
The critique of it
Trials are short, screened, and structurally incapable of observing long-term real-world users, which deflates the estimate.
Also reports
Severe symptoms ~3%.

Davies & Read 2019

PMID 30292574 • 14 studies, antidepressants generally
The critique of it
Pooling online surveys with RCTs inflates the estimate, because people recruited through withdrawal-focused channels are those who had a bad time (Jauhar & Hayes; PMC12315658).
Also reports
~46% of affected people endorsed the most extreme severity ratings.
Look at the placebo columns, because they explain the whole dispute. Roughly half of what gets counted as "withdrawal" in these datasets happens to people who stopped a dummy pill. That is the most useful single fact in the withdrawal literature, and it comes from duloxetine's own data rather than from either side of the argument.
~15%
answers: "how much of this does the drug cause?"

Placebo-subtracted. The right number for judging pharmacological attribution.

~31%
answers: "how likely am I to feel bad?"

Raw. The right number for the person tapering, because subjective experience does not arrive pre-sorted into pharmacological and nonspecific components.

No winner is offered here, deliberately. Both critiques land, and neither population matches this reader: he is a real-world user rather than a trial subject, and he is also not someone who found this topic by searching for help getting off a drug. The honest report is the range with the argument attached.
