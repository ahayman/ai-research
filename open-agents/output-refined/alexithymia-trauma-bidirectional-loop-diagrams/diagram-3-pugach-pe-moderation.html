Pugach PE Moderation — The Strongest Treatment-Moderation Evidence

Prolonged Exposure (PE) for PTSD secondary analysis with TAS-20 measured at baseline. The directional claim's gold-standard test: when exposure works via emotional engagement, alexithymics engage less in the way the protocol assumes — leading to slower or attenuated outcomes.

Standard PE response. Expected pattern: emotional engagement → habituation → fear-extinction → corrective learning → diagnostic remission.

More likely to retain full PTSD diagnosis at post-treatment AND at 6-month follow-up. Emotional clarity deficits predict dropout. Delayed-effects pattern: gains arrive between post-treatment and follow-up rather than during treatment.

Headline findings (Pugach et al., PubMed 37917810)

  • High-alexithymia patients more likely to retain a full PTSD diagnosis at post-treatment AND at 6-month follow-up.
  • Alexithymia associated with delayed treatment effects on avoidance. Significant reduction from pre-treatment to follow-up but NOT from pre-treatment to post-treatment.
  • Emotional clarity (a related construct) did not moderate overall response but predicted dropout.
  • High-alexithymia group showed marginally lesser magnitude of treatment effect on negative changes in thinking and mood.

The "desensitization without integration" mechanism

Alexithymics show physiological-subjective response disconcordance: attenuated physiological arousal coupled with heightened subjective distress. The opposite of the engagement profile that exposure assumes (sympathetic activation that habituates with repetition; subjective distress decreasing as habituation occurs). If the physiological signal is muted but subjective distress is high, the protocol does not deliver the corrective learning the model predicts. The trauma representation may remain functionally "stuck" — present but unintegrated.

This is a theoretical mechanism extrapolation with face validity, scaffolded by the disconcordance findings — not a directly tested causal claim.

The clinical take-home. The literature is compatible with the reading that alexithymia produces delay and attenuation rather than absolute non-response. PE still works for many high-alexithymia patients, just slower and less completely. Whether sufficient extension and adaptation closes the gap is plausible but underspecified empirically.

Practice implication: Phase-based protocols (Cloitre's STAIR-NT) sequence emotion-regulation skills BEFORE narrative/exposure work. Phoenix Australia consensus: emotional literacy psychoeducation, somatic markers, interoceptive scaffolding before trauma processing for high-alexithymia patients.
