STAIR-NT — Phase-Based Treatment for Alexithymia-Comorbid CPTSD

Cloitre's Skills Training in Affective and Interpersonal Regulation followed by Narrative Therapy. Explicitly designed for survivors with severe affect-regulation deficits (including alexithymia) where standard trauma-focused therapy alone is insufficient. The operational consensus: build emotion-regulation skills BEFORE narrative work.

1

Stabilisation & psychoeducation

Sessions ~1–4 (variable)
Address current safety, build therapeutic alliance, psychoeducation on trauma + alexithymia. Frame the work for the client.
  • Window-of-tolerance literacy
  • Identify current dysregulation patterns
  • Establish containment strategies before any trauma processing
↓
2

STAIR — affect-skills + interpersonal regulation

Sessions ~4–12 (~8 sessions total)
The phase that's mechanistically critical for alexithymic clients. Build the emotion-regulation infrastructure that the narrative phase will depend on.
  • Emotional-literacy work: labelling, granularity, vocabulary expansion
  • Interoceptive scaffolding — gradual, titrated, with explicit cognitive labels rather than open-ended "tune in"
  • Affect-regulation skills: distress tolerance, modulation, behavioural anchors
  • Interpersonal regulation: assertiveness, boundary-setting, recognition of unsafe relationships
  • Build the parsing/labelling capacity that exposure assumes is already present
↓
3

NT — narrative therapy / trauma processing

Sessions ~12–20 (~8 sessions total)
Narrative reconstruction of the trauma — now with the emotional vocabulary, distress-tolerance skills, and affective scaffolding from Phase 2 in place.
  • Construct coherent autobiographical narrative of trauma
  • Integrate sensory/affective fragments into language
  • Process meaning, self-blame, identity disruption
  • Now the engagement profile that exposure assumes is more available — emotional content can be labelled and integrated rather than producing "desensitization without integration"
↓
4

Integration & relapse prevention

Final sessions
Consolidate gains, generalise affect-skills to current life, anticipate triggers and relapse-risk situations.
  • Apply emotion-regulation skills outside trauma context
  • Build self-monitoring system for re-emergence
  • Plan for managing future high-affect-load situations

Why phase-based vs. straight exposure for alexithymic clients

Standard trauma-focused therapy (PE, CPT alone)

Assumes the client can engage emotionally with trauma material — feel the activation, label it, habituate. For alexithymic clients, this assumption fails. Result: physiological-subjective response disconcordance, attenuated/delayed gains, higher dropout, more likely to retain full PTSD diagnosis at post-treatment and 6mo follow-up.

STAIR-NT (Cloitre's phase-based approach)

Builds the labelling/parsing capacity FIRST. The narrative phase gets the engagement profile that standard protocols assume from the start. Operational consensus: emotion-regulation skills must be addressed before — not during — narrative trauma work for clients with severe affect-regulation deficits.

The mechanistic justification. Cloitre's foundational work on childhood-abuse survivors found that, after controlling for PTSD symptomatology, emotion-regulation deficits and interpersonal problems contributed to functional impairment equal to PTSD itself. STAIR-NT was developed precisely because traditional trauma-focused therapy was insufficient for complex-trauma survivors with severe affect-regulation deficits — the operationalised acknowledgement that emotion-regulation deficits including alexithymia must be addressed first.
Honest evidence label. STAIR-NT has RCT-level support for CPTSD generally RCT. Alexithymia-specific phase-based RCTs (i.e., randomising high-alexithymia clients to STAIR-NT vs. straight PE) are sparse. Practice consensus across Cloitre, Frewen, Lanius, Phoenix Australia, and the Schimmenti developmental-trauma school is well-developed; the formal alexithymia-specific evidence base is thinner than the consensus suggests.

Practical implication for the user. If you are alexithymic and weighing trauma therapy, asking a prospective therapist whether they sequence affect-skills work before or alongside trauma processing — and whether they use PE/CPT alone for clients with severe alexithymia — is a reasonable question to ask. The literature supports the case for phased / hybrid approaches even where the formal alexithymia-specific RCT evidence is thin.
