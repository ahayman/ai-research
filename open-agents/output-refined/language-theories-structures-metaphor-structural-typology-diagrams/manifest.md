## Diagram Descriptions

### Diagram 1: Morphological Typology Tree
- **Type**: Mermaid flowchart (top-down)
- **Elements**: 4 main branches, 12 leaf nodes (3 per type: 2 examples + characteristics)
- **Key Insight**: The four types are regions on a continuum defined by morpheme-to-word ratio and degree of morpheme fusion. Isolating languages have 1:1 ratio, polysynthetic can exceed 10:1.
- **Accessibility**: Top node "Morphological Typology" branches into four color-coded categories. Green = Isolating (Mandarin, Vietnamese): one morpheme per word, grammar in word order. Amber = Agglutinative (Turkish, Japanese): many morphemes, clean boundaries, one affix = one meaning. Pink = Fusional (Latin, Russian): multiple meanings fused into single inseparable morphemes. Purple = Polysynthetic (Inuktitut, Mohawk): entire sentences compressed into single words.

### Diagram 2: Arabic Root Derivation — k-t-b
- **Type**: Mermaid flowchart (top-down)
- **Elements**: 1 root node, 4 category groups, 13 derived words
- **Key Insight**: Arabic morphology is a two-dimensional matrix: the consonantal root provides the semantic axis (k-t-b = writing) while vowel patterns (wazn) provide the functional axis (CaCaCa = basic verb, CāCiC = doer, maCCaC = place). The intersection generates meaning.
- **Accessibility**: Central root node k-t-b (writing) branches into four groups. Green = Verb Forms: kataba (he wrote), kattaba (he made write), kataba (he corresponded), istaktaba (he dictated). Amber = Participles: katib (writer), maktub (written/fate). Pink = Nouns: kitab (book), kutub (books), kitaba (act of writing), kuttab (scribes). Purple = Places: maktab (office), maktaba (library), mukataba (correspondence).

### Diagram 3: Word Order Distribution
- **Type**: SVG horizontal bar chart
- **Elements**: 7 bars for word order types, summary proportion bar, section groupings
- **Key Insight**: SOV (41%) and SVO (35%) together account for 76% of all languages. Subject precedes object in 83% of languages. Object-initial orders (OVS, OSV) represent barely 1%.
- **Accessibility**: Horizontal bar chart showing language count and percentage for each word order type. Bars grouped by subject-first (SOV at 41%, SVO at 35%), verb-first (VSO at 7%, VOS at 2%), and object-first (OVS at 0.8%, OSV at 0.3%). A "No dominant order" category shows 14%. Bottom summary bar shows proportional distribution. Note states subject precedes object in 83% of all languages.

### Diagram 4: Morphosyntactic Alignment
- **Type**: SVG diagram with labeled boxes and brackets
- **Elements**: Two parallel alignment diagrams, comparison box, split ergativity section
- **Key Insight**: Nominative-accusative groups S+A (foregrounding the actor), while ergative-absolutive groups S+P (foregrounding the undergoer). About 83% of languages are primarily nominative-accusative.
- **Accessibility**: Left panel (blue): Nominative-Accusative alignment. S (intransitive subject) and A (transitive agent) grouped as Nominative; P (transitive patient) marked as Accusative. Latin examples provided. Right panel (purple): Ergative-Absolutive alignment. S and P grouped as Absolutive; A specially marked as Ergative. Basque examples provided. Below: comparison box explaining the conceptual difference, and split ergativity examples from Georgian (by aspect), Dyirbal (by animacy), and Hindi (by verb type).

### Diagram 5: Head-Directionality Correlations
- **Type**: Mermaid flowchart (left-right)
- **Elements**: 2 main branches with 6 phrase-type correlations each, 3 mixed-language exceptions
- **Key Insight**: Languages tend toward consistency in head position across all phrase types. This clustering (the Harmony Principle) was Greenberg's most profound discovery.
- **Accessibility**: Center node "Head-Directionality Harmony Principle" branches left to Head-Initial (green): VO order, prepositions, noun-genitive, noun-adjective, aux-verb, complementizer-clause, with English/Irish/Arabic examples. Branches right to Head-Final (purple): OV order, postpositions, genitive-noun, adjective-noun, verb-aux, clause-complementizer, with Japanese/Korean/Turkish examples. Below: Mixed examples (amber) showing German, Mandarin, and Persian each mixing head directions.

### Diagram 6: Greenberg's Universals Network
- **Type**: Mermaid flowchart (top-down)
- **Elements**: 4 absolute universals, 3 word order sources, implicational chains for word order, number, and gender
- **Key Insight**: Greenberg's 45 universals form a network of if-then relationships. Word order properties cluster into harmonious bundles; number categories follow a subset hierarchy; gender propagates from nouns through pronouns to adjectives.
- **Accessibility**: Three sections. Red = Absolute Universals: #1 (S before O), #3 (VSO = prepositional), #34 (dual implies plural), #36 (noun gender implies pronoun gender). Blue = Word Order Implications: SOV predicts postpositions (#4) and genitive-before-noun (#2); VSO always has prepositions (#3) and aux-before-verb (#16); SVO tends toward prepositions and noun-before-genitive. Light blue = Implicational Chains: prepositions predict sentence-initial question particles (#9); trial implies dual implies plural; noun gender implies pronoun gender implies 3rd-person gender implies adjective gender. Bottom = Modifier Order (#20): demonstrative-numeral-adjective-NOUN pattern.

### Diagram 7: Morphological Type Comparison
- **Type**: HTML table with styled morpheme cells
- **Elements**: 4 language rows, 5 columns (type, language, expression, breakdown, properties)
- **Key Insight**: The same meaning "I could not read the books" requires 7 separate words in Mandarin (1:1 ratio), 2 words with 8 morphemes in Turkish (4:1), 4 words with fused endings in Latin (2:1), and approximately 1 massive word in Inuktitut (6:1+).
- **Accessibility**: Four-row comparison table. Row 1 (green/Isolating/Mandarin): 7 monomorphemic words, each carrying one meaning. Row 2 (amber/Agglutinative/Turkish): 2 words with 8 cleanly separable morphemes in fixed order. Row 3 (pink/Fusional/Latin): 4 words where endings fuse multiple meanings (e.g., -os = accusative + plural simultaneously). Row 4 (purple/Polysynthetic/Inuktitut): ~1 word containing ~6 morphemes encoding the entire proposition. Colored bars visualize relative morpheme density.

### Diagram 8: Interlocking Typological System
- **Type**: Mermaid flowchart (top-down)
- **Elements**: 5 parameter nodes with bidirectional connections, 5 language profile strips
- **Key Insight**: Typological parameters form a mutually constraining system. Rich morphology enables case systems and agreement, which license free word order and pro-drop. Languages are not arbitrary feature bundles but coherent, integrated systems.
- **Accessibility**: Top section shows five interconnected parameters: Morphological Type, Word Order, Case System, Agreement, and Pro-Drop, with bidirectional arrows indicating mutual constraints. Below: five language profiles as horizontal strips. Japanese (green): agglutinative, SOV, case particles, no agreement, radical pro-drop. English (blue): largely isolating, SVO, minimal case, minimal agreement, non-pro-drop. Arabic (amber): root-and-pattern, VSO/SVO, 3 cases, rich agreement, agreement pro-drop. Latin (pink): fusional, free order, 6 cases, rich agreement, pro-drop. Mandarin (purple): isolating, SVO, no case, no agreement, discourse pro-drop.
