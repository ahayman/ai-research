## Diagram Descriptions

### Diagram 1: The Assessment-Placement-Confirmation Loop
- **Type**: Flowchart (circular feedback loop)
- **Elements**: 6 loop nodes, break point, alternative trajectory
- **Key Insight**: Assessment creates the conditions that justify its own findings -- low IQ scores lead to low-expectation placements that ensure continued low scores on re-assessment
- **Accessibility**: Circular flow from standard IQ test to low score to segregated placement to reduced expectations to no skill development to re-assessment and back. Break point shows alternative assessment (Raven's, Leiter, eye-tracking) or presuming competence (Donnellan 1984) leading to inclusive placement with AAC and academics.

### Diagram 2: Wechsler vs. Raven's -- The 30-Percentile Gap
- **Type**: Flowchart (comparative)
- **Elements**: Two test result distributions, gap statistics, processing speed data
- **Key Insight**: The same children scored 30 percentile points higher on a nonverbal test. The difference is the test, not the child.
- **Accessibility**: Wechsler results: 0% high intelligence, 33% intellectual disability range. Raven's results: 33% high intelligence, 5% intellectual disability range. Average gap: 30 percentile points. Maximum individual discrepancy: 70+ percentile points. Autistic participants 23-42% faster on Raven's than controls.

### Diagram 3: What Standard IQ Tests Actually Measure
- **Type**: Flowchart (decomposition/comparison)
- **Elements**: Two parallel decompositions showing neurotypical vs. non-speaking autistic test-taking
- **Key Insight**: For neurotypical test-takers, barriers are minimal and the score reflects cognitive ability. For non-speaking autistic test-takers, barriers dominate and the score reflects the sum of access failures.
- **Accessibility**: Neurotypical: IQ score primarily reflects cognitive ability with minimal barriers from verbal expression, motor response, compliance, instruction comprehension, and test conventions. Non-speaking autistic: cognitive ability obscured by major barriers -- cannot speak, apraxia/dyspraxia, atypical engagement, verbal instructions, never-taught conventions. Conclusion: the score measures the barrier, not the brain.

### Diagram 4: The Presuming Competence Spectrum
- **Type**: Flowchart (spectrum/continuum)
- **Elements**: 6 positions from presume incompetence to presume competence, with risks at each end
- **Key Insight**: Every position carries risks. Donnellan's argument is that the risks of underestimation (devastating, irreversible) outweigh the risks of overestimation (unnecessary supports) -- except in the specific FC context where overestimation carries its own harms.
- **Accessibility**: Left to right spectrum: Presume incompetence (deny education, communication, autonomy) to Test empirically (adapted assessments) to Stretch boundaries (Lloyd-Thomas 2025) to Least dangerous assumption (Donnellan 1984) to Presume competence (maximal access). Left risk: devastating underestimation, lost decades. Right risk: unnecessary resources, potentially accepting unverified FC output.

### Diagram 5: Alternative Assessment Methods Comparison
- **Type**: Flowchart (comparison matrix)
- **Elements**: 5 assessment tools rated across 6 barrier dimensions
- **Key Insight**: The Wechsler scales impose the highest barriers. Raven's and eye-tracking are the most accessible. No current test fully solves the problem for individuals with severe motor difficulties.
- **Accessibility**: Wechsler: high barriers across all dimensions. Raven's: no language demands, low motor, high autism validity. Leiter-3: no language, gesture-based instructions. TONI-4: no language, low motor. Eye-tracking: no language, no motor, minimal compliance, but emerging and unstandardized.

### Diagram 6: The "Profound Autism" Debate
- **Type**: Flowchart (stakeholder map)
- **Elements**: Support group, opposition group, meta-irony highlight, key tensions
- **Key Insight**: Non-speaking autistic individuals -- the people most affected by the label -- are least represented in the decision-making process
- **Accessibility**: Support: Lancet Commission, parents of high-support-needs children, some researchers and clinicians. Opposition: ASAN, autistic self-advocates, researchers noting lack of predictive validity, clinicians warning label is dehumanizing. Meta-irony: non-speaking individuals least represented. Key tensions: label conflates current behavior with immutable capacity; Zimmerman's trajectory demonstrates the error.

### Diagram 7: Jordyn Zimmerman's Timeline
- **Type**: Timeline (Mermaid timeline)
- **Elements**: 3 sections (Before AAC, Pivot Point, After AAC), 15+ events
- **Key Insight**: Zimmerman's intelligence did not change at age 18. Only her access to a communication method changed. Her case proves assessment methods can be catastrophically wrong.
- **Accessibility**: Before AAC (18 years): autism diagnosis, segregated classrooms, no AAC, no literacy instruction, presumed incompetent. Pivot point (age 18): receives Proloquo2Go, standard technology, independent use. After AAC: bachelor's (Ohio University), master's (Boston College), Board Chair of CommunicationFIRST, Apple Distinguished Educator, presidential appointments, Forbes Accessibility 100 (2025).
