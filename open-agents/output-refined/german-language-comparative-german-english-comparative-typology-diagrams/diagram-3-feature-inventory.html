Feature Inventory: What English Lost / What German Kept

Proto-Germanic features traced through two divergent descendants

Proto-Germanic Ancestor (c. 500 BCE)

SOV base + V2 | 6 cases | 3 genders | Strong/weak adjective declension | Productive ablaut | Separable prefixes | Subjunctive mood | Demonstrative-based articles

German (Retained)
Feature
English (Lost / Changed)
Retained
4 Cases
Nominative, accusative, dative, genitive. Marked on articles, adjectives, pronouns, some nouns. Enables flexible word order.
Case System
Lost
0 Cases (on nouns)
Only vestigial pronoun case (I/me, he/him, who/whom). Possessive 's is a clitic, not a case ending.
Compensation: English fixed word order to rigid SVO to mark subject/object roles
Retained
3 Genders
Masculine (der), feminine (die), neuter (das). Agreement on articles, adjectives, pronouns. Every noun assigned a gender.
Grammatical Gender
Lost (12th-13th c.)
0 Genders
No grammatical gender. "The" for all nouns. Natural gender only in 3rd person pronouns (he/she/it).
Retained
V2 + SOV
Strict V2 in main clauses. SOV in subordinate clauses. Sentence bracket (Satzklammer). Any constituent can fill position 1.
V2 Word Order
Lost (13th-15th c.)
Rigid SVO Only
SVO in all clause types. Limited inversion after negatives ("Never have I..."). Lost productive V2 by ~1400-1550.
Compensation: English developed do-support for questions ("Do you...?") and negation ("I don't...")
Retained + Elaborated
48-Slot Adjective System
3 paradigms (strong/weak/mixed) x 4 cases x 3 genders + plural. ~16 distinct endings. Choice depends on preceding determiner.
Adjective Declension
Lost
Zero Inflection
"Big" is "big" in all contexts. No change for gender, number, case, or definiteness. One surviving trace: blond/blonde.
Retained (semi-productive)
~170 Strong Verbs
7 ablaut classes. Compounds retain ablaut: anfangen/fing an. System entrenched even if new verbs are weak (gegoogelt).
Strong Verb Ablaut
Frozen
~200 Irregular Verbs
sing/sang/sung survives but class is closed. Every new verb since ~15th c. is regular: googled, texted, tweeted. Children regularize: "I runned."
Highly Productive
Separable Prefix Verbs
auf-, an-, ab-, aus-, ein-, vor-, zu-. Prefix separates in main clause, rejoins in subordinate. New compounds coined freely.
Verb + Particle
Less Systematic
Phrasal Verbs
look up, turn on, give in. Same Germanic phenomenon but more idiomatic, less predictable. Particle is separate word with freer positioning.
Partially Productive
Konjunktiv I + II
Konjunktiv I for indirect speech (er habe). Konjunktiv II for counterfactuals (er hatte, ich wurde). Still used in formal/written German.
Subjunctive Mood
Vestigial
Traces Only
"If I were...", "I suggest he go", "God save the Queen." Replaced by modals (would, could, might).
Elaborated
16 Article Forms
der/die/das x 4 cases x 2 numbers = 6 distinct forms (der, die, das, dem, den, des). Primary vehicle for case/gender marking.
Article System
Stripped
2 Article Forms
"the" (invariable), "a/an" (phonologically conditioned). No case, gender, or number encoded. Minimal system.
English Innovations (No German Equivalent)
Not Needed
Simple Inversion
Questions by V2 inversion: "Sprichst du Deutsch?" Negation with nicht: "Ich spreche nicht Deutsch."
Do-Support
Gained (15th-17th c.)
Periphrastic "do"
"Do you speak German?" / "I don't speak German." Unique among Germanic languages. Compensates for V2 loss.
Absent (regional variant)
"Ich lese" = both
No standard progressive. "Ich lese" means "I read" and "I am reading." Rheinische Verlaufsform (am Lesen) is regional, informal.
Progressive Aspect
Gained
Full Progressive System
"I am reading" vs. "I read." Productive across all tenses and aspects. Genuine English innovation filling a grammatical slot German leaves empty.
German: Retained from Proto-Germanic
English: Lost from Proto-Germanic
English: Gained through innovation
Compensatory strategy

The pattern: German preserved the Proto-Germanic architecture (case, gender, V2, declension). English shed it under contact pressure and developed compensatory innovations (rigid SVO, do-support, progressive). Both systems are equally complex -- the complexity is just distributed differently.
