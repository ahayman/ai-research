English Path (Innovative)
5th century
Anglo-Saxon Settlement of Britain
Angles, Saxons, Jutes bring West Germanic to Britain. Old English emerges with full Proto-Germanic morphology.
5 cases 3 genders V2 order Adjective declension
8th - 11th century
Viking Contact (Danelaw)
Old Norse speakers settle northern/eastern England. Mutual intelligibility with shared roots but different endings leads to inflection erosion as accommodation strategy.
Inflection erosion begins Gender weakening (north)
1066
Norman Conquest
French becomes language of court, law, church for ~300 years. ~10,000 French words enter English. Germanic/Latinate register split created. English survives as vernacular, loses institutional support for formal norms.
French vocabulary layer Register stratification Accelerated simplification
12th - 15th century
Middle English Transformation
Grammatical gender collapses (12th-13th c.). Case system disintegrates. V2 becomes minority pattern by 1400. Word order rigidifies to SVO.
Gender: 3 -> 0 Cases: 5 -> 0 V2 order lost Adjective declension lost Rigid SVO emerges
15th - 17th century
Early Modern English Innovations
Do-support develops for questions and negation. Progressive aspect (I am reading) becomes productive. Strong verbs freeze as closed class.
Do-support Progressive aspect Productive ablaut frozen

Modern English

Weakly synthetic / analytic | Rigid SVO | 0 cases | 0 gender | Invariable adjectives | Hybrid vocabulary (Germanic + French + Latin) | Do-support | Progressive aspect
German Path (Conservative)
6th - 8th century
High German Consonant Shift
Southern dialects shift voiceless stops: p->pf/f, t->z/ss, k->ch. Creates systematic sound correspondences with other Germanic languages. Morphology preserved intact.
p -> pf/f t -> z/ss k -> ch All morphology retained
8th - 11th century
Old High German Period
Full morphological system preserved: case, gender, V2, adjective declension, productive ablaut. No major foreign conquest disrupts the system.
6 -> 4 cases (gradual) 3 genders V2 + SOV
11th - 14th century
Middle High German
Minor simplifications: instrumental case absorbed into dative, some vowel changes (umlaut becomes phonemic). Core morphological architecture stable.
4 cases stable 3 genders stable Umlaut becomes phonemic
1534
Luther's Bible Translation
Martin Luther's Bible becomes a standardizing force for written German. Establishes East Central German as the basis for the emerging standard language.
Standardization begins Morphology codified
17th - 19th century
Modern Standard German Emerges
Grammatical codification. Adjective declension system (strong/weak/mixed) formalized. Compound productivity continues. Native word-coining preferred over borrowing.
Adjective 3 paradigms Productive compounding Native vocabulary strategy
