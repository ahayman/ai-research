Britton 2021 vs Goldberg 2022 — Different Studies, Different Methods, Different Figures

Two separate measured-rate studies of meditation-related adverse effects (MRAE). Earlier summaries sometimes blended the figures. The honest reading cites each study's numbers separately and notes that both exceed the field's prior 20-25% expectation threshold.

Britton, Lindahl, Cooper, Canby, Palitsky (2021)

PMC8845498 · Clinical Psychological Science, 9(6), 1185-1204
Defined the MRAES-MBP measurement framework. Sample drawn from MBCT (Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy) trials. N≈96. Sensitive instrument capturing mild and transient experiences as well as severe ones — the high "any side effect" rate reflects the breadth of the screening, not just severe events.
83% reported any meditation-related side effect (MRSE)
58% reported negatively-valenced effects
37% reported negative impact on functioning
14.1% reported effects lasting longer than one day
6.4% reported effects lasting longer than one month

Goldberg, Lam, Britton, Davidson (2022)

PMC8636531 / PubMed 34074221 · Psychotherapy Research, 32(3), 291-305
Population-based US sample with lifetime meditation exposure. N=434. Different methodology from Britton 2021: a population-based survey of people with any prior meditation experience, not a clinical trial sample. The two studies are not comparable head-to-head — they are sampling different populations with different instruments.
32.3% general-item MRAE rate (single screening item)
50.0% one-or-more-specific-item rate (across detailed checklist)
10.4% MRAE duration ≥ 1 month
1.2% functional impairment lasting ≥ 1 month
Most common adverse-effect types: anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity.
Documented risk factor: childhood adversity.
Field expectation: roughly 20-25%. Both Britton and Goldberg's measured rates exceed the prior threshold the field had treated as the upper bound. Earlier summaries that anchored on "20-25%" understated the measured prevalence. The honest reading: the order-of-magnitude is higher than the field expected.
The honest composite. Across the two studies and varying definitions, between roughly a third and half of meditators in measured samples report some meaningful adverse effect. Roughly 6-15 per cent (depending on study and threshold) report effects lasting longer than a day to a month. Roughly 1 per cent report functional impairment lasting longer than a month. Anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity are among the most common types. Childhood adversity is a documented risk factor. This is a Tier 1 finding in the three-tier evidence framework.

Cite Britton's figures and Goldberg's figures separately. Do not conflate. The two are different studies with different methodologies.
