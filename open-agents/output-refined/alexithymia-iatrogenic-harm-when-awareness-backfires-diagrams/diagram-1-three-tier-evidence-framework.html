Three-Tier Evidence Framework

The harm literature for body-focused / interoception-based interventions sits across three evidence tiers. The category labels every major claim explicitly so you can weight conclusions by what the data actually supports.

Tier 1

Well-established

Direct empirical evidence from measured samples. Replicated. Uncontested at the order-of-magnitude level.

  • Meditation-related adverse effects are common in measured samples; rates exceed the field's prior 20-25% expectation threshold. Britton 2021 PMC8845498; Goldberg 2022 PMC8636531
  • Anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity are among the most common adverse-effect types. Goldberg 2022 PMC8636531
  • Childhood adversity is a documented risk factor for meditation-related adverse effects. Goldberg 2022 PMC8636531
  • The 32 expert teachers in the Varieties of Contemplative Experience study reported 56 of 59 categories of meditation-related challenges (95%; avg 8.5 per expert). Lindahl, Britton et al. 2017 PMC5443484
  • Treleaven's 36 specific modifications for trauma-survivor practitioners are established practical guidance for trauma populations. Treleaven & Britton 2018, W.W. Norton
  • Dissociative-subtype PTSD requires specific protocol modifications when integrating mindfulness. Lanius et al. 2024 PMC11257171
  • High-alexithymia patients show attenuated and delayed response to standard prolonged exposure for PTSD. Pugach et al. PubMed 37917810
Tier 2

Mechanistically supported — theoretical extrapolation with adjacent evidence

Mechanism is well-attested in adjacent literatures. The mechanism is plausibly at work in the alexithymia case, but the alexithymia-stratified data does not exist to test it directly.

  • Body-attention focus amplifies symptom intensity in somatic-symptom-disorder populations (somatosensory amplification mechanism). Barsky framework; A Neural Circuit Framework for SSA, Psychiatryonline
  • Mindfulness can deepen depersonalisation in already-dissociating individuals. Lindahl PMC9043097
  • Both very-low and very-high interoceptive accuracy can co-occur with high alexithymia (the parsing-deficit pole). Aaron, Blain, Snodgress, Park 2020 PMC7076086 — pilot only
  • The parsing-deficit alexithymia profile is plausibly at higher risk for body-focused interventions on mechanistic grounds (loud signals + absent labelling architecture). Synthesis from Garfinkel 2015 + Aaron 2020 + SSA framework
  • Hypervigilance-with-mislabelling profiles are plausibly at higher risk on the same mechanistic grounds. Synthesis from SSA framework + somatic-symptom-disorder literature
  • The somatization pathway from trauma to body-symptom expression in alexithymic populations is theoretically well-supported. Sifneos / Nemiah / Taylor / Bagby psychosomatic frame
Tier 3

Theoretical only — direct evidence absent

Synthesis of Tier 1 + Tier 2 leads to a specific subtype-stratified prediction, but no published RCT has tested it directly. The category honours this absence rather than papering over it.

  • The specific claim that interoceptive-awareness training is risky for parsing-deficit alexithymics specifically — no RCT has tested this in subtype-stratified samples. Norman et al. 2019 PMC10270453 explicitly notes absence of adverse-event data from the four trials reviewed
  • Subtype-stratified prediction of which alexithymia profile will be harmed by which protocol — extrapolated from Tier 1 + Tier 2, not directly tested. Synthesis only
  • Whether alexithymics specifically have higher dropout from MBSR or interoceptive training — no identified RCT explicitly reports this. Search across MBSR / MBCT / MABT / interoceptive-training literatures yields no subtype-stratified dropout-rate analyses
  • Whether body-focused mindfulness specifically harms parsing-deficit alexithymics versus detection-deficit alexithymics — not tested. Aaron 2020 quadratic relationship is suggestive but pilot only
Why this framework matters. The harm literature is real (Tier 1). The mechanism case for parsing-deficit alexithymia caution is plausible (Tier 2). But the specific prediction about which alexithymia profiles get harmed by which protocols sits in Tier 3 — a working hypothesis with mechanism support, not an established empirical fact. Treating Tier 3 claims with Tier 1 confidence is overclaiming. Treating Tier 1 findings with Tier 3 skepticism is underclaiming. The category labels each claim's tier explicitly so the user can decide how heavily to weight it.
