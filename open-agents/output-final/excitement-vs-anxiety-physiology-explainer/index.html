Physiological Differences Between Excitement and Anxiety: How Your Body Tells Them Apart

#Overview

Excitement and anxiety are both high-arousal emotional states. They share the same foundation: the sympathetic nervous systemThe branch of the autonomic nervous system that mobilizes the body for action. Produces the "fight-or-flight" response. Active in both excitement and anxiety. activates, the heart beats faster, breathing quickens, and adrenaline enters the bloodstream. Because their starting points overlap so heavily, distinguishing between them is a genuine physiological challenge -- not a failure of attention or effort. Research by Alison Wood Brooks (2014) demonstrated that the two states are so physiologically similar that people can reappraise anxiety as excitement more easily than they can calm down from anxiety.

For individuals with alexithymiaA trait characterized by difficulty identifying, describing, and distinguishing one's own emotions. Associated with reduced interoceptive accuracy. Exists on a spectrum. -- a trait involving difficulty identifying and describing one's own emotions -- this overlap creates a specific problem. Alexithymia is associated with reduced interoceptive accuracyThe objectively measured ability to detect internal body signals, such as counting heartbeats without taking a pulse., meaning the brain's system for reading internal body signals operates with less precision (Murphy et al., 2017). When the body sends signals that could mean either "this is exciting" or "this is dangerous," a person with alexithymia may receive only a blurred message: "something intense is happening." Research by Shah et al. (2016) found that interoceptive impairment, rather than autism itself, predicts difficulty with emotional identification.

This document provides a systematic, evidence-based guide to the measurable physiological differences between excitement and anxiety. It covers where the two states diverge in the autonomic nervous system, in neurochemistry, and in body sensation patterns. It then translates those differences into a practical body-checking approach designed for people who find it difficult to identify emotions through introspection alone.

What this document covers: A detailed breakdown of the physical differences between excitement and anxiety, grounded in peer-reviewed research, with practical strategies for distinguishing between the two states using body-based signals.

Why it matters for alexithymia: When the usual path to emotion identification -- "just notice how you feel" -- does not work reliably, an alternative path is needed. That alternative path runs through specific, concrete body signals. This document maps those signals.

Key takeaways:
  • Excitement and anxiety begin with the same sympathetic nervous system activation but diverge in measurable ways within seconds
  • The primary physiological differentiators are vagal toneA measure of the activity of the vagus nerve, reflecting parasympathetic nervous system function. #Prerequisites

Before reading this explanation, you should understand:

  • Emotions have physical components. Every emotion produces changes in the body -- heart rate, breathing, muscle tension, temperature, digestion. These physical changes are not side effects of emotions; they are part of how the brain constructs emotional experience.
  • The nervous system has two main branches. The sympathetic nervous system (SNS) speeds the body up for action. The parasympathetic nervous systemThe branch of the autonomic nervous system that promotes rest, recovery, and digestion. Slows heart rate and supports calm states. (PNS) slows the body down for rest and recovery. Both are always active; the balance between them shifts depending on the situation.
  • InteroceptionThe sense of the internal state of the body. Includes awareness of heartbeat, breathing, hunger, temperature, pain, and muscle tension. is the sense of your body's internal state. Just as vision detects light and hearing detects sound, interoception detects signals from inside the body -- heartbeat, breathing, gut activity, muscle tension, temperature. Interoceptive accuracy varies between individuals.

#The Shared Starting Point

Both excitement and anxiety begin with a trigger: an upcoming event, a social situation, a new opportunity, a piece of news. The brain's amygdala performs a rapid assessment of the stimulus. Within milliseconds, the sympathetic nervous system activates.

This initial activation is nearly identical for both states. The body produces epinephrine (adrenaline) and norepinephrine. Heart rate increases. Breathing quickens. Blood pressure rises. Alertness sharpens. At this stage, the body is mobilizing for action, but it has not yet committed to a direction -- approach or avoidance.

This shared starting point is the core reason excitement and anxiety feel similar. The physiological overlap is not a flaw in perception. It is a real feature of how the nervous system works. Schachter and Singer (1962) demonstrated in their foundational research that the same state of physiological arousalThe level of physiological activation in the body. Both excitement and anxiety are high-arousal states. can be experienced as different emotions depending on context and cognitive appraisalThe mental process of evaluating the meaning of a situation, which influences which emotion is experienced from a given state of arousal..

The divergence happens in what comes next: how the autonomic nervous system patterns itself, which neurochemicals dominate the response, and where in the body the sensations concentrate.

#Where They Diverge: The Autonomic Nervous System

The autonomic nervous system (ANS) is the body's automatic control system. It regulates heart rate, breathing, digestion, and other functions without conscious effort. The ANS has two main branches: the sympathetic (activating) and the parasympathetic (calming).

#Polyvagal Theory

Stephen Porges' Polyvagal TheoryStephen Porges' theory proposing three hierarchical autonomic states (ventral vagal, sympathetic, dorsal vagal) that underlie different emotional and behavioral responses. (2011) proposes a more detailed model with three states, based on the vagus nerve -- the longest nerve in the body, running from the brainstem to the gut:

  1. Ventral vagal stateThe most recently evolved autonomic state in Polyvagal Theory. Associated with safety, social engagement, and flexible positive arousal. (social engagement/safety). The newest evolutionary system. Active when a person feels safe and socially connected. Supports calm, flexible arousal. This state involves the myelinated (insulated) branch of the vagus nerve.
  2. Sympathetic state (fight/flight). Active when the body detects a threat requiring action. Heart rate increases, muscles tense, breathing accelerates. This is the classic anxiety response.
  3. Dorsal vagal stateThe oldest autonomic state in Polyvagal Theory. Associated with shutdown, collapse, and dissociation in response to extreme threat. (shutdown/freeze). The oldest evolutionary system. Active during extreme or inescapable threat. Heart rate drops, energy collapses, dissociation may occur. This involves the unmyelinated branch of the vagus nerve.

The key insight for distinguishing excitement from anxiety lies in mixed states. Excitement involves sympathetic activation combined with maintained ventral vagal engagement -- the body is energized but still operating within a framework of safety. Porges describes this as the "play" state. Anxiety involves sympathetic activation with ventral vagal withdrawal -- the body is energized and the safety system has disengaged.

#Measurable Differences

Three autonomic markers distinguish excitement from anxiety:

Heart rate variability (HRV)The variation in time intervals between heartbeats. Higher HRV indicates greater autonomic flexibility. Tends to be higher in excitement than anxiety.. HRV measures the variation in time between heartbeats. Higher HRV indicates that the parasympathetic system is actively modulating the heart, reflecting autonomic flexibility. In excitement, HRV tends to remain moderate or high because vagal tone is preserved. In anxiety, HRV drops because vagal tone withdraws (Behnke et al., 2022).

Respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA)The natural variation in heart rate that occurs with breathing. A marker of parasympathetic function. Preserved in excitement, reduced in anxiety.. RSA is the natural speeding and slowing of heart rate that occurs with each breath -- the heart speeds up slightly during inhalation and slows during exhalation. RSA is a specific marker of parasympathetic function. In excitement, RSA is preserved. In anxiety, RSA is reduced, reflecting diminished vagal influence on the heart (Kreibig, 2010).

Skin conductance. Both states increase skin conductance (sweat gland activity), but anxiety produces a sharper, more sustained increase compared to excitement's more moderate rise (Kreibig, 2010).

#The Neurochemical Split

Within seconds of the initial arousal, the brain's neurochemical response begins to diverge. This divergence is one of the most reliable biological markers separating excitement from anxiety.

#The Anxiety Pathway: Cortisol and the HPA Axis

When the brain appraises a situation as threatening, the amygdala signals the hypothalamus, which activates the HPA axisThe hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis. A neuroendocrine system that produces cortisol in response to perceived threat or stress. (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis). This is the body's stress response system. The sequence runs as follows:

  1. The hypothalamus releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH)
  2. CRH triggers the pituitary gland to release adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH)
  3. ACTH signals the adrenal glands to produce cortisol

Cortisol is a glucocorticoid hormone that sustains the stress response over minutes to hours. It increases blood sugar, suppresses immune function, and maintains vigilance. Elevated cortisol is a hallmark of anxiety. It produces the grinding, sustained quality that distinguishes anxiety from brief alarm -- the feeling that does not resolve even after the initial trigger passes.

#The Excitement Pathway: Dopamine and the Reward System

When the brain appraises a situation as rewarding, the ventral tegmental area (VTA) releases dopamine into the nucleus accumbens and prefrontal cortex. This is the brain's reward and motivation circuit. Dopamine produces approach motivation -- the drive to move toward something, engage with it, and explore it. The norepinephrine released during excitement operates within a dopaminergic context, supporting focused energy rather than anxious vigilance.

Critically, positive excitement produces minimal or no cortisol elevation. The HPA axis does not significantly activate. This means the sustained, depleting quality of anxiety is absent.

#What This Means in the Body

The cortisol/dopamine divergence produces detectably different physical experiences over time:

#Where You Feel It: Body Sensation Maps

Research by Nummenmaa et al. (2014) asked over 700 participants across cultures to mark on body silhouettes where they felt activation or deactivation during different emotions. The results showed statistically separable body sensation maps for different emotions, published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences.

While the study examined broad categories (happiness, fear, anger, sadness, surprise, disgust, anxiety) rather than "excitement" specifically, the patterns for happiness/positive states versus anxiety/fear provide a useful framework. Research on positive emotions generally aligns with the happiness pattern (Behnke et al., 2022).

#Excitement Sensation Pattern

  • Chest: Warmth, openness, a sense of expansion. The chest may feel "full" in a pleasant way.
  • Limbs: Energy, tingling, warmth. Arms and legs feel ready to move, not restrained.
  • Hands: Warm. Blood flow to extremities is maintained because the body is not in defensive mode.
  • Face: Warmth, flushing. Facial muscles may be active (tendency toward smiling).
  • Stomach: "Butterflies" may be present but with a quality of fluttering openness rather than constriction.
  • Breathing: May be faster than baseline but remains relatively deep. Breaths reach the lower lungs.

#Anxiety Sensation Pattern

  • Chest: Tightness, constriction, pressure. The chest may feel "clamped" or compressed.
  • Throat: Tightness, lump-like sensation. Swallowing may feel difficult.
  • Shoulders and neck: Tension, elevated shoulders, stiffness. Muscles are bracing.
  • Stomach: Churning, nausea, "knots." The sensation has a twisting or clenching quality.
  • Hands: Cold, clammy. Blood flow diverts from extremities to core muscles (part of the fight/flight response).
  • Breathing: Shallow, rapid, and restricted to the upper chest. Breaths do not reach the lower lungs.
  • Jaw: Clenching, teeth grinding, tension.

Important Limitations

Individual variation exists. These patterns represent statistical tendencies across populations, not universal rules. Autistic individuals may have atypical body sensation patterns due to differences in sensory processing (DuBois et al., 2024). Use these maps as a starting framework and refine based on your own observation over time.

#Interoception: Your Body's Reporting System

Interoception is the sensory system that detects and processes signals from inside the body. It includes awareness of heartbeat, breathing, hunger, thirst, temperature, pain, muscle tension, and gut activity. The insular cortexA brain region that is the primary hub for interoceptive processing, integrating body signals with emotional awareness. (insula), a brain region located deep within the lateral sulcus, is the primary hub for processing these signals (Quadt et al., 2018).

Interoception operates in three measurable dimensions (Garfinkel et al., 2016):

  1. Interoceptive accuracy: The objective ability to detect body signals. Measured through tasks like heartbeat counting (can you accurately count your own heartbeats without taking your pulse?).
  2. Interoceptive sensibility: How much you believe you are aware of body signals. This is your self-reported confidence in your body awareness.
  3. Interoceptive awareness: The correspondence between your accuracy and your confidence. High awareness means your confidence matches your actual ability.

The interoceptive processing pipeline works as follows: body organs generate signals (heart beating, gut contracting, muscles tensing). These signals travel through the vagus nerve and spinal cord to the brainstem. The brainstem routes them to the insular cortex. The insula integrates these signals with contextual information from memory and the environment. The result is a conscious perception of body state, which the brain uses to construct an emotional label.

This pipeline -- from raw body signal to emotional label -- is where the process of distinguishing excitement from anxiety occurs. And it is where alexithymia creates difficulty.

#When the Signal Gets Confused: Alexithymia and Autism

#Alexithymia as an Interoceptive Deficit

Murphy et al. (2017) presented evidence that alexithymia represents a general failure of interoception, not just an emotional processing problem. In their research, individuals with high alexithymia showed reduced accuracy on both emotional and non-emotional interoceptive tasks. This means the difficulty is not limited to "reading emotions" -- it extends to reading any internal body signal.

Alexithymia is not a binary condition. It exists on a spectrum, measured by tools such as the Toronto Alexithymia Scale (TAS-20). Approximately 50% of autistic individuals score in the clinical range for alexithymia, compared to roughly 10% of the general population (Poquérusse et al., 2018).

#The Autism-Alexithymia Distinction

An important finding from Shah et al. (2016) is that alexithymia, not autism itself, predicts interoceptive impairment. Autistic individuals without alexithymia can have typical interoceptive accuracy. This means the difficulty with emotion identification is driven by the alexithymia trait rather than by autism per se.

However, autism does contribute additional challenges. Garfinkel et al. (2016) found discrepancies between dimensions of interoception in autism -- some autistic individuals have low accuracy but high sensibility (they are confident about body signals that they are not accurately detecting), or high accuracy but low sensibility (they detect signals accurately but do not consciously register them). These mismatches create further confusion.

DuBois et al. (2024) reviewed interoception research in autism and noted that sensory processing differences in autism can alter how body signals are experienced, adding another layer of complexity.

#Why Excitement Gets Misread as Anxiety

Several factors converge to create a bias toward labeling ambiguous arousal as anxiety:

  1. Signal blur. Reduced interoceptive accuracy means the subtle differences between excitement and anxiety body patterns (warm hands vs. cold hands, deep breathing vs. shallow breathing) may not be clearly detected.
  2. Negativity bias. When an arousal signal is ambiguous, the brain's threat-detection systems tend to default to a negative interpretation. This is an evolutionary safety feature -- it is less costly to falsely label excitement as danger than to falsely label danger as excitement.
  3. Learned associations. If past experiences of high arousal have frequently led to negative outcomes (social difficulties, sensory overload, burnout), the brain may have learned to associate any high arousal with threat.
  4. Alexithymia's low emotional granularityThe ability to make fine-grained distinctions between similar emotional states. Lower granularity is associated with alexithymia.. With reduced emotional granularity (the ability to make fine distinctions between similar emotions), the brain may have only broad categories available: "calm" or "activated." When only these broad categories exist, "activated" easily defaults to "anxious."

#A Practical Guide to Body Signal Checking

Because introspective emotion labeling ("What am I feeling?") may not work reliably with alexithymia, an alternative approach uses systematic body signal checking. Instead of trying to identify the emotion directly, you check specific physical markers and use the pattern of results to determine which state is more likely.

#The Body Check Sequence

Work through these checks in order. Each check produces a data point. The overall pattern across all checks is more informative than any single check.

1. Breathing pattern.

Place one hand on your upper chest and one on your lower abdomen. Notice where the movement is.

Excitement signal: Breath moves your lower hand. Breathing may be faster than normal but reaches the belly.

Anxiety signal: Breath moves only your upper hand. Breathing is shallow and rapid, confined to the upper chest.

2. Muscle state.

Scan your shoulders, jaw, and hands. Notice whether they are tense or loose.

Excitement signal: Muscles feel energized and ready, like they want to move. Shoulders are down. Jaw is unclenched.

Anxiety signal: Muscles are tight and bracing. Shoulders are elevated toward ears. Jaw is clenched. Hands may be fisted.

3. Hand temperature.

Notice whether your hands feel warm or cold. Touch the back of one hand to your cheek for comparison.

Excitement signal: Hands are warm. Blood flow to extremities is maintained.

Anxiety signal: Hands are cold or clammy. Blood flow has diverted to core muscles.

4. Stomach quality.

Focus on the sensations in your gut area. Try to determine the specific quality.

Excitement signal: Fluttery, open, rising sensation. Like bubbles or gentle movement.

Anxiety signal: Churning, knotting, sinking sensation. Like twisting or clenching. Nausea may be present.

5. Chest sensation.

Notice the feeling in your chest cavity -- the area behind your sternum.

Excitement signal: Warmth, openness, expansion. The chest feels like it is opening outward.

Anxiety signal: Tightness, pressure, constriction. The chest feels compressed or clamped.

6. Behavioral impulse.

Notice the direction your body wants to move. Do not analyze the thought -- notice the physical pull.

Excitement signal: Approach. The body leans forward, toward the stimulus. There is a "want to get to it" quality.

Anxiety signal: Avoidance. The body pulls back, away from the stimulus. There is a "want to get away" quality.

7. Time course check (if possible).

Wait 15 to 30 minutes and check again.

Excitement signal: Energy sustains or builds. You feel more alive, not more depleted.

Anxiety signal: Energy drains. Fatigue sets in. The cortisol-driven response is depleting over time.

Interpreting Results

If most checks point in the same direction, the identification is relatively straightforward. If results are mixed, you may be experiencing a mixed state -- both excitement and anxiety simultaneously. This is physiologically possible and normal. Starting a new job, for example, commonly produces both. In mixed states, acknowledging both emotions rather than forcing a single label is the most accurate response.

#The Reappraisal Strategy

Alison Wood Brooks (2014) conducted a series of experiments at Harvard Business School and found a counterintuitive result: when people feel anxious before a performance, telling themselves "I am excited" works better than telling themselves "I am calm."

#Why It Works: Arousal Congruence

The principle of arousal congruenceThe principle that emotional states sharing similar arousal levels are easier to shift between than states with different arousal levels. explains this finding. Excitement and anxiety are both high-arousal states. Calmness is a low-arousal state. Shifting from high arousal to low arousal requires changing both the cognitive label (from negative to neutral) and the physiological state (from activated to deactivated). This is a large shift and the body resists it.

Shifting from anxiety to excitement requires changing only the cognitive label -- from negative valenceThe positive or negative quality of an emotional experience. Excitement has positive valence; anxiety has negative valence. to positive valence. The arousal level stays the same. This is a smaller shift, and research shows it is more achievable.

#How to Apply Reappraisal

  1. Notice that you are in a high-arousal state.
  2. Run the body check sequence to gather data.
  3. If the context supports either interpretation (the situation could be exciting or threatening), explicitly state to yourself: "I am excited about this."
  4. This is not self-deception. It is selecting between two equally valid interpretations of the same physiological state.
  5. Brooks (2014) found that participants who reappraised anxiety as excitement performed better on math tasks, public speaking, and karaoke singing than those who tried to calm down.

Limitations

ReappraisalThe cognitive strategy of re-interpreting the meaning of a physiological state. In this context, reinterpreting anxiety arousal as excitement. works best when the situation genuinely has potential for positive outcomes. If the body is responding to a real threat, reappraising anxiety as excitement is not appropriate. The body check sequence helps distinguish between contexts where reappraisal is useful (the arousal might be excitement) and contexts where the anxiety signal should be heeded (the arousal is clearly defensive).

#Building Interoceptive Skills Over Time

Interoceptive accuracy is not entirely fixed. Research suggests it can be improved through structured practice, though the evidence base is still developing.

Mahler's Interoception Curriculum

Kelly Mahler (2016) developed a structured curriculum for building interoceptive awareness, specifically designed for neurodivergent individuals. The five-step framework:

  1. Notice: Direct attention to a specific body area without trying to interpret the signal. "What is happening in my stomach right now?"
  2. Name: Use concrete, descriptive language for the sensation. Not "I feel bad" but "my stomach feels tight and my hands are cold."
  3. Link: Connect body sensations to contexts and outcomes over time. "When my chest feels warm and open, good things tend to follow."
  4. Understand: Build personal patterns of which body sensations correspond to which emotional states.
  5. Manage: Use the accumulated knowledge to make informed decisions about how to respond.

Body Scanning Practice

Regular body scanning -- systematically moving attention through each body region and noting sensations -- builds the neural pathways involved in interoception. Starting with 2 to 5 minutes per day and focusing on nonjudgmental observation (describing what is present, not evaluating whether it is good or bad) is a common recommended approach.

External Cue Strategies

When internal signals are unreliable, external cues can supplement body-based information:

  • Wearable devices. Heart rate monitors and HRV trackers provide objective data about autonomic state. Comparing the device reading with your subjective experience helps calibrate interoceptive accuracy over time.
  • Context analysis. Systematically evaluating the situation ("Is this objectively threatening, or could it be an opportunity?") provides a second data source alongside body signals.
  • Trusted person check. Asking someone you trust, "Does this situation seem dangerous or exciting?" can provide external validation when internal signals are ambiguous.

#Common Misconceptions

Misconception: "Excitement and anxiety feel completely different and should be easy to tell apart"

Reality: Research shows they share the same foundational sympathetic nervous system activation -- increased heart rate, adrenaline, and heightened alertness. Brooks (2014) demonstrated they are so physiologically similar that reappraising one as the other is easier than trying to calm down. The differences exist in secondary markers (cortisol, vagal tone, muscle tension patterns) that require interoceptive skill to detect.

Misconception: "If you cannot tell what you are feeling, you are not paying enough attention"

Reality: Alexithymia involves a measurable deficit in interoceptive processing, not a lack of effort. Murphy et al. (2017) showed that alexithymia represents a general failure of interoception affecting both emotional and non-emotional body signals. Telling someone with alexithymia to "just pay more attention" is comparable to telling someone with reduced visual acuity to "just look harder."

Misconception: "Autistic people do not feel emotions as strongly"

Reality: Research consistently shows autistic individuals feel emotions with equal or greater intensity. The difficulty lies in identifying, differentiating, and describing the emotions -- not in experiencing them. Shah et al. (2016) found that interoceptive impairment, not autism per se, predicts emotional identification difficulty.

Misconception: "The 'butterflies in your stomach' feeling is always anxiety"

Reality: Both excitement and anxiety produce gastrointestinal sensations through the gut-brain axis. The qualitative difference lies in the nature of the sensation: excitement tends to produce fluttery, open sensations while anxiety produces churning, constricting sensations. Individual variation exists.

Misconception: "You should try to calm down when feeling anxious"

Reality: Brooks (2014) found that reappraising anxiety as excitement is more effective than trying to calm down because it is arousal congruent. It requires only a shift in valence (negative to positive) rather than both a shift in valence and a shift in arousal level (high to low).

#Summary

Key Points

  1. Excitement and anxiety share the same sympathetic nervous system activation. The initial arousal response -- faster heart rate, increased adrenaline, heightened alertness -- is nearly identical for both states.
  2. The states diverge in three measurable ways: vagal tone (maintained in excitement, withdrawn in anxiety), neurochemistry (dopamine-dominant vs. cortisol-dominant), and body sensation patterns (warmth and openness vs. tension and constriction).
  3. Alexithymia reduces interoceptive accuracy, making it harder to detect the subtle physical differences between the two states. This is a neurological difference, not a failure of effort.
  4. A systematic body-checking approach -- examining breathing pattern, muscle state, hand temperature, stomach quality, chest sensation, behavioral impulse, and time course -- provides more reliable data than trying to "identify the feeling" through introspection alone.
  5. Reappraising anxiety as excitement (Brooks, 2014) is an evidence-based strategy that works because both states share the same arousal level, requiring only a shift in valence.
  6. Interoceptive skills can be developed over time through structured practice, including Mahler's Interoception Curriculum, body scanning, and external cue strategies.
