Overview & Motivation

Speaker diarization is the task of determining "who spoke when" in an audio recording containing multiple speakers. Traditional approaches decompose this into a pipeline of separately optimized modules: voice activity detection (VAD), speaker embedding extraction, and clustering. While functional, this pipeline has a fundamental flaw — it cannot natively handle overlapping speech, a common phenomenon in natural conversation where multiple people speak simultaneously.

Core Insight

EEND reformulates speaker diarization as a multi-label classification problem. For each time frame, the network outputs independent speech activity probabilities for each speaker. When two speakers overlap, both outputs are active simultaneously — something the traditional "one label per frame" clustering approach fundamentally cannot represent.

End-to-End Neural Diarization (EEND) was introduced by Fujita et al. at Interspeech 2019. The key innovation was training a single neural network end-to-end with a permutation-free objective function, enabling the model to directly minimize diarization errors rather than optimizing each pipeline component in isolation. This approach proved especially powerful for conversational speech with frequent speaker overlaps.

What "End-to-End" Means Here

Unlike the traditional pipeline where VAD, feature extraction, embedding, and clustering are separate modules, EEND uses a single neural network that takes acoustic features as input and directly outputs per-frame, per-speaker activity labels. There are no intermediate hand-designed representations or separately trained components. The entire system is optimized with a single loss function that directly measures diarization accuracy.

Traditional Pipeline vs. EEND

To understand why EEND represents a paradigm shift, it helps to contrast the two approaches visually. The traditional pipeline breaks diarization into sequential steps, each optimized independently. EEND collapses these into a single differentiable network.

Core Architectures

EEND has evolved through several architectural generations, each improving the model's ability to capture speaker-relevant temporal patterns. All share the same fundamental formulation — multi-label classification per frame — but differ in the neural network backbone.

BLSTM-EEND (2019)

The original EEND architecture used a Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BLSTM) network. The input is a sequence of acoustic features (typically log-Mel filterbank features), and the BLSTM processes them in both forward and backward directions to capture temporal context.

Log-Mel Features → Linear Layer → BLSTM Layers (x5) → Linear + Sigmoid → Per-Speaker Posteriors

Each BLSTM layer sees the hidden states from its neighboring frames (previous in the forward pass, next in the backward pass). This provides temporal context but limits the model to only indirect access to distant frames through the recurrent chain. For diarization, where speaker identity must be consistent across an entire recording, this indirect long-range dependency proved to be a limitation.

Historical Note

The BLSTM-EEND was the proof of concept that end-to-end diarization was viable. It demonstrated that a single neural network could learn to jointly detect speech activity and assign speaker labels, outperforming x-vector clustering on recordings with overlapping speech.

SA-EEND: Self-Attention EEND (2019)

Recognizing the limitations of recurrent context, Fujita et al. replaced the BLSTM backbone with Transformer encoder blocks using multi-head self-attention (MHSA). This architecture, called SA-EEND, became the foundation for all subsequent EEND research.

Why Self-Attention Works Better

In speaker diarization, the model must recognize that the voice heard at minute 1 belongs to the same speaker heard at minute 10. Self-attention provides direct pairwise attention between all frames in the sequence — every frame can attend to every other frame in a single layer. This makes it naturally suited for capturing global speaker characteristics across the entire recording, whereas BLSTM must propagate this information hop by hop through the recurrent chain.

The self-attention mechanism computes attention weights between all pairs of frames:

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(Q KT / √dk) V

where Q, K, V are query, key, and value projections of the frame embeddings, and dk is the key dimension. Each attention head can learn to focus on different aspects: some heads capture speaker identity patterns, others capture speech activity dynamics.

Multi-Head Self-Attention in Diarization

Research has shown that different attention heads in SA-EEND specialize for different roles. Some heads develop strong diagonal attention patterns (local context for activity detection), while others develop block-diagonal patterns that correspond to speaker turns (global speaker identity). This emergent specialization is key to why self-attention outperforms recurrence for diarization.

Permutation Invariant Training (PIT)

One of the fundamental challenges in training EEND is the speaker permutation problem. When the model outputs activity probabilities for speaker 1 and speaker 2, there is no inherent reason why output channel 1 should correspond to a specific speaker. The assignment is arbitrary.

The Permutation Problem

Consider a recording with speakers A and B. The model might output:

  • Channel 1 = Speaker A activity, Channel 2 = Speaker B activity
  • Channel 1 = Speaker B activity, Channel 2 = Speaker A activity

Both assignments are equally valid. If the ground truth labels happen to use the first mapping but the model learns the second, a naive loss function would penalize the model even though its predictions are correct — just in a different order. This ambiguity makes standard cross-entropy training impossible.

How PIT Solves This

Permutation Invariant Training (PIT), originally developed for speech separation (Kolbaek et al., 2017), solves this by evaluating the loss under all possible speaker permutations and using only the minimum loss for backpropagation.

LPIT = minπ ∈ Perms(S) ∑t=1T ∑s=1S BCE(ŷt,s, yt,π(s))

where π ranges over all permutations of S speakers, ŷ is the model output, y is the ground truth, and BCE is binary cross-entropy. For each training sample, the permutation that yields the lowest loss is selected, and only that permutation's gradients are used for parameter updates.

Computational Cost of PIT

PIT requires evaluating S! (S factorial) permutations for each training sample. For 2 speakers, this is trivial (2! = 2). For 3 speakers (3! = 6), it is still manageable. But for large numbers of speakers, the factorial explosion makes PIT intractable. This is one reason the original SA-EEND was limited to a fixed, small number of speakers (typically 2-3), and why the EDA mechanism was later developed.

Utterance-Level vs. Frame-Level PIT

EEND uses utterance-level PIT (also called Deep Clustering PIT or DC-PIT), where a single best permutation is found for the entire recording. This is more stable than frame-level PIT used in some speech separation systems, because in diarization, speaker identities should be consistent across the entire recording. The best permutation is computed by summing the frame-level losses across all frames, then selecting the permutation with the minimum total loss.

Encoder-Decoder Attractor (EDA)

The original SA-EEND with PIT requires a fixed number of output channels (one per speaker), determined at training time. This is a serious limitation for real-world recordings where the number of speakers is unknown and variable. The Encoder-Decoder-based Attractor (EDA) module, introduced by Horiguchi et al. (2021), elegantly solves this problem.

How EDA Works

Instead of using a fixed output layer, EDA uses an LSTM-based encoder-decoder to generate speaker-wise attractors in an autoregressive manner. Each attractor is a learned vector that represents one speaker. The number of attractors (and thus the number of detected speakers) is determined dynamically during inference.

Frame Embeddings (from Transformer) → LSTM Encoder → LSTM Decoder (autoregressive) → Attractors a1, a2, ..., aS → Dot Product with Embeddings → Per-Speaker Activities

Attractor Generation

  1. The Transformer encoder produces frame-wise embeddings e1, ..., eT.
  2. These embeddings are passed through the LSTM encoder, producing a context vector.
  3. The LSTM decoder generates attractors one at a time, starting from a zero vector.
  4. At each decoding step, the decoder outputs a candidate attractor and an existence probability.
  5. Generation continues until the existence probability drops below a threshold (e.g., 0.5).

Diarization via Dot Products

Once attractors are generated, diarization results are computed as the dot product between each attractor and each frame embedding, followed by a sigmoid activation:

ŷt,s = σ(as · et)

This is elegant because when two speakers overlap at frame t, the embedding et will have a large dot product with both speakers' attractors, naturally producing high activity probabilities for multiple speakers simultaneously.

Attractor Interpretation

Each attractor can be thought of as a "template" vector representing what a particular speaker sounds like in the embedding space. The dot product measures how well each frame matches each speaker template. This is conceptually similar to speaker embeddings in clustering-based systems, but here the attractors are generated dynamically by the network rather than extracted by a separately trained model.

Transformer-Based Attractors (EEND-TA)

Recent work has replaced the LSTM-based EDA with Transformer-based attractor decoders (EEND-TA). These use cross-attention between learned speaker queries and the frame embeddings, similar to the decoder in DETR (object detection). This improves both convergence speed and final accuracy, achieving state-of-the-art DER of 14.49% on DIHARD III.

Streaming: BW-EDA-EEND

Standard EEND requires the entire recording to be available before processing, which makes it unsuitable for real-time applications like live transcription or video conferencing. Block-Wise EDA-EEND (BW-EDA-EEND), developed at Amazon, adapts EEND for streaming scenarios.

Block-Wise Processing

Instead of processing the entire recording at once, BW-EDA-EEND divides the audio into fixed-size blocks (e.g., 10 seconds) and processes them incrementally. The key challenge is maintaining speaker consistency across blocks without access to future audio.

Incremental Transformer Encoder

The standard Transformer encoder uses bidirectional self-attention (each frame attends to all other frames). BW-EDA-EEND replaces this with a causal/left-context-only Transformer encoder that:

  • Attends only to frames in the current and previous blocks (no future lookahead).
  • Uses block-level recurrence to carry hidden states from one block to the next, propagating speaker identity information across time.
  • Achieves linear time complexity in the length of the recording (vs. quadratic for full self-attention).

Streaming Attractor Update

The EDA module generates attractors for each block using information from the current block and the accumulated hidden states from previous blocks. New speakers can be detected as they appear, and the attractor set grows dynamically. Previous attractors are carried forward and refined with each new block.

Performance Characteristics

For unlimited-latency BW-EDA-EEND (which still processes in linear time), degradation is moderate compared to offline EDA-EEND — especially for 2-speaker recordings with a context size of 10 seconds. For limited-latency (block-by-block) operation, accuracy is comparable to offline clustering-based methods while supporting true real-time processing.

Further Advances: FS-EEND and LS-EEND

More recent streaming variants include:

  • FS-EEND (Frame-wise Streaming EEND, ICASSP 2024): achieves frame-synchronous operation with non-autoregressive attractor extraction, enabling even lower latency.
  • LS-EEND (Long-form Streaming EEND, TASLP 2025): unifies embedding extraction, attractor generation, and diarization into a single causal network with linear complexity, achieving state-of-the-art online DER: 12.11% on CALLHOME, 27.58% on DIHARD II, 19.61% on DIHARD III.

EEND-VC: Vector Clustering Hybrid

Despite its advantages, pure EEND struggles with two practical scenarios: long recordings (more than ~10 minutes) and recordings with a large number of speakers. EEND-VC (End-to-End Neural Diarization with Vector Clustering), introduced by Kinoshita et al. (2021), combines the strengths of both paradigms.

Architecture

EEND-VC operates in two stages:

  1. Local EEND Segmentation: The input recording is split into short, overlapping chunks (e.g., 10-30 seconds). A standard EEND model processes each chunk independently, producing local diarization labels and speaker embeddings for each chunk.
  2. Global Vector Clustering: The speaker embeddings from all chunks are clustered (typically using agglomerative hierarchical clustering or spectral clustering) to resolve cross-chunk speaker identity, stitching the local diarization results into a coherent global output.
Long Recording → Split into Chunks → EEND per Chunk → Local Labels + Embeddings → Vector Clustering → Global Diarization

Why Hybrid?

⇅

Scalable to Any Length

Each chunk is processed independently, so memory usage is bounded. A 2-hour meeting is no harder than a 10-second segment — just more chunks.

∞

Unlimited Speakers

The clustering step has no fixed speaker limit. New speakers in new chunks simply produce new embedding vectors that form new clusters.

≈

Overlap Handling Preserved

Within each chunk, the EEND model handles overlapping speech natively. The hybrid approach keeps this core advantage.

Permutation Resolution

Since EEND is run independently on each chunk, the speaker label assignment (which output channel maps to which speaker) is independent across chunks. Speaker A might be output 1 in chunk 1 but output 2 in chunk 3. The vector clustering step resolves this cross-chunk permutation by matching speaker embeddings, ensuring globally consistent speaker labels.

pyannote Implementation

The popular pyannote.audio framework implements an EEND-VC variant where the EEND segmentation model only predicts speaker activity (not embeddings), and a separate pre-trained speaker embedding extractor provides the vectors for clustering. This modular approach simplifies training and leverages established, high-quality embedding models.

Handling Overlapping Speech

The ability to handle overlapping speech is EEND's signature advantage over traditional clustering-based methods. Here is exactly how and why it works.

The Multi-Label Formulation

Traditional clustering methods assign each frame to exactly one speaker (hard assignment). This means overlapping speech — where two or more speakers talk simultaneously — is fundamentally unrepresentable. Overlap detection modules can be bolted on after the fact, but they are separate from the diarization system and add error.

EEND uses independent sigmoid outputs for each speaker at each frame. Unlike softmax (which forces outputs to sum to 1), sigmoid allows each output to be independently activated:

# Traditional clustering: one speaker per frame (mutually exclusive) frame_label = argmax([p_speaker1, p_speaker2]) # Only ONE speaker # EEND: independent probabilities per speaker (multi-label) speaker1_active = sigmoid(logit_1) > 0.5 # Can be True speaker2_active = sigmoid(logit_2) > 0.5 # Can ALSO be True simultaneously

Learning Overlap Patterns

During training on data that contains natural overlapping speech, the network learns that certain acoustic patterns (superimposed voices) should activate multiple output channels simultaneously. The binary cross-entropy loss applied independently to each speaker channel naturally handles this: for a frame where both speakers are active, both channels should output high probability, and the loss penalizes anything else.

With EDA Attractors

In the EDA formulation, overlap handling is even more intuitive. When two speakers talk simultaneously, the frame embedding et captures characteristics of both voices. The dot product as · et will be large for both matching speaker attractors, naturally producing high activity for both speakers at that frame.

Overlap Detection Performance

In evaluations, EEND-based methods consistently outperform clustering-based methods specifically on the overlap regions of recordings. The improvement is most dramatic on datasets with high overlap ratios (such as meeting data), where EEND can reduce overlap-related DER components by 30-50% compared to clustering with overlap detection post-processing.

Integration with Self-Supervised Models (WavLM)

Self-supervised learning (SSL) models like WavLM, wav2vec 2.0, and HuBERT have revolutionized speech processing by learning rich, general-purpose speech representations from massive unlabeled audio data. Integrating these representations into EEND has produced significant improvements.

Architecture: WavLM + EEND

The integration replaces the traditional log-Mel filterbank front-end with WavLM features:

Raw Audio → WavLM (frozen or fine-tuned) → Weighted Layer Sum → Linear + LayerNorm → Conformer Encoder → EDA / Clustering → Diarization

Key details of the integration:

  • Weighted layer sum: WavLM outputs from each transformer layer are combined using learnable weights per layer, allowing the model to select the most diarization-relevant representations across different abstraction levels.
  • Conformer encoder: A CNN-augmented Transformer that captures both local and global temporal dependencies, often replacing the plain Transformer encoder in the diarization backbone.
  • Optional fine-tuning: WavLM can be frozen (feature extraction only) or jointly fine-tuned with the diarization model for further improvements.

Results

▼

State-of-the-Art DER

WavLM-based EEND achieves new SOTA on AMI and AISHELL-4 datasets, substantially outperforming pyannote baselines.

✎

Data Efficiency

With only 5% of training data (14.4 hours), WavLM-updated systems outperform baselines trained on the full dataset. SSL features provide powerful speaker representations with minimal supervision.

DiariZen: EEND-VC with WavLM

The DiariZen system combines WavLM features with a Conformer-based EEND model within the EEND-VC framework, achieving state-of-the-art diarization across multiple benchmarks. This represents the current frontier of EEND performance, combining the best of self-supervised representations, end-to-end neural diarization, and clustering-based scalability.

Strengths & Weaknesses

Understanding when EEND excels and where it falls short helps practitioners choose the right approach for their specific use case.

Strengths

  • Native overlapping speech handling — no separate OSD module needed
  • End-to-end optimization directly minimizes DER
  • Simpler deployment: single model replaces a multi-module pipeline
  • Lighter and faster inference than multi-stage clustering systems
  • Easily incorporates modern deep learning advances (Transformers, Conformers, SSL features)
  • Excels on conversational data with frequent turn-taking and overlap
  • Hybrid variants (EEND-VC) combine scalability with overlap handling
  • Streaming variants enable real-time applications
  • EDA/attractor mechanisms handle variable speaker counts

Weaknesses

  • Pure EEND struggles with long recordings (>10 min) due to quadratic attention
  • PIT-based training limits maximum speaker count (factorial complexity)
  • Requires large amounts of diarization-annotated training data
  • Performance degrades with many speakers (7+) without hybrid approaches
  • Domain mismatch: models trained on one dataset may not generalize well
  • Training is computationally expensive compared to clustering pipelines
  • Streaming variants introduce latency-accuracy tradeoffs
  • Less mature ecosystem compared to clustering-based methods
  • Speaker permutation issues in chunk boundaries (EEND-VC) require careful stitching
Practical Guidance

For most real-world applications in 2025, EEND-VC (or its pyannote implementation) offers the best balance of accuracy, scalability, and overlap handling. For low-latency streaming applications, LS-EEND is the current state of the art. Pure EEND or EEND-EDA remains useful for short recordings (under 5 minutes) with 2-4 speakers, such as telephone conversations.
