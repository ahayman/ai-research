A Personal Mission: The Origin Story

Spoken's origin is deeply personal. In 2015, founder Michael Bond watched his grandmother suffer a stroke that left her with severe aphasia. Her mind was intact -- she understood everything happening around her -- but she had completely lost the ability to speak. When Bond looked for modern tools to help her communicate, he was stunned by what he found: speech therapy tools that, in his assessment, had not significantly evolved in 50 years.

Bond decided to build something better. That same year, he entered the Start-Up Chile accelerator program with his early concept for Spoken. At the program's Demo Day in November 2015, Spoken won first place against 18 competing startups, validating Bond's vision that AI-powered communication technology could fill a critical gap in the AAC landscape.

Development continued over the following years, with the app officially launching in 2019. In February 2022, Spoken joined AlphaLab Health, a startup accelerator run by Allegheny Health Network and Innovation Works in Pittsburgh. This partnership gave Bond's team access to clinical resources, healthcare industry mentorship, and the funding needed to accelerate development of the app's AI capabilities.

Natural Voices: Beyond the Robot

One of the most common complaints about traditional AAC devices is their robotic, synthetic-sounding voices. For someone who has lost the ability to speak, being forced to communicate through a voice that sounds nothing like a human adds another layer of difficulty to an already challenging situation.

Spoken offers a wide selection of lifelike, customizable voices covering a variety of accents and identities -- no robotic text-to-speech. All voices feature adjustable speed and pitch, allowing users to find a vocal identity that feels authentic to them.

Four Ways to Communicate

Spoken is not a one-size-fits-all input system. The app offers four distinct input methods, recognizing that different users have different abilities and preferences.

👇

Tap-to-Type

Tap predictive word buttons to build sentences. Fastest method, requires least motor precision.

⌨

Keyboard Typing

Standard text input via on-screen or external keyboard. Maximum control over word choices.

✍

Handwriting

Write words on a canvas. App recognizes handwriting and converts to speakable text.

🎨

Drawing

Draw objects and Spoken identifies them. Trained on Google Quick, Draw! data for accurate recognition.

Drawing Recognition: A Unique Innovation

The drawing canvas, trained on data from Google's Quick, Draw! game, can recognize simple sketches and translate them into words. If a user draws a rough cup shape, Spoken can identify it as "cup." This is particularly useful for people with certain types of aphasia who have difficulty recalling the names of objects but can visualize them.

Cross-Platform Availability

In a market where many leading AAC apps -- including Proloquo2Go and TouchChat -- are available exclusively on iOS, Spoken stands out by supporting iOS, Android, and Mac. This cross-platform availability is more than a convenience; it is a matter of accessibility and equity.

Strengths and Weaknesses

+ Strengths

  • AI that genuinely learns from individual communication patterns
  • Only major AAC app on iOS, Android, and Mac
  • Voice cloning via ElevenLabs (10 seconds of audio)
  • No setup required -- download and start communicating
  • Free basic tier ensures communication is never paywalled
  • Modern, adult-appropriate interface design
  • Four input methods: tap, type, handwrite, draw
  • Incognito mode for private conversations
  • Active development with regular feature updates

- Weaknesses

  • Requires literacy -- inaccessible to pre-literate users
  • No symbol/picture-based communication option
  • Voice cloning needs internet and ElevenLabs subscription
  • Full features require paid subscription
  • iOS pricing substantially higher than Android
  • Limited clinical evidence base vs. established AAC
  • AI may not fully capture personal tone and identity
  • Typing/tapping challenging for severe motor impairments

User Reception

Spoken has received generally positive reviews. On the Educational App Store, it holds a 4.5-star rating. On Google Play, it has approximately 3.83 out of 5 stars from around 290 ratings.

"My wife had a brain tumor removed and lost speech temporarily while the brain recovered. She had full comprehension and knew the words to say she just couldn't speak them."

-- App Store user review, describing how Spoken provided a communication bridge during recovery

The app has been recognized by the National Aphasia Association as a resource for people living with aphasia, and has been featured in SLP (speech-language pathology) resource guides at multiple universities, including Towson University's library research guide for speech-language pathology students.

The Road Ahead

Spoken continues to evolve rapidly. The broader trajectory of AI in AAC is promising -- as large language models continue to improve, apps like Spoken stand to benefit directly from advances in natural language processing.

The challenge ahead is balancing AI efficiency with personal identity. Users want to communicate quickly, but they also want to sound like themselves -- not like an algorithm's best guess at what a "typical" person would say. Spoken's approach of learning from individual speech patterns positions it well for this balance.
