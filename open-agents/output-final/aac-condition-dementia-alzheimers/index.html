What Is Dementia?

Dementia is not a single disease but an umbrella term describing a collection of symptoms caused by disorders affecting the brain. It is characterized by progressive deterioration in cognitive function—including memory, thinking, orientation, comprehension, calculation, learning capacity, language, and judgment—beyond what might be expected from the usual consequences of biological aging. Dementia results from a variety of diseases and injuries that primarily or secondarily affect the brain.

Alzheimer's disease is the most common form of dementia, contributing to an estimated 60–70% of all cases worldwide. It is a progressive neurodegenerative disease that slowly destroys memory and thinking skills, and eventually the ability to carry out the simplest tasks. In most people with the disease, symptoms first appear in their mid-60s, though early-onset forms can begin as early as one's 30s.

The relationship between dementia and communication is profound and deeply consequential. Communication difficulties are among the earliest symptoms of dementia and tend to worsen progressively as the disease advances. Language deficiencies are evident in all patients with Alzheimer's disease, with significantly lower results in verbal expression, hearing comprehension, repetition, reading, and writing. These impairments affect not only the person living with dementia but ripple outward to family members, caregivers, and social networks, often leading to isolation and reduced quality of life.

Why This Matters for AAC

Dementia represents the largest single population that could benefit from AAC intervention. An estimated 23% of all individuals who could benefit from augmentative and alternative communication have a diagnosis of Alzheimer's or another form of dementia. Because communication decline is progressive and inevitable, early planning and intervention with AAC tools can extend a person's ability to participate in meaningful interaction far longer than would otherwise be possible.

Types of Dementia

While Alzheimer's disease is by far the most common cause, dementia can result from numerous underlying conditions. Each type of dementia has a somewhat different effect on communication, making it important for clinicians and caregivers to understand the specific diagnosis. Communication disorders have proven to be among the strongest predictors for discriminating among dementia subtypes.

Alzheimer's Disease (60–70% of cases)

The most common form of dementia, caused by the accumulation of amyloid plaques and tau tangles in the brain. Communication effects include early word-finding difficulties (anomia), progressive loss of vocabulary, circumlocution (talking around a word they cannot find), and eventual inability to produce or comprehend language in late stages. Memory loss and language decline progress together, with naming and semantic memory being especially vulnerable.

Vascular Dementia (10–20% of cases)

Caused by reduced blood flow to the brain, often from stroke or small-vessel disease. Communication effects can vary widely depending on the location of brain damage. Patients may have trouble speaking coherently or understanding the speech of others. Unlike Alzheimer's, onset may be abrupt with a stepwise decline pattern, and cognitive impairment can be patchy rather than uniform. Language difficulties may co-occur with focal neurologic signs such as weakness on one side of the body.

Lewy Body Dementia (5–10% of cases)

Characterized by abnormal deposits of alpha-synuclein protein (Lewy bodies) in nerve cells. Communication effects often involve fluctuations in alertness and attention that make conversation unpredictable, visual hallucinations that may become topics of conversation, and soft or slurred speech related to Parkinson-like motor symptoms. Memory loss typically develops later than in Alzheimer's, but attention fluctuations create significant communication challenges from early on.

Frontotemporal Dementia (FTD) (5–10% of cases)

Involves progressive degeneration of the frontal and temporal lobes of the brain. FTD has three main variants, two of which directly and dramatically affect language: semantic dementia (loss of word meanings and object knowledge), progressive nonfluent aphasia (difficulty producing speech), and behavioral variant FTD (personality and behavioral changes that affect social communication). FTD tends to occur at a younger age, often between 45 and 65.

Mixed Dementia

Many people, especially those over age 80, have a combination of two or more types of dementia—most commonly Alzheimer's disease combined with vascular dementia. Communication effects may blend characteristics of multiple dementia types, making diagnosis and intervention planning more complex. Post-mortem brain studies have shown that mixed pathology is far more common than previously thought.

Clinical Note

Accurate diagnosis of dementia type is important for communication intervention planning. A Speech-Language Pathologist (SLP) can conduct a comprehensive language assessment to characterize the specific pattern of communication difficulties, which may also contribute to differential diagnosis. The pattern of language breakdown varies significantly across dementia types and should guide AAC selection.

Stages & Progression

Dementia is typically described using the Global Deterioration Scale (GDS), developed by Dr. Barry Reisberg, which categorizes Alzheimer's disease and related dementias into seven stages from no cognitive decline to very severe cognitive decline. Understanding these stages is essential for timing AAC interventions appropriately.

Stages 1–3: Pre-Dementia & Early

No Cognitive Decline to Mild Cognitive Decline

Stage 1 (No decline): Normal function with no detectable memory problems.

Stage 2 (Very mild decline): Normal age-related forgetfulness. The person may forget names or where they placed familiar objects but shows no symptoms detectable during a medical examination or recognized by friends and family.

Stage 3 (Mild decline): Early-stage, often identifiable as mild cognitive impairment (MCI). Increased forgetfulness, slight difficulty concentrating, and subtle word-finding problems begin. Friends and family may begin to notice difficulties. This stage can last 2–7 years.

Communication: Mostly intact, with occasional word-finding pauses, reduced verbal fluency, and subtle difficulty following complex conversations. This is the ideal stage to begin AAC planning and introduce simple supports like daily planners and calendars.

Stages 4–5: Early to Mid Dementia

Moderate Cognitive Decline

Stage 4 (Moderate decline): Early dementia. Clear-cut deficits in memory and cognition. Difficulty managing finances, planning meals, or navigating unfamiliar areas. Withdrawal from challenging situations is common. This stage lasts approximately 2 years.

Stage 5 (Moderately severe decline): Mid-stage dementia. The person can no longer survive independently. They may not remember their own address or phone number, become confused about date and time, and need help choosing appropriate clothing. This stage lasts approximately 1.5 years.

Communication: Word-finding difficulties become more pronounced. The person frequently loses track of thoughts and conversations, has trouble understanding what others are saying, and may use substitutes or circumlocutions (e.g., "the thing you sit on" instead of "chair"). They may repeat stories or questions. Active AAC intervention—including memory books, communication boards, and visual aids—becomes essential at this stage.

Stages 6–7: Late Dementia

Severe to Very Severe Cognitive Decline

Stage 6 (Severe decline): The person begins to forget the names of close family members, has little memory of recent events, and requires substantial assistance with daily activities. Personality and emotional changes become marked, including delusions, compulsions, anxiety, and agitation. This stage lasts approximately 2.5 years.

Stage 7 (Very severe decline): Final stage. The person has very little or no ability to speak, cannot walk independently, and requires assistance with virtually all activities. Muscles grow rigid, swallowing becomes impaired. Average duration is 1.5–2.5 years.

Communication: In Stage 6, communication is severely impaired. The person may produce only simple phrases or single words, often out of context. They may confuse family members. In Stage 7, verbal abilities may be reduced to a handful of intelligible words or none at all. Non-verbal communication—touch, facial expression, tone of voice, music—becomes the primary modality. AAC at this stage focuses on supporting caregiver communication to the person and using sensory-based approaches.

Key Insight: The AAC Window

There is a critical "window of opportunity" for introducing AAC tools—ideally in Stages 3–4, when the person can still learn to use new supports and participate in customizing them with personally meaningful content. By Stage 5–6, the capacity to learn new systems is significantly reduced. Tools introduced early become familiar routines that persist longer into the disease progression than new systems would.

How Dementia Affects Communication

Communication breakdown in dementia is multi-faceted, affecting virtually every aspect of language and social interaction. Understanding the specific mechanisms of communication decline is essential for selecting appropriate AAC strategies. Communication difficulties result from the interplay of cognitive deficits (memory, attention, executive function) with language-specific impairments.

Language Production Deficits

  • Anomia (word-finding difficulty): The hallmark communication deficit in Alzheimer's disease. Individuals cannot retrieve the names of people, objects, or concepts, even when they clearly know what they want to say. This manifests as pauses, tip-of-the-tongue experiences, and empty speech filled with pronouns ("that thing") and vague terms.
  • Circumlocution: When a specific word cannot be found, the person "talks around" it—describing the object's function, appearance, or associated context. For example, saying "the thing you write with" instead of "pen" or "the place where you buy food" instead of "grocery store."
  • Semantic paraphasias: Substituting a related but incorrect word (e.g., saying "fork" when meaning "spoon," or "daughter" when meaning "sister").
  • Reduced verbal fluency: Generating fewer words overall, with progressively shorter and simpler sentences.
  • Loss of topic maintenance: Difficulty staying on a topic, with frequent tangential shifts and repetition of previously stated information.
  • Empty speech: Producing many words that convey little meaningful content, with overuse of indefinite terms ("stuff," "things," "you know").

Language Comprehension Deficits

  • Difficulty with complex sentences: Trouble understanding sentences with embedded clauses, passive constructions, or abstract language.
  • Reduced auditory processing speed: Difficulty keeping up with normal conversation pace, especially in group settings or noisy environments.
  • Loss of semantic knowledge: Gradual erosion of the meaning of words and concepts, starting with less-common vocabulary and eventually affecting everyday words.
  • Difficulty following multi-step instructions: Inability to process and execute sequences of verbal directions.

Pragmatic & Social Communication Deficits

  • Turn-taking breakdown: Difficulty knowing when to speak and when to listen in conversation.
  • Reduced theory of mind: Decreasing ability to consider the listener's perspective or knowledge.
  • Inappropriate social behavior: Loss of conversational filters and social norms, especially in frontotemporal dementia.
  • Reduced ability to repair communication breakdowns: When misunderstood, difficulty clarifying or rephrasing.
  • Loss of conversational cohesion: Utterances that do not clearly connect to each other or to the topic at hand.

Non-Verbal Communication Changes

While non-verbal communication is often described as "relatively preserved" compared to verbal abilities, research indicates that 42% of patients with severe dementia show reduced comprehension of gestures and other non-verbal cues. As verbal communication deteriorates, non-verbal channels become increasingly important yet may also be affected, creating a progressively narrowing window of expressive and receptive communication capacity.

The Cognitive Load Factor

A critical factor in dementia-related communication difficulty is cognitive overload. People with dementia must exert heightened mental effort to find the right words or construct sentences. This increased cognitive load means that environmental distractions, time pressure, emotional stress, or complex questions can significantly worsen communication performance even when the underlying language capacity is still partially intact. Reducing cognitive load is a key principle of AAC and communication partner strategies.

Primary Progressive Aphasia (PPA)

Primary Progressive Aphasia deserves special attention in any discussion of dementia and AAC because it represents a group of dementias where language impairment is the primary and earliest symptom, rather than memory loss. PPA is a rare neurological syndrome in which the earliest and most prominent symptoms are difficulties with speaking, word finding, and understanding language.

Diagnostic Criteria

PPA is diagnosed when three core criteria are met:

  1. A language impairment that interferes with the use or comprehension of words
  2. Neurological confirmation that the disease is neurodegenerative and progressive
  3. The aphasia arises in relative isolation, without equivalent deficits in behavior or episodic memory (at least during the first two years)

PPA and AAC

PPA represents perhaps the most compelling case for AAC intervention in dementia because:

  • Language is the primary target: Unlike typical Alzheimer's where memory loss is the leading symptom, PPA patients lose language first while other cognitive abilities remain relatively intact, making them excellent candidates for AAC systems that bypass spoken language.
  • Preserved cognition enables learning: Because memory and reasoning may remain intact for years, PPA patients can learn to use high-tech AAC devices, apps, and strategies effectively.
  • Progressive nature allows proactive planning: Knowing that language will continue to decline, clinicians and patients can proactively build personalized AAC systems while the person can still participate in the process.
  • Longer preservation of communication: Most patients with PPA live for 3 to 12 years after diagnosis. AAC can help maintain functional communication throughout much of this period.
AAC Success in PPA

Companies like Lingraphica specialize in AAC devices for people with PPA and aphasia. Their devices offer customizable icon-based communication with voice output, structured around daily communication needs. Research has shown that AAC can be particularly effective for PPA patients when introduced early, as these individuals retain the cognitive capacity to learn new communication systems for a longer period than those with typical Alzheimer's disease.

Communication Challenges by Stage

Understanding the specific communication challenges that occur at each stage of dementia is essential for selecting and timing AAC interventions appropriately. The following breakdown maps communication abilities and deficits to disease progression, along with recommended AAC approaches for each stage.

Early Stage (Mild)

GDS Stages 3–4 — Communication Still Largely Functional

What you may observe:

  • Occasional word-finding pauses, especially for proper nouns and less-common words
  • Subtle difficulty following complex or multi-party conversations
  • Occasional repetition of questions or stories (often unaware)
  • Slight reduction in vocabulary diversity
  • Writing may show increased spelling or grammatical errors
  • May avoid social situations that feel linguistically demanding

What is preserved:

  • Automatic/social speech ("Hello," "Thank you," "I'm fine")
  • Comprehension of simple, direct language
  • Ability to read familiar text
  • Non-verbal communication skills largely intact
  • Procedural memory (how to do familiar tasks)

Recommended AAC approach: Introduce low-tech supports proactively—daily planners, calendars, written reminders, phone contact lists with photos. Begin creating memory books and communication notebooks with the person's active participation. This is the critical window for learning new tools.

Middle Stage (Moderate)

GDS Stage 5 — Communication Significantly Affected

What you may observe:

  • Frequent and noticeable word-finding difficulties across all word categories
  • Reliance on circumlocution ("the thing you cook in" for "oven")
  • Difficulty following and participating in group conversations
  • May substitute related words (semantic paraphasias)
  • Loses track of thoughts mid-sentence
  • Increasing difficulty understanding longer or more complex utterances
  • May forget names of family members and friends
  • Repetitive questioning and storytelling
  • Reading comprehension declines significantly

What is preserved:

  • Can often recognize and respond to emotional tone
  • Automatic phrases and social conventions
  • Ability to read aloud (even when comprehension is reduced)
  • Response to visual cues and familiar images
  • Non-verbal emotional expression

Recommended AAC approach: Active use of memory books, communication boards, and picture-based choice systems. Introduce or expand use of visual aids for daily routines. Communication partners should simplify language, reduce options, and use yes/no question formats. Photograph-based reminiscence aids support social interaction.

Late Stage (Severe)

GDS Stages 6–7 — Communication Profoundly Impaired

What you may observe:

  • Speech may be limited to single words, short phrases, or repetitive utterances
  • May produce jargon or neologisms (made-up words)
  • Often cannot initiate or sustain conversation
  • May echo what is said to them (echolalia)
  • In very late stages: mutism (no verbal output at all)
  • Comprehension of spoken language is severely limited
  • May not recognize family members or caregivers
  • Loss of ability to read or write

What is preserved:

  • Response to familiar voices, music, and singing
  • Sensitivity to emotional tone, touch, and facial expression
  • Procedural memory for deeply familiar actions or songs
  • Some reflexive social responses (smiling, eye contact)

Recommended AAC approach: Focus shifts to supporting caregivers in communicating to the person rather than expecting output from them. Use sensory-rich approaches: music, familiar objects, photographs. Maintain social closeness through gentle touch, speaking in a calm and warm tone, and presenting familiar stimuli. Simple visual or tactile cues may elicit brief responses. The goal becomes maintaining connection and comfort rather than information exchange.

How AAC Helps People with Dementia

Augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) for dementia takes a fundamentally different approach than AAC for many other conditions. Rather than building up communication skills, the goal is to compensate for declining abilities and capitalize on preserved strengths. The basic premise is that AAC can use external aids incorporating stimuli highly relevant to a person's daily life to compensate for what they can no longer recall independently while leveraging their ability to recognize material they cannot spontaneously retrieve.

Core Principles of AAC for Dementia

  • Recognition over recall: People with dementia often retain the ability to recognize familiar faces, objects, and words even after they can no longer spontaneously recall them. AAC tools present visual and verbal cues that trigger recognition.
  • Personalization is essential: Generic symbols or images are far less effective than personally meaningful photographs, objects, and content drawn from the individual's own life.
  • Simplicity increases usability: As cognitive demands increase with disease progression, AAC systems must become progressively simpler. What works in early stages may need to be dramatically simplified for middle and late stages.
  • Caregiver involvement is critical: Unlike AAC for many other populations, dementia AAC requires active participation from communication partners, who must be trained to introduce, cue, and support use of the tools.
  • The goal evolves over time: Early-stage AAC may support independent communication. Middle-stage AAC supports shared communication. Late-stage AAC supports connection and caregiver communication to the person.

What AAC Can Support

Expressive Communication

  • Making choices and expressing preferences
  • Answering questions about needs (pain, hunger, toileting)
  • Participating in social conversation
  • Sharing personal stories and memories
  • Expressing emotions and feelings
  • Participating in daily activities

Receptive Communication

  • Understanding daily schedules and routines
  • Recognizing family members and caregivers
  • Following simplified instructions
  • Comprehending choices presented visually
  • Orienting to time, place, and situation
  • Understanding safety-related information
Research Evidence

Research has demonstrated that AAC strategies support receptive and expressive language, social participation, decision-making, and reminiscence-based interactions in people with dementia. Memory books and communication wallets have been shown to improve language outcomes within research settings. The use of voice output AAC boards with personal photographs increased on-topic, factual statements in individuals with moderate Alzheimer's disease.

Low-Tech AAC Solutions

Low-tech AAC approaches are often the most effective and practical communication supports for people with dementia. They are inexpensive, familiar in format, easy to customize, require no batteries or technical knowledge, and can be used by caregivers with minimal training. The simplicity of low-tech solutions aligns well with the principle of reducing cognitive load.

Memory Books & Communication Wallets

What They Are

Memory books (also called life story books or reminiscence books) are personalized collections of photographs, simple text, and meaningful images organized into categories. Communication wallets are smaller, portable versions designed for daily use. They serve as both memory aids and conversation starters, providing cues that help the person recognize and discuss important information about their life.

Typical Contents

  • Personal identity: Name, age, hometown, spouse/partner name with photos
  • Family: Photos and names of family members with relationship labels
  • Daily routine: Visual schedule of daily activities with pictures and simple text
  • Preferences: Favorite foods, activities, music, TV shows
  • Life history: Career, hobbies, significant life events, accomplishments
  • Current information: Where they live, their caregivers, their schedule

Effectiveness

Research has consistently shown that memory books increase on-topic conversation, reduce repetitive questioning, and improve social engagement when used with trained communication partners. They capitalize on the preserved ability to recognize familiar content even when free recall is impaired.

Communication Boards

What They Are

Flat displays of pictures, symbols, or words organized by topic or context. They can be laminated sheets, placemats, or wall-mounted boards positioned in relevant locations (kitchen, bathroom, bedroom).

Common Types

  • Choice boards: Present 2–4 options for meals, activities, or clothing using photographs
  • Needs boards: Display common needs (hungry, thirsty, tired, pain, bathroom) with simple icons
  • Routine boards: Visual step-by-step guides for daily activities (brushing teeth, getting dressed)
  • Feelings boards: Simple emotional vocabulary with facial expression images
  • Location-specific boards: Placed in the bathroom, kitchen, or bedroom with context-relevant vocabulary

Environmental Supports

Signs & Labels

Clear, high-contrast signs on doors (BATHROOM, KITCHEN, BEDROOM), drawers, and cupboards help with orientation and independence. Labels should use both words and pictures, with large clear fonts on a contrasting background.

Color Coding

Using consistent colors to indicate categories (e.g., red for danger/stop, green for go/yes) across the living environment provides visual cues that support comprehension when verbal understanding is impaired.

Visual Schedules

Large, clearly visible daily schedules with photographs or simple icons representing activities help reduce anxiety by providing predictability and supporting orientation to time and routine.

Other Low-Tech Approaches

  • Cue cards: Large-print cards with frequently needed phrases ("I need help," "I am in pain," "Where is the bathroom?")
  • Photograph albums: Organized collections of family photos with names and captions for reminiscence conversations
  • Calendars with visual cues: Large-format calendars with picture representations of upcoming events
  • Written conversation prompts: Cards with conversation starter questions for visitors and caregivers
  • Object-based communication: Using real objects to present choices (e.g., holding up a cup and a glass to offer a drink choice)
Design Principles for Low-Tech AAC in Dementia
  • Use real photographs rather than abstract symbols whenever possible—they are more easily recognized
  • Keep layouts simple and uncluttered—limit to 2–6 items per page or board
  • Use high contrast (dark text on light background, or vice versa)
  • Include both pictures and text to support multiple recognition pathways
  • Make materials durable and cleanable (laminated, waterproof)
  • Place materials in context—mealtime boards in the kitchen, hygiene boards in the bathroom
  • Update content regularly as the person's abilities and needs change

High-Tech AAC Solutions

While low-tech approaches form the backbone of AAC for dementia, technology-based solutions offer additional capabilities that can be especially valuable for people in early-to-middle stages of dementia and for those with Primary Progressive Aphasia. The key challenge with high-tech AAC in dementia is balancing functionality with simplicity—the interface must be intuitive enough for someone with declining cognitive abilities to use.

Tablet-Based Communication Apps

Dedicated AAC Apps

Several AAC apps have been adapted for or specifically designed for people with dementia and aphasia:

  • Proloquo2Go (iOS): A symbol-based communication app that allows users to create sentences using icons that are then read aloud. Can be simplified for dementia users by reducing the number of displayed symbols.
  • TouchChat HD (iOS): Offers a specialized "Communication Journey: Aphasia" vocabulary file designed for individuals with aphasia, with simplified layouts and high-frequency vocabulary.
  • TalkTablet (iOS, Android, Windows, Kindle): A more affordable option with straightforward programming and good tech support, suitable for those who need a simpler interface.
  • Lingraphica devices: Purpose-built AAC devices specifically designed for people with aphasia and PPA, with customizable icon-based communication and built-in language therapy activities.

Memory and Communication Apps

Beyond dedicated AAC apps, several technology tools serve as digital equivalents of memory books and communication aids:

  • Digital photo frames: Rotating displays of family photos with names, serving as constant memory cues and conversation starters
  • Custom memory apps: Purpose-built applications that present personal photographs, videos, and voice recordings organized by category
  • Video calling apps: Simplified tablet interfaces that allow one-touch video calling with family members, maintaining social connection
  • Reminder and scheduling apps: Apps that provide visual and auditory prompts for daily routines, medication, and appointments

Speech-Generating Devices (SGDs)

SGDs produce spoken output when the user selects symbols, pictures, or words on a display. Research with people with moderate Alzheimer's disease has demonstrated that a customized communication board containing 16 personal pictures with digitized voice output improved on-topic communication. When a person with dementia touches a picture, its label is spoken aloud, which serves as both an expressive communication aid and a self-cuing strategy.

Voice Banking

For people with PPA who know their speech will deteriorate, voice banking allows them to record samples of their own voice while it is still clear. These recordings can later be used to create a personalized synthetic voice for a speech-generating device, maintaining a piece of their identity even after natural speech is lost. This is best done soon after diagnosis when speech remains largely intact.

Smart Home Technology

Voice-activated assistants (Amazon Alexa, Google Home) and smart home systems can support daily functioning by providing reminders, answering questions, controlling lights and temperature, and playing music—all through simple voice commands. However, as speech deteriorates, these tools may become less accessible, and their utility in dementia is limited to the early-to-middle stages in most cases.

Important Considerations for High-Tech AAC in Dementia
  • Timing: High-tech tools must be introduced early enough for the person to learn the interface. Waiting too long makes learning new technology extremely difficult.
  • Simplicity: Interfaces must be dramatically simplified compared to standard AAC configurations. Fewer buttons, larger icons, clearer labels, and reduced navigation depth are essential.
  • Ongoing modification: As cognitive abilities decline, the technology setup must be progressively simplified by a caregiver or clinician.
  • Caregiver burden: Technology introduces maintenance, troubleshooting, and charging responsibilities. The caregiver burden of maintaining high-tech devices must be weighed against the benefits.
  • Cost: Dedicated AAC devices can be expensive. Tablet-based apps offer a more affordable alternative but may require more customization.
  • Backup plan: Always have low-tech AAC options available as backup, since technology can fail, run out of battery, or become too complex at any time.

Caregiver Communication Strategies

Caregivers are the essential partners in any AAC intervention for dementia. Research consistently demonstrates that training caregivers in communication strategies is at least as important as providing AAC tools to the person with dementia. Caregiver communication training has been shown to improve interaction quality, reduce behavioral symptoms, and maintain social engagement. However, studies have noted that only about 61% of communication strategies commonly shared online are evidence-based, underscoring the importance of formal, professional training.

Evidence-Based Communication Techniques

Verbal Strategies

  • Simplify language: Use short, simple sentences with common words. Present one idea at a time.
  • Slow down: Speak at a measured pace, pausing between sentences to allow processing time.
  • Use closed-ended questions: Replace open questions ("What do you want to eat?") with limited choices ("Would you like chicken or fish?").
  • Repeat and rephrase: If not understood, repeat using simpler words rather than increasing volume.
  • Avoid corrections: Do not point out errors or test memory. Focus on maintaining the flow of interaction.
  • Use orienting information naturally: Weave in helpful context ("Your daughter Maria is coming to visit after lunch") rather than quizzing.
  • Match the stage: In early stages, support normal conversation. In middle stages, facilitate interaction with AAC. In late stages, focus on tone, touch, and emotional connection.

Non-Verbal Strategies

  • Maintain eye contact: Approach from the front, get to eye level, and ensure the person can see your face.
  • Use gestures and pointing: Supplement verbal communication with natural gestures and pointing to relevant objects or pictures.
  • Monitor facial expression: Ensure your face conveys warmth and patience. People with dementia remain sensitive to emotional tone even in late stages.
  • Use gentle touch: Appropriate touch (hand on shoulder, holding hands) provides reassurance and connection, especially as verbal comprehension declines.
  • Reduce environmental distractions: Turn off background noise, move to a quiet space, minimize visual clutter during important conversations.

Supporting AAC Tool Use

Do

  • Model AAC use yourself first—point to pictures while you talk
  • Place AAC tools where they are naturally needed
  • Allow ample response time (at least 10–15 seconds)
  • Celebrate and respond to any communication attempt
  • Use AAC tools consistently across all caregivers
  • Keep tools updated with current, relevant content
  • Work with an SLP to customize tools and strategies

Don't

  • Force or demand use of AAC tools
  • Test or quiz the person using the tools
  • Overwhelm with too many options or pages
  • Give up if the person initially resists—try again gently
  • Assume the person cannot understand because they cannot speak
  • Change tools or layouts frequently without reason
  • Neglect your own well-being—caregiver burnout is real

Communication Partner Training Programs

Structured training programs for caregivers have been shown to be effective. Research has identified three main themes for communication strategies in personalized dementia care:

  1. Understanding the person: Learning the individual's history, preferences, communication style, and remaining strengths
  2. Communication techniques: Verbal techniques, non-verbal techniques, and use of external aids (AAC tools)
  3. Workforce support: Ongoing training, supervision, and emotional support for professional caregivers

Balancing meaningful conversation with care-task-related communication has been highlighted as a trainable skill that improves both care quality and the person's well-being. Professional training from a Speech-Language Pathologist is recommended, as they can tailor strategies to the individual's specific communication profile and stage of disease.

Caregiver Self-Care

Nearly 12 million Americans provide unpaid care for people with dementia, contributing over 19 billion hours of care annually. Communication breakdown is one of the most emotionally challenging aspects of caregiving. Caregivers should seek respite care, join support groups, and access professional training—not only for the person with dementia's benefit but for their own emotional sustainability.

Evidence & Research

The research base for AAC use in dementia is growing, though it remains more limited than for some other AAC populations. Here is a summary of the current evidence and key research findings.

What Research Shows

Memory Books & Communication Wallets

Evidence level: Moderate

Memory books and communication wallets are the most-studied AAC intervention for dementia. Research has consistently shown that they improve language outcomes within research settings—specifically increasing on-topic, factual statements during conversations. However, the evidence for generalization to functional, everyday communication outcomes outside the research setting is more limited. Studies suggest that caregiver training is essential for transfer of benefits to daily life.

Voice Output Communication Aids

Evidence level: Emerging

A study of 30 individuals with moderate Alzheimer's disease provided customized communication boards with 16 personal pictures and digitized voice output. Results showed that voice output enhanced attention to the communication board and increased verbal output during interactions. The combination of visual and auditory stimulation appeared to capitalize on multiple recognition pathways.

AAC for Primary Progressive Aphasia

Evidence level: Moderate to Strong

PPA represents the strongest evidence base for high-tech AAC in dementia-related conditions. Because PPA patients retain cognitive abilities longer while specifically losing language, they can learn and use AAC devices effectively. Research supports early introduction of AAC in PPA, with evidence that patients who begin using AAC tools while speech is still partially intact maintain functional communication longer.

Communication Partner Training

Evidence level: Strong

Multiple systematic reviews have found that training communication partners (family and professional caregivers) in dementia-specific communication strategies improves interaction quality, reduces problematic behavior, and enhances quality of life for both the person with dementia and the caregiver. This is the most robustly supported intervention in the dementia communication literature.

Research Gaps

  • Most AAC research in dementia has focused on Alzheimer's disease; less is known about AAC outcomes for vascular dementia, Lewy body dementia, and other subtypes
  • Long-term effectiveness studies are limited—most studies follow participants for weeks to months, not years
  • The specific characteristics of AAC tools that are most effective (e.g., number of symbols, photograph vs. symbol, with/without voice output) are not yet well-defined through comparative research
  • Cost-effectiveness data for AAC in dementia are largely absent
  • Few studies have examined high-tech AAC in moderate-to-severe dementia stages
  • Cultural and linguistic diversity in AAC design for dementia has received very limited research attention
Key Researchers & Resources

The American Speech-Language-Hearing Association (ASHA) provides practice guidelines for AAC intervention in dementia through its Practice Portal. The Alzheimer's Association (alz.org) publishes comprehensive annual reports on Alzheimer's disease facts and figures. UCSF's Memory and Aging Center and Northwestern's Mesulam Institute are leading research centers for PPA. The WHO maintains global statistics and guidelines on dementia care.

Key Considerations

Implementing AAC for people with dementia involves navigating several important clinical, ethical, and practical considerations that differ from AAC for other populations.

Ethical Considerations

  • Autonomy and decision-making: As dementia progresses, the person's ability to make decisions about their own communication tools and strategies diminishes. Advance care planning should include communication preferences.
  • Dignity preservation: AAC tools should support the person's identity and dignity, not infantilize them. Using age-appropriate materials, real photographs, and personally meaningful content is essential.
  • Consent and participation: Involve the person with dementia in AAC planning to the greatest extent possible, especially in early stages when they can still express preferences.
  • Presuming competence: Even in late stages, assume the person understands more than they can express. Never speak about them as if they are not present.

Assessment Considerations

  • Comprehensive evaluation: AAC assessment should include formal language testing, observation of communication in natural settings, caregiver interview, and review of medical/neurological status.
  • Ongoing reassessment: Because dementia is progressive, AAC tools and strategies must be regularly reassessed and modified as abilities change. What works at one stage may not work six months later.
  • Feature matching: AAC selection should match the person's current abilities, sensory status (vision, hearing), motor skills, and cognitive level—not just their language deficits.
  • Environmental assessment: Consider the physical environment, daily routines, primary communication partners, and settings where communication is most needed.

Implementation Considerations

Factor Consideration Recommendation
Timing When to introduce AAC As early as possible, ideally at MCI or early dementia stage. Do not wait for a "crisis."
Training Who needs training All regular communication partners: family, paid caregivers, medical staff, social visitors.
Consistency Using tools across settings AAC tools should travel with the person—home, day program, medical appointments, family visits.
Simplification Adjusting over time Plan for progressive simplification. Create a "stages roadmap" that anticipates decreasing complexity.
Cultural sensitivity Language, values, preferences AAC content should reflect the person's cultural background, native language, and family values.
Multimodal approach Using multiple strategies together Combine low-tech and high-tech tools with communication partner strategies for best outcomes.

Funding & Access

Access to AAC assessment and intervention for people with dementia can be complicated by funding restrictions, insurance limitations, and a shortage of SLPs with expertise in both dementia and AAC. Key considerations include:

  • Medicare may cover AAC evaluation and some devices if documented as medically necessary
  • Many low-tech AAC solutions can be created at low or no cost by caregivers with professional guidance
  • Some AAC apps are available as free downloads with optional paid features
  • Organizations such as the Alzheimer's Association and local Area Agencies on Aging may provide communication resources
  • SLPs in home health, skilled nursing facilities, and memory care programs may provide AAC services
Common Pitfall

One of the most common mistakes in dementia AAC is waiting too long to begin. Families and clinicians often focus on memory and behavioral symptoms while overlooking communication needs until a crisis point. By that time, the person may have lost the ability to participate in creating personalized AAC tools or to learn new strategies. The message is clear: begin AAC planning at the earliest sign of communication difficulty, even if the need seems mild.
