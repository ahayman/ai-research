For over 100,000 people worldwide, the simple act of speaking is not simple at all. Whether due to ALS, cerebral palsy, autism, stroke, or other conditions that affect speech and mobility, these individuals rely on technology to give them a voice. Among the tools serving this community, Grid by Smartbox stands out as one of the most comprehensive AAC platforms available -- not just enabling communication, but offering users genuine independence through environment control, computer access, and accessible entertainment.

But Grid is also a product of contradictions. Its Windows version is a powerhouse of accessibility features, while its iPad counterpart feels comparatively stripped-down. Its eye gaze technology is among the best in the industry, yet its App Store reviews reveal persistent bugs and a frustrating British English bias. This deep dive examines everything Grid offers, where it excels, where it falls short, and who it serves best.

The Company Behind Grid

Smartbox Assistive Technology began in 2006 as a small family venture. Founded by Paul, Alyson, Barney, and Dougal Hawes, the company set out with a focused mission: provide alternative communication solutions to people in the UK who cannot communicate through speech. What started as a family operation has since grown into one of the top four global AAC solution providers.

Today, Smartbox employs approximately 750 people, with UK headquarters in Bristol and Malvern and offices in the US, Germany, France, the Netherlands, and Australia. Under CEO Dougal Hawes -- the last founding family member still leading the company -- Smartbox continues to push the boundaries of what AAC technology can achieve.

How Grid Works: Communication That Adapts

At its core, Grid is a communication platform that speaks on behalf of users who cannot speak for themselves. It achieves this through two complementary approaches: symbol-based communication for those who think in pictures, and text-based communication for literate users who type what they want to say.

Symbol Communication: Super Core

The heart of Grid's symbol-based approach is Super Core, a research-based vocabulary system developed by Smartbox's speech and language therapy team. Super Core organizes over 3,500 words and phrases into colour-coded grids where each word consistently occupies the same position, enabling users to develop motor memory -- a critical factor for communication speed.

SwiftKey Integration: All text input in Grid benefits from SwiftKey-powered word prediction, significantly reducing the number of selections needed to construct sentences. A built-in Word Finder tool helps clinicians locate any word and demonstrates the navigation path to reach it.

Eye Gaze: Where Grid Truly Leads

If there is one area where Grid and its parent ecosystem outshine virtually all competitors, it is eye gaze technology. For users who cannot touch a screen -- people with severe motor impairments from conditions like ALS, locked-in syndrome, or advanced cerebral palsy -- controlling a device with their eyes is not just a feature. It is their entire interface to the world.

Interaction Modes

  • Dwell selection -- Hold gaze on a target for a configurable time to activate it (most common for AAC users)

Environment Control: Independence Beyond Words

Built-in Transmitters

Grid Pad devices come with two built-in transmitters: a GEWA infrared transmitter for controlling TVs, set-top boxes, and any IR-compatible device, plus a radio control transmitter for RF devices. Additional EasyWave and Z-Wave transmitters can be added for broader smart home integration.

Smart Home Platforms

Grid 3 integrates with two smart home control platforms: Servus (dedicated environment control for disability) and Nous (modern IoT device control). The practical impact is profound -- a person with ALS who can only move their eyes can communicate, control the television, adjust room lighting, and send a text message, all without anyone else touching a single button.

Important: Environment control is available only in Grid 3 for Windows. Grid for iPad does not support environment control of any kind.

Mind's Eye: AI Art for Everyone

Described as the world's first AI art expression tool for people with disabilities, Mind's Eye was developed in partnership with the MND Association. Users type what's on their mind, select from over 100 style preferences, and the AI generates images they can save and share. It represents a new frontier in creative accessibility.

Full Computer Access

Beyond dedicated apps, Grid 3 provides complete Windows computer control through an accessible toolbar. Users get on-screen keyboards with SwiftKey prediction, mouse pointer tools, and shortcuts -- enabling use of Microsoft Word, Excel, web browsers, and virtually any Windows application through alternative access methods.

Grid 3 only: Accessible apps and computer access are not available on Grid for iPad. The iPad version cannot control other iPad applications from within Grid.

Grid Pad Hardware: Purpose-Built for AAC

Strengths & Weaknesses

Strengths

  • Unmatched eye gaze ecosystem with three camera options and purpose-built hardware integration
  • Comprehensive environment control: IR, radio, Z-Wave, Servus, and Nous smart home integration
  • Full Windows computer access through Grid 3 with accessible toolbar
  • Purpose-built accessible apps for Netflix, YouTube, Spotify, Disney+, and social media
  • Research-backed Super Core vocabulary with 3,500+ words developed by SLT professionals
  • Rugged Grid Pad hardware: drop-tested to 1m, IP54, aluminium chassis, daylight-readable
  • Mind's Eye AI art tool for creative expression
  • 200 voices across 32 languages with 30+ language support
  • SwiftKey-powered word prediction for faster communication
  • Online Grids community store with hundreds of user-created grid sets
  • Cloud sync between Grid 3 and Grid for iPad via Dropbox

Weaknesses

  • British English default vocabulary even in US English version (confusing for language learners)
  • Audio and stability bugs: sound stopping after updates, crashes when pressing speak buttons
  • Dramatic platform gap: Grid for iPad lacks environment control, computer access, and accessible apps
  • No iPhone or Apple Watch support -- no backup when primary device is unavailable
  • Complex initial setup requiring significant clinician or caregiver expertise
  • App freezing when typing reported by multiple users (Dec 2024)
  • Customer support responsiveness concerns reported in reviews
  • Windows-centric: most powerful features are Windows-only
  • High cost for full ecosystem (typically funded through healthcare programs)
  • Changing grid sizes forces users to re-learn word locations, disrupting motor plans

Who Is Grid Best For?

Grid 3 (Windows) is ideal for:

  • Users who need eye gaze access -- ALS/MND, locked-in syndrome, severe cerebral palsy
  • Users who want environment control -- smart home integration, IR/radio device control
  • Users who need full computer access -- work, education, entertainment, web browsing
  • Clinical and school settings -- where multiple user profiles are needed on shared devices
  • Users investing in a complete ecosystem -- Grid Pad hardware with long-term AAC commitment

Grid for iPad is best for:

  • Touch-based communicators -- users who primarily need symbol or text communication
  • Budget-conscious users -- more affordable entry point with monthly subscription option
  • Portability-focused users -- lighter, more portable than Grid Pad devices
  • Secondary device users -- people who use Grid 3 primarily and want cloud-synced content on an iPad

Conditions Grid supports:

ALS / MND, Autism Spectrum Disorder, Cerebral Palsy, Down's Syndrome, Learning Difficulties, Stroke / Aphasia, Brain Injury, Rett Syndrome, and any condition affecting speech and/or mobility.

Conclusion

Grid by Smartbox occupies a unique position in the AAC landscape. No other platform offers the same combination of communication, environment control, computer access, and accessible entertainment in a single ecosystem. For users with severe motor impairments who need eye gaze access and full computer control, Grid 3 with a Grid Pad device is arguably the most capable AAC solution available.

But that capability comes with caveats. The iPad version is a shadow of the Windows experience. Bugs and British English defaults frustrate users. The setup complexity demands clinical support. And the cost of the full ecosystem -- while typically funded through healthcare programmes -- is substantial.

As Smartbox pursues its ambitious 3x user growth target by 2030 under Verdane's partnership, the question is whether the company can maintain the depth that makes Grid exceptional while broadening its accessibility. The platform's 100,000-plus current users testify to its value. Whether Smartbox can reach three times that number will depend on addressing the rough edges that currently hold it back.
