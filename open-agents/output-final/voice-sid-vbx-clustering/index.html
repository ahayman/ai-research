VBx Clustering

Variational Bayes HMM over X-Vectors — a mathematically principled approach to speaker diarization that models speakers as hidden Markov model states and uses Bayesian inference to assign speech segments to speakers.

Introduction

Speaker diarization answers the question "who spoke when?" in an audio recording. At the heart of most modern diarization systems lies a clustering step: given a sequence of speaker embeddings (x-vectors) extracted from short segments of audio, group them so that all segments from the same speaker end up in the same cluster.

VBx (Variational Bayes HMM over X-vectors) is a clustering back-end for speaker diarization developed by the BUT Speech@FIT group at Brno University of Technology. Published by Landini, Profant, Diez, and Burget (2022), it reformulates the clustering problem as inference in a Bayesian Hidden Markov ModelA probabilistic model where observed data (x-vectors) are generated by hidden states (speakers), with temporal transitions modeled by an HMM and all parameters treated in a Bayesian framework., yielding a robust, principled, and computationally efficient method that achieves state-of-the-art results on major benchmarks.

Key Insights
  • VBx treats speaker diarization as sequence modeling, not independent vector clustering — it respects the temporal structure of conversation.
  • It combines an HMM for speaker turn dynamics with a PLDA-based emission model for speaker identity.
  • Inference is performed via Variational Bayes, avoiding the intractability of exact Bayesian inference while yielding soft probabilistic assignments.
  • Achieves competitive or state-of-the-art DER on CALLHOME (4.42%), AMI, and DIHARD benchmarks.

Why Clustering Matters in Speaker Diarization

A typical speaker diarization pipeline consists of three major stages:

  1. Voice Activity Detection (VAD) — detect which portions of audio contain speech.
  2. Embedding Extraction — convert short speech segments into fixed-dimensional vectors (x-vectors, ECAPA-TDNN embeddings) that encode speaker identity.
  3. Clustering — group embeddings so that each cluster corresponds to one speaker.

The clustering stage is where all speaker identity decisions are made. A perfect embedding extractor would place same-speaker vectors close together and different-speaker vectors far apart, making clustering trivial. In practice, embeddings are noisy: short segments, overlapping speech, background noise, and channel variability all degrade embedding quality. This makes the choice of clustering algorithm critical for final diarization accuracy.

The clustering algorithm must solve two intertwined problems simultaneously:

  • How many speakers are there? (model selection)
  • Which speaker produced each segment? (assignment)

Traditional clustering methods (k-means, spectral, AHC) treat each embedding independently. VBx's key innovation is recognizing that consecutive embeddings are not independent — speakers take turns, and a speaker is likely to continue speaking in the next segment. By modeling this temporal structure explicitly, VBx achieves substantially better results.

Traditional Clustering Approaches

Before VBx, speaker diarization systems relied on classical clustering algorithms. Understanding their strengths and limitations motivates the VBx approach.

Agglomerative Hierarchical Clustering (AHC)

AHC is a bottom-up approach that has been the workhorse of speaker diarization for decades. It begins by treating each embedding as its own cluster and iteratively merges the two closest clusters until a stopping criterion is met (typically a threshold on inter-cluster distance, often measured by cosine similarity or PLDA scores).

AHC Characteristics

  • Deterministic: Given a threshold, always produces the same result.
  • No fixed k: The number of clusters is determined by the stopping threshold.
  • Greedy: Merge decisions are permanent and cannot be undone — an early mistake propagates through all subsequent merges.
  • Widely used: The standard baseline in diarization and a key initialization step for VBx.

Spectral Clustering

Spectral clustering constructs a similarity graph (typically using cosine similarity between embeddings), computes the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian matrix, and then applies k-means in the spectral embedding space. It has become popular in modern diarization systems (e.g., pyannote).

  • Strength: Captures global structure in the similarity graph; can find non-convex clusters.
  • Weakness: Requires the number of speakers as input (or heuristics to estimate it from eigenvalues); computational cost of eigen decomposition scales poorly with large numbers of embeddings; treats each embedding independently with no temporal modeling.

K-Means Clustering

K-means partitions embeddings into k clusters by minimizing within-cluster variance. It iteratively assigns each embedding to the nearest centroid and recomputes centroids.

  • Strength: Simple, fast, well-understood.
  • Weakness: Requires knowing k in advance; assumes spherical, equally-sized clusters; sensitive to initialization; does not model temporal dependencies; tends to split large clusters and merge small ones when speaker participation is unbalanced.

Limitations of Traditional Methods

Fundamental limitations shared by all traditional clustering methods:
  • No temporal modeling: They treat each embedding as an independent data point, ignoring that speech is sequential and speakers take turns. Shuffling the order of embeddings would not change the clustering result.
  • Hard assignments: Each embedding is assigned to exactly one cluster with no notion of uncertainty.
  • Speaker count estimation: Either require the number of speakers as input (k-means, spectral) or rely on a single threshold to determine it (AHC), with no principled model selection.
  • No probabilistic framework: Cannot naturally incorporate prior knowledge about speaker distributions or express confidence in assignments.

The VBx Innovation: Speakers as HMM States

VBx addresses every limitation listed above by reformulating speaker diarization as inference in a Bayesian HMM. The core idea is elegant:

  • Each speaker is a hidden state in the HMM.
  • The sequence of x-vectors are the observations (emissions) generated by the hidden speaker states.
  • Transition probabilities between states model speaker turn-taking: a speaker is likely to continue speaking (high self-loop probability), and speaker changes follow a transition distribution.
  • Speaker-specific emission distributions are derived from a pre-trained PLDAProbabilistic Linear Discriminant Analysis: a generative model that decomposes x-vector variability into between-speaker and within-speaker components, providing a natural likelihood function for speaker identity. model.

The Generative Model

VBx assumes the observed sequence of x-vectors X = {x1, x2, ..., xT} is generated by the following process:

  1. Initialize up to S potential speakers, each with a speaker embedding ys drawn from a prior based on the PLDA between-speaker distribution.
  2. At each time step t, a hidden state zt indicates which speaker is active, drawn from the HMM transition distribution conditioned on zt-1.
  3. The observed x-vector xt is emitted from the speaker-specific distribution associated with state zt.

The joint probability of all observed and latent variables factorizes as:

Joint Probability p(X, Z, Y) = p(X | Z, Y) · p(Z) · p(Y)
= ∏t p(xt | zt, yzt) · ∏t p(zt | zt-1) · ∏s p(ys)

where:

  • Z = {z1, ..., zT} are the hidden speaker state assignments
  • Y = {y1, ..., yS} are the speaker embeddings (latent speaker representations)
  • p(xt | zt, yzt) is the PLDA-derived emission probability
  • p(zt | zt-1) is the HMM transition probability
  • p(ys) is the Bayesian prior on speaker embeddings

PLDA Emission Model

The emission model is derived from a two-covariance PLDAThe two-covariance PLDA decomposes x-vector variability into a between-speaker covariance matrix (capturing how speakers differ) and a within-speaker covariance matrix (capturing session/segment variability for a single speaker). pre-trained on a large corpus of speaker-labeled x-vectors. The PLDA provides:

  • Between-speaker covariance (Σb): Captures how speaker embeddings vary across speakers, defining the prior p(ys).
  • Within-speaker covariance (Σw): Captures how embeddings from the same speaker vary across segments, defining the likelihood p(xt | ys).

After whitening and length normalization of x-vectors, the emission probability for each time step is modeled as a Gaussian:

Emission Probability p(xt | zt = s) = N(xt; ys, Σw)

Automatic Speaker Count Determination

A distinctive feature of VBx is its ability to automatically determine the number of active speakers. The model is initialized with more potential speakers than actually present. Through the Bayesian inference process, the Automatic Relevance Determination (ARD)A Bayesian mechanism where the model learns to set the mixing weights (speaker probabilities) of redundant speakers to zero, effectively pruning them from the model during inference. principle causes redundant speaker states to have their posterior probabilities driven to zero, effectively removing them from the model.

Variational Bayesian Inference Explained

The goal of inference in VBx is to compute the posterior distribution over hidden variables given the observations:

Posterior (Intractable) p(Z, Y | X) = p(X, Z, Y) / p(X)

This posterior is intractable to compute exactly because the marginal likelihood p(X) requires summing over all possible speaker assignments for all time steps — an exponentially large space. Variational BayesAn approximate inference technique that finds the closest tractable distribution to the true posterior by maximizing a lower bound on the log marginal likelihood (the ELBO). It converts an intractable integration problem into an optimization problem. (VB) approximates this posterior with a tractable distribution q(Z, Y) that factorizes as:

Mean-Field Approximation q(Z, Y) = q(Z) · q(Y) = q(Z) · ∏s q(ys)

This factorization (called the mean-field approximation) assumes that speaker assignments and speaker embeddings are independent in the approximate posterior, making inference tractable.

The ELBO Objective

VB optimizes the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO), a lower bound on the log marginal likelihood:

Evidence Lower Bound ELBO(q) = Eq[log p(X, Z, Y)] - Eq[log q(Z, Y)]
= Eq[log p(X | Z, Y)] - KL(q(Z) || p(Z)) - KL(q(Y) || p(Y))

Maximizing the ELBO is equivalent to minimizing the KL divergenceKullback-Leibler divergence measures the "distance" between two probability distributions. Minimizing KL(q || p) makes the approximate distribution q as close as possible to the true posterior p. between q(Z, Y) and the true posterior p(Z, Y | X).

Coordinate Ascent Updates

The ELBO is maximized using Coordinate Ascent Variational Inference (CAVI), which alternates between updating two groups of variables while holding the other fixed:

E

E-step: Update q(Z) — Speaker Assignments

Fix the speaker embeddings q(Y) and update the posterior over state sequences q(Z). This step uses the forward-backward algorithm (a standard HMM algorithm) to compute the expected state occupancy for each time step. The result is a soft assignment: for each time step, a probability distribution over which speaker is active.

M

M-step: Update q(Y) — Speaker Embeddings

Fix the speaker assignments q(Z) and update the posterior over speaker embeddings q(Y). Each speaker's posterior embedding is computed as a precision-weighted combination of the prior (from PLDA between-speaker covariance) and the weighted average of assigned x-vectors.

R

Repeat Until Convergence

Alternate E and M steps for a fixed number of iterations (typically 10). The ELBO is monitored to verify convergence. In practice, VBx converges rapidly — most of the improvement occurs within the first few iterations.

Why VB, not EM? Standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) would find point estimates for speaker embeddings. Variational Bayes maintains full posterior distributions, which provides natural regularization, enables automatic model selection (speaker count estimation), and avoids overfitting to short recordings.

The VBx Pipeline: AHC → VB Refinement

VBx is not a standalone system — it is the clustering back-end in a complete diarization pipeline. The full pipeline has four stages:

1

Feature Extraction

Compute acoustic features (typically 64-dimensional log-Mel filterbank energies, also called fbank features) from the audio waveform, using a 25ms window with 10ms hop. Voice Activity Detection (VAD) identifies speech regions, and only speech frames are retained.

2

X-Vector Extraction

A neural network (e.g., ResNet101 or ECAPA-TDNN) processes overlapping segments of acoustic features (typically 1.5s windows with 0.75s shift) to produce fixed-dimensional speaker embeddings. These embeddings are then centered, whitened, and length-normalized to match the training conditions of the PLDA model.

3

AHC Initialization

Agglomerative Hierarchical Clustering is run on the cosine similarity matrix of all x-vector pairs. The AHC stopping threshold is deliberately set to under-cluster (i.e., produce more clusters than expected speakers). This gives the subsequent VB stage more freedom to explore the solution space and converge to the correct number of speakers. The AHC output provides initial speaker labels for VBx.

4

VB Refinement

The VBx algorithm takes the initial labels and iteratively refines them using the Bayesian HMM framework described above. It simultaneously re-estimates speaker embeddings, adjusts speaker assignments, and determines the correct number of speakers through ARD. The output is an RTTM file with final speaker labels for each time segment.

Why AHC + VBx, not VBx alone?
  • VBx requires an initialization — it cannot start from scratch because the VB optimization landscape has many local optima.
  • AHC provides a reasonable (but imperfect) starting point from which VBx can refine.
  • The AHC threshold is tuned to over-segment (produce too many clusters), because VBx is better at merging spurious clusters than splitting ones that were incorrectly merged.
  • This two-stage approach consistently outperforms either method used alone.

Hyperparameter Tuning

VBx has three key hyperparameters that significantly affect performance and require careful tuning, typically via grid search or cross-validation on a development set:

Parameter Symbol Role Effect Typical Range
FA FA Scaling factor for the PLDA eigenvoice subspace (speaker variability) Controls the discriminability between speakers. Higher FA makes the model more confident about speaker differences; too high leads to over-splitting. This is the most important hyperparameter. 0.1 – 1.0
Fb Fb Scaling factor for the within-speaker variability (PLDA noise term) Controls tolerance for within-speaker variation. Higher Fb allows more variability within a speaker cluster; too high causes under-clustering (merging different speakers). Most relevant for adapting to different recording conditions. 1 – 30
Ploop Ploop HMM self-loop (stay) probability Controls temporal smoothing (minimum speaker turn duration). Higher Ploop discourages frequent speaker changes; too high prevents detecting short turns. Affects diarization granularity. 0.5 – 0.99

How Hyperparameters Interact

The three parameters are interrelated:

  • FA and Fb together determine the signal-to-noise ratio in the PLDA model. Increasing FA while keeping Fb constant amplifies speaker differences relative to within-speaker noise.
  • Ploop interacts with FA: when FA is high (strong speaker discrimination), a lower Ploop can be tolerated because the emission model alone provides sufficient evidence for correct assignments. When FA is low, a higher Ploop helps by enforcing temporal continuity.
  • Optimal values depend on the recording condition: telephone conversations (CALLHOME), meetings (AMI), and diverse conditions (DIHARD) each require different tuning.
Additional Configuration Parameters:
  • AHC threshold: Controls the stopping point of initial clustering. Tuned to under-cluster (over-segment).
  • Number of VB iterations: Typically fixed at 10. Most convergence occurs within 3–5 iterations.
  • Maximum initial speakers (S): Set higher than expected. Surplus speakers are pruned by ARD.
  • Minimum segment duration: Post-processing parameter to eliminate implausibly short speaker segments.

DVBx: Discriminative Training of Hyperparameters

A significant limitation of standard VBx is the need for extensive grid search to tune hyperparameters. In 2024, Klement et al. (ICASSP 2024) introduced DVBx — a discriminative training framework where FA, Fb, Ploop, and even the PLDA parameters can be jointly fine-tuned end-to-end to directly minimize diarization error.

DVBx uses a differentiable loss called EDE (Expected Diarization Error) that better correlates with DER than standard binary cross-entropy. This eliminates the need for manual grid search and achieves comparable or better performance to manually tuned systems.

Integration with X-Vector & ECAPA-TDNN Embeddings

VBx's performance depends heavily on the quality of input embeddings. Two architectures dominate:

ResNet101 X-Vectors

  • Used in the original VBx paper
  • Deep residual network trained on VoxCeleb
  • 256-dimensional embeddings
  • Strong baseline performance
  • Well-matched to the PLDA model trained alongside it

ECAPA-TDNN Embeddings

  • Emphasized Channel Attention, Propagation and Aggregation TDNN
  • Multi-scale feature aggregation with channel- and context-dependent attention
  • 192-dimensional embeddings (typical)
  • 34.3% relative DER improvement (4.58% to 3.01%) over ResNet x-vectors with VBx
  • Better at capturing fine-grained speaker characteristics

When upgrading from ResNet to ECAPA-TDNN embeddings, the PLDA model must be retrained on the new embedding space. The VBx hyperparameters may also need re-tuning, as the different embedding geometry affects optimal FA and Fb values.

Self-Supervised Embeddings: Recent work has also explored using WavLM and other self-supervised models as front-ends for VBx. While these can provide richer representations, they require careful adaptation of the PLDA model and VBx hyperparameters to work effectively.

Performance on Standard Benchmarks

VBx has been evaluated extensively on three standard diarization benchmarks, each presenting different challenges:

CALLHOME

  • Telephone conversations
  • 2–7 speakers per call
  • Conversational speech with overlaps
  • Standard benchmark since the 2000s

AMI Meeting Corpus

  • Meeting recordings
  • Typically 4 speakers
  • Beamformed and Mix-Headset conditions
  • Longer recordings with clear turn structure

DIHARD II

  • Diverse, challenging domains
  • Clinical, web video, restaurant, meeting, etc.
  • Highly variable recording conditions
  • Considered the hardest benchmark

VBx vs. EEND and Other Backends

The speaker diarization field has two major paradigms: clustering-based (like VBx) and end-to-end neural diarization (EEND). Understanding their trade-offs is essential for choosing the right approach.

Aspect VBx (Clustering-based) EEND (End-to-End Neural)
Architecture Modular: embedding extractor + PLDA + HMM + VB inference Single neural network (e.g., SA-EEND with self-attention)
Overlapping Speech Cannot handle — single speaker per time step Handles naturally — multiple output labels per frame
Speaker Count Determined automatically via Bayesian ARD; no fixed limit Fixed maximum at training time; struggles with many speakers
Long Recordings Handles naturally — processes full recording at once Requires chunking and stitching; challenging for long meetings
Training Data Embedding extractor and PLDA trained on speaker verification data (abundant) Requires labeled diarization data with speaker timestamps (scarce)
Interpretability High — speaker embeddings, transition probabilities, and assignment posteriors are all interpretable Low — neural network internals are opaque
Computational Cost Efficient — VB inference is fast; main cost is embedding extraction Higher — self-attention is quadratic in sequence length

Hybrid Approaches: The Best of Both Worlds

Recent research increasingly combines VBx with EEND methods to leverage their complementary strengths:

  • EEND-VC (Vector Clustering): Uses EEND for local diarization within short windows, then applies vector clustering (including VBx) to stitch results across windows and handle large speaker counts.
  • DiariZen with cVBx: Replaces standard clustering with constrained VBx (cVBx), achieving state-of-the-art performance by combining EEND's overlap detection with VBx's robust clustering.
  • TS-VAD + VBx: Uses VBx for initial speaker clustering to generate speaker profiles, which then guide a Target-Speaker Voice Activity Detection (TS-VAD) model for final output.
The Emerging Consensus
  • Neither pure VBx nor pure EEND dominates across all conditions.
  • VBx excels when speaker counts are unknown or large, recordings are long, and overlapping speech is minimal.
  • EEND excels at handling overlapping speech and short, well-defined conversations.
  • The best systems combine both: EEND for local analysis with VBx-style clustering for global speaker identity management.

Running VBx

The typical workflow involves:

  1. Extract features and x-vectors using the provided neural network
  2. Compute cosine similarity scores between all x-vector pairs
  3. Run AHC with a configured threshold to get initial labels
  4. Run VBx with specified FA, Fb, and Ploop values
  5. Evaluate the output RTTM against reference using standard scoring tools (dscore, pyannote.metrics)
# Example: Run VBx on CALLHOME
python VBx/vbhmm.py \
  --init-smoothing 7 \
  --lda-dim 128 \
  --Fa 0.3 \
  --Fb 17 \
  --loopP 0.99 \
  --xvec-ark-file xvectors.ark \
  --segments-file segments \
  --plda-file models/plda \
  --xvec-transform models/transform.h5 \
  --output-rttm output.rttm

The discriminatively-trained variant is available at github.com/BUTSpeechFIT/DVBx, which allows end-to-end fine-tuning of all VBx parameters.

Strengths & Weaknesses

Strengths

  • Temporal modeling: Explicitly models speaker turn-taking through HMM transitions, unlike all traditional clustering methods.
  • Automatic speaker count: Determines the number of speakers via Bayesian ARD without requiring it as input.
  • Principled framework: Grounded in Bayesian probability theory with a clear generative model and well-defined inference procedure.
  • Soft assignments: Provides posterior probabilities over speaker assignments, not just hard labels.
  • Efficient: VB inference is fast (seconds per recording); the computational bottleneck is embedding extraction, not clustering.
  • Robust initialization: AHC + VBx two-stage approach is more robust than either method alone.
  • Open source: Freely available reference implementation with pre-trained models.
  • State-of-the-art: Competitive or best results on CALLHOME, AMI, and DIHARD benchmarks.
  • Data efficient: Leverages speaker verification data (abundant) rather than requiring scarce diarization annotations for PLDA training.
  • Scalable speaker counts: No fixed upper bound on number of speakers (unlike EEND).

Weaknesses

  • No overlap handling: The HMM assigns exactly one speaker per time step; cannot represent simultaneous speakers. DER degrades substantially when overlap is scored.
  • Hyperparameter sensitivity: Performance depends heavily on FA, Fb, Ploop, and AHC threshold; optimal values differ across domains.
  • Initialization dependence: VB can converge to local optima; quality of AHC initialization matters.
  • PLDA assumptions: Assumes Gaussian distributions in the embedding space, which may not hold for all embedding types (especially self-supervised features).
  • Cross-domain transfer: PLDA and hyperparameters trained/tuned on one domain may not transfer well to another without re-tuning.
  • Short segments: X-vectors from very short speech segments (<0.5s) are unreliable, limiting temporal resolution.
  • Not end-to-end: Modular pipeline means errors in earlier stages (VAD, embedding extraction) cannot be corrected by VBx.
  • Single-channel only: Standard VBx does not exploit multi-channel information (though MS-VBx extends it).
