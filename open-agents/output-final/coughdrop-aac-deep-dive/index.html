    When Brian Whitmer co-founded Instructure in 2008, he helped build Canvas LMS into one of the most widely-used learning management systems in the world, eventually reaching over 15 million users. But the project that would become most personal to him started not in a boardroom, but at home -- watching his daughter Becca struggle to communicate.

    Becca has Rett Syndrome, a rare neurological disorder that took away her ability to speak and severely limited her hand control. Whitmer's search for an AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication) solution for his daughter led him to a frustrating realization: the AAC industry was decades behind modern technology, filled with expensive proprietary solutions built on outdated software.

    So he built something better. CoughDrop launched in 2014 as a cloud-based, cross-platform, open-source AAC application -- one of the first of its kind. For nearly a decade, it stood as proof that AAC technology could be modern, accessible, and open. Then, in February 2023, everything changed.

    The Origin Story: A Father's Mission

    Brian Whitmer's journey into AAC technology was deeply personal. Around 2012-2013, as his daughter Becca's communication needs grew, he evaluated the available AAC options and found them lacking. The apps were expensive, locked to single devices, and built with what he described as "poor design decisions and old technology." Worse, AAC providers seemed intent on locking customers into proprietary ecosystems -- a pattern Whitmer recognized from his earlier work disrupting the education technology market.

    "Augmentative Communication is a lot like the LMS space was, in need of a reminder of how things can be better."

    -- Brian Whitmer, CoughDrop founder

    Drawing on his background in Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) and the lessons learned from building Canvas, Whitmer founded CoughDrop, Inc. in early 2014. He assembled a remarkable advisory network: over 30 assistive technology specialists, occupational therapists, and speech-language pathologists contributed to the app's design. The goal was clear -- build an AAC solution that was modern, affordable, cross-platform, and open.

    The name "CoughDrop" itself carries meaning. Just as a cough drop soothes the throat and helps one speak more comfortably, the app was designed to ease the path to communication for those who struggle to produce speech.

    A Technical Revolution in AAC

    The Web-First Architecture

    CoughDrop's technical architecture was revolutionary for the AAC space. While competitors like Proloquo2Go and TouchChat were locked to iOS, CoughDrop was built on a modern web stack that could run on essentially any device with a modern web browser.

    This architecture solved one of the most persistent pain points in AAC: device dependency. Traditional AAC apps stored everything locally on a single device. If that device broke, was lost, or simply ran out of battery, the user lost their voice. With CoughDrop, a communicator could log into any device and immediately access all their customized boards, vocabulary, and settings.

    The Open Source Commitment

    On January 19, 2016, CoughDrop took a step that was almost unheard of in the AAC industry: the entire application was published as open-source software under the AGPLv3 license. The source code was made available on GitHub, allowing anyone to inspect, modify, and contribute to the codebase.

    This was not a marketing gimmick. It was a philosophical statement. Whitmer believed that communication is a fundamental human right, and the tools that enable it should not be locked behind proprietary walls. The open-source approach meant:

    • Transparency -- Anyone could audit the code to ensure data privacy and security
    • Community Contribution -- Developers could submit improvements and bug fixes
    • Forks and Derivatives -- Organizations could build custom versions for specific needs
    • Interoperability -- The codebase served as a reference implementation for AAC standards
    • Sustainability -- Even if the company disappeared, the code would live on

    CoughDrop also championed the Open Board Format (.obf), announced in January 2015. This vendor-neutral file format allows communication boards and vocabularies to be exported from one AAC app and imported into another -- a radical concept in an industry where switching costs have traditionally been enormous.

    Features That Set CoughDrop Apart

    Customizable Boards

    Configurable grid sizes from 4 to 112 buttons with multiple symbol sets, custom images, and recorded audio.

    Cloud Sync

    Automatic synchronization across unlimited devices. Changes on one device appear everywhere instantly.

    Team Collaboration

    Free supervisor accounts for parents, SLPs, and teachers. Remote modeling and secure messaging built in.

    Eye Gaze Access

    Compatible with hardware eye trackers and offers built-in camera-based head/eye tracking without extra hardware.

    Data Analytics

    Tracks every button press, generates usage reports, monitors progress over time for clinical documentation.

    Offline Support

    Works fully offline via HTML5 technologies. Changes sync automatically when connectivity returns.

    Quick Core Vocabulary System

    CoughDrop's pre-built Quick Core vocabulary sets represent hundreds of hours of clinical input. Each set maintains consistent motor plans -- the physical location of core words stays the same across board levels, so muscle memory transfers as the user advances.

    Quick Core Vocabulary: Base vs. Expanded Word Counts

    • Quick Core 24 -- 24-button grid with ~600 base words, expandable to ~1,000. Ideal for beginning communicators.
    • Quick Core 60 -- 60-button grid with ~2,000 base words, expandable to ~3,500. For intermediate users.
    • Quick Core 112 -- 112-button grid with ~4,500 base words, expandable to ~12,000. For advanced communicators.

    Cloud Sync and Offline Capability

    CoughDrop's cloud synchronization was a game-changer for the AAC community. Any change made to a board on one device is automatically synced to all connected devices:

    • A therapist can modify vocabulary during a session and have changes appear on the student's home device
    • Parents can add new words in the evening and find them on the classroom device the next morning
    • If a device breaks or is lost, the communicator can log into a replacement and continue without interruption

    Using HTML5 technologies including Application Cache and IndexedDB, the app also stores boards and vocabulary locally. Changes made offline are synced when connectivity is restored.

    Supervisor and Team Collaboration

    CoughDrop recognized that AAC is a team effort. The app provides free supervisor accounts for anyone supporting a communicator -- parents, teachers, SLPs, occupational therapists, and aides.

    • Remote board access -- View and edit boards from your own device
    • Remote modeling -- Demonstrate button selections on the communicator's device in real-time
    • Secure messaging -- Communicate within the platform
    • Data sharing -- Usage logs and reports accessible to all authorized supervisors
    • Board management -- Modify boards without taking the communicator's device

    Eye Gaze and Switch Access

    For users who cannot reliably touch a screen, CoughDrop supports multiple alternative access methods:

    • Hardware eye trackers -- Compatible with Tobii, Forbes Enable Eyes, and others
    • Camera-based tracking -- Uses a device's built-in camera for head/eye tracking without additional hardware
    • Switch scanning -- Support for external switches including the Blue2 for one- and two-button control
    • Dwell activation -- Configurable dwell time for eye gaze or head tracking selection
    Camera-Based Eye Gaze: A Standout Innovation

    CoughDrop's open-source camera-based eye/head tracking allows AAC usage on any mobile device without expensive dedicated hardware. This dramatically lowers the cost barrier for users who need alternative access methods.

    The Acquisition: Forbes AAC and MavWare

    On February 28, 2023, CoughDrop announced that it had been acquired by Forbes AAC, through a newly created subsidiary called MavWare, LLC. Forbes AAC is a manufacturer of dedicated speech-generating devices (SGDs), including the ProSlate series (iOS-based) and WinSlate series (Windows-based).

    The acquisition was framed as a marriage of hardware expertise and software innovation. Forbes' dedicated AAC hardware would gain CoughDrop's sophisticated software platform, while CoughDrop would benefit from Forbes' manufacturing capabilities, distribution network, and relationships with insurance providers.

    What Changed

    The most controversial aspect: Forbes decided to stop releasing future CoughDrop updates under an open-source license.

    "Forbes decided to stop releasing updates with an open-source license... because they own the copyright, they are free to do this for ongoing updates, but the previously-open-sourced code can't be un-open-sourced, so it will still be available online as-is."

    -- CoughDrop blog announcement

    What Was Preserved

    • User-created content (boards, vocabularies, recordings) was unaffected
    • Community board sets like Quick Core retained their Creative Commons licenses
    • The free 2-month trial continued
    • Free supervisor accounts remained available
    • Existing lifetime licenses were honored
    • The Open Board Format remained an open standard

    The Open Source Controversy

    The open-source license change struck many observers as deeply ironic. CoughDrop had been founded specifically in reaction to the AAC industry's proprietary, lock-in-oriented business model. Brian Whitmer had publicly criticized vendors for trying to trap customers in closed ecosystems. The open-source release in 2016 was one of the most celebrated moments in CoughDrop's history.

    Now, under new ownership, CoughDrop was becoming the very thing its founder had set out to disrupt.

    The Community Impact

    No community code contributions. No transparency for security audits. No meaningful independent forks. Users are now fully dependent on Forbes AAC for all updates, bug fixes, and feature development -- the exact vendor lock-in CoughDrop was created to prevent.

    The SweetSuite Fork

    The OpenAAC organization took custody of the existing CoughDrop codebase and renamed it SweetSuite AAC (github.com/open-aac/sweet-suite-aac). The fork represents the last open-source state of CoughDrop before the license change.

    However, the fork has seen limited development activity. Maintaining a complex web application with a Rails backend and Ember frontend requires significant ongoing effort, and the AAC open-source community has limited developer resources. The fork exists more as an archival reference than a viable alternative product.

    Pricing History: CoughDrop's First Increase in 9 Years

    On July 1, 2023, CoughDrop implemented its first price increase since launch. While modest by AAC industry standards -- many competing apps cost significantly more -- the timing, coming just months after the acquisition and license change, drew attention from the community.

    Strengths and Weaknesses

    Strengths

    • True cross-platform -- Runs on 7+ platforms including web, iOS, Android, Windows, Mac, and Chromebook
    • Cloud sync -- Seamless sync across unlimited devices with offline capability
    • Team collaboration -- Best-in-class supervisor tools, remote modeling, and messaging
    • Data analytics -- Comprehensive tracking and reporting for clinical documentation
    • Affordable -- $9/mo or $295 lifetime is cheaper than most AAC alternatives
    • Eye gaze innovation -- Camera-based tracking without expensive hardware
    • Vocabulary depth -- Up to 12,000 words with consistent motor plans
    • Open Board Format -- Interoperable board files reduce switching costs

    Weaknesses

    • Open source loss -- Proprietary transition undermines original mission and community trust
    • Phone experience -- Poor formatting, small buttons, crashes reported on phones
    • Android reliability -- Sync issues and internet connectivity requirements
    • No free tier -- Requires subscription after trial (Cboard is free)
    • Forbes dependency -- Product direction tied to hardware manufacturer
    • Community fragmentation -- SweetSuite fork lacks resources
    • Undocumented API -- The REST API is "completely undocumented"
    • Premium access bugs -- Reports of subscription content access issues

For a family in a developing country or a resource-limited setting, Cboard's free availability is transformative. Both support the Open Board Format, making it possible to migrate vocabularies between them.

    Current Status and Future Outlook

    CoughDrop continues to receive regular updates under Forbes AAC ownership. The integration with Forbes' hardware products -- particularly the ProSlate and WinSlate speech-generating devices -- positions CoughDrop as the software backbone of an integrated AAC ecosystem. Forbes' relationships with insurance providers and Medicaid may also improve access for users who need funded devices.

    However, the long-term trajectory raises questions:

    • Will the product continue to serve the broader AAC community, or will it become primarily a vehicle for selling Forbes hardware?
    • Will pricing remain stable, or will the absence of open-source competition enable further increases?
    • Will the innovative spirit that created features like camera-based eye gaze tracking persist under corporate ownership?

    The AAC community is watching closely. CoughDrop remains one of the most capable and affordable AAC applications available. But the story of its transition from a father's open-source passion project to a proprietary product owned by a hardware manufacturer carries a lesson about the fragility of open-source commitments -- and the ongoing tension between commercial sustainability and community values in assistive technology.

    Key Takeaways

    CoughDrop was a pioneering open-source, web-based AAC app that genuinely disrupted the industry. Its cross-platform cloud architecture, team collaboration tools, and affordable pricing remain strong differentiators. However, the 2023 Forbes AAC acquisition and the end of open-source licensing mark a significant philosophical shift. Users gain the stability of a hardware-backed company but lose the transparency and community ownership that defined CoughDrop's identity.
