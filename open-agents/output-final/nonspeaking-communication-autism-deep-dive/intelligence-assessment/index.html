Intelligence, Assessment & Measuring Non-Speaking Minds When the Test Measures the Barrier, Not the Brain

The IQ score assigned to a non-speaking autistic person cascades through every domain of their life — and the current system has been catastrophically wrong more often than its defenders acknowledge.

Validation Status

This explanation draws on independently validated research. The Dawson/Mottron Raven's finding, Donnellan's least dangerous assumption, the Jaswal 2024 study and its rebuttal, the "profound autism" debate, and Zimmerman's case details were all confirmed. One correction applied: a "stretch boundaries" blog post was misattributed to John Elder Robison; it is by Peter Lloyd-Thomas (Epiphany ASD blog, 2025). The 2025 Cued Looking Paradigm is a bioRxiv preprint, not peer-reviewed.

1. Why Assessment Matters

The IQ score assigned to a non-speaking autistic person is not a neutral data point. It cascades through every domain of their life: educational placement, access to communication technology, eligibility for services, and whether anyone attempts to teach them to read, write, or participate in their own care decisions. For decades, non-speaking autistic individuals have been assessed using instruments that require the very skills they lack — verbal responses, reliable motor output, sustained attention to examiner-directed tasks — producing scores that confirm the prior assumption of intellectual disability in a self-reinforcing cycle.

Key Takeaways
  1. Standard IQ tests systematically underestimate autistic cognitive ability. The Dawson-Mottron finding (2007) demonstrated a 30-percentile-point average discrepancy between WechslerThe most widely used IQ tests (WISC for children, WAIS for adults), including verbal comprehension, working memory, and processing speed components. scores and Raven's Progressive MatricesA nonverbal test of abstract reasoning using visual patterns. The test-taker identifies the missing piece from a matrix of designs. scores for the same autistic children.
  2. The assessment creates the conditions that justify its own findings. Low scores lead to low-expectation placements, which deny cognitive stimulation, which ensures continued low scores on re-assessment.
  3. No current test fully solves the problem. Even "nonverbal" tests require motor responses. Truly motor-free assessment remains an unsolved problem. Eye-tracking approaches are promising but not yet standardized.
  4. The consequences of underestimation are catastrophic and potentially irreversible. Jordyn Zimmerman received no communication tool until age 18. She subsequently earned two degrees, was named to the Forbes Accessibility 100, and became Board Chair of CommunicationFIRST.

2. Why Standard IQ Tests Fail

Figure 1 — What standard IQ tests actually measure: for neurotypical test-takers, barriers are minimal and the score reflects cognitive ability. For non-speaking autistic test-takers, barriers dominate and the score reflects the sum of access failures.

The Wechsler Intelligence ScalesThe most widely used IQ tests worldwide. WISC for children, WAIS for adults. Include Verbal Comprehension, Working Memory, and Processing Speed indices. are the most widely used IQ tests worldwide. They include a Verbal Comprehension Index that requires spoken responses, a Working Memory Index that relies on auditory verbal processing, and a Processing Speed Index that requires rapid motor responses. Every one of these systematically disadvantages non-speaking individuals.

But the problem runs deeper than verbal demands. Even the "nonverbal" Performance IQ subtests require pointing, placing blocks, assembling puzzles, or other fine motor actions. For individuals with motor planning difficulties — apraxiaDifficulty planning and executing voluntary motor movements despite intact motor function. Common in non-speaking autism. and dyspraxia, which are common in non-speaking autism — these demands create a barrier unrelated to the cognitive skill being tested.

Then there are the compliance demands. Standard IQ tests require sitting at a table with an examiner, attending to examiner-directed stimuli, and responding within time limits. These demands presuppose behavioral compliance, joint attention, and social engagement — all of which may be atypical in autism for reasons that have nothing to do with intelligence.

The Core Problem

When you give a standard IQ test to a non-speaking autistic person, you are measuring the sum of barriers — verbal expression demands, motor response demands, compliance demands, instruction comprehension demands, and test-taking convention knowledge — rather than the cognitive ability you are trying to assess. The score tells you almost nothing about the brain behind it.

3. The Dawson-Mottron Finding: A 30-Percentile Gap

Figure 2 — The same autistic children scored 30 percentile points higher on a nonverbal test. The difference is the test, not the child. (Dawson et al. 2007)

In 2007, Michelle Dawson, Isabelle Soulieres, Morton Ann Gernsbacher, and Laurent Mottron published a landmark paper in Psychological Science: "The Level and Nature of Autistic Intelligence." They administered both the Wechsler and Raven's Progressive MatricesA nonverbal test of abstract reasoning using visual patterns. Measures fluid intelligence -- the ability to reason and solve novel problems independent of prior knowledge. to 38 autistic children, 26 autistic adults, and matched non-autistic controls.

These are the same children. The difference is the test, not the child.

Raven's taps fluid intelligenceThe ability to reason and solve novel problems independent of prior knowledge. What Raven's Progressive Matrices measures. — the ability to reason and solve novel problems — without requiring language. The Wechsler, by contrast, heavily weights crystallized intelligenceAccumulated knowledge and skills. What verbal IQ subtests heavily weight. and verbal ability. A follow-up fMRI study (Soulieres et al., 2009) found that autistic participants used distinctive perceptual processing strategies during Raven's, engaging posterior brain regions more heavily than controls.

Important Caveat

The original study included speaking autistic individuals, not non-speaking participants specifically. Even Raven's requires a motor response (pointing to the correct answer), which may be difficult for individuals with severe motor planning difficulties. The finding demonstrates systematic underestimation but the precise magnitude in non-speaking populations remains unknown.

4. The Assessment-Placement-Confirmation Loop

Figure 3 — The self-reinforcing cycle: standard IQ test produces low score, which determines segregated placement, which denies cognitive stimulation, which ensures continued low scores on re-assessment. The cycle can be broken through alternative assessment or presuming competence.

The most insidious aspect of assessment failure is its self-reinforcing nature. A non-speaking autistic child reaches school age and is assessed. On the Wechsler, the child scores IQ below 70 — the "intellectual disability" range. This score enters the child's IEPIndividualized Education Program. Specifies educational goals, services, and placement for a student with disabilities under IDEA.. The IEP team places the child in a segregated "life skills" classroom where goals focus on self-care and behavioral compliance rather than academic content. Literacy instruction is not provided because "the data show" the child cannot learn to read.

Two years later, the child is re-evaluated. The same standardized tests are administered. The child, who has received no academic instruction and no cognitive stimulation beyond basic life skills, performs at the same or lower level. The original placement is confirmed. The cycle continues.

Each re-assessment reinforces the previous one because the educational environment has been shaped by the assessment rather than challenging it. The assessment does not merely measure current ability — it shapes future ability by determining the educational environment. This makes the first assessment a high-stakes, potentially self-fulfilling prophecy.

The cycle can be broken. When a clinician uses an adapted assessment — Raven's, Leiter-3An entirely nonverbal intelligence test. Instructions are given through gestures and demonstration rather than spoken language., or emerging eye-tracking methods — the results may reveal abilities the standard test missed. Or a clinician may invoke the least dangerous assumptionDonnellan's (1984) principle: when data are inconclusive, assume competence rather than incompetence, because the consequences of wrongly presuming incompetence are far more severe. and provide AAC and literacy instruction without waiting for assessment confirmation.

5. The Contested Jaswal Literacy Study

In 2024, Vikram Jaswal, Ariel Lampi, and Allison Stockwell published "Literacy in nonspeaking autistic people" in the journal Autism. The study enrolled 31 non-speaking autistic participants, all with at least one year of experience using letterboard communication through RPM or S2C methods. Participants played a tapping game on an iPad, and 17 of 31 (55%) showed a reaction-time pattern consistent with knowledge of English orthographic conventions — a "literacy signature" — despite most never having received formal literacy instruction.

The study generated immediate controversy. A published rebuttal, "Illusions of literacy in nonspeaking autistic people" (Evidence-Based Communication Assessment and Intervention, 2024), raised several substantial criticisms:

  • All participants were recruited from RPM/S2C programs and regularly used letter boards, so letter familiarity could explain the results without literacy
  • No comparison condition without the experimenter reading sentences aloud
  • No control for potential facilitated influence in participants' communication histories
  • The "literacy signature" could reflect letter familiarity from extensive letterboard use rather than actual literacy
Honest Assessment

The study reveals something about non-speaking participants' letter processing, but whether it constitutes evidence of "literacy" is genuinely disputed. It must be presented alongside the published rebuttal. Neither uncritical acceptance nor reflexive dismissal is warranted.

6. The Presuming Competence Spectrum

Figure 4 — The continuum from presuming incompetence (with devastating consequences) to presuming competence (with different risks). Every position carries trade-offs. Donnellan argues the risks of underestimation are categorically worse.

How should clinicians, educators, and families act when they do not know what a non-speaking person understands?

In 1984, Anne M. Donnellan (1943–2024) published "The Criterion of the Least Dangerous Assumption" in Behavioral Disorders. The core principle: "In the absence of conclusive data, educational decisions should be based on assumptions which, if incorrect, will have the least dangerous effect on the student."

Applied to non-speaking autism: if you do not know whether a non-speaking student can learn to read, it is less dangerous to assume they can (and provide literacy instruction that may be unnecessary) than to assume they cannot (and withhold literacy instruction they may have needed). The consequences of wrongly presuming incompetence — denying education, communication, autonomy, for an entire lifetime — are far more severe than the consequences of wrongly presuming competence.

Peter Lloyd-Thomas, a parent of an autistic son, proposed the "stretch boundaries" middle position on his Epiphany ASD blog in 2025: neither "presume competence" nor "presume incompetence," but continuously test and expand what each individual can do without defaulting to either extreme. This perspective comes from a parent, not from an autistic adult, which shapes the framing.

7. Alternative Assessment Methods

Figure 5 — Comparison of assessment tools rated across barrier dimensions. The Wechsler imposes the highest barriers. Eye-tracking eliminates the most barriers but remains unstandardized.

If standard IQ tests are the wrong instrument, what are the alternatives?

Raven's Progressive Matrices

The nonverbal test that produced the 30-percentile-point advantage. No language demands, low motor demands (pointing). Demonstrated validity for autistic populations. Limitation: still requires pointing, which may be impossible for individuals with severe motor difficulties.

Leiter International Performance Scale (Leiter-3)

Entirely nonverbal. Instructions given through gestures and demonstration rather than spoken language. Described by its publisher as "ideal for use with those who are on the autism spectrum."

Test of Nonverbal Intelligence (TONI-4Test of Nonverbal Intelligence. Minimizes both language and cultural demands. Uses visual patterns requiring pointing or gesturing.)

Minimizes both language and cultural demands. Uses visual patterns requiring pointing or gesturing to indicate the correct answer.

Eye-Tracking-Based Assessment

The most promising emerging approach. A pilot study found that cognitive scores based on eye tracking were closely related to scores from standardized neuropsychological tests. A 2025 Cued Looking Paradigm (currently a bioRxiv preprint, not yet peer-reviewed) showed that 83% of minimally verbal participants demonstrated hidden language abilities through involuntary gaze patterns.

The Unsolved Problem

Even "nonverbal" tests typically require motor responses, sustained attention to examiner-directed tasks, and understanding of implicit test-taking conventions. Truly motor-free, instruction-free assessment remains the holy grail. Eye-tracking approaches this ideal but is not yet standardized or widely available.

The "Best Day" Problem

Non-speaking individuals' performance can vary dramatically across days, settings, emotional states, and the presence or absence of familiar people. A single assessment session captures one snapshot. An individual who performs well in a calm, familiar environment may perform entirely differently in an unfamiliar clinic under fluorescent lights with a stranger asking questions. This variability is not a sign of inconsistent ability — it reflects the sensitivity of performance to context, a well-documented feature of autism. Assessment relying on a single session risks misrepresenting ability in either direction.

8. The "Profound Autism" Label Debate

Figure 6 — The "profound autism" debate: stakeholder positions, with the meta-irony that non-speaking individuals are least represented in the decision-making process about the label applied to them.

In 2021, the Lancet Commission on the Future of Care and Clinical Research of Autism proposed the administrative term "profound autism"Proposed administrative term (Lancet Commission, 2021) for autistic individuals who cannot live independently and require 24-hour care. 2025 INSAR consensus criteria established through a Delphi process. for autistic individuals who cannot live independently and require 24-hour care.

Arguments For

Parents of high-support-needs children argue that their children's needs are invisible when grouped with the entire autism spectrum. A distinct category could drive targeted research funding and service provision. They describe children who fall "off the cliff" of services at age 21.

Arguments Against

Research has found the label "does not predict strengths or help plan supports" (PMC10576471, 2023). It has been called "isolating, dehumanizing, and misleading" (MDPI, 2023). ASAN and other organizations oppose it as reviving discredited functioning categories under a new name.

The Meta-Irony

Non-speaking autistic individuals — the people most directly affected by the label — are the least represented in the decision-making process about whether and how it should be applied. A label partly justified by lack of communication was developed without full communication access for those it labels.

10. The Zimmerman Case Study

Figure 7 — Jordyn Zimmerman's trajectory: 18 years without communication access followed by academic and advocacy achievements that demonstrate definitively what becomes possible when the assessment barrier is removed.

Jordyn Zimmerman's trajectory is the single most important illustration in this entire domain — not because it is typical, but because it demonstrates definitively what becomes possible when the assessment barrier is removed.

For 18 years, Zimmerman was placed in segregated "life skills" classrooms. No one gave her a communication tool. She received no literacy instruction. Her assessments presumably classified her as having significant intellectual disability.

At age 18, she received Proloquo2Go — a standard AAC app on an iPad. She used it independently from the start, with no facilitator involvement. This is not a contested communication method.

After receiving communication access:

  • Bachelor's degree in Education Policy from Ohio University
  • Master's of Education from Boston College
  • Board Chair of CommunicationFIRST
  • Apple Distinguished Educator
  • Two-time appointee to the U.S. President's Committee for People with Intellectual Disabilities
  • Named to the inaugural 2025 Forbes Accessibility 100
  • Published in The New York Times and The Boston Globe
What the Case Proves

Her intelligence did not change at age 18. Her brain at 17 was the same brain at 19. What changed was access. The assessment system that classified her as intellectually disabled was catastrophically wrong, and the consequences of that error spanned 18 years.

Her case does not prove all non-speaking individuals have hidden academic ability. But it proves that the current assessment system can be catastrophically wrong, that the consequences of underestimation are devastating and irreversible, and that the ceiling of possibility is far higher than standard assessment suggests.

11. The Unresolved Question

The tension at the heart of this domain remains unresolved.

The "presume competence" position demands that every non-speaking individual receive communication access and academic instruction regardless of assessment scores. The evidence strongly supports this: standard assessments are unreliable, the consequences of underestimation are devastating, and individual cases demonstrate extraordinary untapped potential.

The "measure accurately" position demands better tools and more rigorous evidence before making population-level claims about hidden competence. Critics note that individual success stories cannot establish what proportion of non-speaking autistic people have dramatically underestimated abilities.

Both positions contain truth. The answer is not to choose between them but to pursue both simultaneously: presume competence and act accordingly (provide communication, provide education, expect growth), while simultaneously developing better assessment tools that can measure abilities without barriers.

The Worst Response

The worst response — and the historical default — is to do neither: to withhold communication access because assessments say a person cannot learn, while relying on assessments that cannot measure what they claim to measure. Until assessment tools exist that can measure non-speaking minds without requiring those minds to speak, point, sit still, and follow verbal instructions, every IQ score assigned to a non-speaking autistic person should be treated as provisional at best and misleading at worst.

12. Edge Cases and Honest Complications

Co-occurring intellectual disability is real

Not everyone whose abilities are hard to measure has hidden competence. Some individuals have cognitive limitations that affect their capacity across domains. The challenge is that current assessment tools cannot reliably distinguish between genuine intellectual disability and test-induced underestimation. Even on the Raven's, 5% of autistic children in the Dawson-Mottron sample scored in the intellectual disability range.

High Raven's scores do not guarantee functional abilities

Intelligence is not a single dimension. A person who demonstrates strong visual-spatial reasoning may still struggle with language processing, executive function, or daily living skills. Assessment should identify specific abilities and needs, not produce a single number that determines a person's entire educational trajectory.

Late-life cognitive changes

Some non-speaking autistic individuals experience cognitive changes in adulthood, including regression, catatonia, and skills loss. Assessment methods designed for stable ability levels may not capture dynamic trajectories.

The intersection with poverty, race, and culture

Non-speaking autistic individuals in low-resource settings may never receive any form of adapted assessment. Children of color and children in poverty are already more likely to be placed in restrictive educational settings, and the additional barrier of non-speaking autism compounds this disparity. The intersection is virtually unstudied, but the consequences are likely severe.
