Symbol-Based Communication PECS, Makaton, and Visual Support Systems

How pictures, symbols, and signs serve as communication media for non-speaking autistic individuals — and why most programs stall at teaching requesting

Validation Status

This explanation draws on research that was independently validated with an overall confidence rating of 9/10. All major claims confirmed. Two corrections incorporated: Makaton's origin (Botleys Park Hospital) and Blissymbols vocabulary count (6,183 from 2025 BCI authorized vocabulary).

1. What Is Symbol-Based Communication?

Symbol-based communication systems use pictures, symbols, signs, or visual supports as the primary medium of expression for individuals who cannot rely on speech. These systems are often the first form of AACAugmentative and Alternative Communication: tools, strategies, and techniques that supplement or replace spoken communication. introduced to non-speaking autistic children, and they remain in widespread use across educational settings worldwide.

The major systems — PECSPicture Exchange Communication System: a structured six-phase behavioral teaching program developed by Bondy and Frost (1985) that teaches communication through physical picture exchange., MakatonMakaton: a communication system using simplified signs from national sign languages, graphic symbols, and speech together. Developed at Botleys Park Hospital in the 1970s., and BlissymbolsBlissymbols (Blissymbolics): a compositional graphical language of 6,183 symbols built from approximately 120 base elements that can be combined to construct complex meanings. — differ in their theoretical foundations, teaching methods, modalities, and evidence bases, but they share a common premise: that visual representation can bridge the gap between communicative intent and expression.

Why does it exist? Many non-speaking autistic individuals have relative strengths in visual-spatial processing. Speech is transient — once spoken, it disappears. Symbols are persistent — a picture on a card or screen stays visible, can be examined, pointed to, and exchanged. Symbol-based communication capitalizes on this persistence and on documented autistic cognitive strengths in visual processing to create a communication channel that works with, rather than against, the individual's neurological profile.

Key Takeaways
  • PECS is the most rigorously studied symbol-based system for autism, with multiple RCTs and meta-analyses
  • Makaton is the most widely adopted system in the UK (100,000+ users) but lacks proportionate controlled evaluation
  • Blissymbols offers the highest compositionalityCompositionality: the property of a system where complex meanings can be built from simpler elements according to systematic rules. (building complex meanings from ~120 base elements) but has seen limited adoption in autism
  • ARASAAC demonstrates the best iconicityIconicity: the degree to which a symbol visually resembles its referent. High iconicity means the symbol looks like what it represents. across grammatical categories and is free and open-source
  • Most programs stall at teaching requesting — the full range of communicative functions requires intentional programming beyond basic phases

2. PECS (Picture Exchange Communication System)

PECS was developed in 1985 by Andy Bondy, PhD, and Lori Frost, MS, CCC-SLP, at the Delaware Autism Program. It is grounded in applied behavior analysis (ABA) principles and B.F. Skinner's analysis of verbal behavior — specifically, the concept of the mandMand: in Skinner's verbal behavior analysis, a request motivated by a need and reinforced by receiving what was requested. The most basic communicative function. (a verbal operant motivated by a need and reinforced by receiving what was requested).

PECS is not "pointing at pictures." It is a structured behavioral teaching program with six specific phases, each building a distinct communicative skill.

Figure 1: PECS Six Phases — progression from basic exchange (Phase I) through picture discrimination (Phase III) to spontaneous commenting (Phase VI). Many programs stall at Phase III, never reaching the higher communicative functions.

The Six Phases

Phase I: How to Communicate. The learner picks up a picture of a desired item and physically hands it to a communication partner, who immediately provides the item. A physical prompter behind the learner guides the exchange initially (errorless learning). No verbal prompts are used. The critical skill is communicative initiation — the learner must travel to the partner and make an exchange, not just point.

Phase II: Distance and Persistence. The learner learns to travel increasing distances to reach their communication partner and communication book. This teaches persistence and generalization.

Phase III: Picture Discrimination. The learner selects the correct picture from an array of multiple options. This requires discriminating between symbols to request a specific item.

Phase IV: Sentence Structure. The learner constructs sentences on a sentence strip, combining an "I want" symbol with an item picture to form a two-part message. This introduces combinatorial communication.

Phase V: Responding to Questions. The learner uses pictures to answer "What do you want?" — a shift from self-initiated requesting to responsive communication.

Phase VI: Commenting. The learner uses pictures to respond to "What do you see?" "What do you hear?" and to make spontaneous comments. This represents the transition from instrumental communication to referential communication. In Skinner's framework, this is the tactTact: in Skinner's verbal behavior analysis, a label or comment on something in the environment. Referential communication rather than instrumental..

The Phase III Stall

Critical Limitation

A critical limitation of PECS in practice is that many programs stall at or before Phase III. Requesting is taught, but the higher communicative functions — commenting, questioning, narrating, expressing emotions — require intentional programming that many implementation settings do not provide. Many PECS users are taught to get their basic needs met but not to share observations, ask questions, tell jokes, or express how they feel.

Evidence base: A meta-analysis (Flippin et al., 2010, ASHA) found PECS effective for increasing communication frequency but noted that evidence for speech production gains was weaker. A 2025 systematic review of 37 RCTs in China showed significant improvements across cognitive function, social skills, and mental health. PECS has the strongest evidence base among dedicated symbol systems for autism.

3. Makaton

Makaton was developed in the 1970s at Botleys Park Hospital in Chertsey, Surrey, UK, by Margaret Walker, Katherine Johnston, and Tony Cornforth (the name "Makaton" is derived from their first names: MArgaret, KAtherine, TONy).

Makaton uses key word signing — simplified signs from British Sign Language (or the national sign language of the country) used alongside speech to emphasize important words. It is not a full sign language. A Makaton user would sign the key words while speaking a full sentence: "Do you WANT [sign] JUICE [sign] or WATER [sign]?"

Makaton combines three modalities: manual signs, graphic symbols, and speech used simultaneously. Many individuals naturally drop signs as speech develops — Makaton often functions as a bridge to spoken language rather than a permanent alternative.

Adoption: Over 100,000 children and adults use Makaton in the UK, with programs in more than 40 countries. It has been popularized through BBC's children's programming (Something Special, featuring Mr. Tumble).

Evidence base: Makaton generally shows positive effects on communication and speech development. However, the program was adopted on a massive scale without the kind of rigorous controlled evaluation its reach warrants. Most evidence comes from UK clinical practice and observational studies rather than randomized controlled trials.

4. Blissymbols

Blissymbols is a compositional graphical language developed by Charles K. Bliss (1897–1985) between 1942 and 1949 while he was a refugee, first at the Shanghai Ghetto and then in Sydney. Inspired by Chinese characters, Bliss created a system of symbols built from approximately 100–120 base elements that can be combined according to systematic rules to construct complex meanings.

The current Blissymbolics Communication International (BCI) authorized vocabulary contains 6,183 approved symbols (2025 BCI-AV), all constructed from those base elements.

How compositionality works: Unlike picture systems where each concept requires a separate image, Blissymbols allow users to construct novel meanings by combining elements. The symbol for "house" combined with the symbol for "knowledge" produces "school." The symbol for "water" combined with "container" produces "cup" or "glass." A user who knows the base elements and the composition rules can construct meanings they have never been explicitly taught — a generative capacity that no fixed-picture system provides.

Potential alignment with autistic cognition: The compositional, rule-based, pattern-driven nature of Blissymbols may align well with the systematic cognitive style associated with autism — but this hypothesis has not been formally tested. It represents a research opportunity.

5. How Symbol Transparency Affects Learning

Symbol transparencyTransparency (guessability): how easily a symbol's meaning can be guessed without instruction. Transparent symbols are immediately obvious; opaque symbols must be explicitly taught. — how easily a symbol's meaning can be guessed without instruction — directly affects learnability. Research distinguishes three levels:

Transparent symbols (high iconicity): The symbol visually resembles its referent so closely that meaning is immediately guessable. A photograph of a cup means "cup." No teaching needed.

Translucent symbols: The connection between symbol and meaning can be understood once explained but is not immediately obvious. A line drawing of a cup requires some inference.

Opaque symbols: The connection must be explicitly taught. Blissymbols for most abstract concepts are opaque — you cannot guess their meaning from looking at them.

6. The Communicative Functions Gap

One of the most significant limitations across all symbol-based systems is the gap between what is typically taught and the full range of human communication.

Figure 4: Communicative Functions Hierarchy — the gap between what symbol-based programs typically teach (requesting) and the full range of human communication (commenting, questioning, narrating, humor, emotion).

Requesting (Skinner's mand) is the most commonly taught function and has the strongest evidence base. A child learns to exchange a picture for a desired item. This is functional and important — getting basic needs met reduces frustration and challenging behavior.

But human communication extends far beyond requesting. Rejecting (saying no), social routines (greetings, turn-taking), commenting (sharing observations), questioning (requesting information), and narrating (telling stories, explaining, expressing emotions, humor) all represent communicative functions that most symbol-based programs do not systematically address.

The gap matters because a person who can only request is communicating instrumentally but not socially. They can get crackers but cannot say "I love you," cannot observe "that cloud looks like a dog," cannot ask "when is Dad coming home," and cannot express "I feel anxious." Advancing beyond requesting requires intentional, systematic programming — and many implementation settings lack the resources, training, or mandate to provide it.

7. Developmental Pathways

Symbol-based communication leads to different outcomes for different individuals, and all outcomes represent successful communication.

Figure 5: Four equally valid developmental pathways from symbol-based communication — bridge to speech, bridge to literacy, lifelong primary mode, or bridge to high-tech AAC.

Path A: Bridge to speech. For some individuals, symbols reduce communicative frustration, creating conditions favorable to speech emergence. As speech becomes more reliable, symbol use naturally decreases.

Path B: Bridge to literacy. Symbol understanding can transfer to written word recognition. An individual who learns that a particular PCS symbol means "go" may subsequently learn that the written word "go" means the same thing.

Path C: Lifelong primary mode. For some individuals, symbols remain the primary communication method throughout life, with vocabulary expanding and communicative sophistication increasing over time. This is a valid and successful outcome.

Path D: Bridge to high-tech AAC. Symbol skills learned through low-tech methods (PECS, communication boards) transfer to high-tech AAC apps and speech-generating devices.

8. Visual Supports: The Broader Ecosystem

Beyond formal symbol systems, a broader ecosystem of visual supports structures the communicative environment for autistic individuals:

Visual schedulesVisual schedule: a sequence of pictures or symbols showing the order of activities, providing predictability and reducing transition anxiety. — sequences of pictures or symbols showing the order of activities — provide predictability and reduce transition anxiety. Strong evidence supports their effectiveness in reducing challenging behavior and increasing independence.

(Carol Gray, 1991) — short narratives using text and pictures to explain social situations and expected behaviors — have moderate evidence for effectiveness.

Choice boards, first-then boards, and visual timers provide concrete, persistent visual information that supports communication, decision-making, and self-regulation.

These supports work because autistic cognition often favors persistent visual information over transient auditory information. Speech disappears as soon as it is spoken. A visual schedule stays visible. This persistence provides the processing time that many autistic individuals need to understand and respond to communicative demands.

9. Common Misconceptions

Misconception: "PECS is just pointing at pictures"

Reality: PECS is a structured six-phase behavioral teaching program. The physical exchange mechanism — picking up a picture and handing it to a partner — teaches communicative initiation in a way that pointing does not. The exchange requires the learner to travel to the partner, gain attention, and complete a social transaction.

Misconception: "Using signs or symbols will discourage speech"

Reality: Research across PECS and Makaton consistently shows that symbol and sign use does not inhibit speech and may facilitate it. The mechanism likely involves reduced communicative frustration and the provision of a visual scaffold for language.

Misconception: "Symbol-based communication is only for young children or people with intellectual disability"

Reality: Symbol-based systems are used across the lifespan and across cognitive profiles. Many adults use symbol-based AAC as their primary communication mode. Visual supports benefit autistic individuals regardless of cognitive ability.

10. Edge Cases and Exceptions

PECS and motor difficulties: The physical exchange mechanism in PECS requires sufficient motor coordination to pick up a picture and hand it to a partner. Individuals with severe motor impairments may need adapted versions or alternative systems.

Cultural variation in symbol transparency: A symbol that is iconic in one cultural context may be opaque in another. Most commercially available symbol sets reflect Western cultural contexts, creating potential barriers for families from other cultural backgrounds.

Digital-native symbols: Younger AAC users may be more familiar with emojis and digital icons than traditional symbol sets, raising questions about whether standard AAC symbol libraries need updating for contemporary users.
