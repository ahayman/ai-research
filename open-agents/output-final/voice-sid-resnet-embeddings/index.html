ResNet-Based Speaker Embeddings

How 2D convolutional networks transform spectrograms into discriminative speaker representations -- from VGGVox to Squeeze-and-Excitation ResNets on VoxCeleb benchmarks.

Speaker Identification Deep Learning 2D CNN Architectures March 2026

Overview

ResNet-based speaker embeddings represent one of the most successful families of deep neural network architectures for speaker identification and verification. Inspired by the dramatic success of Residual Networks (ResNets) in image recognition, researchers at the Visual Geometry Group (VGG) at Oxford adapted these architectures to treat audio spectrograms as single-channel images, enabling powerful 2D convolutional feature extraction for speaker discrimination.

The core idea is deceptively simple: compute a spectrogram from raw audio, feed it into a 2D CNN originally designed for images, and train the network to produce a compact embedding vector (typically 128-512 dimensions) that captures the speaker's vocal identity. Distances in this embedding space directly correspond to speaker similarity.

Key Insight

Unlike 1D approaches (TDNN/x-vectors) that treat audio frames as sequential features, 2D ResNet approaches treat spectrograms as images. This allows the network to learn joint time-frequency patterns -- capturing formant structures, harmonic relationships, and spectral envelope shapes that are distinctive to individual speakers.

How 2D CNNs Process Spectrograms as Images

The fundamental insight behind ResNet-based speaker embeddings is that a spectrogram -- a 2D representation of audio with frequency on one axis and time on the other -- can be processed exactly like a grayscale image. Each pixel's intensity represents the energy at a particular frequency and time step.

Spectrogram Computation

The input pipeline typically works as follows:

  1. Raw audio is segmented into short overlapping frames (e.g., 25ms windows with 10ms hop).
  2. A Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) is applied to each frame, producing a magnitude spectrogram.
  3. The spectrogram (or sometimes log-mel filterbank features) is treated as a single-channel 2D image with dimensions [Frequency bins x Time frames].
  4. Mean and variance normalization is applied per frequency bin -- no voice activity detection, silence removal, or other speech-specific preprocessing is required.

VGGVox Input Format

In the original VGGVox system, input spectrograms have dimensions 512 x 300 (512 frequency bins by 300 time frames), corresponding to approximately 3 seconds of audio. The network can accept variable-length inputs due to its fully convolutional design with an average pooling layer over the time dimension.

Why 2D CNNs Excel at Speaker Discrimination

Standard 2D convolutions slide a small kernel (e.g., 3x3 or 7x7) across both the frequency and time axes of the spectrogram simultaneously. This gives them a fundamentally different inductive bias compared to 1D approaches:

Local Receptive Fields in Both Dimensions

A 2D convolutional kernel at position (f, t) in the spectrogram sees a small patch spanning a few frequency bins and a few time frames. This means it naturally learns to detect:

  • Frequency-local patterns: Formant peaks, harmonic spacing, and spectral tilt within a narrow band.
  • Time-local patterns: Onset/offset transients, micro-prosody, and within-phoneme dynamics.
  • Joint time-frequency patterns: Frequency modulations, formant transitions, and spectro-temporal modulation features that encode speaker identity.

Hierarchical Feature Learning

As features pass through successive convolutional layers and residual blocks:

  1. Early layers learn low-level spectro-temporal edges and textures (similar to Gabor-like filters in vision).
  2. Middle layers combine these into phoneme-level spectral patterns, formant contours, and harmonic structures.
  3. Deep layers capture high-level speaker identity features that are invariant to phonetic content, encoding the distinctive "voice quality" that makes a speaker recognizable.
2D vs 1D Convolution on Spectrograms

A 1D convolution along the time axis treats each frequency bin independently and cannot model cross-frequency relationships within its kernel. A 2D convolution naturally captures correlations between frequency bins, which is crucial for detecting formant patterns and harmonic series -- both key speaker-discriminative features.

VGGVox Architecture

VGGVox was introduced by the Visual Geometry Group at Oxford University as part of the VoxCeleb project. It was the first large-scale CNN system to demonstrate that speaker identification could be treated as an image classification problem, achieving strong results by directly applying VGG-style networks to spectrograms.

VGG-M Based Trunk (VGGVox v1)

The original VGGVox used a modified VGG-M architecture -- a lighter variant of VGGNet designed for efficiency. Key modifications for speech:

VGGVox (VGG-M Trunk) Layer Architecture

Conv1 96 filters, 7x7 kernel, stride 2, BN + ReLU 253 x 148
MaxPool1 3x3 pool, stride 2 126 x 74
Conv2 256 filters, 5x5 kernel, stride 2, BN + ReLU 62 x 36
MaxPool2 3x3 pool, stride 2 30 x 17
Conv3 384 filters, 3x3 kernel, pad 1, BN + ReLU 30 x 17
Conv4 256 filters, 3x3 kernel, pad 1, BN + ReLU 30 x 17
Conv5 256 filters, 3x3 kernel, pad 1, BN + ReLU 30 x 17
FC6 9x1 conv (frequency), avg pool over time 1 x 1 x 4096
FC7 / Agg Fully connected 1024-d, L2 normalization 1024-d embedding

Key Design Decisions

  • Modified FC6 layer: The standard VGG-M fc6 layer (9x8 kernel) was replaced with a 9x1 convolution over frequency followed by average pooling over time. This makes the network invariant to temporal position but not frequency -- critical since frequency position carries speaker identity information while temporal position does not.
  • Parameter reduction: This modification reduced parameters from 319M (standard VGG-M) to approximately 67M, significantly mitigating overfitting.
  • Batch normalization: Applied before ReLU activations throughout, aiding training convergence on the spectrogram domain.
  • No speech preprocessing: Only per-frequency-bin mean/variance normalization -- no VAD, silence removal, or hand-crafted features.

VGGVox v2: ResNet Trunk

The second generation of VGGVox (introduced with VoxCeleb2) replaced the VGG-M trunk with modified ResNet-34 and ResNet-50 architectures, yielding substantial improvements. These ResNet variants became the foundation for what is now called "Thin ResNet-34" in the speaker recognition community.

Evaluation Protocols

VoxCeleb1 defines three standard evaluation protocols of increasing difficulty:

  • VoxCeleb1-O (Original): The standard test pairs. Most commonly reported benchmark.
  • VoxCeleb1-E (Extended): A larger set of test pairs covering more speakers.
  • VoxCeleb1-H (Hard): Pairs where both speakers are of the same gender and nationality, making discrimination more challenging.
Construction Pipeline

Both datasets were created semi-automatically: (1) Identify celebrity names from public lists, (2) Download YouTube interview videos, (3) Use a CNN-based active speaker detection system to locate face tracks with aligned audio, (4) Verify speaker identity using face recognition. This pipeline produces naturalistic, noisy, real-world speech data -- far more challenging than studio recordings.

Thin ResNet-34 Architecture

The "Thin ResNet-34" is a modified version of the standard ResNet-34 from image recognition, adapted for speaker verification spectrograms. It is called "thin" because the channel widths are reduced compared to the original ResNet-34, keeping the model compact while maintaining strong performance.

Architecture Details

Thin ResNet-34 Layer Configuration

Conv1 64 filters, 7x7 kernel, stride 2, BN + ReLU H/2 x W/2 x 64
MaxPool 3x3 pool, stride 2 H/4 x W/4 x 64
Stage 1 3 residual blocks, 32 channels, 3x3 convs H/4 x W/4 x 32
Stage 2 4 residual blocks, 64 channels, stride 2 downsample H/8 x W/8 x 64
Stage 3 6 residual blocks, 128 channels, stride 2 downsample H/16 x W/16 x 128
Stage 4 3 residual blocks, 128 channels, stride 2 downsample H/32 x W/32 x 128
Aggregation NetVLAD / GhostVLAD / ASP pooling K x D vector
FC + Norm Fully connected, L2 normalization 256-512d embedding

Why "Thin"?

The standard ResNet-34 for ImageNet uses channel widths of [64, 128, 256, 512] across its four stages. The Thin ResNet-34 for speaker verification reduces these to [32, 64, 128, 128] (or similar reduced configurations). This "thinning" serves multiple purposes:

  • Reduced parameters: Speaker spectrograms have less visual complexity than natural images, so fewer channels suffice.
  • Faster training: The model trains efficiently on VoxCeleb2's ~1M utterances.
  • Better generalization: Fewer parameters reduce overfitting risk on limited speaker data.
  • Practical deployment: Smaller models are feasible for real-time speaker verification.

Residual Connections

Each residual block in Thin ResNet-34 follows the standard ResNet pattern:

output = ReLU(BN(Conv(ReLU(BN(Conv(input))))) + input)

The skip (identity) connections allow gradients to flow directly through the network, enabling training of the 34-layer deep architecture without vanishing gradient issues. At stage boundaries where spatial dimensions change, a 1x1 convolution with stride 2 adapts the shortcut path.

Performance Highlight

Despite being shallower and having fewer parameters than ResNet-50, Thin ResNet-34 with NetVLAD aggregation achieved an EER of 3.57% on VoxCeleb1, outperforming the previous VGGVox VGG-M baseline (4.19% EER) by a significant margin. Adding AM-Softmax loss further improved this to 3.22% EER with GhostVLAD aggregation.

Strategic SE Placement

Research has shown that applying SE blocks only in the first stages of the ResNet can better capture speaker information for verification tasks. Early layers benefit most from channel attention because they process lower-level spectral features where frequency-channel emphasis directly relates to formant and harmonic selection. In later layers, the features are already highly abstract and channel re-weighting provides diminishing returns.

Squeeze-and-Excitation: A Deep Dive

Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) blocks, introduced by Hu et al. (CVPR 2018), add a lightweight channel-attention mechanism to any CNN. For speaker recognition, they provide a particularly elegant solution: dynamically amplifying frequency channels that carry speaker identity while suppressing noise and content-dependent channels.

The Two Operations

1. Squeeze: Global Information Aggregation

The "squeeze" operation performs global average pooling across the spatial (time x frequency) dimensions of each feature map channel, producing a channel descriptor vector of size C (one scalar per channel):

z_c = (1 / H*W) * sum(x_c(i,j))   for all (i,j) positions

This compresses each 2D feature map into a single number representing its "average activation level." The squeeze step gives the network a global receptive field -- even in early layers, each channel's descriptor reflects information from the entire spectrogram, not just the local convolutional receptive field.

2. Excitation: Adaptive Channel Re-weighting

The "excitation" operation learns to predict which channels are most important for the current input, using a small bottleneck fully-connected network:

s = Sigmoid(W2 * ReLU(W1 * z))

Where W1 reduces dimensionality by ratio r (typically r=8 or r=16), and W2 restores it. The sigmoid output s is a vector of per-channel scaling factors between 0 and 1. Each channel of the feature map is then multiplied by its corresponding scale factor.

Why SE Matters for Speaker Recognition

  • Formant emphasis: Channels that detect formant-frequency patterns can be amplified for speakers with distinctive vocal tract resonances.
  • Noise suppression: Channels activated primarily by background noise or channel artifacts can be attenuated.
  • Content invariance: Channels that fire for specific phonetic content (rather than speaker identity) receive lower weights.
  • Negligible overhead: SE blocks add less than 1% extra parameters and computation while consistently improving EER by 5-15% relative.
Reduction Ratio Trade-off

The reduction ratio r in the SE bottleneck controls the model capacity vs. efficiency trade-off. Larger r values (e.g., r=16) reduce parameters but may lose fine-grained channel relationships. Smaller r values (e.g., r=4) provide more expressive attention but increase computation. r=8 is the most common choice in speaker verification systems, balancing discrimination power with efficiency.

Pooling & Aggregation Strategies

A critical component of any speaker embedding system is the aggregation layer that converts variable-length frame-level features (output of the CNN trunk) into a fixed-dimensional utterance-level embedding. The choice of aggregation strategy has a major impact on performance.

Method Mechanism Output Dim Strengths
TAP Temporal Average Pooling: simple mean over time D Simple, fast, good baseline
TSTP Temporal Statistics Pooling: concatenated mean + std over time 2D Captures variability; widely used
SAP Self-Attentive Pooling: attention-weighted mean over time D Focuses on informative frames
ASP Attentive Statistics Pooling: attention-weighted mean + std 2D Best of SAP + statistics; strong results
NetVLAD Differentiable VLAD encoding with K learned cluster centers K x D Rich encoding of temporal feature distribution
GhostVLAD NetVLAD + "ghost" clusters that absorb irrelevant info K x D (K < total) Filters noise; best with Thin ResNet

NetVLAD: The Aggregation That Defined Thin ResNet-34

NetVLAD (Vector of Locally Aggregated Descriptors) was a key innovation that propelled Thin ResNet-34 to state-of-the-art performance. It works by learning K cluster centers and encoding each frame's residual (difference from its nearest cluster center) into the output:

  1. For each frame-level feature vector x_t, compute soft-assignment weights to K clusters.
  2. For each cluster k, accumulate the weighted residuals (x_t - c_k).
  3. Concatenate all K residual vectors into a single K*D descriptor.
  4. Apply L2 normalization (intra-cluster then global).

GhostVLAD extends this by adding "ghost" clusters that absorb noisy or irrelevant frames, preventing them from polluting the speaker embedding.

Aggregation Impact

Switching from simple average pooling to NetVLAD on the same Thin ResNet-34 trunk improved EER from approximately 4.5% to 3.57% on VoxCeleb1 -- a relative improvement of over 20%, demonstrating that aggregation strategy is as important as the trunk architecture itself.

Training & Loss Functions

Training ResNet-based speaker embedding systems involves choosing between classification-based and metric-learning-based loss functions. Modern systems almost universally use angular margin losses, which push embeddings to be well-separated on a hypersphere.

Classification-Based Losses

Softmax Cross-Entropy

The baseline approach: add a classification head with N outputs (one per training speaker) and train with standard softmax cross-entropy. The embedding is extracted from the penultimate layer at inference time. Simple but effective, though it does not explicitly optimize the embedding geometry.

AM-Softmax (Additive Margin Softmax)

Adds a fixed angular margin m to the target speaker's cosine similarity before the softmax:

L = -log( exp(s*(cos(theta_y) - m)) / (exp(s*(cos(theta_y) - m)) + sum_j exp(s*cos(theta_j))) )

This forces the network to learn more discriminative embeddings by requiring a larger angular separation between speakers. Parameters: margin m (typically 0.2) and scale s (typically 30).

AAM-Softmax (Additive Angular Margin Softmax / ArcFace)

Adds the margin to the angle itself rather than the cosine, producing a geometrically more uniform angular margin:

L = -log( exp(s*cos(theta_y + m)) / (exp(s*cos(theta_y + m)) + sum_j exp(s*cos(theta_j))) )

AAM-Softmax is currently the most popular loss for speaker verification training, consistently outperforming plain softmax by 20-40% in relative EER reduction.

Metric Learning Losses

Loss Mechanism Notes
Triplet Loss Minimize d(anchor, positive) while maximizing d(anchor, negative) Hard to tune; requires careful mining
Prototypical Classify into speaker classes using prototype (mean) vectors Effective with multiple negatives per batch
GE2E Generalized End-to-End loss: similarity matrix across batch Used in Google's speaker systems

Training Recipe

A typical strong training recipe for ResNet-34 speaker embeddings: Train on VoxCeleb2 dev set (5,994 speakers), use AAM-Softmax loss (m=0.2, s=30), SGD optimizer with cosine annealing learning rate schedule, 2-second random crops for data augmentation, online noise/reverb augmentation (MUSAN + RIR datasets), and fine-tune with large-margin on longer utterances.

How Local Frequency Patterns Enable Speaker Discrimination

The power of 2D CNNs for speaker recognition lies in their ability to detect and combine local spectro-temporal patterns that are characteristic of individual speakers. Understanding what these patterns represent acoustically reveals why this approach is so effective.

Formant Frequencies and Vocal Tract Shape

Every person has a unique vocal tract -- the air passage from the larynx through the mouth and nose. This produces characteristic formant frequencies (resonance peaks) that appear as concentrated energy bands in spectrograms. The first two formants (F1 and F2) primarily encode vowel identity, while higher formants (F3-F5) encode speaker-specific vocal tract geometry.

2D convolutional filters naturally learn to detect these horizontal energy bands and the spacing between them. A 3x3 kernel spanning a few frequency bins can detect formant peaks, while deeper layers learn the characteristic formant spacing ratios that distinguish one speaker from another.

Harmonic Structure and Pitch

When a speaker produces voiced sounds, the vocal cords vibrate at a fundamental frequency (F0), creating harmonics at integer multiples. In a spectrogram, these appear as evenly-spaced horizontal lines. The spacing between harmonics encodes:

  • Average pitch: Higher F0 means wider harmonic spacing.
  • Pitch variability: Changes in harmonic spacing over time reflect prosodic patterns.
  • Voice quality: The relative amplitudes of harmonics encode breathiness, creakiness, and other quality markers.

Spectral Tilt and Source Characteristics

The rate at which spectral energy decreases from low to high frequencies (spectral tilt) is speaker-dependent, reflecting glottal pulse shape and vocal effort. 2D CNN filters in early layers learn these global spectral slope features, while deeper layers combine them with formant information for robust speaker discrimination.

What the Network Learns at Each Depth

Layers 1-2 (Early)

Edge detectors in time-frequency space: horizontal edges (formant boundaries), vertical edges (onset/offset), diagonal edges (frequency sweeps). Similar to Gabor filters in visual cortex.

Layers 3-8 (Low-Mid)

Harmonic patterns, formant bandwidth detectors, spectral tilt features. Combinations of edges into texture-like spectro-temporal modulation patterns.

Layers 9-20 (Mid-Deep)

Phoneme-level spectral templates, formant transition patterns, speaking style features. Beginning to abstract away phonetic content toward speaker identity.

Layers 21-34 (Deep)

High-level speaker identity features that are largely invariant to what is being said. These encode the composite "voice quality" or "vocal fingerprint" of the speaker.

1D TDNN (x-vector) vs 2D ResNet Approaches

The two dominant paradigms in speaker embedding are Time-Delay Neural Networks (TDNNs), which produce x-vectors, and 2D ResNets, which produce r-vectors. Both achieve state-of-the-art performance but differ fundamentally in how they process audio features.

1D TDNN / x-vector

  • Input: Sequence of frame-level features (MFCC or filterbanks) treated as 1D temporal signal
  • Convolution: 1D along time axis only; each layer sees all frequency bins simultaneously
  • Frequency handling: Treats frequency bins as channels (or input features); no convolution across frequency
  • Context: Captures wide temporal context through dilated convolutions
  • Architecture: Shallow (5-7 layers for ECAPA-TDNN)
  • Key models: x-vector, ECAPA-TDNN
  • Strengths: Long temporal context, computationally efficient, translational invariance in time

2D ResNet / r-vector

  • Input: 2D spectrogram treated as single-channel image
  • Convolution: 2D across both time and frequency dimensions
  • Frequency handling: Convolves across frequency bins, learning cross-frequency patterns
  • Context: Local receptive field grows through depth
  • Architecture: Deep (34+ layers)
  • Key models: VGGVox, Thin ResNet-34, ResNetSE34
  • Strengths: Captures frequency-domain structure, formant patterns, harmonic relationships

Hybrid Approaches: Best of Both Worlds

Recent research on hybrid CNN-TDNN architectures (such as TB-ResNet and ECAPA CNN-TDNN) combines 2D ResNet's frequency modeling with TDNN's temporal context. These hybrids have shown 11.4% relative improvement in EER over ECAPA-TDNN alone, suggesting that both time and frequency inductive biases are valuable for speaker recognition.

RawNet3 & SincNet: Raw Waveform Variants

While ResNet-based systems operate on pre-computed spectrograms, an alternative family of models processes the raw audio waveform directly, learning the feature extraction front-end jointly with the speaker embedding.

SincNet

SincNet (Ravanelli & Bengio, 2018) replaces the first convolutional layer with parametrized sinc functions that implement learnable band-pass filters:

  • Each filter is defined by only two parameters: a low cutoff frequency (f1) and a high cutoff frequency (f2).
  • The filter impulse response is computed as sinc(2*pi*f2*t) - sinc(2*pi*f1*t), windowed by a Hamming function.
  • The network learns to place band-pass filters at acoustically meaningful frequencies, naturally discovering formant bands and pitch-related frequencies.
  • Remaining layers are standard 1D CNNs.
SincNet's Advantage

By constraining the first layer to learn band-pass filters, SincNet achieves faster convergence and more interpretable features compared to unconstrained 1D convolutions on raw waveforms. The learned filter cutoffs can be visualized and often correspond to linguistically meaningful frequency bands.

RawNet3

RawNet3 (Jung et al., Interspeech 2022) is a more advanced raw-waveform model that hybridizes ideas from ECAPA-TDNN and earlier RawNet architectures:

ParamFbank Layer

Replaces SincNet's sinc filters with a parameterized analytic filterbank that learns real-valued frequency responses. Transforms 1D waveform into 2D feature maps, serving as a learnable spectrogram.

Res2Dilated Blocks

Multi-scale residual blocks with dilated convolutions, derived from ECAPA-TDNN architecture. Captures temporal context at multiple resolutions.

Feature Map Scaling

Applies channel-wise scaling (related to SE blocks) to emphasize speaker-discriminative feature channels after each block.

Max Pooling Aggregation

Uses attentive statistics pooling with channel-dependent frame-level attention for utterance-level representation.

Strengths & Weaknesses

Strengths

  • Rich frequency modeling: 2D convolutions capture cross-frequency patterns (formants, harmonics) that 1D systems miss, providing complementary information in ensembles.
  • Proven architecture: Directly benefits from decades of image recognition research -- ResNet, SE blocks, batch normalization, etc. are well-understood and highly optimized.
  • Strong baseline performance: ResNet-34 with AAM-Softmax achieves sub-1% EER on VoxCeleb1-O with relatively simple training.
  • Flexible input: Can handle variable-length utterances through temporal pooling, with no fixed input size requirement.
  • Well-supported toolkits: Available in VoxCeleb Trainer, WeSpeaker, SpeechBrain, and pyannote-audio with pre-trained models.
  • Good noise robustness: 2D patterns in spectrograms are relatively stable under additive noise compared to frame-level features.
  • Compact embeddings: Produces 256-512 dimensional embeddings suitable for efficient similarity computation and storage.
  • SE blocks improve accuracy with negligible cost: Less than 1% parameter increase for 5-15% relative EER improvement.

Weaknesses

  • Limited temporal context per layer: Small 3x3 kernels require many layers to build long-range temporal dependencies, unlike dilated TDNNs.
  • Frequency invariance is a double-edged sword: 2D convolutions apply the same filter at all frequency positions, but speaker features are frequency-position-dependent (formants at specific bands).
  • Deeper than necessary: 34 layers is deep for the relatively simple structure of spectrograms; some capacity may be wasted compared to shallow ECAPA-TDNN.
  • Fixed spectrogram front-end: Unlike RawNet3/SincNet, the STFT parameters are not learned jointly, potentially discarding useful raw waveform information.
  • Sensitivity to hyperparameters: AM/AAM-Softmax margin and scale values significantly affect performance and require careful tuning.
  • Slightly behind ECAPA-TDNN as single model: On VoxCeleb1-O, ECAPA-TDNN typically edges out ResNet-34 by 0.1-0.3% absolute EER.
  • Computational cost: More FLOPs than ECAPA-TDNN for similar embedding quality due to 2D convolutions over the full spectrogram.
  • Short utterance degradation: Performance drops more sharply on very short utterances (<1s) compared to attention-based systems.
Bottom Line

ResNet-based speaker embeddings remain a cornerstone of modern speaker recognition systems. While they may be slightly outperformed by ECAPA-TDNN as single models on VoxCeleb benchmarks, their complementary frequency-domain modeling makes them indispensable in winning ensemble systems. The addition of SE blocks (ResNetSE34) and advanced pooling (ASP/NetVLAD) has kept this architecture family competitive through 2023-2025 challenge cycles. For practical deployments, pre-trained ResNet-34 models from WeSpeaker or pyannote-audio offer an excellent balance of accuracy, speed, and simplicity.
