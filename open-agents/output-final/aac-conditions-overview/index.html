Who Uses AAC?

Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) encompasses all methods of communication beyond oral speech that help people with communication challenges express themselves. AAC ranges from simple gestures and picture boards to sophisticated speech-generating devices and brain-computer interfaces.

The conditions that lead to AAC use span a wide spectrum — from developmental differences present at birth, to sudden acquired injuries, to progressive diseases that gradually erode the ability to speak. Each condition presents unique communication challenges, and AAC solutions must be carefully matched to individual needs, abilities, and goals.

Understanding AAC

AAC is not a single technology but a spectrum of tools and strategies. It includes unaided systems (like sign language and gestures), low-tech solutions (communication boards and picture books), and high-tech devices (tablet apps and dedicated speech-generating devices). The goal is always the same: ensuring every person has a way to communicate their thoughts, needs, and feelings.

Acquired Neurological Conditions

3 conditions — Resulting from injury or sudden medical events
Stroke & Aphasia
Acquired

Aphasia is a language disorder caused by brain damage, most commonly from stroke. It affects the ability to speak, understand, read, and write while leaving intelligence intact. AAC plays a critical role both in acute care and long-term recovery, serving as both a communication tool and a rehabilitation aid.

Traumatic Brain Injury
Acquired

TBI results from an external force to the head and can cause a wide range of communication problems, from mild word-finding difficulties to complete inability to speak. Unique among AAC conditions, TBI often involves cognitive-communication disorders alongside speech impairments, requiring AAC that supports memory, attention, and executive function.

Locked-in Syndrome
Acquired

A devastating condition where a person is fully conscious and cognitively intact but unable to move or speak, typically caused by brainstem stroke or late-stage ALS. Communication depends entirely on AAC — from eye blinks to cutting-edge brain-computer interfaces. Quality of life research shows most locked-in individuals report meaningful lives when given communication access.

Progressive & Degenerative Conditions

Progressive

A progressive neurodegenerative disease that destroys motor neurons controlling voluntary movement. Up to 80-95% of people with ALS eventually lose the ability to speak. Neuromuscular Conditions

1 condition group — Progressive muscle weakness affecting speech and access
Muscular Dystrophy & Spinal Muscular Atrophy
Neuromuscular

A group of genetic conditions causing progressive muscle weakness and degeneration. While cognitive abilities are typically fully preserved, weakening respiratory muscles gradually affect voice strength and clarity. Revolutionary gene therapies (Zolgensma, Spinraza) are changing the trajectory of these conditions, but AAC planning remains essential for progressive stages.

Structural & Medical Conditions

1 condition group — Physical changes to speech anatomy
Head & Neck Cancers / Laryngectomy
Structural

Cancers of the larynx, throat, tongue, and oral cavity can profoundly impact speech, especially when surgical removal of the voice box (laryngectomy) is required. Unlike progressive conditions, voice loss can be sudden and permanent. Multiple alaryngeal speech options exist, including tracheoesophageal speech, electrolarynx devices, and AAC technology.
