PRC-Saltillo: 60 Years of Giving Voice

From a discarded Teletype machine in 1969 to AI-powered communication in 2026

The story begins in a Cleveland, Ohio hospital in 1966. Barry Romich, an engineering student at Case Western Reserve University, and Ed Prentke, an engineer at Highland View Hospital, shared a conviction that technology could give voice to those who had none. They founded the Prentke Romich Company (PRC) -- and in doing so, helped launch the entire field of augmentative communication technology.

In the late 1990s, Dave Hershberger -- then PRC's Director of R&D -- founded Saltillo as a sister company to develop the TouchChat line. In 2019, Hershberger became CEO and merged both companies into PRC-Saltillo, uniting decades of expertise under one roof.

How WordPower Works

The vocabulary system designed by SLP Nancy Inman at the Kennedy Krieger Institute

Nancy Inman, M.A.T., CCC-SLP, created WordPower while evaluating a young man with cerebral palsy who used a head stick to point to words on a low-tech board. She recognized that he needed the same word-based approach on dynamic-display AAC devices -- but nothing like that existed. So she built it herself.

WordPower is a word-based vocabulary system (as opposed to symbol-based or icon-sequence systems). It focuses on high-frequency core words that research shows account for the vast majority of everyday communication.

🔢

Core Words First

High-frequency words (pronouns, common verbs, key adjectives) are prominently placed on the main page of every layout.

🎨

Color Coding by Part of Speech

Words organized by grammatical category using consistent colors -- verbs, nouns, pronouns, adjectives each get their own color.

📍

Consistent Motor Planning

Core words remain in the same physical position across vocabulary sizes, building automaticity over time.

🔮

Next-Word Prediction

After selecting a word, contextually likely follow-up words surface to reduce button presses.

⌨

Word Completion

Begin typing a word and have it completed, extending vocabulary reach far beyond visible buttons.

🔗

Core-Fringe Bridge

Category pages embed core vocabulary alongside topic-specific words for always-available functional communication.

- Reinstallation Issues -- some bugs require full delete/reinstall to resolve

WordPower Origin: Nancy Inman's Story

▼

Nancy Inman developed WordPower while working at the Kennedy Krieger Institute. She was evaluating a young man with cerebral palsy who used a head stick to point to words arranged on a low-tech communication board. She recognized that he needed the same word-based approach on the dynamic-display AAC devices that were just emerging -- but nothing like that existed.

So she built it herself. The result was WordPower: a word-based vocabulary featuring high-frequency words, color-coded by category, with keyboard and word prediction support. Unlike icon-sequence systems (like Minspeak/Unity) that require learning abstract icon combinations, WordPower presents actual words directly -- making it more intuitive for users who have some literacy or are developing it.

Today, WordPower remains one of the most widely used word-based AAC vocabularies, with Nancy Inman continuing to be involved in its development and evolution.

PRC-Saltillo Hardware Ecosystem

▼

TouchChat software runs not only on standard iPads and iPhones but also on dedicated PRC-Saltillo hardware devices:

  • Accent 1400/1000/800 -- High-powered dedicated speech-generating devices with large screens
  • Accent PRiO-mini -- Compact dedicated device
  • NovaChat 8/10 -- Saltillo-branded communication devices (8" and 10" screens)
  • Via Pro -- 11" screen device with touch and switch compatibility
  • TouchChat Express -- Dedicated hardware solution

This ecosystem means users can start with the affordable iPad app and later transition to a dedicated device (often insurance-funded) without relearning their vocabulary. The consistency between app and hardware is a significant advantage for long-term AAC users.

TouchChat vs Proloquo2Go: Detailed Breakdown

▼

Proloquo2Go by AssistiveWare remains TouchChat's most direct competitor. The choice between them often comes down to philosophy and priorities:

  • Price: TouchChat is ~$100 cheaper ($299.99 vs ~$399.99)
  • Vocabulary Philosophy: WordPower is word-based with prediction; Crescendo is a hybrid symbol-and-word system with stronger emphasis on developmental progression from symbols to text
  • Interface: TouchChat is simpler and more approachable; Proloquo2Go is more feature-dense with deeper developmental scaffolding
  • Head Tracking: TouchChat has it built in; Proloquo2Go does not offer it natively
  • Symbol Libraries: Both include SymbolStix; Proloquo2Go also bundles PCS symbols at no extra cost
  • Motor Planning: Proloquo2Go places slightly stronger emphasis on consistent motor planning across all levels
  • AI Features: TouchChat has AI message editing and image generation (2026.1); Proloquo2Go has limited AI

Bottom line: TouchChat is better for users who want a word-based approach, need head tracking, or are budget-conscious. Proloquo2Go is better for users who want a developmental progression from symbols to words with stronger scaffolding.

The Verdict

TouchChat HD with WordPower occupies a compelling position in the AAC landscape. It combines clinical credibility (WordPower's SLP-designed vocabulary), hardware flexibility (built-in head tracking and switch scanning), and forward-looking technology (AI message editing and image generation) at a price point that undercuts its primary competitor.

The 2026.1 update signals that PRC-Saltillo is actively investing in the platform's future rather than resting on its legacy.

The app is not without rough edges. iOS-only availability, layout inconsistencies between vocabulary levels, and software stability concerns are legitimate drawbacks. But for individuals and families in the Apple ecosystem who need a powerful, customizable, word-based AAC tool -- particularly one that supports head tracking out of the box -- TouchChat HD with WordPower remains one of the strongest options available.

For a $299.99 investment that can literally give someone their voice, that is a proposition worth serious consideration.
