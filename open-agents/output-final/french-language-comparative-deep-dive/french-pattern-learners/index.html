French for Pattern Learners Decision Trees, Systematic Rules, and the Meta-Pattern

Eight decision trees and one meta-pattern that cover the vast majority of French grammatical challenges — a systematic, algorithm-based toolkit designed for pattern-oriented learners

Capstone Chapter

This chapter synthesizes all preceding categories of the deep dive into a unified, pattern-based learning framework. It draws on phonology, morphology, syntax, verbs, gender, semantics, pragmatics, history, writing systems, and typological comparison.

Every preceding chapter in this deep dive has documented a specific structural domain of French. This capstone chapter does something different. It synthesizes all of those domains into a unified, pattern-based learning framework designed specifically for the kind of learner who thrives on explicit rules, systematic patterns, decision treesA systematic algorithm for making grammatical choices through sequential binary decisions. Each node asks a yes/no question and routes to the next step or a final answer., and structural insight.

The central claim is strong: eight decision trees and one meta-patternThe overarching principle that French is more morphologically synthetic than English, from which approximately 80% of specific differences can be derived. can cover the vast majority of French grammatical challenges that an English speaker will encounter. Each tree is designed to be complete (covering all standard cases), systematic (following consistent logic), and predictive (allowing you to handle new examples without prior memorization).

Key Takeaways
  • One meta-pattern (French = more morphologically synthetic) predicts ~80% of all French-English differences
  • Eight decision trees provide algorithmic solutions for: gender prediction, verb conjugation, adjective placement (BAGS), liaison, passé composé vs. imparfait, subjunctive (WEIRDO), article selection, and clitic pronoun ordering
  • French grammar is ~80% rule-governed for gender, ~90% regular for verbs, and 100% regular for the imparfait — these are not small margins
  • The trees interact in predictable ways: gender cascades through articles, adjectives, and participles; verb conjugation feeds into tense/aspect choice; liaison interacts with adjective placement
Cognitive Science Basis

Research on autistic cognitive strengths consistently identifies pattern recognition, rule extraction, and systematic learning as areas of particular strength. This cognitive profile is precisely what the decision-tree approach leverages: it converts implicit, statistically-acquired grammatical knowledge into explicit, rule-based algorithms. Where a typical language learner might "feel" that a noun is feminine, the pattern learner applies a systematic test and arrives at the same answer through a different — and often more reliable — cognitive route.

1. The Meta-Pattern: One Principle That Predicts Everything

The single most powerful insight for a pattern learner approaching French: French is more morphologically syntheticThe degree to which a language encodes grammatical information within word forms through inflection. More synthetic = more information per word (French). More analytic = more separate words (English). than English. French packs more grammatical information into individual words through inflection (verb conjugation, gender agreement, mood marking), while English distributes the same information across multiple words (auxiliaries, prepositions, word order).

The practical algorithm: When you encounter something in French that confuses you, ask: "Is this a consequence of French being more synthetic?" If yes (~80% of the time), expect more morphological complexity per word but fewer total words, and look for the English compensatory strategy. If no (~20%), you have identified a genuinely independent parameter that requires separate learning.

Tree 1 Gender Prediction from Noun Endings

English comparison: English has no grammatical gender. This means English speakers have no prior framework for gender prediction. But the ending-based system provides exactly the kind of rule-based approach that replaces rote memorization with structural analysis.

The research basis: A 2006 corpus study by LysterRoy Lyster (McGill University, 2006). Corpus study of 9,961 nouns from Le Robert Junior Illustré demonstrating that approximately 80% of both masculine and feminine nouns have endings that systematically predict their gender. examined 9,961 nouns and demonstrated that approximately 80% of masculine nouns and 81% of feminine nouns have endings that systematically predict their gender. Gender is not random — it is approximately 80% rule-governed based on morphological form.

Figure 2: The gender prediction algorithm — feminine predictors on the left, masculine predictors on the right, with accuracy percentages for each ending pattern

The Algorithm

Step 1: Check High-Confidence Feminine Endings

  • -tion / -sion / -aison (99%): nation, passion, éducation
  • -té / -ité (95%): liberté, société, université
  • -ure (90%): nature, culture, voiture
  • -ence / -ance (95%): science, patience, chance
  • -ie / -rie (95%): vie, philosophie, boulangerie
  • -ette / -elle (98%): assiette, fourchette, chapelle
  • -ée (95%): idée, journée, année — exception: musée (masculine)

Step 2: Check High-Confidence Masculine Endings

  • -ment (95%): moment, gouvernement, changement
  • -age (90%): voyage, fromage, village — exceptions: image, page, plage (feminine)
  • -eau (95%): bureau, château, tableau
  • -isme (100%): capitalisme, réalisme, tourisme
  • -eur (85%): honneur, bonheur, moteur — exceptions: fleur, couleur, peur, douleur, humeur (feminine)
  • -al / -ail / -eil (95%): journal, travail, soleil
  • -oir (95%): miroir, espoir, pouvoir

Step 3: Apply Weak Predictors as Tiebreakers

  • Words ending in silent -e: ~65% feminine (weak predictor but directional)
  • Words ending in a pronounced consonant: ~80% masculine

Step 4: Not covered? Learn individually (~20% of nouns).

Exception Patterns Are Themselves Patterned

Feminine -eur words cluster around abstract/emotional qualities (fleur, couleur, peur, douleur, humeur). Masculine -e words often end in specific sub-patterns (-age, -isme, -ège). Learning the exception patterns as secondary rules reduces the truly unpredictable items to a small set.

Tree 2 Verb Conjugation: The Three-Group System

English comparison: English has 5 verb forms maximum and compensates with auxiliary stacking. French has 50+ forms per verb but organizes them into three learnable groups with a critical efficiency insight: Group 1 (-er verbs) accounts for approximately 90% of all French verbs and is completely regular.

The 90/10 Principle

  • Group 1 (-er): ~90% of all French verbs. Completely regular. Learn this one pattern and you can conjugate thousands of verbs.
  • Group 2 (-ir with -issant): ~300 verbs. Regular with an -iss- extension in plural forms. Diagnostic: present participle in -issant (finissant), not -ant (partant).
  • Group 3 (everything else): ~350 verbs with identifiable sub-patterns, plus the "big four" truly irregular verbs (être, avoir, aller, faire).

Cross-Tense Pattern Shortcuts

These are the pattern insights that make conjugation feel like a system rather than a memorization task:

The Imparfait Is 100% Regular

For all verbs, even the most irregular ones. Formation: nous-form present stem + -ais, -ais, -ait, -ions, -iez, -aient. Even être: nous sommes → ét- → j'étais. There are zero exceptions.

The conditional = future stem + imparfait endings. Once you know the future tense and the imparfait, the conditional is free:

  • Future: parler + ai = parlerai
  • Conditional: parler + ais = parlerais
  • Same stem, different ending set. One pattern, two tenses.

The subjunctive stem = 3rd person plural present stem. ils parlent → parl- → que je parle. The subjunctive endings (-e, -es, -e, -ions, -iez, -ent) are applied to this stem.

The meta-rule: In any French tense, the stem determines WHAT you are saying, and the ending simultaneously encodes WHO (person), HOW MANY (number), WHEN (tense), and HOW CERTAIN (mood). One ending, four pieces of information. English requires separate words for each.

The Spoken vs. Written Split

A critical insight: French verb conjugation looks more complex on paper than it sounds. In the present tense of Group 1 verbs, the je/tu/il/ils forms are all pronounced identically: "je parle," "tu parles," "il parle," "ils parlent" all sound like /parl/. Only "nous parlons" and "vous parlez" are audibly distinct. This means spoken French effectively has only 3 distinct present-tense forms, not the 6 that written French displays.

The Pattern Within the Pattern

French writing preserves historical morphological distinctions that speech has eroded. This spoken/written gap is itself a pattern: whenever written French seems more complex than pronunciation, you are observing orthographic fossils from an earlier stage of the language.

Group 3 Sub-Patterns

Even the "irregular" Group 3 verbs reveal patterns when examined systematically:

  • -oir verbs (vouloir, pouvoir, devoir, savoir, voir): share stem-change patterns with predictable alternations
  • -dre verbs (prendre, vendre, attendre): share the conjugation endings -ds, -ds, -d in the singular
  • -aindre/-eindre/-oindre (craindre, peindre, joindre): share stem-change patterns with nasal consonant alternation
  • The "big four" (être, avoir, aller, faire): genuinely irregular and among the 4 most frequent verbs; memorize outright

The ~350 Group 3 verbs are not 350 independent memorization tasks. They cluster into approximately 15–20 sub-patterns, each applying to a small family of related verbs.

Tree 3 BAGS Adjective Placement

English comparison: English adjectives always precede the noun ("a white house"). French adjectives default to the opposite position: after the noun. English speakers must reverse their default, then learn the BAGSMnemonic for the four categories of French adjectives that precede the noun: Beauty (beau, joli), Age (jeune, vieux, nouveau), Goodness (bon, mauvais), Size (grand, petit, gros). These are short, high-frequency, evaluative adjectives. exceptions that reverse back — a double inversion.

Figure 3: The BAGS decision tree — three steps to correct adjective placement, with the deeper pattern (short, frequent, subjective) and position-dependent meaning changes

The Algorithm

  1. Is the adjective in the BAGS list? → Place BEFORE the noun (prenominal)
  2. Does the adjective change meaning by position? → Check desired meaning
  3. Default (when unsure): place AFTER the noun → correct ~85% of the time

The BAGS categories: Beauty (beau/belle, joli/jolie), Age (jeune, vieux/vieille, nouveau/nouvelle), Goodness (bon/bonne, mauvais/mauvaise, meilleur/meilleure), Size (grand/grande, petit/petite, gros/grosse, long/longue).

The deeper pattern: BAGS adjectives share three properties: they are short (1–2 syllables), high-frequency, and subjective/evaluative (expressing the speaker's judgment rather than classifying the noun objectively). Post-nominal adjectives tend to be classifying, technical, or specific.

Tree 4 Liaison Rules

English comparison: English has no liaisonThe pronunciation of a normally silent final consonant before a vowel-initial word in French. Follows a systematic three-category system: obligatory, forbidden, and optional (register-dependent). system. Final consonants are always pronounced or always silent regardless of what follows. French liaison follows a systematic three-category system.

Figure 4: The three liaison categories — obligatory (green), forbidden (red), and optional as a register dial (amber)

Obligatory (omission = error)

  • Determiner + noun: les_amis, un_ami, des_enfants
  • Pronoun + verb: ils_ont, nous_avons, on_a
  • Prenominal adjective + noun: un petit_ami, les grands_arbres
  • After très/plus/moins/bien/trop: très_intéressant
  • Fixed expressions: les États-Unis, comment_allez-vous

Forbidden (production = error)

  • After "et" (and): NEVER. "Pain et // eau," not *"pain et_eau"
  • Before aspirate-h words: le // héros, les // haricots
  • After singular nouns: un étudiant // intelligent
  • Before onze and oui

Optional (register-dependent)

  • After plural nouns, after verbs (except être/avoir), after prepositions
  • More liaison = more formal; less liaison = more casual

Tree 5 Passé Composé vs. Imparfait

English comparison: English can express this distinction ("I was eating" vs. "I ate") but does not require it grammatically. French requires the choice on every past-tense verb — there is no ambiguous middle ground.

Figure 5: The six-step decision tree for choosing between passé composé and imparfait, with trigger words and the movie metaphor

The Algorithm

  1. Did the action happen a specific, countable number of times? → Passé composéFrench compound past tense (avoir/être + past participle) for completed, bounded, or narrative-advancing actions. "J'ai mangé" = I ate / I have eaten.
  2. Was the action habitual or repeated without a specific count? → ImparfaitFrench imperfect tense for ongoing, habitual, or background past situations. Formation is 100% regular for all verbs. "Je mangeais" = I was eating / I used to eat.
  3. Did the action have a clear beginning, end, or duration? → Passé composé
  4. Does this advance the story / narrate a plot event? → Passé composé
  5. Is this background description or scene-setting? → Imparfait
  6. Is this a state or condition? Did the state CHANGE? → If changed: Passé composé. If unchanged: Imparfait

The Movie Metaphor

Think of past narration as a movie:

  • Imparfait = the background: scenery, weather, ambient sound, what people looked like and felt
  • Passé composé = the action: plot events, changes, things that happened

You need both: "Il faisait nuit [imparfait, background]. Soudain, un bruit a retenti [passé composé, event]."

Tree 6 Subjunctive Triggers (WEIRDO)

English comparison: English subjunctive is vestigial ("If I were you," "I suggest that he go"). French subjunctive is alive, productive, and grammatically obligatory in all WEIRDOMnemonic for the six categories that trigger the subjunctive in French: Wishes, Emotions, Impersonal expressions, Recommendations, Doubt/Denial, Opinions. All share a common thread: non-factual stance. contexts. This is one of the starkest French-English contrasts.

Figure 6: The WEIRDO radial map — six trigger categories surrounding the central subjunctive, with conjunction list and exception callouts

The WEIRDO Categories

  • W — Wishes/Wants: vouloir que, souhaiter que, désirer que, préférer que
  • E — Emotions: être content que, être triste que, avoir peur que, regretter que
  • I — Impersonal expressions: il faut que, il est possible que, il est important que
  • R — Recommendations/Requests: demander que, exiger que, suggérer que, ordonner que
  • D — Doubt/Denial: douter que, ne pas croire que, ne pas penser que, nier que
  • O — Opinions: il est bon que, il est dommage que, c'est étrange que

The Two-Subject RuleThe subjunctive requires different subjects in main and subordinate clauses. If the subjects are the same, use infinitive instead: "Je veux venir" (not *"Je veux que je vienne").

  • Different subjects: "Je veux que tu viennes" (subjunctive)
  • Same subject: "Je veux venir" (infinitive, NOT *"Je veux que je vienne")

Key Exceptions

Watch Out
  • espérer que (to hope that): Takes INDICATIVE in the affirmative despite being a "wish" verb. "J'espère qu'il viendra" (not *vienne). In the negative, it takes subjunctive.
  • croire/penser que: In the AFFIRMATIVE, these take indicative ("Je crois qu'il sait"). Only in the NEGATIVE or INTERROGATIVE do they trigger subjunctive ("Je ne crois pas qu'il sache").

Conjunctions Requiring Subjunctive

Independent of WEIRDO, certain conjunctions always trigger subjunctive: avant que (before), pour que (in order to), bien que (although), sans que (without), à moins que (unless), jusqu'à ce que (until), de peur que (for fear that), pourvu que (provided that). Contrast: après que takes INDICATIVE (what happened after is factual).

The Deep Pattern Behind WEIRDO

The six categories share a common semantic thread: indicative = factual assertion; subjunctive = non-factual stance. Wishes are not facts. Emotions are subjective reactions. Doubt questions factual status. If you internalize this single principle, you can often predict subjunctive usage even for verbs not in the WEIRDO list.

Tree 7 Determiner Selection

English comparison: English has a simpler decision: "the" (specific), "a/an" (indefinite singular), or nothing (generic, plural indefinite, uncountable). French fills every position with an article — there are no bare nouns in French.

Figure 7: The article selection flowchart — five decision branches plus modification rules for negation, quantity, and pre-adjective contexts

The Algorithm

  1. Is the noun specific and identified? → Definite: le/la/l'/les
  2. Is the noun generic (the category as a whole)? → Definite (French uses definite for generic!) — English uses no article here
  3. Is the noun an indefinite countable thing? → Indefinite: un/une/des
  4. Is the noun an uncountable substance in indefinite quantity? → PartitiveFrench du/de la/de l'/des, encoding indefinite quantity for uncountable nouns. "Je bois du café" (I drink [some] coffee). English has no equivalent — it uses bare nouns.: du/de la/de l' — English uses no article here
  5. Is the sentence negative? → Indefinite and partitive become de/d'. Exception: definite articles do NOT change; after être, articles don't change
  6. Adjective before a plural noun? → des → de/d' ("J'ai de bons amis")
  7. After expressions of quantity? → de alone ("beaucoup de livres"). Exception: la plupart des

The Two Most Common English-Speaker Errors

Error Traps
  1. Using no article for generic nouns: *"J'aime café" — WRONG. French requires: "J'aime le café."
  2. Using no article for partitive nouns: *"Je bois café" — WRONG. French requires: "Je bois du café."

Both errors come from the same source: English uses bare nouns where French requires specific grammatical forms. The French partitive article fills a slot that English simply leaves empty.

Tree 8 Clitic Pronoun Ordering

English comparison: English has no cliticA pronoun that attaches to the verb and follows a fixed ordering template. French has 5 clitic columns that must precede the verb in declarative sentences. Maximum 2 clitics per verb. ordering template. "I give it to him" and "I give him it" are both grammatical. All objects follow the verb. French requires all object pronouns BEFORE the verb in a strict fixed order.

Figure 8: The five-column pre-verbal template with example sentences, the imperative exception, and the step-by-step ordering algorithm

The Template (Declarative and Negative Sentences)

Subject + (ne) + [Col 1] + [Col 2] + [Col 3] + [Col 4] + [Col 5] + VERB + (pas)

ColumnPronounsFunction
Col 1me, te, se, nous, vousReflexive / 1st-2nd person
Col 2le, la, les3rd person direct object
Col 3lui, leur3rd person indirect object
Col 4yLocative (replaces à + noun/place)
Col 5enPartitive (replaces de + noun/quantity)

Maximum 2 pronouns per verb. Select from left to right: if you have a Col 1 pronoun, it comes first; if you have Col 2 and Col 3, Col 2 comes first.

Examples

  • "Je le vois" (I see him/it) — Col 2 only
  • "Je le lui donne" (I give it to him) — Col 2 + Col 3
  • "Il me le donne" (He gives it to me) — Col 1 + Col 2
  • "Je m'en souviens" (I remember it) — Col 1 + Col 5
  • "Il y en a" (There are some) — Col 4 + Col 5

The Imperative Exception

In affirmative imperatives, pronouns FOLLOW the verb with hyphens, and the order changes:

Verb + le/la/les + moi/toi/lui/nous/vous/leur + y + en

  • "Donne-le-moi" (Give it to me) — NOT *"Donne-moi-le"
  • "Donnez-les-leur" (Give them to them)

In negative imperatives, the standard pre-verbal order returns: "Ne me le donne pas" (Don't give it to me).

Why Rigidity Is the Pattern Learner's Friend

French requires more forward planning of the verb phrase — you must know all your objects BEFORE producing the verb. But the system is entirely algorithmic: identify the pronouns, assign them to columns, place them in column order, then produce the verb. The system is rigid (which makes it learnable) rather than flexible (which would make it unpredictable).

9. The Master Algorithm

When you encounter any French grammatical challenge, follow this master algorithm:

  1. Identify the category. Gender? Use Tree 1. Verb form? Use Tree 2. Adjective position? Use Tree 3. Connected speech? Use Tree 4. Past tense choice? Use Tree 5. Mood choice? Use Tree 6. Article selection? Use Tree 7. Pronoun placement? Use Tree 8.
  2. Apply the decision tree. Follow it step by step. The trees are designed to be exhaustive.
  3. Check for exceptions. Each tree has a known set of exceptions. These exceptions are themselves systematic — learn the exception patterns as secondary rules, not individual items.
  4. Cross-reference with the meta-pattern. If a rule seems arbitrary, check whether it follows from the meta-pattern (French = more synthetic). If it does, the rule becomes predictable. If it does not (vigesimal numbers, aspirate-h, progressive aspect), it is a historical contingency requiring separate learning.

10. Common Misconceptions

"French grammar is too complex to learn systematically; you just have to absorb it through immersion."

Every major aspect of French grammar follows identifiable patterns. Gender is ~80% predictable from endings. Verb conjugation is ~90% regular. Adjective placement follows the BAGS rule. Liaison has three systematic categories. The subjunctive has the WEIRDO trigger list. Pattern learners can achieve high accuracy through systematic rule application without relying solely on implicit absorption.

"French verb tables need to be memorized individually for each verb."

90% of French verbs are Group 1 (-er) and follow a single, completely regular paradigm. The conditional = future stem + imparfait endings. The imparfait is regular for ALL verbs. Only the "big four" and ~350 Group 3 verbs require individual attention — and even these have sub-patterns.

"You need to feel whether to use passé composé or imparfait."

The distinction is systematic and can be determined through a decision tree. Completed, bounded, countable, or narrative-advancing → passé composé. Ongoing, habitual, descriptive, or background → imparfait. The decision is algorithmic, not intuitive.

"French exceptions make rules useless."

French "exceptions" are themselves often patterned. Feminine -eur words cluster around abstract/emotional qualities. BAGS adjectives share properties (short, frequent, evaluative). The 80% accuracy of the gender prediction system is already far better than the 50% you would get from random guessing.

11. How the Decision Trees Work Together

The eight decision trees are not isolated — they interact in predictable ways.

Gender Cascades Through Articles, Adjectives, and Participles

When you determine a noun's gender (Tree 1), that determination immediately feeds into article selection (Tree 7: le vs. la), adjective agreement (Tree 3: grand vs. grande), and past participle agreement (Tree 5, with être verbs: allé vs. allée). A single gender decision cascades through multiple trees. Get the gender right at the start and all downstream agreement falls into place.

Verb Conjugation Feeds Into Tense/Aspect Choice

Tree 2 tells you HOW to conjugate. But you also need to know WHICH tense. The sequence: (1) Is this a subjunctive context? (Tree 6). (2) If indicative past: passé composé or imparfait? (Tree 5). (3) What conjugation group? (Tree 2).

Liaison Interacts With Adjective Placement

Trees 3 and 4 interact when a BAGS adjective precedes a vowel-initial noun. The combination becomes an obligatory liaison context: "un petit_ami," "les grands_arbres." The special forms bel/nouvel/vieil exist specifically to provide a consonant for liaison.

The Clitic Template Interacts With Negation

ne...pas wraps around the entire clitic-verb complex: "Je ne le lui ai pas donné" — ne comes before ALL clitics; pas comes after the conjugated verb.

Articles Interact With Negation and Quantity

Tree 7 has modification rules that activate in specific contexts: after negation (indefinite/partitive → de), after quantity expressions (→ de alone), before adjective + plural noun (des → de). These are context-dependent modifications within a single system.

12. Why Pattern Learning Works for French

French, as a moderately synthetic fusionalA morphological type where one affix encodes multiple grammatical categories simultaneously. French -aient simultaneously marks person, number, tense, and mood. language, has exactly the kind of grammar that rewards systematic analysis:

  1. High regularity within paradigms: 90% of verbs are Group 1 regular. 80% of genders are predictable. The imparfait is 100% regular. These are not small margins.
  2. Compositional patterns: Complex forms are built from simpler ones (conditional = future stem + imparfait endings; subjunctive = 3rd plural present stem + subjunctive endings).
  3. Systematic exceptions: Even the "irregular" parts contain sub-patterns. Group 3 verbs cluster into families. Gender exceptions cluster around semantic fields.
  4. Finite rule sets: The WEIRDO categories, the liaison three-category system, the clitic template, the article selection tree — each is a finite, enumerable set of rules with no open-ended ambiguity.

The decision trees in this chapter are not simplifications — they are accurate representations of how French grammar actually works, expressed in the format most accessible to a systematic mind.
