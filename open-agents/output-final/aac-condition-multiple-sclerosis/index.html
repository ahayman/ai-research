Overview

Multiple Sclerosis (MS) is a chronic autoimmune disease of the central nervous system (CNS) in which the body's immune system mistakenly attacks myelin, the protective sheath that insulates nerve fibers in the brain and spinal cord. This process, called demyelination, disrupts the ability of nerves to conduct electrical impulses efficiently, leading to a wide range of neurological symptoms that can affect virtually any body system—including speech, language, and communication.

MS is one of the most common neurological conditions among young adults, typically diagnosed between the ages of 20 and 50. The disease is progressive in nature, meaning it generally worsens over time, though the rate and pattern of progression vary enormously between individuals. This unpredictability is one of the hallmarks of MS and creates unique challenges when planning for communication support.

Because MS can damage nerves throughout the brain and spinal cord, it can simultaneously affect the motor control of speech muscles (causing dysarthria), the cognitive processes underlying language (affecting word-finding and fluency), and the physical ability to access communication devices (through tremor, weakness, or visual impairment). This multifaceted impact makes MS an important condition in the field of Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC).

What is Demyelination?

Myelin is a fatty substance that wraps around nerve fibers (axons) much like insulation around an electrical wire. When the immune system attacks myelin in MS, the resulting damage creates lesions or "plaques" of scar tissue (sclerosis). These lesions slow or block nerve signals, causing symptoms that depend on which nerves are affected. Over time, the nerve fibers themselves can also become damaged, leading to more permanent disability.

Types of MS

MS is classified into several distinct types based on its clinical course. Understanding these types is essential for AAC planning because each type has different implications for when and how communication support may be needed.

↻
Relapsing-Remitting MS (RRMS)
~85% of initial diagnoses

The most common form of MS at diagnosis. RRMS is characterized by clearly defined episodes of new or worsening neurological symptoms (relapses or "attacks"), followed by periods of partial or complete recovery (remissions). During remissions, the disease does not appear to progress. Relapses can last days, weeks, or even months, and symptoms during a relapse can range from mild to severe.

AAC Implications: Communication abilities may fluctuate dramatically. A person may need AAC during a relapse but not during remission. This requires flexible, on-demand AAC solutions that can be quickly deployed and then set aside. RRMS is about 2–3 times more common in women than men.

↗
Secondary Progressive MS (SPMS)
~50% of RRMS within 20 years

Many people who initially have RRMS eventually transition to SPMS, where the disease begins to progress more steadily, with or without occasional relapses. The transition happens gradually, and there may be periods of minor improvement, but the overall trend is one of increasing disability. Approximately half of people with RRMS will develop SPMS within 20 years of their initial diagnosis.

AAC Implications: As speech and communication abilities progressively decline, AAC needs become more consistent and long-term. Planning ahead for more robust AAC solutions becomes increasingly important, including voice banking while speech is still intelligible.

↑
Primary Progressive MS (PPMS)
~10–15% of diagnoses

PPMS is characterized by a gradual, steadily worsening course from the very first symptoms, without distinct relapses or remissions. On average, people with PPMS start having symptoms between ages 35 and 39—about a decade later than relapsing forms. Unlike RRMS, PPMS affects men and women in roughly equal numbers.

AAC Implications: Since decline is more predictable, AAC planning can follow a more structured path. Early intervention with voice banking and progressive AAC introduction is particularly valuable. The steady nature of decline means AAC systems need to scale in complexity over time.

⇅
Progressive-Relapsing MS (PRMS)
Rare, ~5%

Now often reclassified under PPMS with active relapses, PRMS features a steady progressive course from the beginning, punctuated by acute relapses. There may or may not be recovery after these relapses, but the disease continues to progress between episodes.

AAC Implications: Combines the challenges of progressive decline with unpredictable acute worsening. AAC strategies must accommodate both the baseline progressive needs and sudden changes during relapses.

Prevalence & Statistics

Multiple Sclerosis is a significant global health condition with an increasing prevalence worldwide. According to the third edition of the Atlas of MS, the number of people living with MS has risen steadily from approximately 2.3 million in 2013 to 2.8 million in 2020 and an estimated 2.9 million in 2023, representing a global prevalence of roughly 36 per 100,000 population.

Key Prevalence Figures
  • Global: Approximately 2.9 million people worldwide (2023), up from 2.3 million in 2013.
  • United States: Nearly 1 million people are estimated to live with MS, making it one of the highest-prevalence countries.
  • Gender Ratio: Women are 3–4 times more likely to develop MS than men (for relapsing forms). PPMS is more equally distributed.
  • Age of Onset: Most commonly diagnosed between ages 20–50, with average onset around 32 years for women and 33.5 for men.
  • Geographic Pattern: Highest prevalence in Western Europe and North America, followed by Central/Eastern Europe, Australia, and New Zealand. Lower prevalence in Asia, Africa, and the Middle East.

These statistics highlight that communication difficulties are not a rare complication of MS—they are a common, often expected feature of the disease. Up to seven in ten people with MS will experience some degree of speech change over the course of their illness, and nearly half will develop clinically identifiable dysarthria.

Disease Course & Progression

Understanding the progression of MS is critical to AAC planning. Both inflammation and neurodegeneration are present from disease onset, coexisting to varying degrees and in different CNS locations throughout the disease course. This means that even in the earliest stages, the underlying damage that may eventually affect communication is already beginning.

The Relapsing-Remitting Phase

In RRMS, relapses are driven primarily by inflammatory immune attacks on myelin. These attacks can happen anywhere in the CNS, and symptoms depend on which nerves are affected. If an attack damages nerves in the brainstem or cerebellum—regions critical for speech motor control—speech difficulties may appear suddenly, sometimes dramatically.

During remission, the body repairs some of the damage, and symptoms may improve or resolve entirely. However, each relapse can leave behind residual damage, and over years, these accumulated deficits contribute to progressive disability. This means that even if speech returns to normal after each relapse, the risk of lasting speech problems increases over time.

Transition to Progressive Disease

The transition from RRMS to SPMS marks a shift from primarily inflammatory damage to primarily neurodegenerative damage. As nerve fibers themselves begin to deteriorate (not just their myelin coating), disability tends to accumulate more steadily. For communication, this often means a gradual worsening of speech clarity, vocal control, and cognitive-linguistic abilities that does not recover between episodes.

Planning Ahead Is Essential

Because the transition from relapsing to progressive disease can happen gradually and without clear markers, speech-language pathologists recommend that people with MS begin planning for potential communication needs early in their disease course. This includes voice banking (recording one's voice for future synthetic speech) while speech is still clear, and familiarizing oneself with AAC technologies before they become urgently needed.

Charcot's Triad

Historically, the triad of intention tremor (ataxia), nystagmus, and scanning speech was considered the hallmark of MS, described by Jean-Martin Charcot in the 19th century. While this specific triad is not present in all MS patients, the inclusion of scanning speech in the original description underscores just how closely MS has always been associated with speech disruption. Scanning speech, characterized by unnaturally separated syllables with pauses between them, remains one of the recognizable speech patterns in MS.

How MS Affects Communication

MS can affect communication through multiple, often overlapping mechanisms. Unlike conditions that affect a single aspect of communication (such as pure motor speech disorders or isolated aphasia), MS can simultaneously impact the muscles of speech, the cognitive processes of language, the voice, and the physical ability to access alternative communication methods. This multifaceted impact is what makes MS a particularly complex condition from an AAC perspective.

The most frequent speech deviations observed in MS research include impaired loudness control and harshness. Less frequently occurring but still common deviations include defective articulation, restricted use of vocal variations for emphasis, poor pitch control, hypernasality, inappropriate pitch level, and breathiness. Damage to the nerves and reduced nerve impulses weaken the muscles of the lower face, lips, tongue, soft palate, larynx, and respiratory muscles, creating a complex pattern of speech disruption.

Dysarthria in MS

Dysarthria is the primary cause of communication deficit in MS and refers to a group of speech disorders caused by weakness, slowness, or poor coordination of the muscles used for speaking. In MS, dysarthria can present in several forms depending on which areas of the central nervous system are affected, but the most common presentation is a mixed spastic-ataxic type.

Scanning Speech

Scanning speech is one of the most characteristic speech patterns associated with MS and was historically considered pathognomonic (uniquely diagnostic) of the disease. It is a form of ataxic dysarthria resulting from cerebellar damage. In scanning speech, the natural rhythm and flow of speech is disrupted—syllables are produced with roughly equal stress and timing, separated by noticeable pauses, creating a halting, staccato-like quality.

Normal speech has a natural melody: certain syllables are stressed more than others, some words are spoken more quickly, and the overall rhythm flows naturally. In scanning speech, this melody is flattened, and the result sounds robotic or choppy. The coordination of speech with breathing is also disturbed, so there may not be enough breath to produce certain words, while others are spoken with more force than intended.

Clinical Note: Severity is Often Mild

Research shows that while dysarthria is common in MS (affecting roughly 40–56% of patients), its severity is generally mild in many cases, especially early in the disease. This is an important distinction: many people with MS-related dysarthria are still intelligible most of the time but may have noticeable speech changes that worsen under fatigue, stress, or during relapses. Severe dysarthria that renders speech unintelligible is more typically seen in advanced progressive disease.

Impact on Quality of Life

Even mild dysarthria can have profound effects on a person's quality of life. Research documents that dysarthria in MS gives rise to emotional, psychological, and social difficulties. People with MS-related speech changes commonly report irritation at not being understood, feelings of loneliness and social isolation, emotional distress, depression, anxiety, and reduced self-esteem. Many begin to avoid conversations, especially in noisy environments or group settings, leading to progressive social withdrawal even when their speech impairment might be objectively classified as "mild."

Cognitive-Communication Challenges

While dysarthria is the most visible communication challenge in MS, cognitive-communication difficulties are equally important and often underrecognized. Between 45% and 65% of people with MS experience cognitive difficulties that directly impact their ability to communicate effectively, even when their speech muscles work normally.

Word-Finding Difficulties

One of the most commonly reported cognitive-communication complaints in MS is difficulty finding the right words. Known clinically as anomia or dysnomia, this experience is often described as having a word "on the tip of your tongue" but being unable to retrieve it. Qualitative research with MS patients reveals the frustration and embarrassment this causes, particularly in professional or social settings where quick verbal responses are expected.

Word-finding difficulties in MS are not the same as those seen in aphasia (where language networks themselves are damaged). Instead, they are typically related to slowed processing speed and impaired working memory—the brain knows the word but cannot retrieve it quickly enough for conversational flow.

Cognitive Fatigue and Communication

Fatigue is one of the most common and debilitating symptoms of MS, reported by up to 80% of patients. Cognitive fatigue has a particularly devastating effect on communication because it compounds every other communication difficulty. Research shows that people with MS experience rapid fatigue when engaging in mentally challenging tasks that require focused attention, and once fatigued, they tend to make more errors in both written and verbal communication.

The Fatigue-Communication Cycle

When tired, words become slurred, voices grow softer, speech may sound monotone, and word-finding difficulties worsen. This creates a vicious cycle: the effort of communicating increases fatigue, which further degrades speech, which demands even more effort. Many people with MS report that their communication abilities are significantly worse in the afternoon or evening, after sustained cognitive effort, or during periods of heat exposure (Uhthoff phenomenon).

Processing Speed and Conversational Demands

Slowed information processing is the most common cognitive deficit in MS. In conversation, this means a person may need extra time to understand what has been said, formulate a response, and produce it. Fast-paced conversations, group discussions, and situations with background noise become particularly challenging. The person may appear to "lose track" of conversations, miss nuances or humor, or provide delayed responses that seem out of context.

Executive Function and Communication

Problems with executive function in MS can affect the organizational aspects of communication: planning what to say, staying on topic, knowing when to take conversational turns, managing multi-step explanations, and adapting communication style to different listeners. The most common cognitive-linguistic complaints associated with MS include forgetfulness, difficulty finding the right words, and feelings of being overwhelmed by too much information.

Fluctuating Needs

Perhaps the most distinctive aspect of communication challenges in MS, particularly in the relapsing-remitting form, is their fluctuating nature. Unlike conditions such as ALS where abilities progressively decline, or stroke where deficits are relatively stable, MS presents a constantly shifting landscape of communication ability. This creates unique challenges for AAC planning and intervention.

Patterns of Fluctuation

Relapse-Related Changes
An MS relapse may suddenly impair speech for days, weeks, or months, then partially or fully resolve. A person might need a speech-generating device during a relapse but not between relapses. These episodes are unpredictable and can range from mild speech slurring to complete inability to speak.
Daily Fluctuations (Fatigue Cycle)
Many people with MS have better speech in the morning and progressively worse speech as the day goes on. Some people are intelligible before noon but need AAC support by evening. Heat, stress, and physical exertion can also temporarily worsen speech (Uhthoff phenomenon).
Situational Variation
Speech may be adequate in quiet one-on-one conversations but break down in noisy environments, group settings, or phone calls. Emotional stress, illness, or poor sleep can also temporarily degrade speech quality.
Progressive Baseline Decline
Over years, especially as the disease transitions to a progressive phase, the baseline level of communication ability may gradually decrease. "Good days" become less good, and "bad days" become more frequent.
The AAC Flexibility Imperative

The fluctuating nature of MS means that AAC solutions must be highly flexible. Individuals with degenerative, improving, and relapsing-remitting conditions require frequent adjustments to AAC access, commonly to accommodate physical changes. Some adjustments to the AAC software presentation may also be necessary based on increasing or decreasing vocabulary and linguistic complexity. An ideal AAC system for someone with MS should be easy to adopt when needed, easy to set aside when not needed, and adaptable to different levels of ability.

How AAC Helps

Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) encompasses a wide range of strategies, tools, and technologies that supplement or replace natural speech when it is insufficient for effective communication. For people with MS, AAC is not necessarily about replacing speech entirely—more often, it is about augmenting natural speech during times when it is less reliable and providing alternative methods for those times when speech fails completely.

Key Benefits of AAC for People with MS

  • Reducing communication fatigue: Using AAC for some interactions conserves energy for the conversations that matter most, helping manage the fatigue that is pervasive in MS.
  • Maintaining social participation: By providing reliable communication methods, AAC helps prevent the social isolation that commonly accompanies speech difficulties in MS.
  • Supporting employment: AAC tools can help people with MS continue to work by providing reliable communication in professional settings, including meetings, phone calls, and presentations.
  • Emergency communication: Having AAC available ensures that a person can always communicate critical needs, even during severe relapses.
  • Preserving identity: Voice banking technology allows people to use a synthetic version of their own voice, maintaining their vocal identity even when natural speech is no longer possible.
  • Reducing anxiety: Simply knowing that AAC is available as a backup can reduce the anxiety associated with unpredictable speech difficulties, which itself can improve natural speech performance.

Types of AAC for MS

The AAC solutions most appropriate for people with MS span a wide range, from simple low-tech strategies to sophisticated high-tech devices. The right solution depends on the individual's current abilities, the nature and severity of their communication difficulties, their cognitive status, their physical abilities, and the specific communication situations they need to manage.

No-Tech and Low-Tech Solutions

✍
No-Tech Strategies

These approaches require no equipment and can be deployed immediately during unexpected speech difficulties:

  • Writing or drawing: Pen and paper for quick messages when speech fails
  • Gesture and facial expression: Using natural gestures to supplement unclear speech
  • Partner-assisted scanning: A communication partner reads through options while the person indicates yes/no
  • Alphabet supplementation: Pointing to the first letter of each word while speaking to help the listener
📋
Low-Tech Tools

Simple, portable tools that provide reliable communication without power or connectivity:

  • Communication boards: Printed boards with pictures, words, or symbols
  • Picture-based books: Organized collections of visual symbols for common topics
  • Alphabet boards: Simple letter boards for spelling out messages
  • Phrase cards: Pre-printed cards with common phrases for specific situations (medical appointments, ordering food, etc.)

Mid-Tech Solutions

📱
App-Based AAC

Smartphone and tablet apps provide flexible, socially acceptable AAC that can be deployed quickly when needed:

  • Text-to-speech apps: Type a message and the device speaks it aloud (e.g., Speak4Me, Proloquo4Text)
  • Symbol-based apps: Apps with organized grids of symbols for quick message construction (e.g., Proloquo2Go, TD Snap, CoughDrop)
  • Predictive text apps: Apps that anticipate what the user wants to say, reducing the effort of message composition
  • Speech amplifiers: Portable amplification systems that boost a weak or quiet voice

App-based AAC is particularly well-suited to the fluctuating nature of MS because a smartphone is always available, socially normalized, and can serve as a backup communication method that does not draw attention when it is not needed.

High-Tech Solutions

🖥
Dedicated Speech-Generating Devices (SGDs)

For people with advanced MS who rely on AAC as their primary communication method, dedicated SGDs offer robust, purpose-built communication:

  • Eye-tracking devices: Systems like the Tobii Dynavox TD I-Series or Eyegaze Edge that allow users to control a communication device using only eye movements. These are critical for people whose hand function is also impaired.
  • Switch-based systems: Devices controlled by switches that can be activated by any reliable body movement (head, elbow, knee, etc.)
  • Integrated communication systems: Devices that combine AAC with computer access, environmental control, and internet connectivity

The Eyegaze Edge, for example, uses a special eye-tracking camera mounted below the screen that observes one of the user's eyes, with sophisticated image processing software analyzing the camera's images 60 times per second to determine where the user is looking. This allows the user's eyes to become a portal for communication, computer access, and environmental control.

Voice & Message Banking

Voice banking and message banking are proactive strategies that allow people with MS to preserve their vocal identity for future use with AAC technology. Given the progressive nature of MS and the possibility that speech may deteriorate over time, these technologies represent a critical component of long-term communication planning.

Voice Banking

Voice banking is a process in which an individual records a variety of phrases and words while their speech is still clear and strong. These recordings are then used to create a synthetic voice that closely matches the person's natural tone, cadence, and personality. When the person later needs to use a speech-generating device, it can speak in a voice that sounds like them rather than a generic computer voice.

Recent advances in artificial intelligence have dramatically improved the quality of banked voices. AI-powered voice generation can now create remarkably natural-sounding synthetic voices from relatively modest amounts of recorded speech, making voice banking more accessible and the resulting voices more lifelike than ever before.

Voice Banking Services

Several services offer voice banking for people with progressive neurological conditions, including SpeakUnique (which offers both "Voice Build" for creating a synthetic replica and "Voice Repair" for enhancing deteriorated voices), ModelTalker, VocalID, and Acapela My-own-Voice. Funding is now available in some regions specifically for MS patients to access voice banking services. The MS community is increasingly recognized as a priority population for these services.

Message Banking

Message banking takes a different approach: rather than creating a synthetic voice, it records specific phrases in the person's own natural voice for later playback. These might include personally meaningful phrases ("I love you," "Come here, sweetie"), common expressions, laughter, or any phrase that the person wants to preserve exactly as they say it. Message-banked phrases capture not just the words but the person's unique intonation, emotion, and delivery.

When to Start

The most important principle with both voice banking and message banking is to start early, while speech is still at its best. For people with MS, this ideally means beginning during remission periods when speech is clear. Given the unpredictable nature of MS, there is a risk that a severe relapse could permanently reduce speech quality before banking has been completed, so early action is strongly recommended. Even if a person currently has excellent speech and no immediate communication concerns, banking their voice is a valuable precaution.

Broader Assistive Technology

AAC exists within a broader ecosystem of assistive technology (AT) that supports people with MS across many aspects of daily life. Understanding this ecosystem is important because communication needs often intersect with other functional needs, and integrated solutions can provide the most effective support.

Voice-Activated Technology

Voice-activated virtual assistants such as Amazon Alexa, Apple Siri, and Google Assistant have become powerful accessibility tools for people with MS. These systems can control smart home devices, send messages, make phone calls, set reminders, and perform internet searches—all through voice commands. For someone with limited hand function but adequate speech, voice control can turn an entire home into a highly accessible environment.

However, the irony is that voice-activated technology becomes less reliable precisely when it is most needed: when dysarthria makes speech less clear, voice recognition systems may struggle to understand commands. This is an active area of research, with companies working to improve recognition of atypical speech patterns.

Mobility and Positioning

Proper positioning is critical for effective AAC use, and occupational and physical therapists play an essential role in ensuring that AAC devices are properly mounted and accessible. For people with MS who use wheelchairs, AAC devices may be mounted on the chair. Those with tremor may need stabilized device mounts, and those with visual impairment may need specific screen positioning or enlarged displays.

Integrated Solutions

Modern assistive technology increasingly offers integrated solutions where a single device serves multiple functions. For example, an eye-tracking speech-generating device may also provide computer access, email, web browsing, and environmental control (operating lights, doors, television, etc.). This integration is particularly valuable for people with MS who may have multiple functional limitations and benefit from a unified system rather than multiple separate devices.

Evidence & Research

The body of research on communication in MS and the use of AAC continues to grow, though much of the AAC-specific research has historically focused on conditions like ALS and cerebral palsy. Here are key findings from the research literature relevant to MS and AAC.

Dysarthria Prevalence Research

A systematic review and meta-analysis examining the prevalence of dysarthria in the MS population found a pooled prevalence of 44.9%. Individual studies report rates ranging from 40% to 56%, with severity generally classified as mild in the majority of cases and primarily consisting of mixed spastic-ataxic components. Importantly, research has shown that articulatory speech measures can be correlated with the severity of multiple sclerosis, suggesting that speech assessment may serve as a biomarker for disease progression.

Cognitive-Communication Research

Studies on self-reported language impairment in MS have found a significant association between language difficulties and reduced health-related quality of life. Research using international survey methodology has documented that cognitive-communication difficulties are often under-diagnosed and under-treated in MS, partly because they may be less obvious than motor speech problems and partly because patients may attribute their difficulties to fatigue rather than recognizing them as a distinct symptom of MS.

Speech Treatment Evidence

Research on speech therapy for MS-related dysarthria suggests that targeted treatment can be beneficial. Studies on ataxia-tailored speech treatment indicate it may be effective for degenerative cerebellar disease. IMITAF (Intensive Motor-speech Treatment with Augmented Feedback) has shown potential therapeutic benefits for patients with ataxic dysarthria by engaging a cortico-subcortical network involved in procedural motor learning.

Research Gap: AAC and MS

While there is substantial research on dysarthria in MS and on AAC for other populations (ALS, cerebral palsy, developmental disabilities), there is relatively limited research specifically examining the effectiveness and outcomes of AAC interventions for people with MS. This is an area where more research is needed, particularly regarding the unique challenge of fluctuating abilities and the psychological aspects of intermittent AAC use.

Neuroimaging and Speech

Recent neuroimaging research using volumetric and diffusion MRI has identified specific brain abnormalities associated with dysarthria in MS. These studies help explain why some people with MS develop speech problems while others do not, and may eventually help predict who will develop communication difficulties, allowing for earlier AAC intervention.

Key Considerations

When planning AAC for people with MS, several unique factors must be taken into account. MS differs from many other conditions where AAC is used, and these differences have important practical implications.

1. Plan for Fluctuation, Not Just Decline

Unlike conditions with a straightforward progressive trajectory, MS communication abilities can improve, worsen, and improve again. AAC systems must be designed so they can be easily activated when needed and easily set aside when not. A person should not feel "locked in" to using AAC when their speech is adequate, nor should they have to go through a complex setup process when they suddenly need it.

2. Address Cognitive Load

Because MS often affects cognition alongside speech, AAC systems must be cognitively accessible. Overly complex AAC layouts with deep hierarchies and many symbols may be difficult for someone experiencing cognitive fatigue, slowed processing, or executive function challenges. Systems should offer simplified modes for high-fatigue periods and more complete interfaces for better days.

3. Consider Multiple Access Methods

MS can affect hand function, vision, and speech simultaneously, and the relative severity of these impairments may shift over time. An effective AAC plan should include or anticipate multiple access methods: touchscreen for when hands work well, switch access for when fine motor control is limited, eye tracking for when hand and speech function are both severely impaired. The transition between access methods should be as seamless as possible.

4. Manage Fatigue Proactively

AAC system design for MS should actively account for fatigue management. This includes features such as:

  • Quick access to frequently used phrases to reduce message composition effort
  • Robust word and phrase prediction to minimize keystrokes or selections
  • Saved phrases for routine situations (ordering at a restaurant, medical appointments, workplace meetings)
  • Easy ability to switch between typing and symbol-based communication depending on energy level
  • Rate enhancement features that speed up communication with minimal cognitive effort

5. Preserve Identity and Autonomy

For many people with MS, the need for AAC is emotionally complex. It can feel like an acknowledgment of disease progression or a loss of independence. AAC interventions should be presented as empowering tools rather than signs of decline. Using a voice bank of the person's own voice, choosing socially discrete AAC methods (phone apps rather than conspicuous devices), and maintaining maximum independence in communication all help preserve the person's sense of identity.

6. Team-Based Approach

Effective AAC implementation for MS requires collaboration among multiple professionals. Speech-language pathologists (SLPs) assess communication needs and recommend AAC strategies. Occupational therapists optimize positioning and access methods. Physical therapists address seating and mobility. Rehabilitation engineers help with device programming and mounting. Neurologists provide information about disease trajectory. And, critically, the person with MS and their family are at the center of all decisions.

7. Plan for Swallowing Alongside Speech

MS frequently affects swallowing (dysphagia) alongside speech, since many of the same muscles and neural pathways are involved. Communication about food preferences, consistency requirements, and mealtime needs may become an important AAC function. AAC systems should include vocabulary and quick phrases related to eating, drinking, and swallowing safety.

The Psychological Dimension

The unpredictable nature of MS means that people may oscillate between needing and not needing AAC, which can create a complex psychological relationship with these tools. Some people resist AAC because accepting it feels like accepting a worse outcome. Others find great relief in having a backup plan. SLPs and counselors who work with MS patients should address these psychological dynamics directly, normalizing AAC as a practical tool rather than a symbol of loss.

Funding & Access

Obtaining appropriate AAC technology involves navigating a landscape of funding sources, insurance requirements, and organizational support. Understanding these pathways is essential for people with MS and their families.

Insurance Coverage

Speech-generating devices (SGDs) can be funded through several insurance channels in the United States:

  • Medicare: Covers SGDs as durable medical equipment (DME) when medically justified. The evaluation must be performed by an SLP, and the device must be prescribed by a physician.
  • Medicaid: Coverage varies by state but generally covers SGDs. Some states have more generous coverage than others.
  • Commercial Insurance: Many private insurers (Blue Cross Blue Shield, Aetna, etc.) cover SGDs. The key is proper documentation and medical justification.
  • Tablet-Based Devices: iPad-based AAC solutions can be funded when purchased as "bundled" or "dedicated" devices from AAC vendors. Important: when writing funding requests, refer to the device as a "tablet-based, dynamic display communication device," not an iPad, to avoid rejection.

MS Foundation Assistive Technology Program

The Multiple Sclerosis Foundation offers an Assistive Technology Program that can help locate and partially or fully fund devices, including communication devices, for people with MS. This program can be a valuable supplement to insurance coverage, particularly for items that insurance does not cover.

Additional Funding Sources

  • State Vocational Rehabilitation: For individuals who are working or seeking employment, state VR agencies may fund AAC as part of a return-to-work plan.
  • State Assistive Technology Programs: Every US state has an AT program (often called AT Act programs) that may offer device loans, demonstrations, or funding assistance.
  • National MS Society: Offers grants and can connect individuals with local resources.
  • Health Savings Accounts (HSA): Some HSA plans will cover AAC devices as qualified medical expenses.
  • Nonprofit organizations: Various organizations offer grants for assistive technology, including communication devices.
  • AAC manufacturer programs: Some AAC companies offer trial periods, loaner programs, or financial assistance programs.
