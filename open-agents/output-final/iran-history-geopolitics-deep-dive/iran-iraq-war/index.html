Origins

Why Saddam Invaded

The Shatt al-Arab Waterway Dispute

The Shatt al-Arab (Arvand Rud in Persian), formed by the confluence of the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, was Iraq's only navigable access to the Persian Gulf. Sovereignty over this critical waterway had been contested for centuries.

In the 1975 Algiers Agreement, a weakened Iraq under Saddam Hussein (then vice president) conceded to Iran's demand to share sovereignty along the thalweg -- the deepest channel line. Saddam viewed this as a humiliation forced upon him by the Shah's military superiority.

When the Iranian Revolution toppled the Shah in 1979, Saddam saw his opportunity. On September 17, 1980, he publicly tore up the Algiers Agreement on television, declared full Iraqi sovereignty, and five days later launched the invasion.

Fear of the Shia Revolution Spreading

Khomeini's Shia theocratic state explicitly called for exporting its revolutionary ideology throughout the Muslim world. This posed an existential threat to Saddam's Sunni-minority Ba'athist government -- Iraq's population was approximately 55-60% Shia.

Khomeini had lived in exile in the Iraqi Shia holy city of Najaf for 14 years and had deep connections with Iraqi Shia clerical networks. Iranian revolutionary rhetoric specifically targeted Iraq's Shia majority as a potential revolutionary base. In 1979-1980, Iran-linked groups attempted assassinations of senior Iraqi officials, including Deputy Prime Minister Tariq Aziz.

Territorial Ambitions & Arab-Persian Rivalry

Saddam sought to replace Egypt (ostracized from the Arab world after the 1979 Camp David Accords with Israel) as the leader of the Arab world. Conquering Iran's oil-rich, Arabic-speaking Khuzestan province -- which Iraq called "Arabistan" -- would bolster these claims and significantly increase Iraq's oil reserves.

Ba'athist ideology emphasized pan-Arab nationalism. Saddam framed the war as a defense of the Arab nation against Persian expansionism, invoking the seventh-century Battle of Qadisiyyah (636 CE). The conflict was officially dubbed "Qadisiyyat Saddam" by Iraqi propaganda.

Strategic Miscalculation

Saddam's Fatal Assumption: The Iranian Revolution had purged thousands of experienced military officers. Iran's international isolation and the U.S. arms embargo left it unable to acquire spare parts for its American-made F-14 Tomcats, F-4 Phantoms, and Chieftain tanks. Saddam calculated that a swift strike would cause the collapse of the Khomeini regime.

He was catastrophically wrong. The war he expected to last weeks lasted eight years.

Military Operations

Course of the War

September 22, 1980

Iraqi Invasion

200,000 troops, 2,000 tanks, and supporting air power launched across a 644-kilometer front. Iraqi air strikes, modeled on Israel's 1967 preemptive attack, targeted Iranian airfields but failed to destroy Iran's air force on the ground -- a critical strategic failure that would haunt Iraq throughout the war.

October - November 1980

Battle of Khorramshahr -- "City of Blood"

The defining early engagement. Approximately 2,000 Iranian regulars and 2,000 Revolutionary Guards fought Iraqi forces street by street for 34 days. Approximately 7,000 soldiers died on each side. The fierce resistance stalled the Iraqi offensive, consumed reserves, and gave Iran time to mobilize its larger population. The city was known ever after as "Khuninshahr" -- City of Blood.

1981 - 1982

Iranian Reorganization & Counterattack

Iran combined the regular army (Artesh) with the IRGC (Pasdaran) and Basij volunteer militia. A series of major counteroffensives pushed Iraqi forces back across the front.

May 24, 1982

Liberation of Khorramshahr

Operation Beit ol-Moqaddas: Iranian forces spearheaded by the 77th Khorasan Division, Revolutionary Guards, and Basij crossed the Karun River under devastating fire and recaptured the city. An estimated 19,000 Iraqi soldiers were captured. This pivotal victory restored Iranian morale and set the stage for Iran's fateful decision to carry the war into Iraq.

July 1982

Operation Ramadan -- Crossing into Iraq

Iran launched its first major offensive into Iraqi territory, targeting Basra. Mass infantry assaults by Basij and Pasdaran sought to overwhelm Iraqi defenses through sheer force of numbers, with casualty ratios sometimes reaching 5:1 in Iraq's favor. The offensive failed to take Basra but marked a new, bloodier phase of the war.

February 1984

The War of the Cities Begins

Saddam initiated systematic aerial bombardment and missile attacks against Iranian cities to break civilian morale. Iraq struck 11 Iranian cities, killing at least 1,200 civilians in the initial raids. Over the course of the war, Iraq fired 533 ballistic missiles against Iran, hitting 27 cities, killing 2,312 civilians and injuring 11,625.

1984 - 1988

The Tanker War

Iraq attacked Iranian oil tankers and export facilities in the Persian Gulf. Iran retaliated against ships carrying oil from Iraq's Gulf Arab allies. Total: Iraq conducted 283 attacks on merchant vessels; Iran conducted 168. The conflict internationalized the war and drew in the U.S. Navy.

1984 - 1987

War of Attrition

The war settled into a grueling stalemate reminiscent of World War I: trenches, minefields, barbed wire, and fortified bunkers stretching across hundreds of kilometers. Neither side could achieve a decisive breakthrough despite repeated costly offensives.

January 1987

Operation Karbala-5

Iran's last major offensive against Basra. Repeated human-wave assaults against entrenched Iraqi positions produced catastrophic Iranian casualties without achieving a strategic breakthrough. This failure marked the turning point -- Iran could no longer sustain the offensive.

July 1987 - September 1988

Operation Earnest Will

The U.S. reflagged 11 Kuwaiti tankers with American flags and provided Navy escorts -- the largest naval convoy operation since World War II. The American presence led to direct confrontations with Iran.

March 16, 1988

Halabja Chemical Attack

Iraq bombarded the Kurdish city of Halabja with mustard gas, tabun, sarin, and possibly VX nerve agent, killing at least 3,200 Kurdish civilians. Part of the broader Anfal campaign that killed 50,000-100,000 Kurds -- later classified as genocide. The international community's response was shamefully inadequate.

April 18, 1988

Operation Praying Mantis

After the USS Samuel B. Roberts struck an Iranian mine, the U.S. launched the largest American naval engagement since World War II, destroying two Iranian oil platforms, two naval vessels, six armed speedboats, and severely damaging a frigate.

July 3, 1988

Iran Air Flight 655 Shot Down

The USS Vincennes shot down Iran Air Flight 655, a civilian Airbus A300 on a scheduled Tehran-Dubai route. All 290 people aboard -- including 66 children -- were killed. The plane was on a normal flight path, squawking a civilian transponder code, and continuously ascending. The U.S. Navy was operating inside Iranian territorial waters. The U.S. never formally apologized. For Iranians, this remains a defining symbol of American hostility.

July 20, 1988

Khomeini "Drinks the Poison"

After Iraqi offensives recaptured the Fao Peninsula and pushed back Iranian forces, Khomeini accepted UN Security Council Resolution 598. He declared that accepting the ceasefire was "more deadly than drinking from a poisoned chalice," but made the decision "solely on the basis of expediency."

August 20, 1988

Ceasefire Takes Effect

After eight years, the war ended with borders essentially unchanged from where they started. Both nations were economically devastated. Neither side achieved its war aims.

Warfare

The Human Cost of Human Wave Tactics

The Basij Militia

The Basij Mobilization Resistance Force, established by Khomeini in November 1979, recruited hundreds of thousands of volunteers -- many of them teenagers and elderly men -- motivated by revolutionary fervor and religious zeal. Basij volunteers were promised martyrdom and paradise if they died fighting.

These mass infantry assaults sought to overwhelm Iraqi defensive positions through sheer force of numbers. Thousands of Basij and Pasdaran would advance across minefields and into fortified Iraqi positions, often suffering catastrophic casualties. Despite their enormous human cost, these tactics achieved mixed results -- occasionally breaking through Iraqi lines but never achieving a decisive strategic breakthrough.

"Accepting the ceasefire is more deadly than drinking from a poisoned chalice. I have made my decision solely on the basis of expediency and in the interest of the revolution."
-- Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini, July 20, 1988
The 1988 Prison Massacres

In the immediate aftermath of accepting the ceasefire, the regime carried out mass executions of political prisoners, primarily members of the Mojahedin-e Khalq (MEK) and leftist groups. Estimates range from several thousand to 30,000 killed. Ordered by Khomeini through a fatwa, this represented a final act of ideological consolidation -- the regime, humiliated by the "poisoned chalice," moved to eliminate internal dissent.

War Crimes

Chemical Weapons & Western Complicity

Unprecedented Chemical Warfare

Iraq's use of chemical weapons represented the most extensive employment of chemical warfare since World War I and included the first verified combat use of nerve agents in history.

Beginning with mustard gas in 1983, Iraq escalated to tabun (1984), sarin, and possibly VX nerve agent. More than 30 targeted chemical attacks were conducted against Iranian military forces and civilians.

Western Complicity in Chemical Warfare

The international community's response to Iraq's chemical weapons use was shamefully inadequate. The Reagan administration continued to provide intelligence and economic support to Iraq despite knowing Saddam was carrying out the worst chemical attacks in history.

  • Dutch businessman Frans van Anraat was convicted in 2005 of complicity in war crimes for shipping more than 1,100 tons of thiodiglycol (a mustard gas precursor) from a U.S. company to Iraq
  • German, British, and other Western companies supplied dual-use equipment and chemicals
  • Iraq was not severely criticized internationally until after Halabja in 1988 -- the previous five to six years of chemical warfare against Iran received minimal attention
  • The UN investigated and confirmed Iraq's use multiple times but took no enforcement action

For Iranians, the world's silence in the face of chemical attacks remains a powerful source of grievance and distrust of international institutions. It directly influenced Iran's insistence on self-reliance in military matters -- if the world would not protect them, they would develop their own deterrent capabilities.

Geopolitics

International Involvement: Everyone Played a Role

The Central Contradiction

The Iran-Iraq War revealed perhaps the most cynical chapter of Cold War realpolitik. The United States simultaneously armed both sides, the Soviet Union shifted allegiances based on who was winning, France sold cutting-edge weapons to Iraq, Gulf states funded the war to protect their thrones, and Israel secretly sold weapons to the Islamic Republic that publicly called for its destruction. Every major power was playing its own game while hundreds of thousands died.

Second-Largest Arms Supplier to Iraq

Approximately $17 billion in cutting-edge military equipment. Sold 133 Mirage F-1 fighters, "loaned" 5 Super Etendard attack aircraft from its own navy, and supplied ~812 Exocet anti-ship missiles used devastatingly in the Tanker War.

Saudi Arabia

Financial Backer -- $25-30.9 Billion

🇰🇼

Kuwait

Financial Backer -- $6-8.2 Billion

Contributed billions in loans and oil sales on Iraq's behalf. Ironically, Iraq's massive postwar debt to Kuwait (~$14 billion) became a key factor in Saddam's 1990 invasion of Kuwait, triggering the Gulf War.

🇮🇱

Israel

Secret Arms Supplier to Iran

In one of the war's most paradoxical dimensions, Israel secretly sold weapons to the Islamic Republic despite Khomeini's anti-Israel rhetoric. Both nations shared a common enemy in Saddam. Israeli arms sales to Iran reached $500 million between 1981-1983 alone -- including F-4 spare parts, TOW missiles, and HAWK components.

"Better for the two to kill each other than to unite against us"

The Iran-Contra Scandal

In a stunning contradiction of its pro-Iraq tilt, the Reagan administration simultaneously conducted secret arms sales to Iran. Beginning in August 1985, the U.S. authorized Israel to sell American-made TOW anti-tank missiles and HAWK missile parts to Iran. In early 1986, the operation shifted to direct U.S. sales.

Lieutenant Colonel Oliver North, operating from the National Security Council, diverted profits from the Iranian arms sales to fund Contra rebels in Nicaragua, circumventing a congressional ban (the Boland Amendment).

When exposed in November 1986, it revealed the deep contradictions in U.S. policy: Washington was simultaneously arming both sides while publicly supporting Iraq. Of the $30 million Iran reportedly paid, only $12 million reached government accounts. Fourteen officials were charged with crimes.

Attacks on Oil Shipping

In December 1986, Kuwait requested American naval protection for its tankers. The Reagan administration agreed to reflag eleven Kuwaiti tankers with American flags and provide U.S. Navy escorts. Operation Earnest Will (July 1987 - September 1988) became the largest naval convoy operation since World War II.

Iran Air Flight 655 -- July 3, 1988

The USS Vincennes, a guided-missile cruiser, shot down Iran Air Flight 655 -- a civilian Airbus A300 on a scheduled Tehran-Dubai route. All 290 people aboard were killed, including 66 children.

The plane was on a normal flight path, in an assigned airway, squawking a civilian transponder code, and continuously ascending. The U.S. Navy initially claimed the Vincennes was in international waters, but three years later Admiral William Crowe admitted on ABC's Nightline that the ship had been operating inside Iranian territorial waters.

The United States never formally apologized, though it eventually paid $131.8 million in compensation through the International Court of Justice in 1996. For Iranians, this remains a defining symbol of American hostility. The incident is widely regarded as a factor in Khomeini's subsequent decision to accept the ceasefire.

Proxy Warfare

Birth of the Proxy Doctrine: Creating Hezbollah

How the War Created Iran's Most Enduring Proxy

In June 1982, Israel invaded Lebanon. Iran responded by deploying approximately 1,500 IRGC advisers to Lebanon's Bekaa Valley, with Syrian permission. An additional 800 Revolutionary Guards provided political indoctrination, military training, and instruction in guerrilla and terrorist tactics.

Under IRGC supervision, various Lebanese Shia militant factions were consolidated into a single organization: Hezbollah (Party of God), formally established between 1982 and 1985. Iran provided:

  • Financial backing (eventually reaching hundreds of millions of dollars annually)
  • Weapons and military equipment
  • Training in guerrilla warfare, intelligence operations, and terrorist tactics
  • Political and ideological guidance
  • Organizational structure modeled on Iran's revolutionary institutions
Strategic Template Established

Hezbollah's creation established the template for Iran's "forward defense" doctrine -- projecting power through trained, ideologically aligned proxy forces rather than direct conventional military action. This model was later replicated with:

Iraqi Shia militias (Badr Corps, Kata'ib Hezbollah) | Palestinian groups (Hamas, Islamic Jihad) | Houthis in Yemen | Fatemiyoun Brigade (Afghan Shia) | Zainabiyoun Brigade (Pakistani Shia)

Missile Warfare

The War of the Cities & Birth of Iran's Missile Program

Systematic Bombardment of Civilians

Beginning in February 1984, Saddam initiated systematic aerial bombardment and missile attacks against Iranian cities to break civilian morale. The campaign unfolded across five major phases:

  • Phase 1 (February 1984): Indiscriminate attacks against 11 cities, killing at least 1,200 civilians
  • Phase 2 (January 1987): Iraq struck 35 towns and cities using aircraft, missiles, and artillery
  • Final Phase (January-February 1988): Modified Scud missiles ("Al-Hussein" variant) fired directly at Tehran, causing panic and mass evacuations

Lasting Consequence -- Iran's Missile Arsenal

The War of the Cities exposed a critical vulnerability: Iran initially had no surface-to-surface missile capability. It acquired Scud-B missiles from Libya (1985) and Syria (1986). This experience planted the seed for Iran's indigenous ballistic missile development program.

From reverse-engineered Scud technology, Iran eventually developed: Shahab-1 & 2 (short-range) | Shahab-3 (medium-range) | Sejjil (solid-fuel medium-range) | Zelzal, Fateh, and their successors. The missile program became a cornerstone of Iran's deterrence strategy -- compensating for its permanent lack of modern air power.

Legacy

How the War Forged Modern Iran

Military Doctrine: Self-Reliance & Asymmetric Warfare

Self-Reliance (Khod-kafai)

The international arms embargo and the failure of any country to defend Iran against chemical weapons instilled a deep commitment to indigenous arms production. Self-reliance, "holy defense," and export of the revolution entered the military lexicon during the war and were codified as doctrine in the early 1990s.

Asymmetric Warfare

The IRGC's experience using small boats, mines, and anti-ship missiles against the U.S. Navy during the Tanker War placed asymmetric warfare at the center of Iran's deterrence doctrine. Rather than matching American conventional superiority, Iran developed strategies emphasizing irregular warfare, area-denial capabilities, and proxy forces.

Proxy Warfare Doctrine

The successful creation of Hezbollah established the model for Iran's "forward defense" -- projecting influence through proxy forces across the region rather than through direct conventional military action.

Entrenchment of Revolutionary Institutions

The IRGC

Transformed from a loosely organized revolutionary militia into a parallel military establishment with its own army, navy, air force, and special operations capability. Its economic role expanded massively during the war. By war's end, the IRGC controlled significant portions of Iran's GDP and wielded deep political influence.

The Basij

Evolved from a wartime volunteer militia into a permanent internal security and social control organization. The massive sacrifices of Basij volunteers gave the organization enormous political legitimacy. After the war, it became the primary instrument for suppressing domestic dissent -- during the 1999 student protests, the 2009 Green Movement, and the 2022 Mahsa Amini protests.

The War Generation

IRGC and Basij veterans became the backbone of Iran's conservative political establishment. Future Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, President Mahmoud Ahmadinejad, Quds Force commander Qassem Soleimani, and numerous members of parliament were war veterans whose worldview was permanently shaped by the conflict.

The "Sacred Defense" Narrative

The war was framed as an existential struggle against foreign aggression, justifying authoritarian governance, military spending, and revolutionary ideology. Museums, memorials, and annual commemorations keep the war's memory alive as a tool of political mobilization to this day.

Regional & Global Consequences

  • Precipitated the 1990 Gulf War: Saddam invaded Kuwait partly to seize resources to pay off war debts and punish Kuwait for allegedly overproducing oil
  • WMD proliferation accelerated: Both nations pursued missile, chemical, and (in Iraq's case) biological and nuclear weapons programs
  • Deepened Sunni-Shia sectarian tensions: Established a pattern of proxy rivalry between Iran and the Gulf states that defines regional conflicts to this day
  • Validated the revolutionary narrative: The war reinforced the fortress mentality and narrative of resistance against Western betrayal that defines Iranian foreign policy
The Fundamental Lesson Iran Learned

When Iraq used chemical weapons on Iranian soldiers and civilians, the world was silent. When the U.S. shot down a civilian airliner killing 290 people, there was no apology. When Iran was invaded, no international institution came to its defense. The lesson Iran internalized: trust no one, rely only on yourself, and build deterrence through any means necessary.

This worldview -- forged in the trenches, chemical fog, and diplomatic betrayals of the Iran-Iraq War -- remains the operating system of the Islamic Republic's security establishment more than three decades later.
