Qajar Establishment: Reunification Through Brutality

From Tribal Chieftain to Shah

The Qajar dynasty emerged from one of the original Qizilbash Turkmen tribes that had helped found the Safavid Empire three centuries earlier. After the death of Karim Khan Zand in 1779, Iran descended into chaos as competing warlords fought for supremacy. From this turmoil emerged Agha Mohammad Khan Qajar, a figure of exceptional ruthlessness and determination.

Agha Mohammad Khan had been castrated as a child by his family's enemies (the Afsharid dynasty), a trauma that reportedly fueled his legendary cruelty. Nevertheless, he proved a brilliant military and political strategist. Over fifteen years (1779–1794), he systematically defeated all rivals:

  • Conquered the Zand heartland in southern Iran
  • Defeated Lotf Ali Khan, the last Zand pretender, in 1794
  • Reasserted Iranian control over Georgia and parts of the Caucasus
  • Unified Iran's fractured territories under a single authority for the first time since Nader Shah

Tehran as Capital

In a fateful decision, Agha Mohammad Khan chose Tehran — then a modest town near the ruins of ancient Ray — as his capital. The choice was strategic: centrally located, close to Qajar tribal lands in Mazandaran, and positioned to defend against threats from both Russia and internal rivals.

In 1796, Agha Mohammad Khan was formally crowned Shah. His reign was short-lived: he was assassinated by his own servants in 1797, reportedly after ordering a punishment of excessive cruelty.

The Price of Reunification

When the city of Kerman resisted his siege, Agha Mohammad Khan reportedly ordered 20,000 men blinded and had thousands of women and children enslaved. Despite this brutality, he created a unified state that would endure for 130 years and navigate Iran through the most dangerous period of European imperial expansion.

He was succeeded by his nephew Fath-Ali Shah (r. 1797–1834), whose long reign would be defined by catastrophic wars with Russia.

The Russo-Persian Wars: Losing the Caucasus

First Russo-Persian War (1804–1813)

The defining trauma of early Qajar rule was the catastrophic collision with the expanding Russian Empire in the Caucasus. For centuries, Iran had considered Georgia, Armenia, Azerbaijan, and Dagestan as part of its sphere of influence or direct territory.

In 1801, Russia formally annexed the Georgian kingdom of Kartli-Kakheti, an Iranian vassal. In January 1804, General Pavel Tsitsianov attacked and captured Ganja, killing its governor and massacring inhabitants. Iran, under Crown Prince Abbas Mirza, went to war.

Treaty of Gulistan (October 24, 1813)

Territorial losses: Iran ceded Dagestan, all of Georgia, and large parts of modern Armenia and Azerbaijan — including the khanates of Qarabagh, Ganja, Shirvan, Shaki, Baku, Derbent, and parts of Talysh.

Strategic concessions: Only Russia was permitted to maintain warships on the Caspian Sea, leaving Iranian shores permanently vulnerable. Russia also extracted a promise to support Abbas Mirza as heir, inserting Russian influence directly into Iranian succession politics.

Second Russo-Persian War (1826–1828)

In 1826, encouraged by initial Ottoman defeats of Russia and domestic hawks, Iran launched a war to recapture its lost Caucasian territories. It was a catastrophic miscalculation. Russian reinforcements under General Ivan Paskevich turned the war decisively, capturing Tabriz itself — the historic Safavid capital.

Treaty of Turkmenchay (February 22, 1828)

Territorial losses: Iran ceded the Erivan Khanate (much of modern Armenia), the Nakhchivan Khanate, and the remainder of the Talysh Khanate. The Iran-Russia boundary was set at the Aras River — where it essentially remains today.

Financial indemnity: Iran required to pay Russia 20 million silver rubles — a staggering sum for a pre-industrial state.

Caspian rights: Iran lost all remaining naval rights on the Caspian.

Capitulations: Russia gained extraterritorial legal rights over Russian subjects in Iran and allowed free emigration of Armenians, with ~45,000 from Iran and ~100,000 from Ottoman lands migrating to Russian Armenia.

Lasting Significance

The treaties of Gulistan and Turkmenchay are among the most humiliating episodes in Iranian history. Three separate nations — Georgia, Armenia, and Azerbaijan — would eventually emerge from these lost territories upon the dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991. The treaties established a pattern of unequal relations that would define the entire Qajar era.

The Great Game: Iran as Buffer State

The Strategic Context

The Great Game — the 19th-century rivalry between the British Empire and the Russian Empire for influence in Central Asia — placed Iran at the epicenter of global geopolitical competition. For Britain, the paramount concern was protecting India from Russian overland invasion. For Russia, the goal was continued southward expansion toward warm-water ports.

Iran was simultaneously a buffer state separating the two empires, a prize whose resources both coveted, and a pawn whose internal affairs both manipulated.

British Strategy
Contain Russia
Maintain Iran as an independent buffer — strong enough to resist Russia, but not strong enough to threaten British interests. Offer loans, military advisors, and diplomatic support, always with strings attached. Block Iranian claims to Herat and Afghanistan.
Russian Strategy
Expand South
Direct territorial expansion. Having absorbed Iran's Caucasian provinces, Russia conquered the Khanates of Kokand, Bukhara, and Khiva by the 1870s–80s. Economic penetration through loans, trade concessions, and infrastructure projects.

The Anglo-Persian War (1856–1857)

When Naser al-Din Shah attempted to recapture Herat (in modern Afghanistan), which Iran historically claimed, Britain declared war. General Sir James Outram attacked Iranian positions near Bushehr and in southern Mesopotamia.

Treaty of Paris (March 4, 1857)

Iran was forced to withdraw from Herat, relinquish all claims to Afghanistan, and accept British arbitration in future disputes with Afghan states. Iran's territorial ambitions in the east were permanently blocked by British power.

Iran as Client State

The cumulative effect was devastating for Iranian sovereignty. The Persian monarchy became dependent on British and Russian loans for revenue. Both powers had competing investments in Iranian infrastructure — not for Iran's benefit, but as instruments of influence. Iranian domestic politics became a three-way game between the Shah, British interests, and Russian interests, with the Shah steadily losing autonomy.

Economic Exploitation: Concessions and Foreign Debt

The Reuter Concession (1872)

The most egregious example of Qajar economic mismanagement: Naser al-Din Shah granted Baron Julius de Reuter (founder of the Reuters news agency) an extraordinary package of exclusive rights for 70 years, including all railways, all mines (except copper), all future factories and industries, all irrigation systems, and rights to build tramways and public works.

In exchange, Reuter offered the Shah £200,000 upfront and 25% of net revenues.

“The most complete and extraordinary surrender of the entire industrial resources of a kingdom into foreign hands that has probably ever been dreamed of.” — Lord Curzon, future British Viceroy of India

Under domestic outrage and fierce Russian opposition, the concession was cancelled within a year. However, the cancellation led to a second Reuter concession establishing the Imperial Bank of Persia — giving Reuter control over Iran's banking system and the right to issue currency.

The D'Arcy Oil Concession (1901)

Pivotal Decision: The D'Arcy Concession (May 28, 1901)

Mozaffar al-Din Shah granted William Knox D'Arcy exclusive rights to prospect for oil across three-quarters of Iran for 60 years. In exchange: £20,000 cash, an equivalent in company shares, and a promise of 16% of future profits. The terms were grossly unfair even by contemporary standards. The concession was granted by a non-constitutional government, and disputes about calculating “net profits” ensured Iran received far less than even the meager 16% promised.

The Pattern of Exploitation

  • Qajar shahs, chronically short of funds due to extravagant spending and weak tax collection, sold off Iran's economic future for short-term cash
  • Foreign powers gained control over Iranian resources at exploitative terms
  • The Iranian people bore the costs while the shah and his courtiers pocketed the payments
  • Each concession generated public outrage that gradually built into a revolutionary movement

The Tobacco Protest (1891): Iran's First Popular Movement

The Concession

In March 1890, Naser al-Din Shah granted a 50-year monopoly over the growth, sale, and export of all Iranian tobacco to Major Gerald F. Talbot, a British subject. In exchange: an annual payment of £15,000 and one quarter of net profits. The concession threatened the livelihoods of thousands of Iranian farmers, merchants, and bazaar traders.

The Protest Movement

When agents of the Imperial Tobacco Corporation arrived in 1891 and began asserting control, massive protests erupted. The movement was remarkable for its breadth, uniting three normally separate social forces:

Bazaar Merchants
Bazaaris
The commercial class whose livelihoods were directly threatened by the foreign tobacco monopoly.
Shia Clergy
Ulama
Religious leaders who saw the concession as both an affront to Islam and a threat to their own influence.
Reformist Intellectuals
Constitutionalists
A small but growing class influenced by European ideas of constitutional governance and popular sovereignty.

The Historic Fatwa

The protest reached its climax in December 1891 when the most important religious authority in Iran, Grand Ayatollah Mirza Hassan Shirazi (based in Samarra, Iraq), issued an extraordinary fatwa:

“In the name of God, the Merciful, the Beneficent. Today the use of both varieties of tobacco, in whatever fashion, is reckoned war against the Imam of the Age — may God hasten his advent.”

The effect was electrifying. Residents of Tehran immediately stopped smoking. The boycott spread rapidly. Merchants shut down main bazaars throughout the country. Even women in the Qajar royal harem quit smoking, and the Shah's own servants refused to prepare his water pipe.

Pivotal Outcome: Concession Cancelled (January 1892)

The Shah cancelled the concession, but was forced to pay £500,000 in compensation — further indebting Iran to foreign lenders. This was Iran's first successful mass popular movement, demonstrating that collective action could force the Shah to back down. It established the alliance between bazaaris and clergy that would prove decisive in every subsequent Iranian revolution, including 1905–1911 and 1979.

The Constitutional Revolution (1905–1911)

Causes

The Constitutional Revolution was the culmination of decades of accumulated grievances:

  • Foreign economic domination: Concessions like Reuter and D'Arcy had sold off Iran's resources to foreigners
  • Tax policy: A Belgian director of customs was enforcing tariff collections “with bureaucratic rigidity” to pay for Russian loans financing the Shah's extravagant European tours
  • Trade destruction: Tax breaks on foreign textile imports were devastating Iran's domestic craft industries
  • Royal debt: Enormous foreign debts whose interest payments drained the treasury
  • Intellectual ferment: Growing exposure to European constitutionalist ideas
  • The Tobacco Protest precedent: The 1891 movement had proved collective action could succeed

The Revolution Begins (1905–1906)

Merchants protesting the Belgian customs director's policies took bast (sanctuary) in mosques and the grounds of the British legation. The movement rapidly expanded from economic grievances to demands for fundamental political reform.

Pivotal Achievement: Iran's Constitution (1906)

In August 1906, the ailing Muzaffar al-Din Shah signed a royal decree calling for a Constituent Assembly. The first Majlis (National Assembly) opened in October 1906, composed of more than 60 bazaaris, 25 clerics, and 50 landlords and notables. They drafted Iran's first Constitution, signed into law in December 1906 — the first constitution in the Islamic world, predating even the Ottoman Young Turk revolution of 1908.

The Counter-Revolution (1908)

Muzaffar al-Din Shah's successor, Mohammad Ali Shah, was hostile to constitutional government. In June 1908, he launched a coup against the Majlis:

Bombardment of the Majlis (June 23, 1908)

The Shah enlisted the Russian-officered Cossack Brigade, commanded by Colonel Vladimir Liakhov. Liakhov's forces bombarded the Majlis building, killing several constitutionalist leaders. This “Minor Tyranny” (Estebdad-e Saghir) provoked fierce resistance, particularly in Tabriz, where the constitutionalist fighters Sattar Khan and Bagher Khan held out against 40,000 of the Shah's soldiers.

Restoration and Collapse (1909–1911)

In July 1909, constitutionalist forces from Tabriz and the Bakhtiari tribal confederation marched on Tehran and forced Mohammad Ali Shah's abdication. His young son, Ahmad Shah, was placed on the throne, and the constitution was restored.

Democracy Destroyed by Foreign Powers (December 1911)

The revolution effectively ended when the Shah's ministers, backed by 12,000 Russian troops, expelled deputies from parliament. The cause: Iran's attempt to hire an American financial advisor, Morgan Shuster, to reform the tax system — a move both Russia and Britain opposed because an effective Iranian government would threaten their spheres of influence.

The lesson seared into Iranian political consciousness: foreign powers were willing to destroy Iranian democracy when it threatened their interests.

The Anglo-Russian Convention (1907): Dividing Iran Without Consent

On August 31, 1907, in St. Petersburg, Britain and Russia signed the Anglo-Russian Convention, dividing Iran into three zones:

Russian Sphere
Northern Iran
All of northern Iran, bordering Russia's Transcaucasian and Central Asian possessions, was designated as Russia's exclusive sphere of influence.
British Sphere
Southeastern Iran
A zone from Kerman to Bandar Abbas on the coast, bordering British India, was designated as Britain's exclusive sphere.
Neutral Zone
Central & Southwest
The area between the two spheres, where both powers could compete for influence.
Iran Was Not Consulted

The government of Iran had not been consulted about the agreement. It was informed only after the fact. Although Iran refused to recognize the accord's legitimacy, it could not prevent implementation.

Iranian nationalists felt particularly betrayed by Britain, a country many constitutionalists had idealized as a model of democratic governance. The discovery that Britain would carve up Iran in a backroom deal — without even consulting Iran — shattered these illusions and became a pivotal moment in shaping Iranian attitudes toward the West.

The Discovery of Oil (1908): The Resource Curse Begins

The Search

D'Arcy hired geologist George Bernard Reynolds to prospect in the Iranian desert. Conditions were extreme: smallpox, bandits, scarce water, and temperatures exceeding 50°C. After seven years and more than £500,000 spent, D'Arcy and Burmah Oil decided to abandon Iran. They sent Reynolds a telegram ordering him to “cease work, dismiss the staff, dismantle anything worth transporting, and come home.”

Pivotal Moment: Reynolds Delays the Order

Reynolds delayed following these orders. On May 26, 1908, his drill struck oil at Masjed Soleyman in southwestern Iran. It was the first major oil discovery in the Middle East.

The Anglo-Persian Oil Company

The strike led to the establishment of the Anglo-Persian Oil Company (APOC) in 1909 — later renamed Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (AIOC) in 1935 and eventually becoming British Petroleum (BP) in 1954.

Churchill's Strategic Acquisition (1914)

On the eve of World War I, First Lord of the Admiralty Winston Churchill persuaded the British government to purchase a 51% controlling stake in APOC. The Royal Navy was converting from coal to oil, and securing a reliable supply was a matter of national security. Iran's most valuable natural resource was now controlled by the British government itself — not merely a private company, but a foreign state.

The profits flowed overwhelmingly to Britain while Iran received a fraction of revenues. This became a source of deep and lasting resentment that would culminate in Mossadegh's nationalization of the oil industry in 1951.

World War I: Occupation, Famine, and Catastrophe

Neutrality Violated

When World War I erupted in 1914, Iran declared neutrality. It was ignored by all belligerent powers. Iran was occupied by three empires simultaneously:

  • Russian forces occupied the north, including Azerbaijan and Khorasan
  • British forces occupied the south and southwest, protecting oil fields and approaches to India
  • Ottoman forces invaded from the west

Iranian territory became a theater of war, destroying infrastructure, disrupting agriculture, and displacing populations.

The Persian Famine of 1917–1919

A Forgotten Catastrophe: ~2 Million Dead

Causes: Successive droughts (1917–1918), requisition and confiscation of foodstuffs by occupying armies, Russian troops blockading roads in Khorasan (prohibiting grain transfers except for the Russian army), collapsed transport infrastructure, and the 1918 influenza pandemic.

Approximately 2 million Iranians died from hunger, cholera, plague, typhus, and influenza. The famine occurred substantially within British-occupied zones. Britain's priority was feeding its own troops, not protecting the Iranian civilian population.

The Persian famine remains one of the least-known major famines in world history — nearly absent from Western narratives but central to Iranian collective memory as evidence of the human cost of foreign occupation.

Post-WWI Chaos: Soviet Gilan and Tribal Revolts

The Power Vacuum

The end of World War I left Iran in near-total collapse. The central government under young Ahmad Shah was virtually powerless. Russian troops had withdrawn after the Bolshevik Revolution, but new threats emerged. British forces remained in occupation, and tribal warlords operated autonomously.

The Jangal Movement and the Persian Socialist Soviet Republic

In the northern province of Gilan, the Jangal Movement (“Jungle Movement”), led by Mirza Kuchik Khan, was a nationalist, anti-imperialist resistance movement based in the dense Caspian forests since 1915.

In May 1920, the Soviet Navy entered the Caspian port of Anzali, ostensibly pursuing White Russian forces. In June 1920, with Soviet support, Mirza Kuchik Khan proclaimed the Persian Socialist Soviet Republic.

An Uneasy Alliance

Mirza Kuchik Khan wanted national independence and social reform, not Bolshevik revolution. Soviet agents pushed for radical communist policies. A coup within the republic removed Mirza Kuchik Khan from leadership. Internal conflicts between nationalists and Soviet-backed communists paralyzed the movement.

By 1921, the Soviet Union withdrew support. Government forces under Colonel Reza Khan overran the dispersed revolutionaries. Mirza Kuchik Khan fled into the mountains and died of exposure in December 1921. His severed head was reportedly sent to Tehran as proof.

Reza Khan's Coup and the End of the Qajar Dynasty (1921–1925)

The Coup of February 1921

On February 22, 1921, Reza Khan, commander of the Persian Cossack Brigade, marched on Tehran and seized the capital in a largely bloodless coup, collaborating with Sayyid Zia al-Din Tabatabaei, a political journalist with British connections.

British Involvement

The coup was partially assisted by the British government, whose primary motivation was halting Bolshevik penetration of Iran. Britain feared that Soviet influence posed a direct threat to British India. A strong military government, London calculated, would provide a more reliable buffer than the feeble Qajar court.

May 1921

Reza Khan initially considered establishing a republic (inspired by Mustafa Kemal Ataturk's Turkey), but the Shia clerical establishment opposed a republican form of government, fearing it would marginalize their influence. He pragmatically accepted the monarchical form and crowned himself Reza Shah Pahlavi in 1926.

The Role of the Religious Establishment

Clerics as Political Actors

A defining feature of the Qajar era was the emergence of the Shia clerical establishment as an independent political force. The Safavids had created the institutional framework; the Qajars witnessed its maturation into a power center rivaling the state.

The Clerical Divide in the Constitutional Revolution

Pro-Constitution Clerics
Tabatabaei & Behbahani
Argued constitutional government was compatible with Islamic principles. Provided crucial religious legitimacy to the revolution.
Anti-Constitution Clerics
Sheikh Fazlollah Nuri
Argued human legislation was incompatible with divine law (sharia). Executed by the constitutionalists in 1909. Foreshadowed debates culminating in Khomeini's velayat-e faqih.

The Financial Power of the Clergy

Unlike Sunni clerics, who were typically state employees, Shia mujtahids controlled independent revenue streams:

  • Khums — one-fifth tax on certain categories of wealth
  • Zakat — alms tax
  • Religious endowments (awqaf)
  • Voluntary donations from followers

This financial independence meant the clergy could act as a counter-power to the state — a dynamic with no equivalent in the Sunni world and one that made Iranian politics fundamentally different from politics in Ottoman/Turkish, Arab, or Central Asian Muslim societies.

Legacy: How Foreign Interference Created Deep Iranian Distrust

The Qajar era established a pattern of foreign interference that created deep, lasting distrust of Western powers in Iranian political culture:

1
Territorial Theft

Russia stripped Iran of its Caucasian territories through superior military force and imposed humiliating treaties (Gulistan, Turkmenchay).

2
Economic Exploitation

Britain and its agents secured control of Iran's most valuable resources — tobacco, oil, banking, railways — at exploitative terms, enriching foreign shareholders while impoverishing Iranians.

3
Sovereignty Violations

The Anglo-Russian Convention of 1907 divided Iran into spheres of influence without even consulting Iran — treating a sovereign state as a colonial territory in all but name.

4
Democratic Sabotage

When Iranians created their own democratic institutions (the Constitutional Revolution), foreign powers destroyed them — Russia bombarded the Majlis, and both powers undermined parliamentary governance.

5
Humanitarian Catastrophe

Despite Iran's neutrality in WWI, foreign occupation contributed to a famine that killed approximately 2 million people — a catastrophe largely ignored by the responsible Western powers.

6
Coup Support

Britain supported Reza Khan's 1921 coup, installing a military strongman to serve British strategic interests rather than Iranian democratic aspirations.

The Psychological Legacy

In Iranian political discourse, the assumption that foreign powers are secretly manipulating events is not paranoia — it is the logical conclusion drawn from 130 years of documented history in which foreign powers repeatedly did exactly that. This explains why Mossadegh's oil nationalization (1951) generated such passionate support, why the CIA/MI6 coup (1953) was so deeply traumatic, why the 1979 Revolution framed itself as explicitly anti-imperialist, and why “neither East nor West” resonated so powerfully as a revolutionary slogan.
