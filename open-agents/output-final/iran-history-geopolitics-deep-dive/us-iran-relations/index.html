United States & Iran

From friendship to enmity — how two nations built a relationship on trust, shattered it, and locked themselves into a cycle of escalation that neither can escape

1834 – 1953

The Forgotten Friendship

When Iranians Loved America

Before 1953, Iranians viewed the United States not as an imperial aggressor but as a beacon of anti-colonialism — a "third force" that could counterbalance the predatory designs of Britain and Russia. This perception was grounded in reality, and the depth of betrayal Iranians later felt was proportional to the trust they had placed in America as a different kind of power.

⛳

American Presbyterian Missionaries (from 1834)

Established 117 schools around Urmia by 1895, along with hospitals and medical schools. Unlike British, Russian, and French counterparts, American missionaries had no obvious connection to state-sponsored political interests. They built institutions that served Iranians and cultivated genuine goodwill lasting over a century.

📝

W. Morgan Shuster (1911)

American lawyer invited by the Persian government to serve as treasurer-general. Determined to make Persia financially independent, he directly threatened British and Russian interests. When Russia demanded his removal and Britain backed the demand, he was forced out — but became a hero in Iranian national memory. His book The Strangling of Persia documented how European powers crushed Persian sovereignty.

⚔️

Howard Baskerville (1909)

American teacher who died fighting alongside constitutional revolutionaries during the Persian Constitutional Revolution. He became another symbol of American solidarity with Iranian democratic aspirations.

At the turn of the 20th century, anti-imperialist sentiment ran strong among Americans, and many Iranians sympathetic to the Constitutional Revolution saw the United States as a natural ally against both Russian and British imperialism.

August 1953

The 1953 Coup: The Original Sin

🇺🇸

Operation Ajax

CIA-MI6 Joint Operation

On August 19, 1953, Prime Minister Mohammad Mosaddegh — Iran's democratically elected leader — was overthrown in a coup orchestrated by Britain's MI6 (Operation Boot) and the CIA (Operation Ajax). The CIA acknowledged its role only in 2013, admitting it was "undemocratic."

Why Did the United States Do It?

  • Cold War Paranoia: British intelligence skillfully reframed what was fundamentally a commercial dispute over oil nationalization as a Cold War crisis. The Tudeh Party's vocal support for Mosaddegh, though not indicative of his policies, provided a convenient pretext.
  • Oil Interests: Mosaddegh nationalized the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (AIOC, later BP) after it refused to let Iran audit its books. After the coup, the Shah signed over 40% of Iran's oil fields to US companies.
  • The Dulles Brothers: Secretary of State John Foster Dulles and CIA Director Allen Dulles — both connected to the corporate world and ideologically committed to aggressive anti-communism — convinced Eisenhower to approve the operation.
  • The Operator: Kermit Roosevelt Jr. (grandson of Theodore Roosevelt) orchestrated the coup using CIA-funded agents, bribed military officers, hired mobs, and ran a media disinformation campaign.

🇺🇸 The American Framing

A necessary Cold War intervention to prevent Iran from falling into the Soviet orbit. Mosaddegh was unreliable, the communists were gaining influence, and Soviet expansion had to be stopped at any cost.

🇮🇷 The Iranian Reality

The crushing of Iran's first democratic government to serve Western economic interests. A straight line leads from the 1953 coup to the 1979 revolution. The coup became the foundational grievance of Iranian anti-Americanism — proof that America would overthrow a democracy for oil.

1953 – 1979

The Shah Era: America's Policeman in the Gulf

The Twin Pillar Policy

Under the Nixon Doctrine (1969), the US would support allies' defense but not deploy its own troops. When Britain withdrew from the Persian Gulf in 1971, the Nixon administration designated Iran and Saudi Arabia as the "twin pillars" of regional stability. The Shah became America's most important Middle Eastern ally.

Seeds of Revolution

The strategic logic was circular: the US advocated raising international oil prices to enable Iran to afford expensive weapons, which enriched American defense contractors. Meanwhile, the massive arms buildup, the Shah's authoritarian modernization ("White Revolution"), rapid urbanization, and SAVAK's repression created deep social dislocation.

The flood of American military personnel became a visible symbol of foreign domination. Ayatollah Khomeini, exiled in 1964 partly for opposing the Status of Forces Agreement that granted Americans legal immunity in Iran, built his revolutionary movement on opposition to this perceived American occupation.

1979 – 1981

Revolution & Hostage Crisis

⚖️

The Islamic Revolution

The Shah Falls — A New Order Rises

The 1979 revolution stunned the American foreign policy establishment. When Carter admitted the Shah for cancer treatment in October 1979, revolutionary students, fearing a repeat of 1953, stormed the US Embassy on November 4, calling it a "den of spies." 52 Americans were held for 444 days.

1981 – 1988

Reagan Era: Iran-Contra & the Tanker War

Iran-Contra Scandal

Despite publicly vowing never to negotiate with hostage-takers, the Reagan administration secretly sold weapons to Iran through Israeli intermediaries, hoping to secure hostage releases in Lebanon. Proceeds were illegally diverted to fund Contra rebels in Nicaragua. When exposed in 1986, the affair revealed cynical pragmatism beneath anti-Iran rhetoric — confirming Iranian suspicions about American duplicity.

The Tanker War & Operation Praying Mantis

When the Iran-Iraq War's "Tanker War" phase threatened Gulf oil shipments, the US intervened with Operation Earnest Will (1987-1988), escorting reflagged tankers. After the USS Samuel B. Roberts struck an Iranian mine, the US launched Operation Praying Mantis — the largest American naval engagement since World War II — destroying two Iranian oil platforms and sinking two ships.

The USS Vincennes Tragedy (July 3, 1988)

The USS Vincennes shot down Iran Air Flight 655, a commercial Airbus A300, killing all 290 passengers and crew, including 66 children. The United States never formally apologized. In 1990, the ship's captain received the Legion of Merit for "exceptionally meritorious conduct."

🇺🇸 US Perspective

A tragic accident in a combat zone. The crew misidentified the aircraft under the stress of ongoing hostilities.

🇮🇷 Iranian Perspective

Epitomizes American callousness toward Iranian lives. Remains one of the deepest wounds in the relationship. No apology. The captain was decorated.

1989 – 2000

A Decade of Missed Opportunities

🚫 George H.W. Bush: "Goodwill Begets Goodwill" (1989)

Bush signaled an opening in his inaugural address. Iran facilitated hostage releases. But Iran was reportedly furious that their efforts produced no promised goodwill — no relaxation of sanctions, no acknowledgment that Iraq had started the Iran-Iraq War. The opportunity evaporated.

Clinton: Dual Containment

The Clinton administration adopted "dual containment" (1993-94), simultaneously containing both Iran and Iraq — departing from the traditional balance-of-power approach. The 1996 Khobar Towers bombing (19 US Air Force personnel killed) pointed to Iranian involvement, and Clinton seriously considered military retaliation but chose restraint.

🚫 The Khatami Opening (1997)

The surprise election of reformist Mohammad Khatami offered the best opportunity for rapprochement in a generation. Khatami expressed desire to bring down the "wall of mistrust" in a widely publicized CNN interview. Clinton responded with modest gestures. But Tehran's hardliners sabotaged outreach, Clinton was consumed by domestic scandals, and the moment was lost — another missed opportunity that might have altered the entire trajectory.

2001 – 2008

George W. Bush: "Axis of Evil" & Strategic Blunder

Iran Cooperates (2001) After 9/11, Iran provided crucial intelligence, helped organize anti-Taliban forces, and participated constructively in the Bonn Conference establishing post-Taliban Afghanistan. Tehran expected a new chapter.
→
US Response (Jan 2002) Bush labels Iran part of the "Axis of Evil" alongside Iraq and North Korea. Outrage across Iran's political spectrum. The speech effectively destroyed the reformist argument that engagement with America was possible.

🚫 The Rejected Grand Bargain (May 2003)

Iran transmitted a comprehensive proposal through Swiss Ambassador Tim Guldimann, reportedly offering: nuclear transparency, recognition of Israel's right to exist, termination of support for Hamas and Hezbollah's military activities, and cooperation on Iraq. In return, Iran sought security guarantees, normalization, and sanctions relief.

The Bush administration, riding high after the rapid fall of Baghdad, dismissed the offer. Many analysts consider this one of the most consequential missed opportunities in US foreign policy.

The Supreme Irony: Iraq Empowers Iran

The 2003 invasion of Iraq — partly justified by the "Axis of Evil" framework — dramatically empowered Iran. Removing Saddam eliminated Iran's primary adversary, installed a Shia-majority government with deep ties to Tehran, and created a vacuum that Iranian influence rushed to fill.

2009 – 2016

Obama: Outreach & the JCPOA

The Opening

Obama entered office committed to engagement. He sent two private letters to Supreme Leader Khamenei and addressed Iran with unprecedented respect in a Nowruz (Persian New Year) message. Secret backchannel talks in Oman beginning in 2012 laid the groundwork. The election of Hassan Rouhani in 2013 provided a willing partner.

⚖️

The Soleimani Assassination (January 3, 2020)

A US drone strike near Baghdad International Airport killed Major General Qasem Soleimani, commander of the IRGC's Quds Force and widely considered the second most powerful person in Iran. Five Iraqi nationals and four other Iranians were also killed.

🇺🇸 US Perspective

Soleimani was responsible for the deaths of hundreds of US soldiers through IEDs and militias in Iraq. A legitimate military target.

🇮🇷 Iranian Perspective

The assassination of a national hero — equivalent to a foreign power killing a top US military commander. A watershed act of war.

Iran retaliated on January 8 by launching rockets at Al-Asad Air Base in Iraq, causing concussive brain injuries to over 100 Americans but no fatalities — a calibrated response designed to demonstrate capability while avoiding full-scale war.

2021 – 2024

Biden: Failed JCPOA Revival

Biden pledged to return to the JCPOA if Iran came back into compliance. Negotiations resumed in Vienna in April 2021 but were plagued by complications: hardliner Ebrahim Raisi's election as Iran's president, Russia's invasion of Ukraine complicating P5+1 unity, and mutual escalatory steps.

By late 2023, talks had effectively collapsed. Iran had advanced its nuclear program far beyond JCPOA limits, accumulating enriched uranium at 60% purity — just a short technical step from weapons-grade. The window for diplomatic resolution appeared to be closing.

Structural Forces

Domestic Political Forces Shaping US Iran Policy

🏭 AIPAC & the Israel Lobby

The American Israel Public Affairs Committee has consistently advocated for a hardline approach: "crippling" sanctions, opposing diplomatic engagement, fighting the JCPOA, and supporting its dissolution. AIPAC-organized trips to Israel are a rite of passage for new members of Congress, and its donor network provides substantial financial support to political campaigns.

📚 Neoconservative Ideology

Prominent in the George W. Bush administration, neoconservatives advocated for regime change in Iran as part of a broader project to democratize the Middle East through American power. Think tanks like the American Enterprise Institute, Hudson Institute, and Washington Institute for Near East Policy have consistently pushed confrontational Iran policies.

⛱ Evangelical Christian Zionism

Christians United for Israel, with over seven million members, is the largest pro-Israel lobbying group in America — larger than AIPAC itself. Many members of Congress bring theological convictions about Israel's biblical significance into foreign policy positions, creating a powerful constituency supporting hawkish positions toward Iran.

⛽️ Oil & Energy Interests

Complex interests: some companies benefit from Iranian oil reaching markets (lower prices), others benefit from sanctions (reduced competition, higher prices). The broader energy industry's influence on Middle East policy cannot be separated from Iran policy.

Perspectives

How Each Side Views the Other

🇮🇷 "The Great Satan"

Coined by Khomeini on November 5, 1979. Resonates because it is anchored in genuine grievances:

  • The 1953 coup destroying democracy
  • Support for the Shah's repression & SAVAK
  • USS Vincennes shootdown — no apology
  • Decades of sanctions punishing ordinary Iranians
  • Support for Saddam (including tolerance of chemical weapons)
  • Surrounding Iran with military bases

However, many ordinary Iranians — particularly younger generations — distinguish between the American government and American people. Anti-Americanism is less pervasive among citizens than official narratives imply.

🇺🇸 Terrorism, Nukes & Hostages

For most Americans, Iran is associated with:

  • The 1979 hostage crisis — the defining trauma
  • State sponsorship of terrorism (Hezbollah, Hamas)
  • Nuclear weapons proliferation
  • Killing American soldiers in Iraq
  • "Death to America" chants
  • Threats to Israel

The American media narrative tends to present Iran as irrational, with less attention to historical context. The concept of Iranian strategic rationality receives less attention than the "mad mullah" caricature.

Assessment

How US Decisions Created the Current Situation

A sober accounting reveals that many of the conditions the United States now finds most threatening about Iran were directly or indirectly produced by American policy choices:

1 The nuclear program: Accelerated after US withdrew from the JCPOA, removing constraints that had frozen it.
2 Regional proxy networks: Built partly as a deterrent against perceived US/Israeli threats of regime change.
3 Anti-American ideology: The 1953 coup provided the founding narrative for revolutionary anti-Americanism.
4 Iranian influence in Iraq: The 2003 invasion created the vacuum Iran filled.
5 Alliance with Russia & China: US sanctions drove Iran toward alternative great-power patrons.
6 Hardliner ascendancy: Each escalation cycle (Axis of Evil, JCPOA withdrawal, Soleimani) strengthened those who argued engagement was futile.

This is not to absolve Iran's government of responsibility for its own choices — including domestic repression, support for violent proxies, and nuclear ambiguity — but to recognize that the US-Iran confrontation is a co-created tragedy in which each side's actions have consistently produced the opposite of their stated intentions.
