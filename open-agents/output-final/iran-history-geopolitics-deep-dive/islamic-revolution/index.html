The Revolutionary Coalition

The Iranian Revolution was remarkable for the sheer breadth of the coalition that brought down the Pahlavi monarchy. Shared hatred of the Shah was the only glue binding together groups with fundamentally incompatible visions for Iran's future. Understanding who these factions were — and how every single one except the Islamists was destroyed — is essential to understanding what the revolution became.

Khomeini's Clerical Network
Seized Power
The core revolutionary force, operating through Iran's thousands of mosques, seminaries, and religious associations — an organizing infrastructure SAVAK could never fully penetrate. Institutionalized through the Islamic Republican Party (IRP), founded by Beheshti, Khamenei, and Rafsanjani.
Outcome: Captured every institution of the state. Velayat-e faqih enshrined in the constitution.
National Front / Freedom Movement (Liberals)
Eliminated
Heirs of Mossadegh's nationalist movement. Mehdi Bazargan's Freedom Movement blended Islamic values with democratic principles. Bazargan became the first post-revolutionary prime minister.
Outcome: Bazargan forced to resign on the day of the embassy seizure (Nov 4, 1979). National Democratic Front banned August 1979. Politically neutralized within months.
Mojahedin-e Khalq (MEK)
Crushed
Islamist-Marxist synthesis led by Massoud Rajavi. The most organized armed opposition group. Played a significant military role in the revolution itself.
Outcome: Launched armed insurgency June 1981 after Banisadr's ouster. Assassinated dozens of senior officials. Regime responded with mass arrests, torture, executions. Survivors fled to Iraq. Estimated 4,000–5,000 MEK prisoners executed in 1988 massacres.
Tudeh Party (Communist)
Crushed
Iran's communist party, aligned with the Soviet Union. Supported the revolution and even supported the Islamic Republic initially, hoping to maintain influence.
Outcome: Despite their support for the revolution and the new regime, the entire leadership was arrested in 1983. Members executed alongside MEK in the 1988 prison massacres.
Fedaiyan-e Khalq (Marxist Guerrillas)
Crushed
Marxist guerrillas who had conducted armed operations against the Shah since the 1970s. Fought in the final armed insurrection of February 9–11, 1979.
Outcome: Suppressed alongside other leftist groups. Members imprisoned and executed in the 1988 massacres.
Bazaari Merchants
Co-opted
The traditional merchant class with centuries-old ties to the Shia clergy. They financed the revolution, funded strike activities, and supported the families of those killed or imprisoned.
Outcome: The historic bazaari-clergy alliance was preserved. The new economic order protected bazaari interests from modern corporate competition. Many clerics came from bazaari families. Survived by merging with the ruling class.
Students & Intellectuals
Purged
University students inspired by Ali Shariati and Third World liberation movements. Among the most active revolutionary participants.
Outcome: Universities closed for three years (1980–1983). 20,000 teachers dismissed. Students screened for ideology. Campuses attacked by Hezbollahi mobs. Student unions banned.

"By the end of 1979, the secular middle class and liberals had been vanquished and the only surviving players on the Iranian political stage were the Islamist intellectuals, the young urban poor, and the devout bourgeoisie."

Khomeini's Return & the Ten Days (February 1979)

After 14 years of exile — Turkey, Iraq, and finally France — Khomeini had conducted an extraordinarily effective media campaign from Neauphle-le-Chateau outside Paris. He gave interviews to the world's press, distributed messages on cassette tapes throughout Iran, and presented himself as a moderate figure seeking to end tyranny and restore dignity. Most of the world — and many of his revolutionary allies — believed him.

Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini
Supreme Leader of the Revolution
When asked on the Air France 747 what he felt about returning to Iran after 14 years, he famously replied: "Nothing" (hichi) — either Sufi-like detachment or cold political calculation. 3 to 5 million people lined Tehran's streets to greet him.
Mehdi Bazargan
First Post-Revolutionary PM
Appointed by Khomeini on February 4 as head of a provisional government. Hoped for a French-style republic with Islamic values. Forced to resign on November 4, 1979 — the day the embassy was seized — recognizing he was powerless.
Shapour Bakhtiar
Last Shah-Appointed PM
Appointed in a last-ditch attempt at constitutional transition. Offered dissolving SAVAK, freeing prisoners, free elections. The revolutionary momentum was unstoppable. Went into hiding February 11, fled to Paris.
Abolhassan Banisadr
First Elected President (Jan 1980)
Increasingly clashed with the clerical establishment. Opposed the hostage crisis, warned against theocratic dictatorship. Impeached June 21, 1981 with only one legislator speaking in his defense. Fled Iran disguised as a woman.
February 1, 1979
Khomeini Returns
Lands at Tehran's Mehrabad Airport on a chartered Air France Boeing 747. An estimated 3–5 million people line the streets — one of the largest public gatherings in human history.
February 4, 1979
Parallel Government Established
Khomeini appoints Bazargan as PM of a provisional revolutionary government. Iran now has two governments — Bakhtiar refuses to resign.
February 9–10, 1979
Armed Insurrection
Air Force technicians clash with Imperial Guard at Doshan Tappeh Air Base. Fedaiyan and MEK guerrillas join. Police stations, bases, and government buildings seized. Arms distributed to the public.
February 11, 1979
The Military Declares Neutrality
The Supreme Military Council declares the armed forces "neutral" — effectively abandoning the regime. 450,000 soldiers simply walk away. Bakhtiar goes into hiding. The revolution triumphs.
Pivotal Question

Why Did a 450,000-Strong Military Not Intervene?

  • Designed as personal instrument: Senior generals selected for loyalty, not competence. Earned ~$70,000/year with fringe benefits but were psychologically incapable of acting without the Shah's orders
  • Class divisions: Generals lived in insulated privilege; junior officers came from the same disaffected classes as the protesters and shared their frustrations
  • The Huyser contradiction: The U.S. sent General Huyser to stiffen resolve, but his mission sent contradictory signals. Was America backing the Shah? Bakhtiar? A coup? The generals received no clear directive.
  • Conscripts: Drawn from the same social classes as the protesters, they were the least reliable element

Velayat-e Faqih: Engineering a Theocracy

The foundational political doctrine of the Islamic Republic is velayat-e faqih (Guardianship of the Islamic Jurist) — Khomeini's revolutionary innovation in Shia political thought that would transform a popular uprising into a permanent theocratic state.

Political Theory

The Doctrine

Khomeini first articulated this in lectures in Najaf, Iraq, in 1970, published as Hokumat-e Eslami (Islamic Government):

  • Core argument: In the absence of the Twelfth (Hidden) Imam — whom Twelver Shia believe went into occultation in 874 CE — a qualified Islamic jurist must exercise political authority
  • Unprecedented: Traditional Shia theology held that ALL political authority was illegitimate during the occultation. Khomeini reversed this entirely.
  • Platonic echoes: The faqih resembled Plato's philosopher-king — a ruler whose moral commitment ensures just governance
  • Clerical opposition: Of the dozen Grand Ayatollahs alive at the time, only one besides Khomeini supported velayat-e faqih. Grand Ayatollahs Shariatmadari and Khoei opposed it. Shariatmadari was placed under house arrest and his clerical rank stripped — unprecedented in Shia history.
Rigged Choice — March 30–31, 1979

The Referendum

A single question: "Should the form of government be an Islamic Republic?" Two choices: yes or no. No option for a secular republic, constitutional monarchy, or any other form. 98.2% voted yes. "Islamic Republic" was never defined before the vote. The result was preordained.

The Constitutional Power Structure

Despite Bazargan's hope for a French-style republic, Khomeini's supporters dominated the Assembly of Experts and enshrined velayat-e faqih as the governing principle. The constitution was ratified December 2–3, 1979 with 99.5% approval. It created interlocking mechanisms that made reform virtually impossible:

Supreme Leader (Rahbar)
The most powerful figure. Appointed for life. Delineates general state policies. Commands armed forces. Appoints head of judiciary, state media, and half the Guardian Council. Can dismiss the president. Supervises all branches of government.
Guardian Council (Shura-ye Negahban)
12 members: 6 Islamic jurists appointed by the Supreme Leader + 6 civil jurists nominated by the Chief Justice (also appointed by the Supreme Leader). Vetoes all legislation. Approves or disqualifies ALL election candidates. The de facto filter on Iranian democracy.
Assembly of Experts (Majles-e Khobregan)
88 senior Islamic scholars elected by popular vote — but candidates are vetted by the Guardian Council (appointed by the Supreme Leader). Sole power: electing and theoretically dismissing the Supreme Leader. In practice, has never challenged him. A circular system of self-reinforcing control.
President
Head of government below the Supreme Leader. Elected by popular vote for 4-year terms, limited to two consecutive terms. Can be dismissed by the Supreme Leader.
Majlis (Islamic Consultative Assembly)
290 seats elected by popular vote. Passes legislation — but ALL laws must be approved by the Guardian Council. Candidates vetted by the Guardian Council before they can run.

"The Guardian Council vetoes legislation and disqualifies candidates, ensuring only approved figures enter the system. The Assembly of Experts theoretically oversees the Supreme Leader but is itself vetted by the Guardian Council — appointed by the Supreme Leader. A circular system of self-reinforcing control."

Defining Moment

The Iran Hostage Crisis

November 4, 1979 – January 20, 1981 • 444 Days

The Trigger

On October 22, 1979, President Carter allowed the deposed Shah to enter the United States for cancer treatment. For many Iranians, this was an echo of 1953 — the fear that America would again use an Iranian exile to plot a restoration of the monarchy.

The Seizure (November 4, 1979)

Approximately 300–500 students calling themselves the Muslim Student Followers of the Imam's Line stormed the U.S. Embassy. 66 Americans were taken hostage (later reduced to 52).

Student motivations:

  • Demanded extradition of the Shah to face trial
  • Sought to expose what they believed were CIA operations from the embassy
  • Intended to depose Bazargan's moderate government, which they suspected of normalizing relations with the U.S.
  • Twenty-five years of accumulated rage over the 1953 coup, SAVAK, and American support for the Shah

The initial plan was to hold the embassy for only 48–72 hours. But the takeover proved wildly popular, and Khomeini gave it his full support — transforming it from a student protest into a state-sanctioned act.

Why Khomeini Endorsed It

Khomeini's support was coldly strategic:

  • Destroyed the moderates: Bazargan resigned on November 4, the day of the seizure
  • Consolidated revolutionary fervor: United the public against a foreign enemy
  • Permanent justification: Anti-Americanism became the state religion
  • He called the seizure "the second revolution" — greater even than the first

Operation Eagle Claw — The Failed Rescue (April 24, 1980)

Carter authorized a military rescue involving elements from all four branches. Eight Navy helicopters launched from the USS Nimitz. Two suffered mechanical failures; a third was damaged at the desert staging area ("Desert One"). With only five operational helicopters — six minimum required — the mission was aborted.

During withdrawal, a helicopter collided with a C-130 and exploded, killing eight American servicemen. The disaster humiliated the Carter administration, contributed to his election defeat, and later led to the creation of U.S. Special Operations Command (USSOCOM) in 1987.

The October Surprise

The hostages were released on January 20, 1981 — minutes after Ronald Reagan was inaugurated, 444 days after the seizure. The timing fueled one of the most enduring controversies in American political history.

  • The allegation: Reagan's campaign manager William Casey secretly negotiated with Iranian officials to delay the release until after the November 1980 election
  • Ben Barnes confirmed in 2023 that Casey sent Texas Governor Connally to urge Middle Eastern leaders to pressure Iran against releasing the hostages
  • Banisadr wrote that Khomeini and Reagan "organized a clandestine negotiation"
  • Israeli PM Yitzhak Shamir, asked in 1993 if the delay was part of a deal: "Of course it was"
Permanent Consequence

The Crisis Severed U.S.-Iran Relations Completely

Diplomatic ties have never been restored. "Death to America" became a ritual chant. Any future U.S. president attempting rapprochement would face enormous domestic political costs. The embassy compound became a propaganda museum: the "Den of Spies."

Consolidation: How the Revolution Devoured Its Children

Many believed Khomeini would serve as a spiritual guide rather than a political ruler. From Paris, he had spoken of freedom, democracy, and a government representing all Iranians. What followed was something else entirely: a systematic elimination of every non-Islamist revolutionary ally, carried out in methodical sequence.

The Seven-Step Playbook

Eliminate the Moderates
Bazargan's government was sidelined within months. National Democratic Front banned August 1979. Muslim People's Republic Party banned January 1980.
Use External Crises to Consolidate
The hostage crisis destroyed liberal opposition and made anti-Americanism the state religion. Bazargan forced to resign the same day.
Close the Universities
June 1980: the "Cultural Revolution." Universities shut for three years. 20,000 teachers dismissed. 8,000 military officers purged. Campuses attacked by Hezbollahi mobs.
Purge the Military and Bureaucracy
Professional expertise replaced with ideological loyalty. Students screened for ideology when universities reopened. Books banned, Western texts removed from libraries.
Destroy the Armed Left
MEK insurgency (June 1981) used as justification for mass repression. Thousands arrested, tortured, executed. Tudeh leadership arrested in 1983 despite supporting the regime.
Constitutional Entrenchment
Velayat-e faqih locked into the constitution. Clerical supremacy became legally unassailable. The Guardian Council ensured only approved figures could enter the political system.
War Mobilization
The Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988) provided the ultimate justification for suppressing dissent in the name of national survival.
Mass Atrocity — Summer 1988

The 1988 Prison Massacres

Khomeini issued a fatwa ordering the execution of imprisoned political dissidents who refused to recant their beliefs. In a matter of weeks, an estimated 4,000–5,000 prisoners (some estimates go higher) were executed — primarily MEK members but also Tudeh and Fedaiyan prisoners. One of the most concentrated mass killings in the history of the modern Middle East.

Ethnic Minority Suppression

On December 17, 1979, Khomeini declared on Radio Iran that ethnic identities were contrary to Islamic doctrines — nationalism within minority groups was a plot by Islam's enemies. This ideological stance securitized all ethnic demands, treating calls for cultural rights as existential threats.

The Kurdish Rebellion (March 1979 onward)

  • Kurdish groups initially campaigned for political autonomy within Iran, not separatism
  • Negotiations attempted but collapsed. August 19, 1979: Khomeini declared a "jihad" against Iran's Kurds
  • The IRGC launched a major offensive in spring 1980
  • Approximately 10,000 people killed, including 1,200 Kurdish political prisoners executed
  • The suppression was critical in establishing the IRGC's legitimacy and operational capability
Ahwazi Arabs (Khuzestan)
Oil Province, 80% of Hydrocarbons
Demanded cultural and political rights. Despite Khuzestan holding ~80% of Iran's hydrocarbon resources, the Arab population lived in extreme poverty. Uprisings met with military force. IRGC established permanent control.
Baloch (Sistan-Balochestan)
Sunni Minority
Faced religious as well as ethnic discrimination. Sporadic armed resistance suppressed. The province remains one of Iran's least developed regions to this day.

Women's Rights: The Speed of Regression

March 7, 1979
Mandatory Hijab Announced
Just weeks after the revolution, Khomeini announced a mandatory Islamic dress code for women.
March 8, 1979
100,000 Women Protest
On International Women's Day, more than 100,000 women gathered in Tehran to protest mandatory veiling — one of the first mass demonstrations against the new government. A temporary retraction followed — tactical retreat, not genuine concession.
July 1980
Offices "Islamified"
Unveiled women refused entry to government buildings. Women could be fired for not wearing hijab at work.
1983
Full Enforcement
Hijab mandatory for all women in all public spaces, enforced by the newly established morality police (Gasht-e Ershad).

Legal Rollback

  • 1967 Family Protection Law repealed — women lost rights in divorce, custody, and limits on polygamy
  • Women barred from becoming judges
  • Beaches, sports facilities, and public transportation sex-segregated
  • Marriage age for girls lowered to 9 (later raised to 13)
  • Women's testimony valued at half a man's in court
  • Dress code violations became criminal offenses punishable by fines, imprisonment, or flogging

Economic Restructuring: Bonyads & the IRGC Empire

The revolution seized vast assets from the Pahlavi family and their associates. What was created to embody Islamic economic justice became instead a parallel economy controlled by the clerical and military elite — a state within the state.

Bonyads (Revolutionary Foundations)
30–40% of GDP
Bonyad-e Mostazafan ("Foundation of the Oppressed") is the largest, managing billions across manufacturing, agriculture, construction. ~5 million Iranians work in bonyad enterprises. Tax-exempt, audit-exempt, answerable only to the Supreme Leader. Created to redistribute wealth to the poor; in practice, instruments of patronage enriching the clerical establishment.
IRGC Economic Empire
Military-Industrial Complex
Founded in April 1979 as a parallel military force. Expanded into construction, oil and gas, manufacturing, telecom, agriculture. Receives preferential government contracts and licenses. Generates revenue for strategic programs while building loyalty through patronage and employment among Iran's poorer populations.

Foreign Reactions

United States
Catastrophic Strategic Loss
Lost the cornerstone of its Persian Gulf security architecture. Led to the Carter Doctrine (Jan 1980): any attempt to control the Gulf would be repelled by force. Created U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM). Permanent severing of diplomatic relations.
Soviet Union
The "Lesser Satan"
Surprised even more than Washington. Expected their ally, the Tudeh, to benefit. Instead, Khomeini designated the USSR the "Lesser Satan," suppressed the Tudeh, and treated Soviet ideology as anathema. The December 1979 Afghanistan invasion further poisoned relations.
Israel
Alliance to Enmity
One of the most dramatic reversals in modern diplomacy. Under the Shah: military ties, intelligence sharing, oil trade (60% of Israel's supply), joint missile programs. Within weeks: all ties severed, PLO recognized, Israeli mission handed to Arafat. Elimination of Israel became a core foreign policy principle.
Gulf Arab States
Fear of Export
Gulf monarchies feared revolutionary export to their Shia populations. Saudi Arabia saw Khomeini's claim to lead all Muslims as a direct challenge. When the Iran-Iraq War began, they provided Iraq tens of billions. The GCC was founded in 1981 largely in response to the Iranian threat.

The Revolution's Legacy: The Central Paradox

What Was Gained

  • End of the Pahlavi dictatorship and SAVAK
  • Iranian sovereignty and independence from American domination
  • Elements of popular participation (elections, parliament) — however constrained
  • A sense of national dignity after the humiliations of 1953 and decades of perceived subservience

What Was Lost

  • The broad coalition's democratic aspirations — crushed in favor of one faction
  • Women's rights and legal equality
  • Freedom of expression, association, and the press
  • Academic freedom and intellectual diversity
  • Ethnic minorities' hopes for autonomy
  • Thousands of lives in purges, the 1988 massacres, and the Iran-Iraq War

"What began as an authentic and anti-dictatorial popular revolution based on a broad coalition of all anti-Shah forces was soon transformed into an Islamic fundamentalist power-grab. The revolution against tyranny produced a new form of authoritarianism — one clothed in religious legitimacy rather than royal prerogative, but no less determined to monopolize power."

— Abolhassan Banisadr, reflecting from exile: "The revolution that was meant to free Iran from one form of despotism had merely replaced it with another."
