Recurring Pattern

The Cycle That Defined 25 Years

From 1989 to 2015, Iran's political trajectory followed a tragically predictable cycle. Understanding this pattern is essential to understanding why hardliners kept winning -- and why the JCPOA was both a breakthrough and, ultimately, fragile.

This cycle repeated with Rafsanjani (1989-1997), Khatami (1997-2005), and was only partially broken by Rouhani (2013-2015).

1989 - 1997

The Rafsanjani Era: Pragmatic Reconstruction

Post-War Rebuilding

When Ali Akbar Hashemi Rafsanjani was inaugurated on August 3, 1989 -- exactly two months after Khomeini's death -- he inherited a nation devastated by eight years of war. The Iran-Iraq conflict had cost an estimated $1 trillion in direct and indirect damage. Infrastructure was destroyed, the economy was state-dominated, and hundreds of thousands of veterans required support.

His election coincided with a fundamental constitutional restructuring: a 1989 referendum abolished the office of prime minister and expanded presidential powers, while Ali Khamenei, as the new Supreme Leader, was still consolidating authority.

Economic Liberalization

Rafsanjani represented a pragmatic strand of the revolutionary elite. Whereas Khomeini had famously declared "the economy is for donkeys," Rafsanjani understood survival required development.

  • Privatization: Opening the state-dominated economy to private investment
  • Technocratic governance: Placing Western-educated technocrats in key positions
  • Foreign investment: Seeking international capital for reconstruction
  • Infrastructure development: Major projects in war-damaged regions

Initial results were encouraging -- robust GDP growth and improved employment during his first term (1989-1993).

The Cycle Begins

Rafsanjani's second term (1993-1997) saw the pattern emerge. Soft oil prices triggered a debt crisis. Conservative factions loyal to the Supreme Leader blocked liberalization as "un-Islamic." The government was forced to reschedule debt, reinstitute exchange restrictions, and shelve reforms. Despite economic pragmatism, political liberalization remained minimal -- a series of assassinations of intellectuals (the "Chain Murders") occurred under his watch.

Result: Economic pragmatism without political freedom bred frustration. The stage was set for a reformist earthquake.

1997 - 2005

The Khatami Era: The Reform Movement

The 1997 Election Earthquake

Mohammad Khatami's election on May 23, 1997 was a political earthquake. A former culture minister with a background in philosophy and theology, Khatami captured 70% of the vote with nearly 80% turnout. His supporters cut across Iranian society: women, youth, intellectuals, the urban middle class, and even rural populations.

Khatami popularized transformative vocabulary: "civil society" (jame'eh-ye madani), "rule of law," "Islamic democracy," and most famously, a "Dialogue of Civilizations" -- a direct counterpoint to Huntington's "Clash of Civilizations."

"We have a great respect for the American civilization and we have a deep admiration for the American people."
-- President Mohammad Khatami, CNN interview, January 1998 -- the most conciliatory language any Iranian official had used toward the U.S. since 1979

Reform Achievements

  • Press freedom: Dozens of new reformist newspapers and magazines -- Salam, Neshat, Jame'eh -- created a vibrant public sphere of debate pushing boundaries on accountability and women's rights
  • Civil society growth: NGOs proliferated, women's rights advocacy expanded, artistic and cultural expression flourished
  • Municipal elections: Local council elections held for the first time in 1999, empowering citizens
  • International engagement: Diplomatic isolation eased through rapprochement with European nations and Gulf states
  • The UN proclaimed 2001 the "Year of Dialogue Among Civilizations" based on Khatami's proposal
The 1999 Student Protests -- Precursor to the Green Movement

On July 8, 1999, students at Tehran University protested the closure of the reformist newspaper Salam. That night, plainclothes vigilantes and riot police raided a Tehran University dormitory, beating students and throwing some from balconies. At least one student was killed.

Five days of protests erupted across Tehran and other cities. Approximately 50,000 students protested in Tehran alone. The Basij and Ansar-e Hezbollah attacked protesters with batons, tear gas, and live ammunition. Over 1,500 students were arrested; many were tortured.

The critical moment: President Khatami refused to support the students, condemning the violence but ultimately siding with the system over the street. This devastated his credibility and exposed the fundamental limitation: even a president with a massive mandate was unable to challenge the security establishment.

Why Reform Failed: The Structural Trap

The reform movement failed because of a structural imbalance built into the constitution. Elected institutions were systematically overruled by unelected bodies controlled by conservatives loyal to the Supreme Leader:

Missed Opportunity -- The 2003 Grand Bargain

In May 2003, weeks after the U.S. toppled Saddam Hussein, Iran transmitted a remarkable diplomatic proposal to Washington through Swiss Ambassador Tim Guldimann. The "grand bargain fax" offered virtually everything on the table:

Iran offered: Full nuclear transparency with intrusive inspections, cooperation against al-Qaeda, acceptance of the two-state solution (de facto Israel recognition), termination of support for Hamas and Islamic Jihad, potential transformation of Hezbollah into a purely political organization.

Iran sought: End of sanctions, repatriation of frozen assets, recognition of legitimate security interests, access to peaceful nuclear technology.

The Bush administration rejected it out of hand. "We're not interested in a grand bargain," declared Undersecretary of State John Bolton. Officials flush with military victory and convinced Iran's regime was collapsing saw no reason to negotiate.

This rejection is widely regarded as one of the greatest missed opportunities in modern Middle Eastern diplomacy. It taught Iran that even maximum diplomatic flexibility would be rebuffed, strengthening hardliners who argued negotiation with America was futile.

Nuclear Crisis

Iran's Nuclear Program

Origins: Started with American Help

Iran's nuclear program began in the 1950s under the Shah as part of the U.S.-supported "Atoms for Peace" initiative. The United States helped Iran build its first research reactor at Tehran University in 1967. By the 1970s, Washington had agreed in principle to allow Iran a full nuclear fuel cycle -- the same capability it later deemed unacceptable under the Islamic Republic.

This historical irony is not lost on Iranians, who frequently cite Western support for the Shah's nuclear ambitions to argue that opposition to their program is political, not technical.

Revival After the War

Khomeini initially suspended the program as un-Islamic. But the experience of the Iran-Iraq War -- Iraq's chemical weapons use and the world's failure to respond -- convinced Iranian leaders nuclear capability was essential.

The program was revived in the late 1980s with external help:

  • Pakistan / A.Q. Khan network: Iran was likely the first customer of Khan's proliferation network (~1987). Provided centrifuge designs, components, and training. Possibly cut Iran's development timeline by a decade.
  • Russia: Completed the Bushehr nuclear power plant and provided fuel assemblies
  • China: Provided nuclear technology in the 1990s before pulling back under U.S. pressure
The 2002 Revelation

On August 14, 2002, the MEK/NCRI held a press conference in Washington revealing two previously unknown nuclear facilities:

Natanz: A large underground uranium enrichment facility designed for thousands of gas centrifuges.

Arak: A heavy-water production plant adjacent to a 40-megawatt reactor with potential plutonium production capabilities.

IAEA inspectors discovered Iran had conducted nuclear activities in violation of its safeguards obligations for 18 years without reporting them. Traces of highly enriched uranium, undeclared enrichment experiments, and links to the Khan network were found. The international crisis had begun.

2005 - 2013

The Ahmadinejad Era: Defiance & Crisis

How Hardliners Won -- The Cycle Completes

Mahmoud Ahmadinejad's 2005 election represented the cycle's culmination: reform fatigue after eight years of Khatami with minimal results, the Bush administration's rejection of the grand bargain validating hardliners, the Guardian Council disqualifying hundreds of reformist candidates, and voter disillusionment causing low turnout.

A former mayor from a modest background with strong IRGC backing, Ahmadinejad campaigned on economic justice and distributing oil wealth to the poor -- resonating with those neglected by technocratic reformists.

Nuclear Acceleration

Under Ahmadinejad, Iran dramatically escalated its program:

April 2006

First Enrichment Success

Iran announced successful uranium enrichment to 3.5%

April 2007

Industrial-Scale Production Declared

Ahmadinejad announced industrial-scale nuclear fuel production. Thousands of additional centrifuges installed at Natanz.

September 2009

Secret Fordow Facility Revealed

Western intelligence exposed a previously undisclosed enrichment facility at Fordow, built deep inside a mountain near Qom -- designed to survive aerial bombardment.

2010

20% Enrichment Begins

Iran began enriching to 20% -- far beyond the 3-5% needed for power generation and significantly closer to weapons-grade (90%).

Inflammatory Rhetoric

Ahmadinejad's provocative statements damaged Iran's international standing and armed advocates for military action:

  • October 2005: Stated Israel should be "wiped off the map" (more literally: "the regime occupying Jerusalem must vanish from the page of time")
  • December 2005: Called the Holocaust a "myth"
  • December 2006: Hosted the "International Conference to Review the Global Vision of the Holocaust" in Tehran, attended by Holocaust deniers including former KKK leader David Duke

This rhetoric alienated potential allies, including Russia and China, who found it increasingly difficult to defend Iran in international forums.

The 2009 Green Movement -- Democracy's Last Stand

The June 12, 2009 presidential election produced the Islamic Republic's most severe political crisis. Official results declared Ahmadinejad the winner with 63% over reformist Mir Hossein Mousavi -- announced suspiciously quickly for a paper-ballot system. Statistical analyses revealed improbable voting patterns.

What followed was extraordinary:

Up to 3 million peaceful demonstrators flooded Tehran's streets -- the largest protests since 1979 -- chanting "Where is my vote?" (Ra'ye man kojast?). Protests spread to dozens of cities from June 2009 through February 2010.

The crackdown was systematic and brutal: Basij paramilitaries attacked with batons, tear gas, and live ammunition. Protesters were beaten, arrested, tortured, and sexually assaulted in detention.

Neda Agha-Soltan

The iconic image: 26-year-old Neda Agha-Soltan, shot dead by a Basij militiaman on June 20, 2009, on Kargar Avenue in Tehran. Amateur video of her death went viral worldwide. She became a symbol of the movement's aspirations and the regime's brutality.

Dozens of protesters were killed, thousands arrested. Many were held at Kahrizak detention center, where prisoners died from beatings and abuse. Opposition leaders Mir Hossein Mousavi and Mehdi Karroubi were placed under house arrest in February 2011, where they remained for years without charges.

The Green Movement ended the fiction that meaningful political change could occur through elections within the existing system. It deepened the divide between Iranian society and the Islamic Republic's ruling institutions irreparably.

Stuxnet: The First Cyberweapon in History

In 2009-2010, Iran's nuclear program was targeted by the most sophisticated cyberattack ever conducted. Stuxnet, jointly developed by the United States and Israel under "Operation Olympic Games," targeted the Siemens PLCs operating gas centrifuges at Natanz.

The worm caused centrifuges to spin at improper speeds while reporting normal operations to monitoring systems, silently destroying approximately 1,000 centrifuges. By 2010, an estimated 2,000 had been rendered inoperable.

U.S. Unilateral Sanctions & EU Oil Embargo

The U.S. imposed additional layers far exceeding UN requirements. The CISADA (2010) targeted refined petroleum imports. Executive orders in 2012-2013 targeted Iran's central bank, oil sector, and financial institutions.

Secondary sanctions -- punishing third-country companies doing business with Iran -- effectively weaponized the dollar's dominance, forcing companies worldwide to choose between Iran and the U.S. financial system.

In January 2012, the EU imposed a comprehensive oil embargo. On March 17, 2012, SWIFT disconnected approximately 30 Iranian banks, including the Central Bank, severing Iran from the global financial system. There was no practical alternative for international payment routing.

Human Cost of Sanctions

While food and medicine were technically exempt, banking restrictions made it extremely difficult for Iranian importers to pay for these goods, creating de facto shortages of pharmaceuticals and medical supplies. Unemployment reached ~20%, with youth unemployment significantly higher. Average annual per capita income loss: approximately $3,000.

Sanctions eroded Iran's middle class, strengthened the IRGC's economic monopoly (the Guards controlled smuggling networks that profited from sanctions evasion), and hardened popular resentment against the West -- reinforcing the very nationalism that made diplomacy harder.

Geopolitical Irony

How the U.S. Invasion of Iraq Empowered Iran

One of History's Greatest Strategic Miscalculations

The 2003 U.S. invasion of Iraq, partly intended to contain Iranian influence, achieved the exact opposite. The U.S. accomplished for Iran -- at no cost to Tehran -- what Iran itself had failed to achieve in eight years of war: the destruction of Saddam Hussein's regime.

Three Ways the Iraq War Empowered Iran

1. Removal of Iran's Primary Enemy

The fall of Saddam removed Iran's most dangerous regional adversary -- the government that had invaded Iran, used chemical weapons, and fought an eight-year war of attrition.

2. The "Shia Crescent"

Iraq's 60-65% Shia majority was empowered for the first time. Many new Iraqi Shia leaders -- from the Dawa Party, Islamic Supreme Council, and Badr Organization -- had spent years in Iranian exile. King Abdullah II of Jordan coined "Shia Crescent" to describe the emerging arc of Iranian influence from Tehran through Baghdad and Damascus to Beirut.

3. Proxy Network Expansion

Iran immediately allocated an additional $26 million to the IRGC Quds Force to arm Iraqi Shia militias. Iranian-trained forces, including the Badr Corps, became active participants in the post-invasion sectarian conflict. Iraq was transformed from an adversary into a zone of Iranian influence.

2011

The Arab Spring: Opportunities & Contradictions

Iran's Response

Iran initially framed the Arab Spring as an "Islamic Awakening" inspired by the 1979 Revolution. The toppling of pro-Western leaders in Tunisia and Egypt seemed to validate Iran's narrative.

But the contradictions were stark:

  • Syria: Iran intervened decisively to save Assad's regime -- its most important state ally and the linchpin of supply routes to Hezbollah. Deployed IRGC advisers, Quds Force operatives, Hezbollah fighters, and organized the Fatemiyoun (Afghan Shia) and Zainabiyoun (Pakistani Shia) brigades.
  • Bahrain: When the Shia-majority population protested against the Sunni monarchy, Iran waged propaganda but was powerless as Saudi and UAE forces crushed the uprising.
  • Yemen: The Houthi movement became another arena of Iranian proxy influence, adding to the Iran-Saudi rivalry.
2013 - 2015

Rouhani & the JCPOA: Breaking the Cycle

A Mandate for Diplomacy

Hassan Rouhani won an outright first-round victory on June 14, 2013 with 50.7% of the vote. A moderate cleric who had served as Iran's chief nuclear negotiator (2003-2005), he campaigned on "reconciliation and peace." With backing from both Khatami and Rafsanjani, he represented the hope that diplomacy could lift sanctions.

Within days of inauguration, Rouhani made a historic phone call with President Obama on September 27, 2013 -- the first direct communication between U.S. and Iranian leaders since 1979.

The Oman Backchannel

Unknown to most, secret U.S.-Iranian talks had been underway since 2012, facilitated by Oman. William Burns (Deputy Secretary of State) and Jake Sullivan (senior adviser, later Biden's NSA) led the American side. These backchannel talks established the framework and personal relationships necessary for complex diplomacy.

November 24, 2013

Interim Agreement (JPOA) Signed in Geneva

Iran and the P5+1 signed the Joint Plan of Action: freezing nuclear program at existing levels, capping enrichment at 5%, halting Arak construction, expanding inspections -- in exchange for ~$6-7 billion in limited sanctions relief.

2013 - 2015

Two Years of Intense Negotiations

Secretary Kerry and Foreign Minister Zarif met on 18 different dates in 11 cities. Kerry broke his leg in a bicycle accident during final talks in Geneva and continued negotiations from a wheelchair. The P5+1 process was coordinated by EU High Representatives Catherine Ashton and then Federica Mogherini.

July 14, 2015

JCPOA Signed in Vienna

The most significant arms control agreement in a generation. After months of negotiations past multiple deadlines, the deal was done.

What the JCPOA Actually Did

Nuclear Restrictions

  • Uranium stockpile reduced by 98% to 300 kg, enriched to max 3.67%, for 15 years
  • Centrifuges reduced from ~20,000 to 6,104, with only 5,060 IR-1 models permitted to enrich
  • Fordow converted to research center with 1,044 non-enriching centrifuges
  • Arak reactor core redesigned and filled with concrete to prevent plutonium production
  • Breakout time extended from 2-3 months to at least 12 months

Monitoring & Verification

  • IAEA Additional Protocol with enhanced inspection access
  • Continuous monitoring with environmental sampling and real-time surveillance cameras
  • Access to uranium mines, mills, centrifuge production, and the entire supply chain

Sanctions Relief

  • UN Security Council sanctions lifted upon IAEA verification
  • U.S. and EU nuclear-related sanctions suspended
  • ~$100 billion in frozen assets released
  • Iranian banks reconnected to SWIFT
  • EU oil embargo lifted

Critics & Supporters

Supporters Said:

The most significant arms control agreement in a generation. Demonstrated that even intractable conflicts could be resolved through persistent negotiation. Obama: it "makes the world safer."

Critics Said (Israel, Saudi Arabia, U.S. Republicans):

  • Sunset clauses meant restrictions would eventually expire, leaving Iran with industrial enrichment
  • Did not address ballistic missiles, regional proxies, or human rights
  • Sanctions relief would fund regional influence expansion
  • Verification provisions were inadequate for past military nuclear work
Historic Significance

Regardless of its fate, the JCPOA represented the most significant diplomatic engagement between Iran and the West since 1979. It demonstrated that the cycle of hope, outreach, rejection, and hardliner empowerment could -- under the right conditions -- be interrupted. Whether the interruption would last was another question entirely.

Analysis

The Big Themes

The Reform Paradox

The Islamic Republic allowed elections that genuinely reflected popular sentiment but structured power to ensure elected officials could never enact fundamental change. Each reformist opening -- Rafsanjani's liberalization, Khatami's civil society, Rouhani's diplomacy -- was constrained by unelected institutions. This structural tension produced cyclical patterns of hope and disillusionment that defined Iranian domestic politics for a quarter-century.

The Nuclear Program as Domestic Politics

The nuclear issue was never purely foreign policy -- it was embedded in domestic power struggles:

  • Reformists (Khatami) used it as a tool for rapprochement
  • Hardliners (Ahmadinejad) used it as a symbol of national pride and resistance
  • Moderates (Rouhani) used it as a bargaining chip for sanctions relief

Each faction's nuclear approach reflected its broader vision for Iran's relationship with the world.

Sanctions as a Double-Edged Sword

Sanctions achieved their proximate goal -- bringing Iran to the negotiating table -- but at enormous human cost and with unintended consequences. They eroded the middle class, strengthened the IRGC's economic monopoly (the Guards profited from smuggling), and hardened anti-Western resentment. The sanctions experience reinforced the very nationalism and siege mentality that made diplomacy more difficult.

The Central Question This Era Posed

Can a system designed to prevent change from within ever reform itself? The quarter-century from 1989 to 2015 suggested the answer was no -- at least not through elections alone. Every reformist president was structurally constrained. The only domain where change proved possible was foreign policy, and even there, the JCPOA's survival would depend on forces entirely beyond Iran's control.
