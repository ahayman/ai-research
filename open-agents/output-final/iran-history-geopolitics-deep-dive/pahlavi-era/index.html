Reza Shah: The Founder (1925–1941)

Reza Khan, a commanding officer of the Persian Cossack Brigade, seized control of the government in a 1921 coup. By 1923 he was prime minister; by 1925, the Constituent Assembly had deposed the Qajar dynasty and crowned him Reza Shah Pahlavi. Iran was bankrupt, virtually devoid of modern infrastructure, and fractured among tribal loyalties. He would change all of that — by force.

Reza Shah Pahlavi
Shah of Iran, 1925–1941
Former Cossack Brigade officer. Modeled his modernization on Ataturk's Turkey: railroads, secular courts, forced unveiling, compulsory education. Ruthless centralizer who crushed tribes and clergy alike.
Mustafa Kemal Ataturk
Reza Shah's Model
Turkish president whose top-down secular modernization became the template. Reza Shah adopted nearly identical reforms: replacing religious law with civil codes, banning traditional dress, founding a modern university system.

The Modernization Program

Infrastructure & Industry

  • Trans-Iranian Railway — the centerpiece, linking the Caspian Sea to the Persian Gulf
  • Highway mileage expanded from ~2,000 to 14,000 miles
  • Modern industrial plants increased 17-fold (excluding oil)
  • New ports, telegraph lines, and government buildings across the country

Education & Law

  • Free, compulsory education for boys and girls
  • Private religious schools shut down and replaced with secular state curriculum
  • University of Tehran founded in 1934 — Iran's first modern university
  • Sharia replaced with civil codes modeled on French law (1925–1928)
  • Standing army of 100,000 men and 90,000-person civil service
Pivotal Decision — 1936

The Forced Unveiling (Kashf-e Hijab)

Reza Shah banned the chador and all forms of veiling in public. Women were encouraged to enter the workforce and universities. While progressive in intent, the forced unveiling provoked deep resentment among devout families — a wound that would fester for decades and feed the revolutionary movement.

Suppression

Crushing Tribes and Clergy

Beginning in 1933, nomadic tribes — Bakhtiari, Qashqai, Lur, Kurd, Baluchi — were forcibly settled. Grazing lands were confiscated, tribal leaders imprisoned or executed. In a notorious 1928 incident, Reza Shah personally entered Qom's Fatima Masumeh Shrine to beat a cleric who had admonished the Shah's wife. The clergy were systematically marginalized: stripped of judicial authority, educational control, and social influence.

Identity

"Persia" Becomes "Iran" (1935)

On Nowruz 1935, Reza Shah requested all foreign governments use "Iran" ("Land of the Aryans") rather than the Greek-derived "Persia." The change was meant to unify the country's diverse ethnic groups — Persians, Azeris, Kurds, Baluch, Arabs, Turkmen — under a single national identity, and to break with the perceived weakness of the Qajar era.

World War II: Invasion & Forced Abdication (1941)

Despite Iran's declared neutrality, Reza Shah had cultivated significant economic ties with Nazi Germany — partly a pragmatic counterbalance to British and Russian influence. The Allies decided this was unacceptable.

Violation of Sovereignty — August 25, 1941

The Anglo-Soviet Invasion

Britain and the Soviet Union launched a coordinated assault: British and Australian naval forces from the Persian Gulf, Soviet troops from the north. Iran's military resistance collapsed within days. The stated purposes: secure the Trans-Iranian Railway as a supply corridor to the Soviets, expel German nationals who might sabotage the Abadan refinery, and secure Iranian oil for the Allied war effort.

August 25, 1941
Anglo-Soviet Invasion Begins
Britain and the USSR invade from south and north simultaneously. Iranian resistance collapses in days.
September 16, 1941
Reza Shah Forced to Abdicate
The British install his 21-year-old son, Mohammad Reza Pahlavi, on the throne — a fact that would forever taint his legitimacy.
1941–1946
Wartime Occupation
Iran divided into British and Soviet zones. Severe inflation, food shortages, and deepening resentment of foreign interference.
July 26, 1944
Reza Shah Dies in Exile
Dies in Johannesburg, South Africa, at age 66 — exiled by the British to Mauritius, then Durban, then Johannesburg.

Oil Politics & the Rise of Mossadegh

The Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (AIOC, later BP) had held a monopoly on Iranian oil since 1901. The terms were colonial in nature: Britain's tax revenue from AIOC exceeded what Iran received in royalties. AIOC refused audits and rejected the 50-50 profit-sharing that Saudi Arabia had already achieved with ARAMCO.

Mohammad Mossadegh
Prime Minister, 1951–1953
Swiss-educated lawyer and aristocrat. Led the National Front coalition. His thesis: no country could be politically independent without economic independence, and Iran could never be free while a foreign company controlled its oil.
Anglo-Iranian Oil Co.
British Oil Monopoly (later BP)
Controlled Iran's most valuable resource since 1901. Refused audits, rejected 50-50 splits, maintained segregated facilities where Iranian workers lived in squalor while British employees enjoyed privilege.

Mossadegh's Goals

  • Establish Iranian sovereignty over Iranian natural resources
  • Audit AIOC's books to verify royalty payments
  • Achieve equitable revenue sharing
  • End the neo-colonial relationship where a foreign company operated as a state-within-a-state
  • Use oil revenues for Iranian development rather than enriching the British Treasury
Democratic Moment — March 15, 1951

The Nationalization Vote

The Iranian Majlis voted unanimously to nationalize the oil industry. On April 28, Mossadegh was appointed prime minister. On May 1, the nationalization law took effect and the National Iranian Oil Company (NIOC) was created. It was the most consequential assertion of Iranian sovereignty in modern history.

The Pivotal Event

The 1953 Coup

Operation Ajax (CIA) • Operation Boot (MI6)

Why Britain Wanted Mossadegh Gone

Churchill and Foreign Secretary Eden responded to nationalization with fury. They launched a worldwide oil embargo enforced by the Royal Navy, threatening any tanker that attempted to purchase Iranian crude. They took the case to the International Court of Justice and UN Security Council — both ruled in Iran's favor or declined jurisdiction. MI6 launched internal propaganda portraying Mossadegh as mentally unstable and dangerously sympathetic to communism.

The embargo devastated Iran's economy, as oil revenues collapsed almost entirely. This was the point: economic warfare to create the conditions for regime change.

Why America Joined: The Dulles Brothers

The Truman administration had been sympathetic to Iran and resisted British pressure. Everything changed with Eisenhower's inauguration in January 1953.

Eisenhower appointed John Foster Dulles as Secretary of State and Allen Dulles as CIA Director — the first and only time in American history that two brothers simultaneously ran the overt and covert sides of U.S. foreign policy. Both were fervent anti-communists.

The British played masterfully on Cold War anxieties:

  • Mossadegh was unstable and incapable of governing
  • Iran's economy was collapsing under the oil embargo
  • The Tudeh Party (communist) would exploit the chaos to seize power
  • Iran would become a Soviet satellite controlling Persian Gulf oil

By April 1953, Allen Dulles authorized $1 million for the CIA's Tehran station "in any way that would bring about the fall of Mossadegh."

Kermit Roosevelt Jr.
CIA Operative, Coup Director
Grandson of Theodore Roosevelt. Entered Iran covertly and directed Operation Ajax from Tehran. Bribed editors, recruited mullahs, hired street gangs, and pressured the Shah for weeks to sign the dismissal decree.
Gen. Fazlollah Zahedi
CIA's Chosen Replacement
Retired general handpicked by the CIA and MI6 to replace Mossadegh as prime minister. Would serve as the Shah's compliant instrument after the coup.
John Foster Dulles
U.S. Secretary of State
Provided diplomatic cover and political messaging for the coup. Fervent anti-communist with deep connections to the international business establishment.
Allen Dulles
CIA Director
Brother of John Foster. Authorized the million-dollar budget and operational plan. Together, the Dulles brothers ran both overt and covert U.S. foreign policy — a unique arrangement in American history.

The Operation: How Democracy Was Overthrown

Preparatory Phase:

  • Roosevelt bribed newspaper editors to publish anti-Mossadegh propaganda
  • Recruited Islamic clergy, paying mullahs to denounce Mossadegh from the pulpit
  • Hired street gang leaders (lutis) to stage provocative demonstrations
  • Pressured the reluctant Shah for nearly three weeks to sign royal decrees dismissing Mossadegh
  • Established networks of agents within the military

The Failed First Attempt — August 15–16, 1953:

Colonel Nassiri attempted to deliver the Shah's dismissal decree, but Mossadegh had been tipped off. Nassiri was arrested. The coup failed. The Shah panicked and fled — first to Baghdad, then to Rome. CIA headquarters cabled Roosevelt to abort the operation.

Roosevelt Refused to Give Up.

The Improvised Success — August 19, 1953:

  • CIA agents distributed copies of the Shah's decree, framing Mossadegh as defying a "legitimate" royal order
  • Paid agents spread panic that the Tudeh Party was about to seize power
  • Organized street mobs — some posed as Tudeh supporters, attacking mosques to provoke backlash
  • Pro-Shah demonstrations marched through Tehran
  • Loyal military units activated

By evening, armored vehicles had rolled through Tehran. Mossadegh's residence was shelled and overrun. Approximately 300 people died. Mossadegh was arrested. General Zahedi was installed as prime minister. The Shah returned triumphantly from Rome on August 22.

The Immediate Aftermath

  • The Shah transformed: The frightened young constitutional monarch became an absolute ruler. He would never again tolerate independent political forces.
  • The 1954 Oil Consortium: Nationalization was effectively reversed. BP retained 40%, five American companies got 40%, Shell got 14%, France got 6%. Iran received a 50-50 profit split — ironically, what Mossadegh had initially sought before AIOC's intransigence drove him to full nationalization.
  • Mossadegh's fate: Military tribunal, three years in prison, then house arrest for life. He died in 1967, never leaving his village of Ahmadabad.
  • Political suppression: The National Front was banned, the Tudeh Party crushed, and independent political activity systematically eliminated.

Post-Coup Iran: Modernization vs. Repression (1953–1979)

The Shah who returned from Rome was a changed man. Never again would he tolerate independent political forces. He built a modernization machine with one hand and a police state with the other — a paradox that would define the next 26 years and guarantee his eventual downfall.

Instrument of Control — 1957

SAVAK: The Secret Police

Established in 1957 with direct assistance from the CIA and Israel's Mossad, SAVAK grew to over 5,300 full-time agents and a vast network of informers penetrating every level of society. Methods included electric shock, extraction of nails and teeth, mock executions, and sleep deprivation. Time magazine described it at the Shah's overthrow as "Iran's most hated and feared institution."

The White Revolution (1963)

Reforms Achieved

  • 2.5 million peasant families received land
  • Women gained the right to vote and run for office
  • Literacy Corps sent to rural villages
  • Health Corps deployed to underserved areas
  • Profit-sharing for industrial workers
  • Nationalization of forests and pastures

Consequences & Failures

  • Land parcels too small, lacking water, credit, or support
  • Millions of dispossessed peasants migrated to urban slums
  • Alienated clergy (lost endowment lands), landlords, and bazaaris
  • Reforms imposed without consent or political participation
  • Created an uprooted underclass — the future foot soldiers of revolution

Key Opposition Figures

Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini
Clerical Opposition Leader
Denounced the Shah as a "wretched miserable man" who had "embarked on the destruction of Islam." His 1963 arrest triggered the 15 Khordad uprising. In 1964, he denounced American military "capitulations" — "They have reduced the Iranian people to a level lower than that of an American dog." Exiled in November 1964 to Turkey, then Iraq for 13 years.
Ali Shariati
Intellectual Revolutionary (1933–1977)
Sorbonne-educated sociologist who synthesized Marxist theory with Shia theology. Reinterpreted Imam Hussain's martyrdom as revolutionary resistance against tyranny. Made Islam appeal to educated, modern Iranians. Died under suspicious circumstances in 1977 (many blamed SAVAK). His democratic Islam was later suppressed by the theocracy he helped inspire.

The Oil Boom & Its Discontents (1970s)

The 1973 oil crisis transformed Iran. Per capita income skyrocketed, but the benefits were distributed with extreme inequality. A new wealthy elite — connected to the royal court, the military, and international business — flourished while urban migrants from failed land reform lived in sprawling slums. The Shah — now styling himself "King of Kings, Light of the Aryans" — spent $200 million on the 1971 Persepolis celebration of 2,500 years of Persian monarchy, an extravaganza that offended millions of Iranians.

The Growing Opposition Coalition

  • Leftists: Tudeh (communist), Fedaiyan-e Khalq (Marxist guerrillas), Mojahedin-e Khalq (Islamist-Marxist guerrillas)
  • Nationalists: National Front and Freedom Movement, carrying on Mossadegh's tradition
  • Islamists: Khomeini's clerical network, operating through mosques and seminaries SAVAK could never fully penetrate
  • Bazaaris: Traditional merchant class, historically allied with the clergy, resenting policies favoring Western businesses
  • Students & Intellectuals: Radicalized by SAVAK repression and inspired by Shariati's ideas

The Final Year: Revolution (1978–1979)

President Carter's human rights pressure in 1977 forced partial liberalization — eased censorship, some political prisoners released. But the concessions opened floodgates. And Carter's signals were contradictory: he toasted the Shah at a 1977 state dinner as ruling "an island of stability" while simultaneously pressing on human rights. The Shah became paralyzed by indecision — compounded by a secret lymphoma diagnosis in 1974 and ongoing chemotherapy.

January 1978
The Insult That Lit the Fuse
A government-planted newspaper article attacked Khomeini by name — calling him a British agent and a closet homosexual. Protests erupted in Qom. Security forces killed seminary students, igniting a cycle of mourning and protest following the Shia 40-day mourning cycle.
August 19, 1978
Cinema Rex Fire
Arsonists barred the doors of an Abadan movie theater and set it ablaze, killing 377–470 people. Later attributed to Islamic militants, but the revolutionary movement blamed SAVAK, and the accusation was widely believed.
September 8, 1978
Black Friday
Martial law declared. Thousands of protesters gathered in Tehran's Jaleh Square, many unaware of the decree. Security forces opened fire, killing approximately 64 civilians. Black Friday destroyed any remaining possibility of compromise. The point of no return.
October–December 1978
The Country Shuts Down
Oil workers shut down production — the ultimate weapon against a petrostate. The bazaars closed. Khomeini, now based in Paris, gave interviews to the world's media, presenting himself as a moderate democratic figure.
January 1979
The Huyser Mission Fails
Carter sent General Huyser to assess whether the Iranian military could hold or attempt a coup. 31 days of meetings revealed deep fractures: senior officers loyal but paralyzed; junior officers sympathetic to the revolution. The mission failed.
January 16, 1979
The Shah Leaves Iran
Ostensibly on "vacation," he would never return. He wandered in exile — Egypt, Morocco, the Bahamas, Mexico, the US, Panama, Egypt again — and died in Cairo on July 27, 1980, at age 60.

Key Foreign Actors Across the Pahlavi Era

United States
Patron & Enabler
Eisenhower: Authorized the coup. Nixon: "Blank check" arms policy, Twin Pillars doctrine. Carter: Contradictory human rights pressure; unable to save the Shah or manage the transition.
United Kingdom
Colonial Oil Power
Churchill: Drove the push for the 1953 coup, refused to negotiate with Mossadegh. Britain remained deeply invested in Iranian oil throughout the entire Pahlavi era.
Soviet Union
Northern Threat
Occupied northern Iran in WWII, attempted separatist republics in Azerbaijan and Kurdistan (1945–46). Supported the Tudeh Party as an instrument of influence. The Cold War backdrop shaped every major decision.
Israel
Secret Partner
Helped establish SAVAK. Iran supplied up to 60% of Israel's oil. Joint missile development program. No formal diplomatic relations — a pragmatic ambiguity allowing cooperation without alienating Arab states.

"The occupation demonstrated Iran's vulnerability to great-power politics and deepened resentment of foreign interference — sentiments that would fuel both the oil nationalization movement and, eventually, the 1979 revolution."
