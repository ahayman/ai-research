Israel & Iran: From Allies to Enemies

The dramatic reversal from strategic partners to existential adversaries — and how two nations that once shared intelligence, oil, and weapons ended up at war

1948 – 1979

The Peripheral Doctrine: A Strategic Marriage

🇮🇱

Ben-Gurion's Strategic Vision

The Alliance of the Periphery

Surrounded by hostile Arab states, David Ben-Gurion forged alliances with non-Arab states on the edges of the Arab world. The logic: the enemy of my enemy is my friend. Iran, Turkey, and Ethiopia — all non-Arab, all threatened by Arab nationalism — became Israel's strategic partners. Iran was the crown jewel of this strategy.

Key Milestones

  • 1950: Iran becomes the second Muslim-majority country to recognize Israel (after Turkey)
  • 1958: The secret "Trident" pact — Israel, Iran, and Turkey — focused on intelligence sharing, military cooperation, and countering Arab nationalism
  • Israel maintained two successive ambassadors in Tehran and operated a full embassy
  • El Al operated direct flights between Tel Aviv and Tehran
🕵️

Intelligence

SAVAK and Mossad maintained deep cooperation. Israeli officers had permanent presence in Tehran.

⛽️

Oil

After the 1967 Arab oil embargo, Iran became Israel's critical energy lifeline via the Eilat-Ashkelon pipeline.

🔨

Trade

Israeli construction firms, agricultural experts, and engineers active throughout Iran.

🏭

Military

Weapons sales, military training, and joint missile development (Project Flower).

🇮🇱 Why Israel Wanted Iran

A powerful non-Arab ally on the periphery of the Arab world. Counterbalance to Nasser's Egypt and Ba'athist Iraq. A reliable oil supplier when Arab states imposed embargos.

🇮🇷 Why Iran Wanted Israel

Counterbalance to hostile Arab neighbors. Israeli technical expertise for modernization. Leverage in Washington, where Israel's friends were powerful. A channel to Kurdish rebels in Iraq.

1979

The Revolution: Ideology Replaces Strategy

⚖️

Khomeini's Anti-Israel Doctrine

From Strategic Partner to "Cancerous Tumor"

The 1979 revolution transformed the relationship virtually overnight. Khomeini severed diplomatic relations, withdrew recognition of Israel, and handed the former Israeli embassy to the PLO. He designated the last Friday of Ramadan as "Quds Day" (Jerusalem Day), calling for Jerusalem's liberation from "Zionist occupation."

Genuine Conviction or Strategic Calculation?

Khomeini's anti-Israel stance combined both:

  • Genuine Elements: Khomeini expressed hostility to Zionism since the early 1960s, well before the revolution made it politically advantageous. His writings contained deeply antisemitic themes, arguing Jews "wish to establish Jewish domination throughout the world."
  • Strategic Elements: The Palestinian cause was the most emotionally resonant issue in the Muslim world. By championing it more aggressively than any Arab leader, Iran could transcend the Sunni-Shia divide and project influence across the entire Islamic world.

The regime's subsequent behavior has reflected this duality. Anti-Israel rhetoric remains central to regime legitimacy, but Tehran has demonstrated pragmatism when necessary.

🔄 The Great Paradox: Iran-Israel Arms Deals During the Iran-Iraq War

Despite Khomeini's rhetoric, Israel clandestinely sold weapons, spare parts, and ammunition to the Islamic Republic throughout the 1980-1988 war. Estimates range from $500 million to $2 billion per year. Items included spare parts for F-4 Phantoms, artillery ammunition, and anti-tank missiles.

Israel's rationale: Keep two potential enemies fighting each other. Saddam's Iraq was the more immediate threat. Maintain influence in Tehran and protect Iran's Jewish community.

Iran's rationale: Desperate need for American-made spare parts that only Israel could supply.

This episode revealed that both sides' positions, however ideologically framed, were fundamentally shaped by strategic calculation.

1990s

The 1990s Shift: Iran Becomes Israel's Primary Threat

Post-Cold War Recalibration

The end of the Cold War and the 1991 Gulf War reshuffled the strategic deck. Iraq, battered and sanctioned, was no longer the primary threat. The Soviet Union's collapse removed a major patron of Arab states. The Oslo Accords suggested Arab-Israeli peace was possible. In this context, Israeli strategic planners — particularly Benjamin Netanyahu — began reframing Iran as Israel's primary existential threat.

Netanyahu's Three-Decade Campaign

Netanyahu's public campaign against Iran's nuclear program became the defining theme of his political career:

Each time previous timelines expired without materialization, Netanyahu simply reset the clock. The Iran nuclear threat became his signature issue and a defining element of Israeli political discourse.

Israeli Domestic Politics & Iran

The Iran threat has profoundly shaped Israeli elections, military doctrine, and defense budgets. Candidates are evaluated on "toughness" toward Iran. The defense establishment organized its force structure and long-range strike planning around the Iran contingency. The threat narrative has also been used to argue against territorial concessions to Palestinians — on the grounds that a Palestinian state could become an Iranian proxy.

2000s – 2020s

The Shadow War: Covert Operations

💻 Stuxnet: The First Cyberweapon (2005-2010)

Jointly developed by the US and Israel under "Operation Olympic Games," Stuxnet targeted Siemens SCADA systems controlling centrifuges at Natanz. The worm caused centrifuges to spin at improper speeds, destroying approximately 1,000 of Iran's 5,000 centrifuges while displaying normal readings to operators. It was the first known cyberweapon designed to cause physical destruction and represented a new frontier in covert warfare.

🔎 The Nuclear Archive Heist (2018)

Mossad operatives conducted a spectacular operation inside Tehran, extracting Iran's entire nuclear archive — approximately 100,000 files — from a warehouse. Netanyahu publicly displayed the documents as evidence of Iran's concealed weapons work (the AMAD Project). The operation demonstrated extraordinary Israeli intelligence penetration inside Iran.

Assassinations of Nuclear Scientists

Between 2010 and 2020, at least five Iranian nuclear scientists were assassinated in operations widely attributed to Mossad:

Jan 2010 Masoud Alimohammadi — Killed by motorcycle bomb
Nov 2010 Majid Shahriari — Killed by magnetic bomb on car
Jul 2011 Darioush Rezaeinejad — Shot dead
Jan 2012 Mostafa Ahmadi Roshan — Killed by magnetic bomb
Nov 2020 Mohsen Fakhrizadeh — "Father" of Iran's nuclear weapons program. Killed in a sophisticated roadside ambush using a remote-controlled machine gun.

Iran blamed Israel for all assassinations. Israel neither confirmed nor denied, maintaining its policy of ambiguity. Additional cyber and sabotage operations included explosions at Natanz (April 2021), fires at military sites, and disruptions to port infrastructure.

2013 – 2025

Syria: The Proxy Battlefield

Israel's "Campaign Between the Wars"

As Iran deepened its military presence in Syria to support the Assad regime, Israel launched a sustained air campaign targeting Iranian military assets. Beginning around 2013 and intensifying after 2017, Israel conducted hundreds of airstrikes on Syrian territory, targeting:

  • Iranian weapons convoys (particularly advanced missiles bound for Hezbollah)
  • Iranian military bases and infrastructure
  • Quds Force and IRGC commanders
  • Iranian drone and air defense systems

Iran accepted these losses as the cost of maintaining its Syrian presence — strategically essential for its land corridor to Hezbollah and for projecting power on Israel's northern border.

The Deterrent Logic

From Small Militia to History's Most Powerful Non-State Armed Actor

Established in 1982 during Israel's Lebanon invasion with direct IRGC sponsorship, Hezbollah grew into Iran's primary deterrent: if Israel attacked Iran, Hezbollah would unleash its arsenal against Israeli population centers, overwhelming Iron Dome through sheer volume.

Degradation of the Proxy Network (2023-2026)

The October 7, 2023 Hamas attack triggered Israeli campaigns that significantly degraded Iran's proxy network. Operations in Gaza devastated Hamas's military capabilities. Operations in Lebanon, including assassination of key Hezbollah leaders, weakened the organization dramatically. By early 2026, Hezbollah was described as "a shadow of the force it once was" — significantly reducing Iran's forward deterrent capability and removing a key restraint on Israeli military action against Iran itself.

The Core Issue

The Nuclear Dimension: Asymmetric Ambiguity

🇮🇱 Israel's Undeclared Arsenal

  • Policy of "deliberate ambiguity" — neither confirms nor denies
  • Estimated ~90 nuclear warheads
  • Deliverable by aircraft, Jericho ballistic missiles, submarine-launched cruise missiles
  • Never signed the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty
  • Bars IAEA from inspecting Dimona facility
  • "Israel will not be the first to introduce nuclear weapons" — creative interpretation of "introduce"

🇮🇷 Iran's Program

  • NPT signatory; maintains program is peaceful
  • IAEA confirmed weapons-related work (AMAD Project) until 2003
  • By mid-2025: 972 lbs uranium at 60% purity — short step from weapons-grade 90%
  • Could produce enough for 5-6 weapons in <2 weeks
  • ~18,000 centrifuges operating
  • Significantly set back by 2025-2026 strikes

🇮🇷 The Hypocrisy Argument

Israel — a non-NPT state with an undeclared nuclear arsenal facing no consequences — demands that Iran, an NPT signatory, be prevented from enriching uranium for civilian purposes. This argument resonates widely in the developing world.

🇮🇱 The Distinction

Israel has possessed nuclear weapons for decades without threatening to destroy any country. Iran's leaders have explicitly called for Israel's elimination, making Iranian nuclear capability an existential threat in a way that Israel's arsenal is not.

Perspectives

How Each Side Views the Other

🇮🇷 Iran's View of Israel

  • An illegitimate colonial entity occupying Muslim land
  • Extension of Western imperialism in the Middle East
  • An oppressor of the Palestinian people
  • A nuclear-armed threat facing no accountability
  • The "Little Satan" to America's "Great Satan"

Iran distinguishes (at least rhetorically) between Judaism and Zionism. Iran maintains a Jewish community — the largest in the Middle East outside Israel — and reserves parliamentary seats for Jewish representatives. However, the line between anti-Zionism and antisemitism is frequently blurred.

🇮🇱 Israel's View of Iran

  • An ideologically driven regime committed to Israel's destruction
  • Primary sponsor of terrorism against Israeli civilians
  • Nuclear proliferation threat that could alter the regional balance
  • A rational but dangerous adversary whose ideology makes deterrence unreliable
  • Organizer of the "Axis of Resistance" designed to encircle Israel

The Begin Doctrine: Israel will not allow any hostile state to acquire nuclear weapons, as demonstrated by the 1981 Osirak strike (Iraq) and 2007 Al-Kibar strike (Syria).

Current State

Military Balance: Qualitative vs. Quantitative

🇮🇱 Israeli Advantages

  • Air Force: F-35I Adir stealth fighters, F-15I Ra'am, F-16I Sufa with precision munitions
  • Missile Defense: Multi-layered — Iron Dome, David's Sling, Arrow-2/Arrow-3
  • Nuclear: Estimated 90 warheads, multiple delivery platforms
  • Intelligence: Among world's most capable; demonstrated ability to operate inside Iran
  • Cyber: World-class offensive capabilities (Stuxnet)
  • US Support: Guaranteed military resupply, intelligence sharing, diplomatic cover

🇮🇷 Iranian Capabilities

  • Missiles: Largest ballistic arsenal in the Middle East — Shahab-3, Emad, Khorramshahr, hypersonic Fattah
  • Drones: Extensive fleet including Shahed-136, proven in Ukraine
  • Proxies: Degraded but still active — Hezbollah, Iraqi militias, Houthis
  • Strategic Depth: 1.6M km², 88M population
  • Asymmetric: Naval mines, anti-ship missiles, Strait of Hormuz threat
  • Limitation: 2025-2026 wars exposed inability to impose unacceptable costs given Israel's layered defense

The June 2025 and February-March 2026 conflicts demonstrated that in conventional warfare, Israel (with US support) can overwhelm Iran's air defenses and strike targets with relative impunity. Iran's retaliatory capacity, while significant, has proven insufficient to impose unacceptable costs given Israel's layered missile defense system.
