Regional Powers & Their Stakes in Iran

Five actors, five sets of calculations — how Saudi Arabia, Russia, China, Turkey, and the EU each pursue their own interests in the Iranian crisis

🇸🇦 Saudi Arabia
🇸🇦

Kingdom of Saudi Arabia

Custodian of the Two Holy Mosques • OPEC Kingmaker • Iran's Primary Regional Rival
Sectarian & Strategic Rival

The Saudi-Iranian rivalry is one of the defining geopolitical contests of the modern Middle East — shaped by sectarian identity, competing visions of Islamic leadership, oil politics, and regional power projection. While often framed as "Sunni vs. Shia," the rivalry is fundamentally political, with sectarianism instrumentalized by both sides.

Foundations

Competing Claims to Islamic Leadership

🇸🇦 Saudi Arabia's Claim

Custodian of Mecca and Medina, claims leadership of ~85% of the world's 1.8 billion Muslims. State legitimacy built on alliance with the Wahhabi religious establishment, which provides religious authority in exchange for resources to propagate conservative Islam globally.

🇮🇷 Iran's Challenge

After 1979, Iran claimed leadership of a revolutionary Islamic movement transcending sectarian lines. Khomeini attacked the Saudi monarchy as "corrupt servants of American imperialism" and argued hereditary kingship was un-Islamic. Iran positioned itself as champion of the "dispossessed."

The 1979 Rupture

Before the revolution, both served as American allies under the Twin Pillars policy. The revolution shattered this framework. Khomeini called for the Saudi monarchy's overthrow, incited Saudi Arabia's Shia minority to revolt, and accused the Saudis of being unfit to control the holy cities.

The 1987 Hajj clash — over 400 dead, including 275 Iranians — brought the two countries to the brink of war and led to a diplomatic break.

Proxy Wars

Battlegrounds of the Saudi-Iran Rivalry

🇾🇪 Yemen

Most destructive theater. Saudi-led coalition intervened March 2015 against Iranian-backed Houthis. Tens of thousands killed. UN called it world's worst humanitarian crisis. Houthis tied down Saudi resources far from Iran.

🇧🇭 Bahrain

70-85% Shia population under Sunni Al Khalifa dynasty. Saudi troops crushed 2011 Arab Spring protests. Iran accused of supporting militant groups. Low-level insurgency continues.

🇮🇶 Iraq

After 2003, the primary arena. Iran cultivated deep relationships with Shia parties and militias. Saudi Arabia struggled to maintain influence in a Shia-majority country.

🇱🇧 Lebanon

Iran backed Hezbollah; Saudi Arabia supported March 14 Alliance and Hariri family. 2017: MBS apparently forced PM Hariri to resign on Saudi television.

🇸🇾 Syria

Most intense proxy conflict. Iran committed billions and deployed IRGC/Hezbollah to defend Assad. Saudi Arabia funded Sunni rebels. Iran's intervention proved decisive — until Assad's fall in late 2024/early 2025.

2023

The China-Brokered Rapprochement

🤝 March 10, 2023: Saudi-Iran Diplomatic Breakthrough

In a diplomatic surprise, Saudi Arabia and Iran announced restoration of diplomatic relations in an agreement brokered by China. Both countries exchanged ambassadors and reopened embassies by mid-2023.

🏛️

Why Saudi Arabia Agreed

Vision 2030 required stability. Yemen quagmire draining resources. Confidence in US security commitments eroded after tepid response to 2019 Abqaiq attacks.

🇮🇷

Why Iran Agreed

Break diplomatic isolation. Reduce regional temperature. Create space for economic recovery. Hedge against US pressure.

🇨🇳

Why China Brokered

Demonstrate global diplomatic capability. Promote stability in its primary oil supply region. Position as alternative to American confrontation.

Status: Suspended Animation

October 7, 2023 complicated the rapprochement. Saudi progress toward Abraham Accords-style normalization with Israel was suspended. By 2025-2026, the deal appeared frozen — diplomatic relations maintained but deeper cooperation failing to materialize amid the US-Israel-Iran conflict.

Vision

MBS's Strategic Calculations

1
Vision 2030: Diversifying from oil dependence requires massive foreign investment, which requires regional stability. War is bad for business.
2
Military Realism: The Yemen war demonstrated the limits of Saudi military power. Direct confrontation with Iran is not in Saudi interest.
3
Hedging: Deepened ties with China, Russia, and India while maintaining the US alliance — creating leverage and reducing dependence on any single power.
4
Abraham Accords: Normalization with Israel would create a powerful counterbalance to Iran while deepening Saudi-US relations — but October 7 suspended this track.

The Abraham Accords & Gulf Normalization

The 2020 Abraham Accords (UAE, Bahrain, Sudan, Morocco normalizing with Israel) were explicitly motivated partly by the shared Iran threat. Results included:

  • US-led integrated regional air defense shield — first tested during Iran's April 2024 attack
  • Israeli defense exports to Accords countries surged from 3% to 12% of total, reaching a record $14.8 billion
  • Embodied Israel's "reverse periphery doctrine" — allying with Arab states against their shared Iranian adversary

Oil Politics & OPEC

Both founding OPEC members, their rivalry extends to oil markets. Saudi Arabia's ability to flood or constrain markets serves as a geopolitical tool. Iran, under sanctions limiting exports, pushes for production cuts. Saudi willingness to maintain high production during low prices is perceived by Iran as economic warfare designed to bankrupt its economy.

🇷🇺 Russia
🇷🇺

Russian Federation

Nuclear Patron • Arms Supplier • Syria Partner • Ukraine Drone Customer
Transactional Partnership

Russia's relationship with Iran stretches back centuries, marked by imperial conquest, territorial loss, and deep Iranian mistrust. The modern partnership is primarily transactional — built on arms sales, nuclear cooperation, and shared opposition to Western dominance — not a true alliance.

History

Centuries of Russian Interference

The Treaties of Humiliation

The Russo-Persian Wars (1651-1828) resulted in catastrophic territorial losses. The Treaty of Gulistan (1813) and Treaty of Turkmenchay (1828) stripped Iran of its Caucasian territories — modern Georgia, Armenia, Azerbaijan, and parts of Dagestan. Under Turkmenchay, Iran paid 20 million rubles in reparations and conceded exclusive Russian Caspian fleet rights.

The memory remains vivid. "Turkmenchay" carries emotional weight comparable to "Versailles" in German history or "Century of Humiliation" in Chinese discourse.

Soviet Era

During WWII, the Soviet Union and Britain jointly occupied Iran, forcing Reza Shah's abdication. Stalin's refusal to withdraw from northern Iran (1946 Azerbaijan crisis) became one of the first Cold War confrontations. Under the Shah, Iran was firmly in the Western camp, making Soviet-Iranian relations antagonistic.

Modern Era

Post-Soviet Rapprochement

Nuclear Cooperation: Bushehr

The most visible symbol: the Bushehr Nuclear Power Plant. Construction began under a German contract in 1975, abandoned after the revolution. Russia signed a completion agreement in 1992; the reactor began producing power in 2010. In 2014, an agreement for two additional reactors was signed, with options for six more.

Arms Sales: Iran's Primary Supplier

S-300 Air defense system delivered in 2016 (contract signed 2007, frozen under international pressure)
Su-35 Fighter jet sale negotiations reported 2023-2024
Mi-28NE Attack helicopters, deliveries began January 2025 — replacing aging pre-revolution AH-1 Cobras
Verba MANPADS Shoulder-fired air defense, ~495 million euro deal reported February 2026
Syria

Fighting Together for Assad

The Syrian Civil War cemented the partnership. In summer 2015, Qasem Soleimani traveled to Moscow (violating UN sanctions) to coordinate a joint campaign. Russia brought airpower; Iran provided ground forces through Hezbollah, IRGC advisors, and imported Shia militias.

In an unprecedented move, Tehran granted Russia access to Hamadan Air Base in August 2016 — the first time Iran allowed foreign military forces on its soil since the revolution's constitution prohibited such arrangements.

Assad's fall in late 2024/early 2025 dealt a blow to both powers, weakening their position in the wider Middle East.

Ukraine

Iranian Drones for Russia

Iran supplied Russia with Shahed-136 (redesignated "Geran-2") kamikaze drones, which became a signature weapon of the Ukraine war. Iran reportedly supplied ~6,000 drones and helped Russia localize production at its Alabuga facility, enabling mass production of several thousand per month.

This partnership transformed the relationship from buyer-seller to co-producers.

📜 January 2025: 20-Year Strategic Partnership Treaty

Covering "all spheres, including defense, counter-terrorism, energy, finance, transport, industry, agriculture, culture, science, and engineering." But notably lacking a mutual defense clause — Russia has no obligation to defend Iran if attacked.

The Partnership's Limits Exposed (2025-2026)

The US-Israeli military campaigns revealed severe limitations:

  • Russia did not intervene to defend Iran during either the 12-Day War or the 2026 strikes
  • Provided intelligence on US military positions but stopped short of direct engagement
  • Evacuated scientists from Bushehr as Israeli strikes threatened the facility
  • After Khamenei's assassination, an Iranian official was quoted: "The war proved the strategic alliance with Moscow is worthless"

Russia, embroiled in Ukraine and under Western sanctions, lacked both the capability and willingness to confront the US and Israel on Iran's behalf.

A strategic partnership, not a military alliance.

— Moscow Times analysis
🇨🇳 China
🇨🇳

People's Republic of China

Iran's Economic Lifeline • Oil Customer • Diplomatic Broker • Belt & Road Partner
Transactional — Asymmetric

The defining feature: China is far more important to Iran than Iran is to China. Beijing has leveraged this asymmetry to extract favorable terms while limiting its commitments.

For Iran, China is literally an economic lifeline — without Chinese purchases, Iran's economy would face catastrophic revenue loss. For China, Iranian oil represents a discounted supply that diversifies its portfolio, though it accounts for only a fraction of total energy imports. Transactions often involve ship-to-ship transfers, falsified documentation, and intermediary companies to circumvent sanctions.

2025-2026

China's Response to the Conflicts

Caution Over Commitment

China's response revealed interests and limitations alike:

  • During the 12-Day War, China prioritized mediation and regional engagement over political alignment
  • After Khamenei's assassination, China faced a "strategic test" — two China-friendly leaders (Assad, Khamenei) eliminated in quick succession
  • Adopted a "cautious and diplomatically focused posture" — verbal condemnation, zero concrete retaliatory action
  • Fundamental constraint: China's economic relationship with the US dwarfs its interests in Iran

China had done very little despite the elimination of its partners, reflecting the fundamental constraint: China's interests in Iran are not existential or even critical.

— CNN analysis

Belt and Road: Unfulfilled Promise

Iran occupies a potentially strategic BRI position at the crossroads of routes connecting China to Europe, the Middle East, and Africa. The China-Central Asia-West Asia Economic Corridor could make Iran a key transit hub. However, sanctions, instability, and now military conflict have prevented this potential from being realized. Iran remains one of BRI's most prominent unfulfilled promises.

🇹🇷 Turkey
🇹🇷

Republic of Turkey

NATO Member • Independent Actor • 534 km Shared Border • Historical Rival & Partner
Pragmatic — Strategic Ambiguity

The Turkish-Iranian relationship stretches back five centuries to the Ottoman-Safavid rivalry. The Battle of Chaldiran (1514) established the frontier between Sunni Ottoman and Shia Safavid domains — roughly the modern border. Despite centuries of conflict, the modern relationship inherits both competitive and cooperative dimensions.

Pragmatism

What Keeps Turkey & Iran Together

🏳️

Shared Kurdish Concern

Both face Kurdish separatist movements (PKK/PJAK). This shared "existential threat" creates a strong bond — coordinated military operations and intelligence sharing.

⛽️

Trade & Energy

$5.5 billion bilateral trade (2025). Iran is a major gas supplier through a 25-year pipeline contract expiring in 2026.

🌎

Geographic Reality

534 km shared border dictates cooperation on smuggling, migration, and water resources.

Erdogan

Erdogan's Positioning

1
NATO Member, Independent Actor: Pursues independent foreign policy — purchased Russian S-400s, maintained economic ties with Iran despite US sanctions, positions as regional mediator.
2
Syria Complicates Relations: On opposite sides of the civil war (Turkey: Sunni rebels; Iran: Assad). March 2025: Turkish FM Fidan accused Iran of supporting Kurdish militias, leading both countries to summon ambassadors.
3
Competing Regional Visions: Both aspire to regional leadership, creating friction in Iraq, Syria, and the Caucasus.
2025-2026

Turkey & the Iran Crisis

Competing Pressures

  • Opposition to Strikes: Erdogan condemned US-Israeli strikes as "illegal" — genuine opposition to military escalation in Turkey's neighborhood plus desire to maintain all relationships
  • Kurdish Anxieties: A governing vacuum in Iran could strengthen Kurdish separatists. The US plan to arm Iranian Kurds puts Erdogan in an "impossible position" — his NATO ally empowering his primary security threat
  • Refugee Crisis: No visa requirement for Iranians means potential massive refugee flows. Reports in January 2026 that Turkey agreed to arrest and return Iranian protesters drew human rights condemnation
  • Balancing Act: Condemning attacks on both sides, prioritizing containment, seeking to preserve trade and border stability while avoiding entanglement

Turkey's Three Strategic Scenarios

A
Status Quo: Maintain stability through diplomacy and bilateral channels
B
Reconstruction Partner: Position Turkey as a key partner if the Iranian regime falls
C
Manage Fallout: Contain the consequences of prolonged regional instability
🇪🇺 European Union / E3
🇪🇺

European Union & the E3

UK, France, Germany • JCPOA Stakeholders • Sanctions Enforcers • Diplomatic Power Without Military Teeth
Structurally Constrained

The E3 were critical stakeholders in the JCPOA. When Trump withdrew in 2018, Europe attempted to maintain the agreement through alternative mechanisms — and largely failed.

2019-2023

INSTEX: The Failed Workaround

In January 2019, the E3 established INSTEX (Instrument in Support of Trade Exchanges), a special purpose vehicle designed to facilitate trade with Iran outside the US-controlled financial system. In practice, INSTEX was a failure — it processed only a handful of small transactions before being discontinued in 2023. European companies were unwilling to risk US secondary sanctions.

2025

The Snapback: Sanctions Reimposed

⚠️ August 28, 2025: E3 Activates Snapback Mechanism

Under UN Security Council Resolution 2231, all pre-JCPOA international sanctions reimposed. The E3 cited: uranium stockpile at 48 times the JCPOA limit, enrichment to 60% purity lacking "any credible civilian justification," and systematic violations. Activated September 27 after a 30-day cooling period.

2026

Response to the Crisis

  • Strong condemnation of regime's brutal repression of protests
  • January 2026: Foreign Affairs Council designated the IRGC as a terrorist organization — a step Europe had long resisted
  • New restrictive measures targeting individuals involved in repression
  • Mixed responses to US-Israeli strikes: concern about proportionality and legality
  • UK House of Commons held debates; public opinion divided
Assessment

Europe's Structural Limitations

1
Dependence on US Financial System: European companies ultimately prioritize access to US markets over Iran trade, making sanctions resistance futile.
2
Internal Divisions: E3 positions not always aligned — France sometimes more hawkish, Germany more trade-oriented.
3
Limited Leverage: Without military or economic tools comparable to the US, Europe's influence is primarily diplomatic.
4
Transatlantic Tensions: JCPOA preservation after 2018 created friction with Washington. Subsequent sanctions alignment reflected European pragmatism about American financial dominance.
