🛡️ The Strategic Doctrine: "Forward Defense"

Defa-e pishro -- fighting enemies on foreign soil so war never reaches home

Origins: The Trauma of the Iran-Iraq War

Iran's forward defense doctrine emerged from the existential trauma of the Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988). During that conflict, Iran faced invasion, chemical weapons attacks, and near-total international isolation. The lesson Iran's leadership drew was stark: Iran must never again be caught fighting a defensive war on its own territory with no allies.

The doctrine holds that Iran should project power and fight its enemies on foreign soil through a network of allied and proxy armed forces, creating strategic depth and deterrence without relying on conventional military parity that Iran could never achieve against the US or Israel.

🎯 Four Strategic Purposes

  • Asymmetric deterrence: The proxy network threatens retaliation across multiple fronts simultaneously, making the cost of attacking Iran prohibitively high
  • Strategic depth: Armed allies in Lebanon, Syria, Iraq, Yemen, and Palestine extend the geography any adversary must operate across
  • Power projection without attribution: Proxy forces allow Iran to exert influence and impose costs while maintaining plausible deniability
  • Ideological legitimacy: Support for Palestinians reinforces Iran's claim to moral leadership in the Muslim world, transcending the Sunni-Shia divide

The Orchestrating Body: IRGC Quds Force

The IRGC Quds Force -- the external operations arm of Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps -- manages the entire proxy network. With an estimated 5,000-15,000 personnel, it handles intelligence, unconventional warfare, arms transfers, financial support, and strategic coordination.

The Quds Force reports directly to the Supreme Leader, bypassing both the regular military and civilian government. Its budget is largely opaque -- estimated at roughly $700 million annually before 2019 sanctions constricted resources.

⚔️ Hezbollah

Iran's crown jewel -- from 1,500 IRGC trainers in 1982 to the world's most powerful non-state army

Hezbollah ("Party of God")

Lebanon

Founded 1982 in direct response to Israel's invasion of Lebanon. Iran dispatched approximately 1,500 IRGC trainers to Lebanon's Bekaa Valley, providing the organizational template, military training, weapons, and ideology for a new Shia militia.

The Dual Identity

Hezbollah evolved into a unique hybrid -- simultaneously a political party winning parliamentary seats, a social services provider running hospitals and schools, a media empire (Al-Manar TV), and a military force rivaling many national armies. This structure was the template later replicated in Iraq.

The arsenal included short-range rockets (Katyusha, Grad), medium-range (Fajr, Raad, Khaibar), and long-range precision missiles (Fateh-110 variants, Scud derivatives), plus anti-ship missiles, anti-tank weapons, surface-to-air missiles, and armed drones. The Radwan Force -- Hezbollah's elite special operations unit -- was trained for cross-border raids into Israeli territory.

The 2006 War: "Divine Victory"

The 34-day war in July-August 2006 was a defining moment. Hezbollah fired ~4,000 rockets at Israel, demonstrated sophisticated infantry tactics, and effectively fought the region's strongest conventional military to a standstill. Israel's air and ground campaign failed to destroy Hezbollah's capabilities or stop the rocket fire.

Syria Intervention (2012-2024)

Hezbollah deployed thousands of fighters to save the Assad regime -- Iran's critical land bridge. Fighters gained extensive combat experience but suffered 1,500-2,000 killed. The intervention gave Hezbollah battlefield credibility at a significant human cost.

📈 Hamas & Palestinian Islamic Jihad

The cross-sectarian alliance that defied the Sunni-Shia narrative

Hamas

Gaza / West Bank

Iran's relationship with Hamas -- a Sunni Islamist movement from the Muslim Brotherhood tradition -- represents one of the most strategically significant cross-sectarian alliances in the Middle East. The European Council on Foreign Relations described it as "a marriage of convenience produced by shared interests on the ground rather than ideological affinity."

The shared interests: opposition to Israel, resistance to American hegemony, and the Palestinian cause -- which Iran has championed since 1979 as a core pillar of its identity.

Critically, Iran transferred not just weapons but the knowledge to manufacture them locally -- rockets, anti-tank missiles, drones -- enabling Hamas to produce weapons even when smuggling routes were disrupted.

October 7, 2023: Iran's Role

The most likely assessment: Iran provided the long-term enabling support -- weapons, training, funding, and strategic encouragement -- that made the attack possible, while Hamas retained operational independence in planning the specific date, targets, and tactics.

As one analyst summarized: "It is unlikely that the October 7 attacks could have happened without decades-long Iranian support" -- even if Iran did not direct the specific operation.

Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ)

Iran's closest Palestinian ally -- deeper and more ideologically aligned than the Hamas connection. Unlike Hamas (which sometimes distanced itself from Iran, notably over Syria), PIJ has been consistently loyal to Tehran.

Iran is virtually PIJ's sole state sponsor. PIJ operatives have explicitly stated: "We buy our weapons with Iranian money" and "An important part of our activity is under the supervision of Iranian experts."

🌊 The Houthis (Ansar Allah)

The low-cost investment that challenged the combined naval power of the Western world

Ansar Allah ("Supporters of God")

Yemen

The Houthis are a Zaydi Shia movement from northern Yemen. While Zaydi Shiism differs from Iran's Twelver Shiism, religious connections provided the foundation for a strategic relationship that has grown dramatically since 2015.

Important Caveat

Characterizing the Houthis as simple "Iranian proxies" overstates Iran's control. They have their own political agenda, leadership, and domestic constituency. Their primary grievances -- Zaydi marginalization, northern underdevelopment, corruption -- are indigenous to Yemen. Iran has leveraged these grievances rather than created them.

The Saudi War That Empowered Them

The Saudi-led coalition intervention (2015-present) became one of the world's worst humanitarian catastrophes: 25,000+ airstrikes, 19,000+ civilian casualties, 377,000+ dead from all causes, 21.6 million people needing humanitarian aid. The war drove the Houthis closer to Iran and justified massive arms transfers.

Red Sea Campaign (Oct 2023 - 2025)

Starting October 19, 2023, the Houthis attacked commercial shipping in the Red Sea and Bab el-Mandeb Strait in solidarity with Palestinians in Gaza. They achieved what no non-state actor had before: effectively disrupting one of the world's most critical shipping lanes.

  • Major shipping companies rerouted around the Cape of Good Hope, adding weeks and significant costs
  • A US-led multinational coalition (Operation Prosperity Guardian) couldn't stop the attacks
  • The Houthis proved resilient despite sustained US/UK airstrikes on their positions
  • Attacks suspended ~October 2025 after a Gaza peace proposal, resumed March 2026 after US-Israeli strikes on Iran

The Cost-Effectiveness Calculation

Iran's investment in the Houthis -- "low hundreds of millions" annually -- was a fraction of spending on Hezbollah or Iraqi militias. Yet it created a strategic actor that challenged the combined naval power of the United States, United Kingdom, and allied nations for 18 months. The Houthis emerged as the sole element of the Axis that strengthened its position after October 7.

🏭 Iraqi Shia Militias

From anti-ISIS volunteers to a state-within-a-state

Popular Mobilization Forces (PMF / al-Hashd al-Shaabi)

Iraq

Formed in June 2014 after ISIS captured Mosul. Grand Ayatollah Sistani issued a fatwa calling all able-bodied men to arms after Iraq's US-trained military collapsed. The PMF became an umbrella for dozens of militias -- but the Iran-aligned factions quickly dominated.

Key Iran-Backed Groups

  • Kata'ib Hezbollah (KH): Most powerful. Founded by Abu Mahdi al-Muhandis (killed with Soleimani). Designated terrorist organization by the US. Responsible for numerous attacks on American forces, including EFPs that killed hundreds during the 2003-2011 occupation.
  • Asa'ib Ahl al-Haq (AAH): Led by Qais al-Khazali. Broke from Sadr's Mahdi Army with IRGC support. Both militant group and political party with parliamentary seats.
  • Badr Organization: One of the oldest Iran-backed groups, formed during the Iran-Iraq War. Led by Hadi al-Amiri. Deeply integrated into Iraqi politics -- has held ministerial positions.
  • Kata'ib Sayyid al-Shuhada: Active in both Iraq and Syria.

Anti-ISIS Role

Iran-backed PMF groups fought in nearly every major battle: defense of Baghdad, Tikrit (March 2015), Fallujah (May-June 2016), Mosul (Oct 2016 - July 2017). Soleimani personally directed many operations, often photographed at the front lines. The campaign gave the PMF both military credibility and political legitimacy.

"Hezbollahization" of Iraq

After ISIS, the PMF transitioned from emergency formation to permanent feature of Iraqi political life:

  • Officially incorporated into Iraq's security forces by parliamentary vote (2016)
  • PMF-affiliated parties won significant parliamentary seats (2018, 2021)
  • Controlled border crossings, ports, and businesses -- extensive economic networks
  • Created a state-within-a-state modeled on Hezbollah's dual structure

Attacks on US Forces

After October 2023, militia attacks on US bases in Iraq and Syria intensified dramatically -- over 150 attacks in the following months, including a drone strike on Tower 22 in Jordan that killed three US soldiers (January 2024). Part of the "unity of fronts" strategy to pressure the US and Israel.

🇧🇷 Syria: The Critical Land Bridge

Iran's most important state ally -- and the costliest loss

Why Syria Was Existentially Important

Syria under the Assad dynasty was Iran's most important state ally -- the only Arab country that consistently aligned with Iran since 1979. Syria served as the critical land bridge connecting Iran to Hezbollah in Lebanon, enabling weapons and personnel transfers through Iraqi and Syrian territory.

When the civil war erupted in 2011, Iran viewed Assad's survival as an existential strategic interest. Losing Syria would sever the connection to Hezbollah, eliminate a key staging area, and prove the Axis of Resistance could be broken.

Scale of Iran's Intervention

  • Military advisers: IRGC and Quds Force officers providing strategy and command
  • Ground forces: IRGC personnel and Basij militia deployed directly
  • Multinational proxy army: Hezbollah from Lebanon (largest contingent), Afghan Shia in the Fatemiyoun Division, Pakistani Shia in the Zeinabiyoun Brigade, Iraqi militias
  • Financial support: Billions in credit lines, fuel shipments, direct cash
  • Military infrastructure: 55 bases and 515 other military points (by 2023), constituting ~70% of all foreign military sites in Syria

The Loss of Syria (December 8, 2024)

  • A rebel offensive led by HTS swept from Aleppo to Damascus in under two weeks
  • Iran's forces -- weakened by years of Israeli strikes and the 2024 Hezbollah devastation -- could not resist
  • Iran began evacuating on December 6: Quds Force commanders, embassy staff, IRGC personnel. ~4,000 Iranian citizens evacuated via Mahan Air
  • Assad fled to Moscow
  • The loss severed the land bridge to Hezbollah, eliminated years of military infrastructure, and demonstrated the limits of forward defense when the home base collapses

Israeli Strikes on Iranian Assets in Syria

Israel conducted hundreds of strikes on Iranian targets in Syria throughout the conflict, operating with near-impunity in Syrian airspace. Targets included IRGC formations, missile storage, Hezbollah weapons shipments, and Iranian bases near the Golan Heights.

The April 2024 strike on the Iranian consulate in Damascus -- killing Brigadier General Zahedi -- triggered Iran's first direct missile and drone attack on Israeli territory.

👤 The IRGC Quds Force: Orchestrator

Soleimani's legacy and the crisis of his successor

Qasem Soleimani
Quds Force Commander 1998-2020
Architect of Iran's modern proxy network. Maintained personal relationships with every proxy leader. Frequently at the front lines. Integrated disparate proxy forces into a coherent strategic framework. Assassinated January 3, 2020.
Esmail Qaani
Successor Commander
Background in the eastern theater (Afghanistan/Pakistan), not the Arab world. More bureaucratic, less charismatic. Adopted "unity of fronts" after Oct 7. By early 2026, reports suggested possible detention or execution on suspicion of being an Israeli spy -- reflecting deep institutional crisis.

Soleimani's Approach

Personal relationships: Direct connections with every proxy leader, from Nasrallah to Iraqi militia commanders to Houthi leadership.

Strategic integration: Turned disparate local movements into a coordinated regional strategy.

Battlefield presence: Unlike most intelligence chiefs, Soleimani appeared at front lines personally directing operations.

Political navigation: Managed tensions between Iran's interests and local proxy agendas across five countries.

His assassination removed a uniquely talented commander, but the organizational structures and relationships he built proved more durable than a single individual -- at least until 2024.

💡 How the Network Serves Iran

Deterrence, influence, and strategic depth through the "ring of fire"

🛡️ The Deterrence Logic

The implicit threat: any strike on Iran would trigger retaliation from all fronts simultaneously -- Hezbollah against Israel, Iraqi militias against US bases, Houthis against Gulf shipping. This "ring of fire" was designed to make attacking Iran prohibitively costly.

What the Network Provides

  • Regional influence: Power over political outcomes in Lebanon, Iraq, Syria, Yemen, and Palestine -- far exceeding what Iran's conventional diplomatic or military power could achieve alone
  • Blocking coalitions: Prevented a unified anti-Iranian alliance from forming in the Arab world
  • Nuclear negotiation leverage: Proxy capabilities provided bargaining chips on the nuclear file
  • Ideological projection: Championship of the Palestinian cause gave Iran moral authority across the Muslim world, transcending sectarian lines
  • Multi-front threat: Forced adversaries to consider threats from multiple directions simultaneously (Israel: Hezbollah north + Hamas south; US: bases across the region; Saudis: Houthi missiles)

🔒 Vulnerabilities

The structural weaknesses that 2024 brutally exposed

Intelligence Penetration

The 2024 pager attack, systematic assassination of Hezbollah's entire command, and precision strikes on IRGC commanders demonstrated that Israel achieved extraordinary intelligence access to organizations that prided themselves on secrecy.

Land Bridge Dependency

The Syria-Lebanon corridor was the critical infrastructure. Assad's fall in December 2024 severed it, making Hezbollah resupply vastly more difficult and cutting off the most important network node.

Local Legitimacy Deficits

Lebanon's 2019-2020 protests targeted Hezbollah. Iraqi protesters in October 2019 burned Iranian consulates and flags. In Yemen, the Houthis rule through coercion in many areas. These proxies face domestic opposition.

Conventional Military Inferiority

When confronted with the full weight of Israeli or American conventional power, proxies cannot ultimately prevail. The 2024 Hezbollah-Israel conflict proved Israel could systematically dismantle decades of buildup.

Over-Extension

The network stretched across five countries, each with unique local dynamics, factions, and challenges. Setbacks in one theater (Syria) cascaded to others (Hezbollah).

Iran's Credibility Gap

When Hezbollah was being dismantled, Iran's response was limited. When Assad fell, Iran evacuated. The perception grew that Iran was willing to "fight to the last Arab" while protecting its own territory.

💣 How October 7 Changed Everything

The attack that was supposed to prove the Axis -- and instead nearly destroyed it

🎯 The Intended Effect

From Iran's perspective, October 7 was designed to:

  • Derail the Abraham Accords normalization between Israel and Saudi Arabia
  • Demonstrate the Palestinian cause's continuing relevance
  • Prove the potency of the Axis of Resistance through the "unity of fronts" strategy

The Unintended Consequences: A Cascade of Catastrophe

  • Hezbollah devastated: Rather than deterring Israel, cross-border attacks invited a full-scale response that decapitated the organization's leadership, destroyed much of the arsenal, and killed ~4,000 members -- the worst setback in 42 years
  • Syria lost: Weakened Hezbollah and Iranian forces created the vacuum that enabled the December 2024 rebel offensive. Assad fell -- a geopolitical disaster few predicted in October 2023
  • Iran directly exposed: Iran's April and October 2024 missile/drone attacks on Israel demonstrated the limitations of its own conventional capabilities, with most intercepted
  • Houthis strengthened (sole winner): Red Sea campaign elevated them from peripheral actor to major strategic force -- proving the decentralized proxy model could succeed even as centralized nodes failed
  • Iran's credibility questioned: Allies and proxies began doubting Tehran's reliability. When Hezbollah was dismantled, Iran's response was limited. When Assad fell, Iran evacuated.
February-March 2026
The Final Test: US-Israeli Strikes on Iran

Joint strikes killed Supreme Leader Khamenei and targeted military infrastructure across Iran. The Axis of Resistance -- weakened by two years of attrition -- was unable to prevent the strikes, though Hezbollah launched new barrages at Israel and the Houthis resumed Red Sea threats.

The network that Soleimani built over two decades, designed to protect Iran from exactly this scenario, had instead contributed to an escalation cycle that brought war to Iran's doorstep.

Down but Not Out

Analysts caution against declaring the Axis dead. After the 2026 strikes, the network entered a phase of "strategic dormancy" -- evolving into a decentralized structure sustained by oil smuggling, cryptocurrency, and reconstruction contracts.

Hezbollah has historically rebuilt after devastating losses (it was far stronger after 2006 than before). Iraqi militias retain deep roots in Iraqi society and politics. The Houthis control most of Yemen's populated territory. The organizational knowledge, ideological commitment, and strategic rationale persist even as capabilities have been severely degraded.

The question is not whether the Axis of Resistance survives, but what form it takes in the post-Khamenei era -- and whether a new Iranian leadership will choose to rebuild, restructure, or rethink the entire forward defense doctrine.
