Augmentation Is the Wrong Word

You will meet the word "augmentation" if you read about RLS. It does not apply to what happened to you, and using it would import a treatment algorithm that has nothing to do with your situation.

The field has moved, and this is why iron matters more now. The 2025 American Academy of Sleep Medicine guideline conditionally recommends against the standard use of ropinirole, pramipexole, rotigotine and levodopa, explicitly because of augmentation risk. Note the wording is "against the standard use," not "against long-term use." The drugs that were first-line for RLS a decade ago are the ones being backed away from now, and iron has moved to the centre of RLS care in their place.
Three honesty notes on the numbers above, because the commonly-quoted pair is mislabelled. First, the gabapentinoid confidence interval (0.2–3.3) overlaps the short-term dopamine-agonist interval (1.4–7.3), so "much lower" is a fair summary and "categorically different" is not. Second, the source meta-analysis is titled "Incidence of Augmentation in Primary RLS Patients May Not Be That High", which means it is routinely cited to maximise a gap its own authors argue is overstated. Third, the AASM 2025 guideline contains no augmentation percentages at all; the figures come from the separate meta-analysis and are frequently attributed to the guideline by mistake. None of this touches the load-bearing point, which is the category distinction itself.
