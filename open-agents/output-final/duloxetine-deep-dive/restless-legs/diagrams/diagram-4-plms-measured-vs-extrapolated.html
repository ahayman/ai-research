Duloxetine and PLMS: What Is Measured, What Is Extrapolated

There is an authoritative-sounding sentence in the review literature saying duloxetine increases periodic limb movements. This is where it comes from, and why it does not hold.

EvidenceWhat it isWhat it can and cannot support
Kolla 2018
PMID 28822709		 Systematic review, 18 prospective studies Can support: antidepressant-induced RLS/PLMS increases are "uncommon" and, for the drugs studied, do not disrupt sleep. Cannot support: anything about duloxetine, which is not mentioned. This directly contradicts the premise that PLMS will silently fragment his sleep.
AMSP pharmacovigilance
PMID 42251748, n=340,099		 Real-world, 99 hospitals, 2001–2016 Can support: duloxetine is not a mirtazapine-class offender (0.01% vs 0.07%). Cannot support: "duloxetine is innocent." It counted severe, newly-diagnosed, inpatient RLS, which is a very high bar, and not "my existing RLS got worse." Absence of severe new cases is not absence of aggravation.
Salín-Pascual 1997
PMID 9515972, n=8		 The only SNRI dose-escalation PSG study Venlafaxine 75mg × 2 nights then 150mg × 2 nights; 6 of 8 showed PLMS >25/hour. This looks like dose-dependence and is not: dose is perfectly confounded with consecutive nights, because the 150mg nights are also nights 3 and 4. There is no clean PLMS dose-response study for any SNRI.
Shao 2024
PMID 38730422, n=1		 The closest on-point evidence that exists 67-year-old woman, duloxetine 60 → 80mg. The next night: creeping sensations in shoulders and arms, urge to move, worse at rest, relieved by movement. Resolved on reduction to 40mg, no recurrence at 6 months. Confounded: escitalopram was cut from 15mg to 10mg at the same time, so the case cannot distinguish a duloxetine increase from an escitalopram reduction. Its onset was overnight, which is decision-relevant for how fast he would know.
The chain has four breaks, and honesty requires naming all of them:
  1. Duloxetine has never been measured for PLMS. The only assertion that it raises PLM is a review extrapolating from venlafaxine.
  2. PLMS dose-dependence is not established for any SNRI. The only escalation study confounds dose with cumulative nights.
  3. The one systematic review of 18 prospective studies says increases are "uncommon" and produce PLMs "that do not disrupt sleep."
  4. The mechanism is coherent but not evidenced. Duloxetine's lack of H1 affinity makes the serotonergic route the only one available to it. Coherence is not evidence.
Verdict: monitor, do not predict. His RLS did get worse on 20mg. That is real, observed, personal, and better evidence about him than any group data here. The dose-dependence is unevidenced for this drug in anyone. A dose increase is a reasonable thing to watch that signal during. It is not a documented hazard. The distinction matters: monitoring means deciding in advance what to watch and how to tell causes apart; predicting means deciding the answer before the experiment.

If someone tells you duloxetine raises your PLMS, ask what they are reading. The sentence exists, it is in a peer-reviewed review, and it sounds authoritative. It has no duloxetine study behind it. The honest answer is that nobody knows, and "nobody knows" is a different thing from both "it is safe" and "it is dangerous."
