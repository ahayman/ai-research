The case for going up, stated as strongly as it deserves

Pain is why you are on this drug at all, and pain has a threshold that you are currently underneath. Below 40mg, duloxetine has been tested for pain three times, in two different conditions, in roughly 350 patients, and it failed on pain every time primary, verified. Twice of those three, the trial had adequate power to detect an effect. The cleanest is Goldstein 2005, which randomised 457 patients with diabetic neuropathy 1:1:1:1 to placebo, 20mg, 60mg and 120mg, giving the 20mg arm and the 60mg arm roughly 114 patients each primary, verified. 60mg separated from placebo. 20mg did not. Same trial, same disease, same number of people in each arm, which means the usual escape route (the low-dose arm was too small to show anything) is closed. Cochrane's own authors say it without hedging: doses of 60mg and 120mg daily are efficacious, “but lower daily doses are not” primary, verified.

The second argument for going up is that the thing you would most fear losing appears to survive the trip. Nicolini 2009 is the only trial that ever contained a fixed 20mg arm for anxiety, and its higher-dose arm did slightly better rather than worse: a HAM-A change of −15.3 points at 60–120mg against −14.7 at 20mg, with response rates of 65% against 60% and remission 44% against 42% primary, verified on significance the magnitudes are secondary-source. Those gaps are small, but they point up rather than down. So the fear that a dose increase would trade your anxiety benefit away for pain relief does not have trial evidence behind it.

Third, there is no evidence that the specific step you are contemplating damages your sleep. One trial in the entire registry ever compared a fixed 20mg arm against 60mg and 120mg head to head, and on that trial's sleep measures 20mg and 60mg are indistinguishable: insomnia 10.2% against 8.6%, somnolence 5.1% against 4.3%, discontinuation for adverse events 15.3% against 14.7% primary, verified. The inflection is at 120mg, where insomnia doubles to 18.8% and somnolence triples to 12.5%. You have been bracing for insomnia at 60mg, and the by-dose data does not put it there.

Fourth, two arguments that circulate against dose increases do not survive contact with their own sources, and you should not carry either into the room. The first is that going up makes coming down harder. It does not, in any way that matters to you, because duloxetine's occupancy curve is steep at the bottom and flat at the top: 60mg down to 20mg costs roughly 16 occupancy points with a capsule available at every step, whereas 20mg down to zero costs roughly 72 points with no capsule below 20mg reasoning, not evidence. The cliff is below your current dose. You are already standing on it, and going up adds a gentle ramp in front of an unchanged drop. The second is that emotional blunting is dose-dependent, which was described in this project's own validation as the strongest argument against an increase. It collapses on inspection: the claim traces back through two reviews to a single 1990 case report, and the one study that measured it directly found the opposite. Aşçibaşi 2020 raised doses in 98 patients and blunting fell (P<0.001) primary, verified. Goodwin 2017 found no difference by any agent, at roughly 46% across the board primary, verified.

Fifth, the POTS concern that appears in the literature is real but dose-qualified to 80–240mg per day primary, verified, which is not the dose you are discussing. It is worth a conversation at 60mg. It is not an argument against it.

The case against going up, stated as strongly as it deserves

The pain payoff is modest even when the threshold is crossed. This is the number that matters most and it is not the one this project spent most of its time arguing about. The fibromyalgia-specific Cochrane review is Welsch 2018, and it reports NNTB 11, with a confidence interval of 9 to 14, for 50% or greater pain relief primary, verified. Its authors describe that as “no clinically relevant benefit.” GRADE quality: LOW. Sponsor: Lilly, on all but one trial. Eleven people take the drug at 60mg so that one of them gets meaningful pain relief, and the review team that produced the number does not think it clears the bar. Set that against the anxiety response at your current dose, where the same arithmetic gives roughly NNT 5.6 reasoning, not evidence. If those numbers hold, you are already on a dose with better evidence for the thing you report benefiting from than the target dose has for the thing you were prescribed it for.

Somnolence is the live risk, and it lands on precisely what you are protecting. Duloxetine's FDA label states that somnolence is one of four adverse events with a significant dose-dependent relationship in fixed-dose studies primary, verified. The incidence is 10–11% against 3% on placebo. If you encounter the figure “1.5%” anywhere, that is a discontinuation rate wearing an incidence's clothes, and it is off by nearly an order of magnitude. In the only trial of duloxetine ever run in ME/CFS, somnolence was 41.4% (12 of 29) at 60–120mg against 10% on placebo primary, verified. That is your diagnosis, at the dose in question, and it is the highest somnolence figure anywhere in this corpus. And the reason it bites is that your reported gain from this drug is waking less tired. Somnolence is the one adverse effect that spends the benefit you actually got.

Here the evidence needs stating precisely, because it is more tangled than any summary of it. The direction is label-confirmed. The magnitude at your specific step is not measured. Two trials compared 30mg against 60mg directly, and somnolence rose sharply at that step: 2.6% to 10.2% in adolescents, and 7.8% to 14.3% in Korean adults primary, verified. But those are 30-to-60 steps, not 20-to-60. The single trial that directly tested 20 against 60 found somnolence essentially flat, 5.1% against 4.3%, and its 20mg arm had only 59 patients, so it is underpowered and its confidence intervals are wide primary, verified. What you have, then, is a label that asserts dose-dependence without publishing the per-dose numbers, two trials showing a real rise one step above you, and one underpowered trial showing nothing at your step. That is a genuine tension, and it does not resolve. Anyone who tells you confidently that 60mg will make you sleepy, or confidently that it will not, is going beyond the data in one direction or the other.

Now the part that matters most, because it lands on you specifically. Of the three sleep-adjacent measures in that trial, insomnia never rises across this range at all, and somnolence sometimes does. But the one that rises at your exact step is fatigue. It went from 0% at 20mg to 6.0% at 60mg, and it is the only clean monotonic rise across the 20-to-60 step in the whole table primary, verified. The adverse-event term “fatigue” is not the same object as your bone-weary exhaustion, and it would be dishonest to pretend otherwise. But it is the closest thing the data has to it, fatigue is your primary disability, and your valued gain from this drug is waking less tired. So the single exception in the table lands precisely on what you are protecting. This project's own brief left it out and put the emphasis on somnolence instead, which is the wrong emphasis on the wrong measure.

The sensory-gating cost may also be dose-dependent, and this is the argument that speaks to the thing you have already noticed. The only human dose comparison that exists is two studies from one laboratory, using the same paradigm, the same design and the same population, one dose apart. One small dose step flipped the filter, and that is a real finding about a real thing. It is also the only mechanism in eight passes that predicts the acute-worse effect you most distinctively reported.

What it does not tell you is how far your own step would move it, and here a tempting bit of arithmetic has to be resisted before a better one replaces it. 20mg to 60mg is a tripling of the milligrams, but milligrams are not the active variable. Serotonergic engagement saturates, so you are already at roughly 72% transporter occupancy and 60mg puts you at roughly 88%, which is a sixteen-point step rather than a tripling reasoning, not evidence. Reading the ladder as “1.5× flipped it, so 3× will flip it harder” treats a saturating curve as a straight line. The concern survives; the dose multiplier does not.

Oranje's filter-flipping step, converted, is +3.7 occupancy points. Your own escitalopram step from 10 to 20mg, the one that cost you a week of function, is +5.8 points. The step you are contemplating is +16.7 points, which is about 4.5 times the step that flipped the filter in a blind crossover and about 2.9 times the step that cost you a week reasoning, not evidence, because the occupancy values are modelled and the comparison crosses two drugs. That is the sharpest form this warning has ever taken, and it is arithmetic on this project's own numbers rather than a feeling.

The trap, which is the most decision-relevant thing on this page

You have a hypothesis: that the crashes aggravate your ME/CFS and fibromyalgia, and that preventing them should therefore improve your pain slowly, at any dose, with no threshold to cross. The standard model says something different: that pain needs the noradrenergic threshold at 40–60mg and nothing below it will do.

Right now, at 20mg, those two models disagree. Your model predicts slow pain improvement without a dose change. The standard model predicts none. That disagreement is a working experiment, and it is running.

At 60mg, the two models make identical predictions. Both say the pain should improve. Going to 60mg satisfies both, which means that if your pain improves afterwards you will never know whether it was norepinephrine crossing a threshold or the accumulated absence of crashes doing what you thought it would do. The experiment does not just pause. It closes, and it does not reopen, because you cannot un-know a dose.

The 20mg window is the only clean test of your own hypothesis that will ever exist, and it is open now. reasoning, not evidence

This is not an argument for staying at 20mg. It is an argument that the information has a value, that the value is finite and time-limited, and that you should price it deliberately rather than discover afterwards that you spent it without noticing. It is entirely coherent to decide the pain matters more than the knowledge and to go up anyway. What is not available is going up and keeping the experiment.

What you can observe before the appointment, and what you cannot

Four variables are already moving. Duloxetine (two weeks), melatonin (nightly), iron (started this week), and possibly a dose change. The iron one deserves a specific note: oral iron acts at roughly 12 weeks, so it cannot explain anything at your appointment secondary — unverified. It is not a confound yet. It becomes one in the spring, which is exactly when you would otherwise be reading your own data for signs that the dose decision worked. Decide what you are measuring, and how you would tell these apart, before you change anything.

Count events. Do not rate feelings. This is the single most useful instrument change available to you, and it exists because of a specific finding about your diagnosis. In Watson's monozygotic twins discordant for ME/CFS, the CFS twins rated themselves at 10.9 on the Epworth scale, which crosses the clinical threshold for excessive daytime sleepiness, while their objective sleep latency was 8.9 minutes and entirely normal primary, verified. The introspective channel is not buried under your fatigue. It is hijacked: pinned near the top, answering a question it was not asked. When you introspect “am I sleepy?”, your fatigue answers. Meanwhile the objective channel, actual sleep propensity, is clean and has enormous headroom. So route around introspection. Ask “did I fall asleep?”, never “do I feel sleepy?” reasoning, not evidence

Your own concern was that you would not notice somnolence, and you put it precisely: “There are different types of exhaustion. Just feeling 'sleepy' isn't a bother. It's mostly that bone-weary, pain-laden exhaustion where my brain is filled with so much fog I can barely function.” Your conclusion was right. Your reason for it was not, and the correction is what makes the problem solvable.

The discriminator that works needs no introspection at all. Count deliberate naps that succeeded and unintended dozes separately, and never merge them. Benefit looks like the first rising while the second stays at zero. Somnolence looks like the second rising, whatever the first does. Both can happen at once, and you should not expect a clean verdict reasoning, not evidence. The reason this axis works is that the two are opposite on the question “did I choose this?”, which is a question your fatigue cannot answer for you. There is a third discriminator, dose-dependence, and it is the sharpest of the lot, but it is unavailable to you before the appointment because you are at one dose. It only exists after the decision it would inform.

The nap test is worth running, and the reason is not the one you would guess. You have been running an aborted version of a standard clinical protocol your entire life. The Multiple Sleep Latency Test allows 20 minutes and scores a no-sleep trial as 20. You lie there for 10 to 20 minutes and give up. So your lifelong hard zero may be a lifelong latency of 18 minutes that you have never once let run to completion. Running the full 20 converts a lifelong binary into a number, and a number can move where a “no” cannot reasoning, not evidence.

But the asymmetry has to come first, because it is the verdict. The test is sound for a positive and near-worthless for a negative. The killer is that duloxetine improved your sleep, which reduces sleep drive, which makes napping harder. Bonnet and Arand measured exactly this: sleep deprivation drove nap latency from 13.4 minutes to 3.8 primary, verified, and their conclusion is that measured sleep tendency is a combination of sleep drive and arousal, with drive dominating. The drug is pushing the outcome both ways at once. If it lowered your arousal, latency should fall. If it improved your nights, sleep pressure falls and latency should rise. These cancel, and nobody knows by how much. A null is therefore uninterpretable, and you should know that before you start so that a null does not read to you as a refutation. If you do nap, cleanly and repeatably at a fixed time on a channel that has been at zero for decades, that is a real observation and it would be the first thing anyone has ever recorded about duloxetine and daytime sleep propensity in any population no evidence exists.

The protocol matters more than it seems: four attempts rather than one, a fixed early-afternoon time, no exertion for 30 minutes beforehand (Bonnet and Arand found a five-minute walk raised latency from 6.9 to 11.9 minutes, which is larger than any plausible drug effect) primary, verified, no caffeine for eight hours, log the previous night's sleep, and write your prediction down before the first attempt. Do it now, because the window closes at the dose change and does not reopen.

One finding runs against your own theory and you should have it. You wondered whether not napping might be causing your poor sleep. Gotts 2015 followed 101 ME/CFS patients with 14-day sleep diaries: 21 of them (20.8%) did not nap at all, so you are in a common fifth rather than an anomalous one, and napping was associated with worse cognition and more daytime sleepiness primary, verified. Cross-sectional, so the direction is unresolvable, and confounded by severity. But the sign is the opposite of your assumption. Your causal arrow may run backwards: not napping may be protecting your night sleep rather than damaging it. That is untested, and it is worth knowing before you treat learning to nap as a goal.

The measurement problem you cannot solve before the appointment

Your episodes are triggered, not spontaneous. That single fact changes the denominator. It is not time. It is exposures reasoning, not evidence.

Two quiet months working from home, avoiding crowds, is worth almost nothing, because the drug was never tested. One theme-park day with no episode afterwards is worth more than two uneventful months. If a significant sensory overload reliably produced an episode 80% of the time at baseline, then three to five comparable overloads survived cleanly is roughly a p<0.05 result, and it is a result about the thing that actually matters reasoning, not evidence. It has one further property the calendar lacks: regression to the mean cannot fake it. Regression explains why a bad stretch is followed by a better one. It does not explain why a specific, repeated, previously reliable trigger stopped producing its effect.

You have one such exposure. Five-plus hours at two crowded festivals, two panic attacks with heart rate above 140 at rest, historically a guaranteed one-to-two-week crash, and no crash. Your family independently braced for it and it never came, which matters because it makes the observation harder to explain as your own expectation.

One is not three. And the time-based arithmetic is worse than it feels: at two weeks, against a baseline of several episodes a month, 13% to 37% of people for whom this drug does absolutely nothing would be seeing exactly what you are seeing reasoning, not evidence. Roughly a one-in-three to one-in-seven chance of a pure fluke. Chance stops being a plausible explanation at about six weeks, and it is dead by three months. But that only kills random fluctuation. Placebo, regression to the mean and natural course all survive the passage of time, because they are systematic rather than random, and they produce exactly the same zero-episode observation. A three-month clean run proves the improvement is real. It says nothing about whether the drug caused it.

Your appointment lands at roughly four weeks. At four weeks you will have between 2% and 14% odds of a pure fluke and, unless chance supplies a trigger, no exposure-conditional data at all. The data arrives after the decision. That is not a reason to delay anything. It is a reason to walk in knowing precisely what you do not yet know, and to say so, because the strongest thing you can bring to that room is an accurate account of the size of your own uncertainty.

There is one asymmetry in your favour and it is underrated. The worse restless legs, the vivid dreams and the floaty feeling are pharmacological signatures. Nobody's expectation produces restless legs reasoning, not evidence. They do not prove the drug is causing the episode benefit, because two real effects of one drug need not be the same effect. The questions worth asking

What nobody knows

Nine evidence-absences. These are the most honest and valuable claims in this project, and they are not being softened. no evidence exists

  1. Nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults. One three-patient case series exists.
  2. Nobody has studied the overwhelm-to-crash cycle pharmacologically.
  3. Nobody has studied whether prior dissociation predicts recurrence.
  4. Nobody has studied this decoupling, meaning a drug that worsens acute reactivity while preventing a delayed sequela, in any human, for any drug. The human evidence explains the acute half; the animal evidence explains the aftermath half; nothing has both.
  5. Nobody has measured what duloxetine, or any SNRI, does to daytime sleep propensity. No MSLT study exists at any dose in any population.
  6. Nobody has recorded a locus coeruleus neuron on chronic duloxetine in an awake animal, let alone a human.
  7. Nobody has studied antidepressant-dose-change-induced dissociation. The whole literature on antidepressants and depersonalisation is 44 papers, most of them burnout studies false-hitting on an unrelated subscale; narrowed properly it is 4 papers, none on topic. No prevalence, no time course, no habituation data. The only named dose-transition syndrome, activation/jitteriness, does not list dissociation among its eleven constituent symptoms.
  8. Duloxetine has never been imaged outside three brain regions, in twenty years: thalamus, midbrain, striatum. Escitalopram has fourteen. Every region the dissociation literature implicates, meaning insula, anterior cingulate and prefrontal cortex, is unmeasured for duloxetine at every dose. This single gap is why the question “what is different about the two drugs?” cannot be answered, and it is a fact about funding priorities rather than about anyone's reasoning.
  9. The link from occupancy loss to withdrawal symptoms is assumed, not validated. The entire hyperbolic-tapering programme rests on it. The systematic review that established the curve says occupancy change “might be related” to withdrawal; the clinical recommendation is built on top of that word. No study measures occupancy change against withdrawal severity in the same people. The recommendation may well be right, and this page relies on it, and it should say what it rests on.

And the quantitative fact underneath all of it

Abanades 2011 fitted individual EC50 values across ten people on the same drug, same dose, same protocol, and found an 8.5-fold spread (0.54–4.61 ng/mL) primary, verified. The population curve does not describe any individual in it. That is why “try and see” is correct clinical practice rather than laziness, why forums show wild variance, and why nothing on this page can tell you what will happen to you.

The occupancy arithmetic behind the dose step and the taper cliff

  • Klein N, et al. (… Kasper S). In vivo imaging of serotonin transporter occupancy at different doses of escitalopram or citalopram. Psychopharmacology. 2006 Oct;188(3):263–72. PMID 16955282. Single dose, midbrain, n=5 per arm: 5mg 60%, 10mg 64%, 20mg 75%. The escitalopram curve on this page is fitted from those three points, giving ED50 ≈ 1.82mg and Emax ≈ 81.8%, and is shown so it can be checked or rejected. Held-out check: the model puts 10mg at 69.2% against an observed 64%, over-predicting by 5.2 points, inside the ±6 SD. Conflation trap: Klein 2007 (PMID 17235610) is a different study — 10 days, n=6.
  • Takano A, Suzuki K, Kosaka J, Ota M, Nozaki S, Ikoma Y, Tanada S, Suhara T. A dose-finding study of duloxetine based on serotonin transporter occupancy. Psychopharmacology. 2006 Apr. PMID 16506079. ED50 = 7.9mg; thalamic occupancy 71.3 ± 5.3% at 20mg. The duloxetine curve is computed from that ED50 and independently reproduces this project's standing figures: 60→20mg = 16.7 points against a standing “~16,” and 20→0 = 71.7 points against a standing “~72.”
  • Arakawa R, Tateno A, Kim W, Sakayori T, Ogawa K, Okubo Y. Time-course of serotonin transporter occupancy by single dose of three SSRIs. Psychiatry Res Neuroimaging. 2016;251:1–6. PMID 27082864. The tracer-matched escitalopram 10mg thalamic figure that pairs with Takano's duloxetine 20mg.
  • Sørensen A, Ruhé HG, Munkholm K. The relationship between dose and serotonin transporter occupancy of antidepressants: a systematic review. Mol Psychiatry. 2022 Jan. PMID 34548628. 17 studies, 294 participants, 309 occupancy measures across ten antidepressants including duloxetine. Establishes that the hyperbolic shape is a property of receptor occupancy rather than of any one drug, and predicts the lived pattern verbatim: “a linear tapering regimen, involving stopping at the lowest manufactured dose, or even half of it, would correspond to increasingly larger reductions in occupancy.” Note the hedge it carries and this page relies on: “might be related to the occurrence of withdrawal symptoms.”

  • Horowitz MA, Taylor D. Tapering of SSRI treatment to mitigate withdrawal symptoms. Lancet Psychiatry. 2019;6(6):538–46. PMID 30850328. Abstract only; full text paywalled and not retrieved.
