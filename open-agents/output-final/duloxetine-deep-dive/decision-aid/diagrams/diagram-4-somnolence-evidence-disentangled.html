Somnolence: The Dose Evidence, Disentangled

This is the most tangled evidence on the page, and it does not resolve. The direction is label-confirmed. The magnitude at your specific step is not measured. Anyone who tells you confidently that 60mg will make you sleepy, or confidently that it will not, is going beyond the data.

⚠ A correction this diagram makes against its own binding brief

The brief states that somnolence "rises at the 20→60 step (2.6%→10.2%, 7.8%→14.3%)." Both of those are 30→60 steps, not 20→60.

The only trial that ever tested 20mg against 60mg directly found somnolence flat: 5.08% → 4.31%.

The brief's version makes the case against a dose increase look stronger than the evidence supports. That is precisely the failure mode §1 of the brief warns about, committed by the brief itself. The correct picture is below, and it is genuinely mixed.

What is actually established

  • Incidence 10–11% vs 3% placebo (11% in pain indications specifically). [primary, verified — FDA label]
  • The FDA label flags somnolence as one of four events with "a significant dose-dependent relationship in fixed-dose studies" (with constipation, insomnia, decreased appetite). The direction is verified. But the label asserts the relationship without publishing the per-dose percentages, and the pooled fibromyalgia safety analysis does not stratify somnolence by dose either. So the magnitude is not established. [primary, verified / no evidence found]
  • "It wears off" is UNSUPPORTED. One open-label Japanese study claims somnolence "resolved with continuing drug administration" [secondary — weak, do not rely], and the pooled fibromyalgia data trend the other way: 9.6% short-term → 11.8% at ≥6 months → 14.0% at 1 year (non-identical samples, so exposure-time artifacts are likely). Somnolence was the only specific adverse event with a significant difference in discontinuation (p=0.003). [primary — PMC2721139]
  • You are bracing for the wrong thing. Insomnia does not rise from 20→60 anywhere (10.2%→8.6% here; 12.7%→13.6% and 9.4%→7.9% in Korean adults). It rises at 120mg.
  • Wilson 95% CIs at 20 vs 60mg overlap almost entirely (insomnia ≈4.8–20.5% vs ≈4.8–15.1%). Even the 120mg spike is suggestive rather than decisive within a single trial. [reasoning — calculated from registry counts]

⚠ The number trap, flagged because this project keeps falling into exactly this one

Secondary sources report duloxetine somnolence as "1.5% vs 0.0%" or "1.1% vs 0.0%." Those are DISCONTINUATION rates, not incidence. The incidence is 10–11% vs 3%. Any source quoting ~1% has conflated discontinuation-due-to with occurrence-of. A plausible-looking number, off by an order of magnitude, from a real document — caught only by reading the label tables directly. [primary, verified]
Why this one matters more than its size suggests
Your reported gain from this drug is waking less tired. Somnolence is the one adverse effect that spends exactly the benefit you actually got. And you told us you might not detect it: "Just feeling 'sleepy' isn't a bother. It's mostly that bone-weary, pain-laden exhaustion where my brain is filled with so much fog I can barely function." Your conclusion was right. Your reason for it was not — the channel is hijacked, not buried. That is what makes it solvable, and it is the next diagram.
