Going to 60mg satisfies both models and destroys the experiment permanently.
If pain improves at 60mg, you will never know whether it was norepinephrine crossing a threshold or the accumulated absence of crashes doing what you thought it would. The experiment does not pause. It closes, because you cannot un-know a dose. The 20mg window is the only clean test of your own hypothesis that will ever exist, and it is open now. This is not an argument for staying. It is an argument that the information has a finite, time-limited value, and that you should price it deliberately rather than discover afterwards that you spent it without noticing.

The separating observation — free, and available at any dose

Your model must run through the fatigue limb, because Okifuji found the direct distress→pain path was null; only distress→fatigue→pain survived. [primary, verified — PMID 20591745]

So: track fatigue and pain in separate columns and never average them. If pain improves while fatigue does not, your model is dead. That falsifier costs nothing, and it works whichever dose you end up on.

Second discriminator — speed. Direct analgesia is fast; allostatic unloading is slow by construction. Pain dropping within 2–4 weeks of a dose change points to the standard model. Pain drifting down over months at an unchanged dose points to yours, or to noise. The noise floor is known: Bergenheim followed 126 women with fibromyalgia for 10–12 years and found only 16% achieved ≥50% improvement, mean 9.2mm (13%) over a decade [primary, verified — PMID 31286111]. Fibromyalgia pain is largely stable across ten years, so a substantial drift down at a fixed 20mg would be genuinely surprising and therefore informative.

⚠ The honesty this owes you: your hypothesis is three claims, and they are not equally supported

  • (1) Overload triggers crashes — ESTABLISHED as clinical description. The Canadian Consensus Criteria 2003 say it verbatim, using your own word: "Overload phenomena may trigger a 'crash' where the patient becomes temporarily immobilized" [primary, verified]. You independently rediscovered a documented phenomenon.
  • (2) Crashes then aggravate the illness — NO EVIDENCE. Never studied. [no evidence exists] This is the load-bearing limb.
  • (3) Therefore preventing crashes improves pain — SPECULATION.
  • Three places adjacent evidence leans against it: allostatic load in CFS correlates inversely with fatigue duration (r=−.26, p=.047) [primary abstract only]; prospectively the AL limb predicting pain is metabolic while parasympathetic dysregulation predicted nothing [primary, verified], and your model runs entirely through the limb that fails; and Okifuji's direct path was null.
  • It survives because it was killed by absence of research, not by logic — a distinction you drew yourself. Your exact intersection is the population these studies exclude: "autistic burnout" AND ("chronic fatigue" OR "ME/CFS" OR "post-exertional") returns one result [primary, verified — search-level]. Survival is not support.
