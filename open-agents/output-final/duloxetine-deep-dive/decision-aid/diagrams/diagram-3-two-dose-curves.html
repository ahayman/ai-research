The Two Dose Curves

This is why "should I go up?" has no single answer. Duloxetine's two effects on you do not share a dose-response. Which curve you are trying to move decides whether an increase makes sense — and nobody asked which one it was for.

0-for-3 below 40mg. ~350 patients. Twice adequately powered. Goldstein is the one that closes the argument: the 20mg and 60mg arms had the same number of patients, so "the low-dose arm was too small" is unavailable.
⚠ Russell 2008 is UNRESOLVED, and Cochrane contradicts itself. Analysis 3.8: PGI-I improved "significantly in favour... at the 20 mg, 30 mg, 60 mg and 120 mg doses." The fibromyalgia narrative: 20mg "did not show significant differences in any of the reported measures." Both are Cochrane's words. Probable reconciliation: PGI-I separates at every dose with no dose effect and never reaches clinical significance, making it a weak instrument rather than evidence for 20mg.

Nicolini 2009 is the only trial that ever contained a fixed 20mg arm for anxiety. The higher-dose arm did slightly better, not worse. So the fear that going up trades the anxiety benefit away has no trial evidence behind it.
What it does and does not establish:
• HAM-A total at 20mg vs placebo: SIGNIFICANT (p=0.01–0.001)
• HAM-A psychic factor: SIGNIFICANT
• HAM-A somatic factor: NOT significant (p=0.07)
• 20mg vs 60–120mg: NEVER TESTED. The non-inferiority analysis pooled the 60–120mg arms against venlafaxine and excluded the 20mg arm entirely. With n=84 vs n=158 there was no power for it.
⚠ Guardrail. "HAM-A somatic anxiety" is NOT your "anxiety without cognitive content." The somatic factor measures bodily symptoms; your episodes are notable for absent cognitions. Mapping them repeats an error this project already made and retracted.
Funding: six of nine authors are Lilly employees or shareholders; both academic authors have Lilly financial relationships. The review states "all five efficacy studies were sponsored by Eli Lilly."
Cochrane's own authors, verbatim: "doses of 60 mg and 120 mg daily are efficacious... but lower daily doses are not." [primary, verified]
The comparison that falls out of putting the two curves side by side
NNT for anxiety response at 20mg ≈ 5.6. NNTB for fibromyalgia pain at 60mg = 11 (9–14), which its own review authors called "no clinically relevant benefit." If those numbers hold, you are already on a dose with better evidence for the thing you report benefiting from than the target dose has for the thing you were prescribed it for. The first number is derived from a secondary-source table and is provisional; the second is primary and verified.

A correction this diagram makes against its own binding brief

The brief quotes Nicolini's arms as "−15.3/55% vs −14.7/45%." Supplement 1 established that those percentages are internally inconsistent: if −14.7 is a 45% decrease, baseline HAM-A ≈ 32.7; if −15.3 is a 55% decrease, baseline ≈ 27.8. Both implied baselines are too high for a GAD registration trial (typically 24–26) and they disagree with each other. Supplement 1's instruction was explicit: "Do not put these percentages in front of the reader as facts." This diagram shows the absolute changes and the response/remission rates instead.
