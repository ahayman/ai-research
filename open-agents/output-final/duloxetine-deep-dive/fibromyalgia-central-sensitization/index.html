The argument

You were prescribed this drug for fibromyalgia. It is worth knowing that the pain evidence has a shape, that the shape is not the one most people describe, and that you are currently standing on the wrong side of it.

The usual story about duloxetine and dose is a smooth curve: a little drug does a little, more drug does more. That story is wrong. So is the opposite story, which surfaces whenever someone reads the trial record in a deflationary mood, that duloxetine barely works for pain at any dose so the dose question does not matter. What the evidence actually shows is a threshold. Below roughly 40mg the analgesic effect is reliably absent. Somewhere between 40 and 60mg it appears. Above 60mg it stops improving while the side effects keep accumulating. Three claims, three different kinds of evidence, and the middle one is the one that concerns you.

Start with the fact that settles it. Goldstein 2005 randomised 457 patients with painful diabetic neuropathy 1:1:1:1 to 20mg, 60mg, 120mg, or placebo, which is roughly 114 patients per arm primary, verified. The 60mg arm separated from placebo on pain. The 20mg arm did not. Those two arms had the same number of patients, in the same trial, on the same endpoint, at the same time. This matters because the standard defence of the low-dose nulls is that the low-dose arms were too small to detect anything, and in a within-trial equal-arm comparison that defence is simply unavailable. When 20mg and 60mg were handed identical statistical opportunity, one of them separated and one of them did not.

If that were the only low-dose null you could still argue about it. It is not. Arnold 2012 tested 30mg in fibromyalgia with 155 patients against 153 on placebo, which is adequate power by any standard, and the pain primary came back P=0.202 primary, verified. So 30mg does not rescue the picture either. This is worth dwelling on for one specific reason: a modelling argument circulates that 30mg is the efficient dose, on the grounds that serotonin transporter occupancy is nearly as high there as at 60mg for half the side effects. That argument was derived from imaging data and, as far as this project can determine, nobody who made it checked whether 30mg had ever been tested. It has. It was, and it failed the pain endpoint in a properly powered trial primary, verified. A dose can be pharmacologically elegant and clinically inert at the same time.

Together with Russell 2008's 20mg arm, that makes low-dose duloxetine 0 for 3 on pain primaries across roughly 350 patients, in two different pain conditions, twice with adequate power primary, verified. And Cochrane says so itself, in its own formal authors' conclusions, in a sentence that no pass of this project quoted until late:

"There is adequate amounts of moderate quality evidence from eight studies performed by the manufacturers of duloxetine that doses of 60 mg and 120 mg daily are efficacious for treating pain in diabetic peripheral neuropathy but lower daily doses are not." primary, verified

That is not a hostile reading of Cochrane. That is Cochrane's concluding sentence.

Before going further it is worth saying why that trial record is so easy to misread, because this project misread it twice, in opposite directions, and both misreadings still circulate. Two of the trials in the table above were mischaracterised. Murakami 2015 gets described as "60mg failed pain." It is not a pain failure: it missed one pre-specified primary analysis at P=0.0988 while separating on both the ≥30% and the ≥50% pain responder rates, which are the exact endpoints Cochrane's number-needed-to-treat is built from, and on six of seven pain secondaries primary, verified. And Chappell 2008 gets described as "60mg failed both co-primaries." It is not a 60mg trial: non-responders were blindly escalated to 120mg at week 13, and 84% of them were, at a mean dose of 113.4mg primary, verified. Reading that trial as evidence against dose-response is backwards, because its design is a dose escalation. Meanwhile the claim that "only about 66 patients have ever received 20mg" is simply an arithmetic error: the real figure is about 193 primary, verified. And on Russell's 20mg arm, Cochrane contradicts itself inside the same review, saying in its narrative that 20mg was significant on nothing and in its own quantitative analysis that it separated on one measure primary, verified. That contradiction is real, this page does not resolve it, and it does not need to: Goldstein's equal arms and Arnold's properly powered 30mg arm settle the dose question without Russell. The pattern to notice is that the errors ran in both directions, deflationary and inflationary, and each was produced by someone reading a real paper carelessly rather than inventing one.

So there is a real analgesic upside at 60mg that does not exist at 20mg. Now the second half, which is the part that gets oversold in the other direction: that upside is small, and it is smaller than almost anyone quotes.

The figure repeated everywhere is a number needed to treat of 8 for at least 50% pain relief, from Cochrane's 2014 mixed-indication review. There is a better number. Welsch 2018 (Cochrane CD010292) is fibromyalgia-specific, newer, and far larger, pooling 18 studies and 7,903 participants, and it reports NNTB 11 with a 95% confidence interval of 9 to 14 for at least 50% pain relief: 31% on drug versus 21% on placebo. The review classifies that as "no clinically relevant benefit" primary, verified. This project spent eight passes arguing over NNTB 8 while a better-matched review reporting NNTB 11 sat unused. Use 11.

But you should have the whole of Welsch rather than the convenient half of it, because the convenient half is how this project has gone wrong repeatedly. Three things travel with that headline. First, "no clinically relevant benefit" is a threshold judgment, not a measurement: Welsch pre-specified a cut-off of NNTB 10, and NNTB 11 missed it by one primary, verified. Second, the same review found a clinically relevant benefit on the 30% pain relief threshold (risk difference 0.10, NNTB 10) and a substantial one on global impression, where 52% on drug reported being much or very much improved against 29% on placebo, NNTB 5 (4 to 8) primary, verified. Third, Welsch pools duloxetine with milnacipran, so it is fibromyalgia-specific but not duloxetine-specific: 7 of its 18 studies are duloxetine primary, verified. The honest summary is that duloxetine's fibromyalgia effect is real, is small, and sits close enough to the line that where you put the line decides the verdict. Cochrane's own bottom line keeps both halves: "A minority of people with fibromyalgia might experience substantial symptom relief" primary, verified.

Two more things belong in the same breath rather than in an appendix, because they change how much the numbers are worth. The evidence is GRADE LOW, downgraded for heavy dropout and narrow, overwhelmingly female trial populations primary, verified. And Eli Lilly, which makes duloxetine, sponsored all but one of the trials in the mixed-indication Cochrane review primary, verified. And the harms need setting beside the benefit, with one piece of arithmetic done carefully, because it is usually done carelessly.

A benefit-to-harm ratio only means something when both numbers come from the same review, since the populations, the endpoint definitions and the drug mix all differ between them. Done that way there are two honest versions. Inside the 2014 duloxetine review: NNTB 8 to help one person, against NNTH 18 (13 to 30) to make one person quit from side effects, with 12.6% stopping. That is roughly 2.2 to 1. Inside the 2018 fibromyalgia-specific review: NNTB 11, against NNTH 14, from 19% quitting on drug versus 10% on placebo. That is roughly 1.3 to 1 primary, verified. Notice what that shows, because it is the more useful observation: the more disease-matched the evidence gets, the worse the trade looks on both sides at once, a smaller benefit and a narrower margin over harm. The figure usually quoted, around 2:1, is produced by taking the benefit from one review and the harm from the other, and it should not be. Whichever valid pairing you take, the benefit does exceed the harm, and not by a comfortable margin, and it rests on low-quality evidence largely produced by the manufacturer.

Now, what is fibromyalgia, mechanically, such that this drug is aimed at it at all? The useful frame is recent and it is official. In 2017 the International Association for the Study of Pain adopted "nociplastic pain" as a third mechanistic category, alongside nociceptive pain (something is damaging tissue) and neuropathic pain (something is damaging the nervous system). Nociplastic pain is defined as "pain that arises from altered nociception despite no clear evidence of actual or threatened tissue damage... or evidence for disease or lesion of the somatosensory system" primary, verified. In plain terms, the pain is real and the machinery that produces it is behaving abnormally, but there is no injury for it to be reporting. Fibromyalgia is the archetype of the category.

The phenotype has four features worth naming because they connect directly to the pharmacology: hyperalgesia (painful things hurt more than they should), allodynia (things that should not hurt do), expanded receptive fields (the map of where it hurts grows), and impaired endogenous inhibition primary, verified. That fourth feature is the one that matters here, and it is the one people skip. Your nervous system is supposed to run its own brakes on incoming pain signals. In central sensitization, those brakes are part of what has gone wrong. So the target is not the pain signal. The target is the failing brake. That is the whole logic of prescribing an antidepressant for a pain condition, and it is a much better logic than "it will cheer you up," which is what patients are often left to assume.

This is also, incidentally, why you are in this category at all. In 973 autistic adults surveyed through the Netherlands Autism Register, 21% reported at least one central sensitivity syndrome diagnosis, the group that includes fibromyalgia and ME/CFS, and 60% scored at or above the clinical cutoff on the Central Sensitization Inventory primary, verified. The authors are careful about what that does not show: there was no control group, no comparison group of people with a central sensitivity syndrome but not autism, and the participants come from a volunteer research register rather than a population sample. So it establishes that this clustering is common in autistic adults. It does not establish why, and this page will not pretend it does.

Why should there be a threshold at all? This is where the mechanism earns its place, because it predicts the shape rather than being retrofitted to it. Your brain runs descending pain-control pathways from the brainstem down to the spinal dorsal horn, and they filter incoming nociceptive traffic before it ever becomes pain. The main descending systems are noradrenergic and serotonergic primary, verified. The noradrenergic one is the analgesic one, and its machinery is specific: norepinephrine released from descending fibres suppresses pain through α2A-adrenoceptors on the central terminals of primary afferent nociceptors (presynaptic inhibition), direct α2 action on pain-relay neurons (postsynaptic inhibition), and α1-mediated activation of inhibitory interneurons primary, verified. Three separate brakes, one transmitter. That is why norepinephrine matters to pain specifically, and it is why "more serotonin" is not a substitute.

The serotonergic limb does not simply add to this, which is the complication that gets left out. Descending serotonergic control is bidirectional: broadly antinociceptive in healthy states, but after nerve injury the descending serotonin system drives facilitation rather than inhibition, and the best-supported mechanism for that pro-nociceptive limb is spinal 5-HT3 receptor signalling from the rostral ventromedial medulla secondary — unverified. So more serotonin is not straightforwardly more analgesia, and in a sensitised state it can point the wrong way.

Now put the pharmacology beside that. Duloxetine is 9 times more selective for the serotonin transporter than the norepinephrine transporter (Ki 0.8 nM versus 7.5 nM) primary, verified, so at 20mg it is predominantly a serotonergic drug. And the serotonin transporter is already close to its ceiling there. Measured occupancy runs 71.3% at 20mg, 80.6% at 40mg and 81.8% at 60mg primary, verified, and an independent London laboratory measured 78% at 20mg at steady state primary, verified; a cross-drug synthesis puts the plateau at "approximately 80%, at the usual minimum recommended dose", with "no substantial increase of SERT occupancy with SSRI and SNRI doses above the usual minimum recommended doses" primary, verified. The norepinephrine transporter shows no such plateau: 29.7% at 20mg, 30.5% at 40mg, 40.0% at 60mg primary, verified.

The inference, and it is an inference

If the serotonin transporter is near its ceiling at your dose, then the extra 40mg cannot be buying much more serotonin, so whatever the step from 20 to 60mg buys is mostly noradrenergic, and descending inhibition is the noradrenergic limb. That is why the threshold sits where it sits reasoning, not evidence.

Two independent evidence streams, the occupancy curve and the clinical trials, put the line in the same place, and a third comparison sharpens it: measured on the same tracer, in the same region, at the same institute, duloxetine 60mg's norepinephrine transporter occupancy (40.0%) is identical to milnacipran 100mg's (40.0%), a therapeutic dose of another FDA-approved fibromyalgia drug, while duloxetine 20mg's (29.7%) sits at milnacipran 25mg's (25.3%), which is milnacipran's sub-therapeutic starting dose primary, verified.

That convergence is elegant, and this project's most reliable finding is that elegance is a warning sign, so here is the disconfirming detail. The occupancy numbers underneath this story are thin to the point of fragility. Every human measurement of duloxetine's norepinephrine transporter occupancy comes from one institute, from 23 subjects, with the same first author on both papers, and the 60mg point that anchors the entire "norepinephrine engages at higher doses" claim is n=2, with a 95% confidence interval running from −20% to +100% primary, verified. It carries essentially no information on its own. The serotonin side is barely better: Takano's 20mg and 60mg confidence intervals overlap across 71.1% to 84.5%, meaning that at n≈3 per dose the study cannot statistically distinguish 20mg from 60mg on serotonin transporter occupancy at all primary, verified. So the mechanism is a coherent account of why a threshold exists, and the trials are the reason to believe there is one. Trust the trials. The imaging is a story about the trials, not a substitute for them.

Which brings this to the thing that actually matters for your next two weeks, and it is not the pharmacology.

You have said you suspect your post-overwhelm crashes aggravate your fibromyalgia and your ME/CFS. If that is right, it implies a completely different route to pain relief: preventing crashes reduces accumulated load, which improves pain slowly, over months, at 20mg, with no threshold to cross. Call that your model. Call the standard account the threshold model: pain needs the noradrenergic step at 40 to 60mg and 20mg does approximately nothing for pain, ever.

The structural problem, and it is the most important paragraph on this page

Those two models make different predictions right now, at 20mg, and identical predictions at 60mg. At 60mg you cross the noradrenergic threshold and you carry on preventing crashes. So if your pain improves at 60mg, both models are satisfied, and you will never know which one was right. Going up does not test anything. It satisfies both accounts simultaneously and it destroys the experiment permanently. The 20mg window is the only clean test of your own hypothesis that will ever exist for you, and it is open right now, and a dose increase closes it irreversibly. reasoning, not evidence

There is a separating observation, and it is free. Your model has to run through the fatigue limb. In the best within-person dataset that exists in fibromyalgia, an ecological momentary assessment study of 81 women sampled three times a day for 30 days, the direct path from emotional distress to later pain was null, and the only surviving route from distress to pain ran indirectly, through fatigue primary, verified. The authors wrote, in as many words, "The failure of emotional distress to predict subsequent pain may seem counterintuitive" primary, verified. So your model predicts fatigue moves first or moves alongside, and pain follows with a lag. The threshold model predicts pain moves without any change in fatigue, and only after a dose increase. Therefore, if your pain improves while your fatigue does not, your model is dead. Track them as two separate lines, not one.

Three other things sharpen that test, and they are worth having before the appointment rather than after it. The first is that "wait and see whether pain improves at 20mg" is not on its own a test of anything, because placebo, expectancy and simple regression to the mean all produce slow improvement under either model reasoning, not evidence. The second is that your model, unlike the threshold model, makes a prediction about contingency. Your benefit would be purchased by crashes that did not happen, so it should arrive step-wise, tracking survived exposures, and it should stall or reverse after a breakthrough crash. The threshold model's benefit is a pharmacological constant that does not care what your month was like. So if a crash does break through, the question to ask is whether your pain steps up and stays up for weeks. If it returns to baseline within days, then the aggravation limb is falsified even though crash-prevention is real reasoning, not evidence, and that matters because it would mean the crashes are unpleasant but not damaging, which is a completely different thing and changes what preventing them is worth.

The third is timing, and it comes with a number. Direct drug analgesia is fast, whereas the slow unloading your model describes is slow by construction. So pain dropping within two to four weeks of a dose change points at pharmacology, and pain drifting down over months at a fixed dose points at your model, or at noise. The reason that is informative rather than hopeless is that the noise floor is known: following 126 women with fibromyalgia for 10 to 12 years, only 16% achieved a 50% improvement, and the mean improvement was 9.2mm, about 13%, over a decade primary, verified. Fibromyalgia pain is remarkably stable over that kind of span. A substantial drift downward at a fixed 20mg would therefore be genuinely surprising, and surprising is exactly what makes an observation worth something. The catch is that you have to write the threshold down in advance. Without a number set beforehand, every outcome reads as confirmation, because "it is working, just slowly" is compatible with anything, and that is precisely the shape an unfalsifiable belief takes as it forms reasoning, not evidence. Something like "at least 30% pain reduction by month four, with at least two survived exposures and no crashes" would do. Failing it has to count.

Now, fairness to your hypothesis, because it deserves better than dismissal and better than flattery. You independently rediscovered a documented clinical phenomenon, and you rediscovered it in the exact words the literature uses. The Canadian Consensus Criteria for ME/CFS, published in 2003, list under neurological manifestations: "There may be overload phenomena: cognitive, sensory... and/or emotional overload, which may lead to "crash" periods and/or anxiety", where the document defines "crash" as "a temporary period of immobilizing physical and/or cognitive fatigue" primary, verified. Sensory overload, then crash, then anxiety. That is your sequence, written down in a primary clinical consensus document twenty-three years ago, using your own word. That is a real find and it validates the phenomenology completely.

What it does not do is establish the load-bearing part. The CCC says crashes happen. It says nothing whatsoever about crashes causing lasting damage, which is the claim your model actually needs reasoning, not evidence. And on that claim the adjacent evidence is not kind. Allostatic load, the framework your model implicitly borrows, has never been measured in fibromyalgia at all: zero papers carry both terms in the title, and of the nine that carry both anywhere, not one measured an allostatic load index in fibromyalgia patients primary, verified, search-level. Where it has been measured in ME/CFS, the 2006 study was non-significant (OR 1.9, CI 0.75 to 4.75) and the 2009 study was marginal (OR 1.26, CI 1.00 to 1.59) and could not distinguish ME/CFS from subsyndromal fatigue primary, abstract only. Worse for the model, that same 2009 study found that longer illness correlated with lower allostatic load (r = −.26, p = .047), which is backwards: a cumulative-damage account predicts the opposite primary, abstract only. Prospectively, in a 781-person study following people for nearly seven years, the allostatic load limb that predicted chronic pain was the metabolic one, and parasympathetic dysregulation predicted nothing at all primary, verified. Your model runs entirely through autonomic and stress-reactivity machinery, and the evidence points at the wrong limb. And stress-induced hyperalgesia, real as it is, has only ever been measured in humans minutes to about 90 minutes after a stressor. Not once at weeks or months no evidence exists. Your model lives on the timescale of a season; the experiments live on the timescale of a lab visit. That gap is about three orders of magnitude and it cannot be waved across.

So the verdict on your hypothesis is a genuinely awkward shape, and the awkwardness is the honest part: descriptively supported, causally unstudied, and the nearest adjacent evidence leans mildly against it. Notice that this is not a refutation, and you have been right before about exactly this distinction. Much of that "absence of evidence" is absence of research: nobody has ever run a prospective study asking whether crash frequency predicts later pain no evidence exists, and your specific intersection, autistic plus sensory-triggered crashes plus ME/CFS plus fibromyalgia, is the population these studies exclude or never assemble in the first place. A null average is compatible with a large individual effect, and the individual-variation evidence here is unusually strong: when one laboratory fitted individual concentration-response values across just ten people, the spread was 8.5-fold primary, verified. The population curve does not describe anybody in it. What is fair to say is that three separate places where adjacent evidence does speak, the inverse duration correlation, the null direct path from distress to pain, and the metabolic-not-autonomic limb, all lean the same way, and intellectual honesty means saying so out loud rather than filing it under "unstudied."

That leaves your own report, which is the most interesting datum on this page. You describe the pain improvement as "noted but minimal" and say the effect is "mostly psychological and with my sleep." Read against everything above, that is not a disappointment and it is not a placebo confession. It is precisely the sub-threshold signature. At 20mg you are running a predominantly serotonergic drug at close to its serotonergic ceiling, and serotonergic things are exactly what you report: mood, sleep, the anxiety. The noradrenergic brakes on the dorsal horn are the analgesic ones and they are the ones the trials say do not engage until somewhere around 40 to 60mg. Your body is reporting the same dose-response the trial record reports, from the inside. The honest thing to tell you is that this is what a real 20mg looks like, that the pain upside at 60mg is real but modest and rests on low-quality manufacturer-sponsored evidence, that the pain question and the crash question are, on current evidence, independent of each other so that observing one tells you little about the other, and that the one thing a dose increase costs you which nothing can buy back is the answer to your own question. Whether that answer is worth months of unrelieved pain is not a question the evidence can settle, and it is not one this page will pretend to settle for you. It belongs to you and your doctor. What this page can do is make sure the window is visible before it shuts.

On Goldstein's arm sizes

The 1:1:1:1 randomisation and n=457 total are consistently reported across multiple independent reviews. The per-arm figures (114/114/114/115) were recovered from a review's tabulation rather than from Goldstein's own text, which was not retrievable. Cite as "≈114 per arm, 1:1:1:1." The design and the result are HIGH confidence; the exact per-arm count is MODERATE-HIGH primary, verified.

One independent synthesis disagrees, and it belongs here rather than in a footnote

Sultan 2008 (PMID 18673529), which is one of the very few genuinely non-conflicted sources in this literature (Oxford Pain Research, no author stock holdings), concluded: "The dose of duloxetine made little difference to the result" primary, verified. That cuts against the threshold. Weigh it against Cochrane's independent formal conclusion that lower doses are not efficacious, and note that Sultan's statement pools all doses including 120mg and rests on the same small 20mg subset discussed below. Two independent syntheses disagree. That disagreement is real and unresolved.

Four corrections this page does not reproduce

Earlier passes of this project asserted each of the following. All four are wrong, and they are listed here because they still circulate.

"Murakami 2015 was a pain failure." False. The trial missed one pre-specified primary analysis at P=0.0988, and it separated on ≥30% and ≥50% pain responder rates, which are the exact endpoints Cochrane's NNT is built from, and on six of seven pain secondaries: worst pain, least pain, pain right now, worst pain in diary, FIQ pain score, and SF-36 bodily pain primary, verified. An earlier pass filed those under "global/function" to support a thesis that duloxetine separates on global measures but never on pain. They are pain measures.

"Chappell 2008 shows 60/120mg failed both co-primaries." Not a 60mg trial. Verbatim from the paper: "If at Visit 8 (Week 13) the patient did not have ≥50% reduction in the [BPI] average pain score, the patient was blindly escalated to 120 mg QD." Of 117 duloxetine patients continuing past week 13, 98 (83.8%) were escalated, at a mean prescribed dose of 113.4mg primary, verified. This is a responder-enriched blind dose-escalation design, so the 6-month co-primary measures a population selected for 60mg non-response and then pushed to 120mg. The authors themselves called it "this supportive study." Reading it as evidence against dose-response is backwards, because the trial's own design is a dose escalation.

"Russell 2008: 20mg failed everything." Cochrane contradicts itself, and this page will not resolve it. Both of the following are Cochrane's own words in the same review:

Fibromyalgia narrative: "The 20 mg dose of duloxetine in Russell 2008 (223 participants) did not show significant differences in any of the reported measures."

Analysis 3.8: "Four studies reported the PGI‑I..., which was significantly in favour of duloxetine at the 20 mg, 30 mg, 60 mg and 120 mg doses... The magnitude of change at each dose was very similar, with no dose effect. However, the magnitude of change failed to reach a level considered to be clinically significant."

primary, verified on both. This explains how two earlier passes reached opposite conclusions while both citing real sources: one followed the narrative, the other followed the analysis. Neither fabricated anything. Cochrane is internally inconsistent, and Russell's full text is paywalled everywhere, which three passes have now failed to get past. The substantive point survives regardless: a measure that separates at 20, 30, 60 and 120mg, shows no dose effect, and never reaches clinical significance at any dose is not evidence that 20mg works. It is evidence that PGI-I is a weak, dose-insensitive instrument.

Design details worth having: Russell randomised 1:2:2:2, so the 20mg arm held 79 patients against 144 on placebo, and patients on 20mg were titrated up to 60mg at week 15 primary, verified. The consequence is that the arm labelled "20mg" at 6 months is not a 20mg arm: those patients had been on 60mg for 13 weeks. No 6-month conclusion about 20mg can be drawn from this trial in either direction. Lilly's own pooled analysis excluded the arm and says why, verbatim: "these data were not included in this analysis because this dose was used as a subtherapeutic control" primary, verified. That establishes that Lilly did not consider 20mg a dose under test. It does not establish that 20mg is inert, because a "subtherapeutic control" designation is a design decision made in advance, not a result.

"Only about 66 patients have ever received 20mg." Refuted: the figure is ≈193. Goldstein's 20mg arm held ≈114 and Russell's held 79 primary, verified. The "66" comes from Sultan 2008 and sits inside the "50% Pain Relief" subsection, so it counts patients with evaluable dichotomous responder data at 20mg, not patients randomised to 20mg. That explanation is plausible from context but unverified, because Sultan's per-arm table was not retrievable by any pass. State the verified census (≈193, from two trials, per-arm confirmed) and do not attempt to explain Sultan's 66. The refutation does not depend on the explanation.

Why this is the better number, and where it is weaker. It is fibromyalgia-specific, where CD007115 is a mixed-indication review spanning diabetic neuropathy, chronic pain and fibromyalgia. It is newer (2018 against 2014). It is far larger. Its confidence interval is much tighter: 9 to 14, against CD007115's 4 to 21. Against that: it pools duloxetine with milnacipran, so it is not drug-specific, and duloxetine supplies only 7 of its 18 studies. The two reviews are therefore not perfectly comparable, but the point estimate moves down and the precision improves substantially, and the fibromyalgia-specific review is the better match for a fibromyalgia patient.

The threshold nuance, stated plainly because omitting it would be the same error in the deflationary direction

Welsch's "clinically relevant" verdicts are decided against pre-specified NNTB cut-offs, and NNTB 11 missed the cut-off of 10 by one point primary, verified. So "no clinically relevant benefit" is a threshold classification, not a finding that the effect is absent: the effect is 31% versus 21%, and it is statistically solid. The same review calls NNTB 10 on the 30% threshold clinically relevant. Reporting the "no clinically relevant benefit" line without the NNTB 10 line beside it would misrepresent the review.

Sponsorship and evidence quality

Verbatim from CD007115 primary, verified:

"The manufacturers of duloxetine, Eli Lilly, were the sponsors of all but one of the included studies" and "The same company sponsored and performed all but one of the included studies (Vranken 2011)."

"Studies were mostly at low risk of bias, although significant drop outs, imputation methods and almost every study being performed or sponsored by the drug manufacturer add to the risk of bias in some domains."

GRADE downgrade reasons: "Substantial dropouts from all trials" and narrow, overwhelmingly female populations. "Nearly all the studies had a dropout rate of more than 20%"; only four trials came in under 20%.

This matters concretely and not just as atmosphere. The most-cited interpretation of Russell's 20mg null, that it was a power artifact "and not due to lack of efficacy in pain relief," comes from a review whose senior author is on Eli Lilly's speaker's bureau primary, verified. That is an interested party's opinion, not a finding. The claim that Russell's 20mg effect was "similar to that seen in the 60 mg/day group" comes from the same source and is almost certainly wrong on arithmetic: Arnold 2012's adequately powered 30mg arm produced a drug-placebo difference of only 0.34 on BPI average pain, while Russell's 120mg arm produced 0.92. A 20mg effect of 0.75 to 0.92 is not plausible when 30mg, a higher dose, adequately powered, produces 0.34 reasoning, not evidence.

What sponsorship does and does not license you to conclude

It does not mean the trials are wrong. Lilly's trials are the reason we know 60mg beats placebo, and they are also the reason we know 20mg does not, which is not a result a sponsor would choose to produce. Goldstein 2005 is a Lilly trial and it is the single most decision-relevant finding on this page, pointing against a larger market reasoning, not evidence.

The rule this suggests: trust the sponsored trials' results, and read the sponsored interpretations of those results with a much colder eye.

Why norepinephrine, and why a threshold

The descending systems. Yoshimura M, Furue H. "Mechanisms for the anti-nociceptive actions of the descending noradrenergic and serotonergic systems in the spinal cord." J Pharmacol Sci. 2006;101(2):107-17. PMID 16766858. primary, verified Verbatim: "the severity of pain sensation is markedly attenuated by the endogenous pain inhibitory systems that predominantly originate at the brain stem... the main descending systems to the spinal dorsal horn are noradrenergic and serotonergic."

The noradrenergic machinery, in detail. Pertovaara A. "Noradrenergic pain modulation." Prog Neurobiol. 2006;80(2):53-83. PMID 17030082. primary, verified In the spinal cord, norepinephrine released from descending pathways suppresses pain by three distinct routes:

  1. Presynaptic inhibition via α2A-adrenoceptors on the central terminals of primary afferent nociceptors, damping transmitter release before the signal crosses the first synapse.
  2. Postsynaptic inhibition via direct α2-adrenergic action on pain-relay neurons in the dorsal horn.
  3. α1-adrenoceptor-mediated activation of inhibitory interneurons, recruiting local inhibition.

Pertovaara also notes the state-dependence that matters for a chronic condition: "While in baseline conditions the noradrenergic system may have little effect, sustained pain induces noradrenergic feedback inhibition of pain." The endogenous analgesic influence originates largely, though not exclusively, from the locus coeruleus primary, verified.

A citation correction found this pass

The original research for this category cited "Noradrenergic pain modulation" to PMID 16766858. That PMID resolves to Yoshimura & Furue, which is a different paper. "Noradrenergic pain modulation" is the title of Pertovaara's paper, PMID 17030082. The three-receptor formulation the original research used is Pertovaara's. Both papers are real and both support the mechanism, so nothing collapses, but the content and the identifier came from different sources. This page cites both correctly. It is the chain's standing failure mode in miniature: a real paper attached to the wrong identifier. A resolving PMID is not verification.

The serotonergic complication. Descending serotonergic control is bidirectional. It is broadly antinociceptive in healthy states, but after tissue and nerve injury the descending serotonin system from the rostral ventromedial medulla drives facilitation, mediated substantially by spinal 5-HT3 receptors expressed on primary afferent terminals, excitatory interneurons and projection neurons secondary — unverified. The consequence for this page is that more serotonin is not straightforwardly more analgesia, and in a sensitised state the serotonergic limb can point the wrong way.

A second correction found this pass

The original research attributed the serotonergic switch to "spinal chloride imbalance switches inhibition to facilitation." That conflates two separate mechanisms. The chloride-gradient story (KCC2 collapse) concerns GABA-A and glycine signalling becoming excitatory after nerve injury; it is not the serotonergic facilitation mechanism. The descending serotonergic pro-nociceptive limb is best supported as 5-HT3-mediated. This page does not reproduce the chloride attribution.

Nociplastic pain. In 2017 the IASP adopted "nociplastic pain" as a third mechanistic descriptor alongside nociceptive and neuropathic pain, defined as "pain that arises from altered nociception despite no clear evidence of actual or threatened tissue damage causing the activation of peripheral nociceptors or evidence for disease or lesion of the somatosensory system causing the pain" primary, verified. Peripheral and central sensitisation, presenting as allodynia and hyperalgesia, are typically present though not specific to it. Fibromyalgia is the archetype: hyperalgesia, allodynia, expanded receptive fields, and impaired endogenous inhibition. That last item is the point of contact with everything above. If the descending brakes are part of what is failing, then a drug that raises transmitter availability in the descending noradrenergic limb is aimed at the right target. Whether it hits it hard enough at any tolerable dose is what the trials answer, and the answer is "somewhat, in a minority."

What must not be said, and why

Two figures circulate that this page will not use. "NET ED50 = 76.8mg, above the standard 60mg maintenance dose" is an extrapolation 28% beyond the highest dose tested, from a hyperbola whose own authors report that a straight line fit their data better (root mean square error 7.3 linear against 11.8 nonlinear), with no confidence intervals, and the model misses its own best-measured point by 9 percentage points primary, verified. The same objection applies to the 2025 paper's ED50 of 92.4mg, and the two estimates disagree by 20%. Neither number should carry weight.

Likewise, "30mg gets you ~79% SERT, so ~97% of the serotonergic benefit" is false precision: the confidence intervals overlap, and the honest 30mg SERT band is ~63–92%. More to the point, 30mg was tested directly in fibromyalgia and failed the pain primary at P=0.202, which settles the question without any modelling at all.

And the reason no occupancy number describes you

Abanades fitted individual EC50 values across 10 people and found an 8.5-fold spread, 0.54 to 4.61 ng/mL primary, verified. Same drug, same dose, same protocol, ten people, an 8.5-fold range in the concentration each needed for the same effect. The population curve does not describe any individual in it. This is why forums show wild variance, why "try it and see" is sound clinical practice rather than laziness, and why every number on this page is a statement about groups rather than about you.

Your hypothesis, in full

The claim splits into three, and they have wildly different evidential status. Conflating them is the main risk, and almost every discussion of this idea conflates them.

ClaimStatus
1Sensory/emotional overload triggers crashes in ME/CFSDescribed in a primary diagnostic consensus document, using the literal word "crash." Not experimentally demonstrated.
2Crashes aggravate fibromyalgia/ME-CFS (cumulative damage)Unstudied. The adjacent evidence leans mildly against.
3Therefore preventing crashes improves pain at 20mg over monthsNever tested by anyone. The nearest proxies are unfavourable.

Claim 1 is your genuine find. Carruthers BM, van de Sande MI. ME/CFS: Clinical Case Definition and Guidelines for Medical Practitioners, the Canadian Consensus Document, 2003 primary, verified.

Criterion 5, Neurological/Cognitive Manifestations, verbatim: "There may be overload phenomena: cognitive, sensory (e.g. photophobia and hypersensitivity to noise) and/or emotional overload, which may lead to "crash" periods and/or anxiety."

Body text, verbatim: "Overload Phenomena: Patients are often hypersensitive to sensory stimulation including noise, bright lights, temperature extremes, and odours... Emotional overload may be unduly stressful. Overload phenomena may trigger a "crash" where the patient becomes temporarily immobilized by physical and/or mental fatigue and recovery is slow."

And: "Stress may cause disorientation, anxiety, worsening of other symptoms, and trigger a 'crash'. Recovery is slow."

The document defines its terms: "'Overload' refers to hypersensitivities to stimuli that have changed from pre-illness status"; "'Crash' refers to a temporary period of immobilizing physical and/or cognitive fatigue."

Provenance discipline, stated at the claim rather than below it

The CCC is expert consensus and clinical description, not experimental evidence. It asserts the phenomenon exists. It does not demonstrate that the overload-to-crash link is causal, and it says nothing whatsoever about crashes causing lasting damage, which is the claim your model actually needs reasoning, not evidence. So the CCC delivers Claim 1 and is silent on Claim 2. Anyone citing it in support of the aggravation hypothesis is citing it for something it does not say.

Claim 2 is where the model is load-bearing and unsupported. The evidence against, item by item:

  • Allostatic load has never been measured in fibromyalgia. "allostatic load"[ti] AND fibromyalgia[ti] returns 0 results; [tiab] returns 9, and not one measured an allostatic load index in fibromyalgia patients primary, verified, search-level. The one fibromyalgia-specific item (Leombruni 2019, PMID 30677771) is a two-page letter with no abstract, and it studies allostatic overload, which is Fava's clinical interview construct, not McEwen's biomarker index. Different construct.
  • In ME/CFS it is non-significant or marginal. Maloney 2006 (PMID 16610956): OR 1.9, 95% CI 0.75–4.75, not significant, and the abstract's confident conclusion overstates its own data. Maloney 2009 (PMID 19414615): OR 1.26, 95% CI 1.00–1.59, lower bound exactly 1.00, and CFS vs subsyndromal fatigue OR 1.10 (0.93–1.31), not significant, so allostatic load does not even distinguish ME/CFS from subsyndromal fatigue primary, abstract only.
  • The direction is backwards. Maloney 2009, verbatim: "Among persons with CFS, the duration of fatigue was inversely correlated with ALI (r = −.26, p = .047)." Longer illness, lower allostatic load. A cumulative-damage model predicts the opposite primary, abstract only.
  • The limb that predicts pain is the wrong limb. Liang & Booker 2024 (PMID 38336697), MIDUS, N=781, ~6.7 years, 27 biomarkers across 7 systems: metabolic dysregulation predicted high-interference pain (RRR 2.00, 1.06–3.79) and ≥3 pain sites (RRR 2.03, 1.08–3.83), while parasympathetic dysregulation predicted nothing, and no allostatic load phenotype predicted chronic pain onset at all primary, verified. Rhudy 2021 (PMID 34033965, N=302) found cardiometabolic allostatic load related to spinal sensitisation measures but, verbatim, "unrelated to measures of pain perception" primary, abstract only. Where allostatic load predicts pain it is the cardiometabolic limb: BMI, waist-to-hip ratio, lipids. Your model runs entirely through acute autonomic and HPA activation. The evidence points at the wrong limb.
  • The best within-person data is null on the direct path. Okifuji 2011 (PMID 20591745), ecological momentary assessment, N=81 women with fibromyalgia, 30 days, 3×/day, lagged mixed-effects models, the highest-resolution dataset that exists on this question primary, verified.

Authors, verbatim: "The failure of emotional distress to predict subsequent pain may seem counterintuitive." An indirect path survives, distress → fatigue → pain, and it is the single best empirical scaffold your model has reasoning, not evidence. The direct path is null.

  • The timescale gap is roughly three orders of magnitude. Stress-induced hyperalgesia is real: Crettaz 2013 (PMID 23950894) found pressure pain threshold dropped in fibromyalgia patients after a Trier Social Stress Test (p=.004), and Löffler 2023 (PMID 36561877) found controls showed stress-induced analgesia while chronic pain patients showed none (interaction p=0.02) primary, verified. But every human experiment measures pain sensitivity minutes to about 90 minutes after a stressor. Not one measures weeks or months later no evidence exists. Your model requires a multi-week-to-multi-month effect and no human experiment tests that timescale at all. Note also that much of Löffler's group difference is the absence of normal analgesia rather than the presence of hyperalgesia, and the hyperalgesia limb rests on subjective ratings rather than thresholds.
  • No intervention study has tested crash-prevention to pain. "allostatic load" AND (intervention OR reduc*) AND randomized[pt] returns 0 results. No RCT has ever tested whether reducing allostatic load improves any outcome primary, verified, search-level.
  • And no prospective study has ever tested whether crash frequency predicts later pain or illness severity. Not one no evidence exists.

What genuinely favours you, stated fairly:

  • The CCC describes your exact sequence using your own word primary, verified.
  • Emotional distress to post-exertional malaise has survey support: Chu 2018 (PMID 29856774), N=150, found 90% (130/144) reported symptom exacerbation with emotional distress primary, verified. The critical correction: Chu never asked about sensory exposure as a trigger. "Sensory overload" appears in that paper as a write-in symptom of PEM, not as a trigger. Chu establishes emotional distress to PEM. It does not establish sensory exposure to PEM.
  • Post-exertional states lasting a week or more are documented: 84% of Chu's sample reported ≥24h, and 24% reported up to more than a week, in some cases several months. Your 1–2 week crash sits comfortably inside the documented range primary, verified.
  • Goertzel 2006 (PMID 16610958) found that within ME/CFS, high allostatic load was associated with worse bodily pain and physical functioning. This is the one datapoint that favours you, and being cross-sectional it cannot establish direction primary, abstract only.
  • Your intersection has essentially no literature. "autistic burnout" AND ("chronic fatigue" OR "ME/CFS" OR "post-exertional") returns 1 result, and that paper mentions chronic fatigue only as something to rule out primary, verified, search-level. That is an absence of research, not a demonstrated null.

The verdict: descriptively supported, causally unstudied, and the nearest adjacent evidence leans mildly against

Three places where adjacent evidence does speak, the inverse duration correlation, Okifuji's null direct path, and the metabolic-not-autonomic limb, all lean the same way. Honesty means saying so rather than filing them under "unstudied."

And unstudied is not false. Your intersection is exactly the population these studies exclude, and Abanades' 8.5-fold individual spread is a standing reminder that population nulls do not describe individuals.

One caveat about the framing itself, because it must not be misread

Nothing in this section is a psychological model of your illness. NICE NG206 (2021) withdrew graded exercise therapy and the deconditioning model, verbatim: "Do not offer people with ME/CFS... any programme that... uses fixed incremental increases in physical activity or exercise, for example, graded exercise therapy", and states that CBT "is not curative" primary, verified. The question examined here is pharmacological: whether a drug preventing a documented ME/CFS phenomenon would slowly reduce pain. You raised it. The evidence answers it unfavourably. Neither the question nor the answer implies the illness is psychogenic, and the allostatic-load evidence in fact points away from a stress-reactivity account and toward a metabolic one.

The trap

Model A (yours): 20mg prevents crashes, so cumulative load falls, so pain improves slowly over months, with no threshold to cross.
Model B (the standing analysis): pain relief requires noradrenergic reuptake inhibition at ~40–60mg. 20mg does approximately nothing for pain, ever.

Going up to 60mg does not discriminate between these models. It satisfies both, and it destroys the experiment permanently.

reasoning, not evidence

If pain improves at 60mg, both models are satisfied and you learn nothing about your own hypothesis, ever. The 20mg window is the only clean test of Model A that will exist for you, it is available for free by waiting, and a dose increase closes it irreversibly.

Conversely: if you hold 20mg for three to six months and pain is unchanged while crashes stay prevented, Model A's pain limb is cleanly falsified and the dose decision becomes trivial.

The information value is asymmetric in time. The cost of waiting is months of unrelieved pain. The benefit is a clean, unrepeatable answer. That is the real trade, and it is not "which model is right," it is "how much is the answer worth against months of pain?" That question belongs to you and your doctor. This page's job is to make sure you know the window exists before it shuts.

Four discriminators, in order of usefulness. Note first that "just wait and see if pain improves at 20mg" is not sufficient, because placebo, expectancy and regression to the mean produce slow improvement under either model reasoning, not evidence.

  1. Order of effects, the strongest test and it is free. Model A's causal chain must run through fatigue, because Okifuji's lagged data says the only surviving prospective path from distress to pain is distress → fatigue → pain. Model A predicts fatigue improves first or concurrently, and pain follows weeks later. Model B predicts pain moves without any change in fatigue, and only after a dose increase. Track fatigue and pain as separate measures. If pain improves while fatigue does not, Model A is falsified, and whatever is helping is not the crash route.
  2. Contingency on exposure, which is unique to Model A. Model A's benefit is purchased by crashes that did not happen, so it is contingent on exposure history and should improve step-wise, tracking survived exposures, and stall or reverse after a breakthrough crash. Model B's benefit is a pharmacological constant, indifferent to life events. The killer within-subject test: if a crash does break through, does pain step up and stay up for weeks? If pain returns to baseline within days, the aggravation limb is falsified even though crash-prevention is real reasoning, not evidence. That would mean the crashes are unpleasant but not damaging, which is a completely different thing and changes what the crash benefit is worth.
  3. Speed of onset. Direct drug analgesia is fast; allostatic unloading is slow by construction reasoning, not evidence. Pain dropping within 2–4 weeks of a dose change indicates a direct pharmacological effect (Model B). Pain drifting down over months with no dose change indicates Model A, or noise. The noise floor is known: Bergenheim 2019 (PMID 31286111) followed 126 women with fibromyalgia for 10–12 years and found only 16% achieved ≥50% improvement, with a mean improvement of 9.2mm (13%) over a decade primary, verified. Fibromyalgia pain is largely stable over 10+ years, so a substantial drift down at a fixed 20mg would be genuinely surprising and therefore informative.
  4. Pre-commit, or the test is worthless. Model A must specify how long and how much in advance reasoning, not evidence. Without a pre-registered threshold, any outcome reads as confirmation ("it's working, just slowly"), which is precisely how an unfalsifiable belief forms. Given Bergenheim's base rate, something like "≥30% pain reduction by month 4, with ≥2 survived exposures and 0 crashes" would do. Failing it must count.

One thing you already know, and what it implies

You report the acute overwhelm as worse while the crash is absent. That dissociation is itself informative: the drug is not damping sensory input, it is decoupling input from the downstream cascade reasoning, not evidence. That is coherent as a serotonergic effect at 20mg, where the drug is predominantly serotonergic.

And noradrenergic analgesia is a different mechanism from whatever is producing your crash benefit. Which means that on current evidence the crash effect and the pain effect are independent: observing one tells you very little about the other, and the crash benefit is therefore a weak argument for staying at 20mg on pain grounds. The crash benefit is worth having on its own terms. It should be valued as itself, not as a down payment on analgesia.

This is not the old framing and must not be read as it

You corrected an earlier version of this project that pitted the pain benefit against the psychological benefit and treated pain as expendable. You want both, you said so, and you were right to object. The narrower and more useful claim here is that the two benefits are real, they are separate, and your CFS hypothesis does not merge them into one.

Four variables are already in motion: duloxetine, melatonin, iron, and a possible dose change. Decide what to measure and how to tell the causes apart before changing anything.

What to raise at the appointment

Nothing here directs a dose. These are the things that are worth being able to say out loud.

  • That the pain response is a threshold, not a slope, so "a bit more" is not the relevant concept: 30mg was tested directly in fibromyalgia and failed the pain primary (P=0.202), so an intermediate step is unlikely to be an intermediate result.
  • That the honest fibromyalgia effect size is NNTB 11 (9–14) for at least half the pain gone, from the fibromyalgia-specific Cochrane review, on LOW-quality, largely manufacturer-sponsored evidence, against NNTH 18 (13–30) for quitting due to side effects.
  • That "no clinically relevant benefit" is a threshold classification that missed its cut-off by one point, and the same review found a clinically relevant benefit at the 30% pain threshold and on global impression.
  • That above 60mg the benefit is flat and the harms are not, so 120mg is a decision with a cost and no expected analgesic gain.
  • That your pain report is currently the sub-threshold signature, not evidence the drug is failing.
  • That the 20mg window is a one-time experiment, that you know what it would show, and what you would give up by closing it.
  • That fatigue and pain need to be tracked separately from now on, whichever way the dose goes, because that is the observation that separates the two models.
