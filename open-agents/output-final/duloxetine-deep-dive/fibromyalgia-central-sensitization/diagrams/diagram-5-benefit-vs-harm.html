The benefit-to-harm ratio, and a caution about how it gets quoted

A ratio is only meaningful when both numbers come from the same review, because the populations, the endpoint definitions and the drug mix all differ. Here are the two valid pairings, and the invalid one that produces the figure usually quoted.

PairingBenefitHarmRatioValid?
Within CD007115 (2014, duloxetine 60mg, mixed indication) NNTB 8 (4–21) NNTH 18 (13–30) ≈ 2.2 : 1 Yes
Within Welsch CD010292 (2018, SNRIs, fibromyalgia-specific) NNTB 11 (9–14) NNTH 14 ≈ 1.3 : 1 Yes
Welsch's benefit against CD007115's harm NNTB 11 NNTH 18 ≈ 1.6 : 1 No — mixes two reviews

Notice what this shows. The fibromyalgia-specific review is less favourable on both sides of the ledger: a smaller benefit and a closer margin over harm. Whichever valid pairing you take, the honest statement is that the benefit exceeds the harm, and not by a comfortable margin, and the more disease-matched the evidence gets, the narrower that margin becomes.

What the numbers are made of, which changes what they are worth

GRADE: LOWDowngraded for substantial dropouts across all trials and narrow, overwhelmingly female populations. Serious adverse events: VERY LOW.
Manufacturer-sponsoredCochrane, verbatim: "Eli Lilly, were the sponsors of all but one of the included studies."
Heavy attrition"Nearly all the studies had a dropout rate of more than 20%." Only four trials came in under 20%.

So: roughly 1 in 11 people get at least half their pain back that they would not have got on placebo, against roughly 1 in 14 who quit the drug from side effects, on low-quality evidence largely produced by the company that sells it. That is real. It is also not comfortable, and it is not the picture a prescribing conversation usually paints. Cochrane's own bottom line keeps the other half in view too: "A minority of people with fibromyalgia might experience substantial symptom relief."

And why 120mg is a different decision entirely

Above 60mg, the two curves stop moving together.

Benefit: flat Cochrane: "120 mg was no more effective than 60 mg" and "no dose effect exists from 60 mg to 120 mg." Lilly's own pooled analysis agrees: "no differences in efficacy outcomes between 60 mg/d or 120 mg/d."
Harm: rising Adverse events are dose-dependent above 60mg.

Flat benefit against rising harm means 120mg is a decision with a cost and no expected analgesic gain. primary, verified
