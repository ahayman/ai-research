Cortisol: He Is Not Backwards, the Question Is Malformed

The textbook answer is that he has it backwards, because ME/CFS is associated with mild hypocortisolism. The textbook answer does not survive its own meta-analysis. He asked "how much?" and the best available evidence says the answer is "the shape".

His theory: "excess cortisol in my body or a fight/flight mechanism that won't turn off."
Figure 9: Same amount, different shape Powell's meta-analysis, verbatim: "Meta-analyses revealed an attenuation of the CAR increase within CFS compared to controls (d = −.34) but no statistically significant differences between groups for other markers"; "total cortisol output (CAR or CP) was rarely associated with fatigue in any population"; "Outcomes reflecting within-day CHANGE in cortisol levels (CAR increase; DCS) may be the most relevant to fatigue experience." primary, verified The curves above are illustrative of that finding, not plotted from its data.
The four-part answer

1. Against the textbook ME/CFS claim, yes, he is backwards. The direction of the (weak) ME/CFS evidence is hypo, not hyper.

2. But the textbook claim does not survive its own meta-analysis on the measure he means. He is talking about how much cortisol is in his body, and Powell found no significant group difference on total cortisol output. "ME/CFS = low cortisol" is not a fact he is contradicting. It is a claim that failed when meta-analysed on the variable he named.

3. If he indexes to the insomnia phenotype instead, which his nap-failure actually resembles (Figure 5), he is arguably right in direction, since that literature reports elevated evening and first-half-of-night HPA output. But that finding is p = 0.07 in eleven people.

4. The deepest answer: both literatures say the absolute level is not the variable. Verdict: he is not backwards. He is asking a question that the evidence in both candidate literatures cannot answer, about a variable the best meta-analysis says is the wrong one. reasoning, not evidence — every premise is primary and verified

This chain's signature failure, appearing in the source literature A confident headline over a non-significant result. Vgontzas' title says "activation of the HPA axis" and its conclusion asserts "an overall increase of ACTH and cortisol secretion". ACTH separated at p=0.04. Cortisol did not, at p=0.07, in eleven people. This is the third documented instance of the same pattern in this project's source literature: it also appears in Maloney's allostatic-load abstract (OR 1.9, CI 0.75–4.75, concluded as "a high level of allostatic load") and in Maier & Seligman's "recovers within 3 days" (a figure Rozeske never measured, because the study tested 0h, 24h and 7d). It is a genre convention, not an accident, and this project should expect to find it in every literature it enters. primary, verified

The measurement is a diurnal salivary panel: waking, waking +30 minutes, and evening, so that the slope and the awakening response are visible rather than a single number the best meta-analysis says is the wrong one. That is a conversation with his doctor, not a conclusion. reasoning, not evidence

Powell's own caveat, carried

Verbatim: "Results should be considered with caution due to heterogeneity in one meta-analysis and the small number of studies." primary, verified The d = −0.34 is small, it is the only significant finding in the analysis, and it is not a diagnostic test.

Why this matters beyond cortisol

The same move appears throughout this page. A question can be well-motivated and still be pointed at the wrong variable. "How much cortisol?" and "how sleepy do I feel?" fail the same way: both return a number, and neither number is measuring the thing that matters. The fix in both cases is to change what gets measured, not to try harder at measuring it. reasoning, not evidence
