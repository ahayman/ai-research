What Actually Rises at His Dose Step

He is braced for insomnia. Insomnia does not rise across his step. Somnolence does not either, in the one trial that measured his dose. The row that moves is fatigue, which is his disability and his valued gain. But that rise does not reach significance, and saying otherwise would repeat this project's own signature failure.

Correction: a claim this project made, including on an earlier version of this page

The standing brief said "somnolence rises at the 20→60 step (2.6%→10.2%, 7.8%→14.3%)". Those are 30→60 steps, from trials with no 20mg arm. They cannot speak to 20→60. In the only trial that measured 20mg directly, somnolence across his step is 5.08% → 4.31%, which is flat (Fisher p = 1.00).

The row that moves across his step is fatigue, and it is the only monotonic rise in the table: 0% placebo, 0% at 20mg, 6.03% at 60mg, 8.93% at 120mg. That row was sitting in this project's own published table, under a spine written about somnolence. The data was not missing. It was printed, and unread. reasoning, not evidence

⚠ And now the correction has to be corrected, because the obvious next headline is not supportable

"Fatigue rises at his step" does not reach significance. 0/59 vs 7/116 gives Fisher exact two-sided p = 0.0969. The 20mg arm's 95% confidence interval runs 0% to 6.11%, and the 60mg arm's runs 2.95% to 11.93%. They overlap. The reason is simply that the 20mg arm is 59 people with zero events: by the rule of three alone, a rate of up to about 5.1% is compatible with observing zero.

Writing "fatigue rises at his step" as a headline would be a confident claim over a non-significant result, which is the exact failure this project has now documented three times in its source literature: Vgontzas' cortisol at p=0.07, Maloney's allostatic load at CI 0.75–4.75, and Maier & Seligman's unmeasured "3 days". Committing it here, in the same document that catalogues it, would make four. reasoning, not evidence

What survives, stated in the form the data supports

1. Insomnia does not rise across his step. 10.17% → 8.62%, p = 0.78. He is braced for the one thing that demonstrably does not happen at this step. primary, verified

2. Somnolence does not rise across his step either, in the only trial that measured it. 5.08% → 4.31%, p = 1.00. The 30→60 figures showing a somnolence rise are from different trials at a different step. primary, verified

3. Fatigue is the only one of the three that moves at his step at all, and it is the only one that separates from placebo at 60mg (0/117 vs 7/116, p = 0.0069). That much is solid: at 60mg, duloxetine causes more fatigue than placebo. primary, verified

4. Whether it rises specifically across 20→60 is underpowered and unresolved. p = 0.0969. Not "no". Not "yes". Unresolved, in 59 people. reasoning, not evidence

Three more limits on the fatigue row, at the claim

It is chronic low back pain, not ME/CFS and not fibromyalgia. Whatever "fatigue" means to a back-pain trial population, it is not what it means to him.

It is a spontaneously reported adverse-event term, not a fatigue measurement. Nobody administered a fatigue scale. The 0% placebo rate is itself the tell: fatigue is near-universal in chronic pain populations, so a 0/117 placebo rate means the term is only capturing fatigue notable enough that a patient volunteered it unprompted as a new problem. That cuts two ways: it makes the 6% at 60mg a higher bar than it looks (one in seventeen volunteered new fatigue against nobody on placebo), and it makes the whole row a blunt instrument.

And the 41% somnolence figure from the ME/CFS trial is a different kind of claim and must be held separately. It is one arm, n=29, at a flexible 60–120mg, unstratified. It says that when duloxetine is given to people with his illness at those doses, two in five report daytime sleepiness. It says nothing whatsoever about what happens going from 20 to 60. Conflating it with a dose-step claim is how the original error was made. primary, verified

The 30→60 somnolence figures, correctly labelled

These are real, and they are not his step and come from trials with no 20mg arm: NCT00849693 (MDD, children/adolescents) 2.6% → 10.2%; NCT00960986 (MDD, Korean adults) 0% → 6.8% and 7.8% → 14.3%. primary, verified

They support "somnolence is dose-dependent somewhere above 30mg". They do not support "somnolence rises when he goes from 20 to 60".

What the label does and does not say

Somnolence is dose-dependent per the FDA label, which lists both insomnia and somnolence among reactions with "a significant dose-dependent relationship in fixed-dose studies"; incidence 10% vs 4% (pooled MDD/GAD) and 12% vs 3% (pooled DPNP/OA/CLBP). The widely-quoted 1.5% is a DISCONTINUATION rate, understating incidence roughly seven-fold. primary, verified — DailyMed But "dose-dependent across the licensed range" is not the same claim as "rises at 20→60", and the trial data says the latter is where it does not.

The folk claim, killed

"Low dose sedating, high dose activating" is not supported. The actual finding is stranger: duloxetine causes insomnia AND somnolence in the same population at the same time. In the ME/CFS trial, 34% insomnia and 41% somnolence, simultaneously, in 29 people. These are not opposite ends of one dose axis. They are two effects happening to different people on the same drug. primary, verified

Where the inflection actually is

Across all three rows, the step that moves everything is 60→120, not 20→60: insomnia 8.62% → 18.75%, somnolence 4.31% → 12.50%, fatigue 6.03% → 8.93%. That converges with the label's own sentence that "there is no evidence that doses greater than 60 mg/day confer additional benefit, while some adverse reactions were observed to be dose-dependent." primary, verified

Why this row is the hard one for him specifically

His primary disability is fatigue. His valued gain is waking less tired. So the one row with any signal at his step is the row that lands on both, and it is also the row his own instrument can least read, because he cannot tell drug-fatigue from disease-fatigue by feel. That is the join to the tracker: the answer is not to rate it harder, it is to count events that do not require introspection. reasoning, not evidence

What this does NOT say

None of this directs a dose, and it is not an argument against going up. Insomnia falls at his step, somnolence is flat at his step, and the fatigue contrast is unresolved in 59 people. The usable conclusion is about measurement, not dose: the row worth watching is the one he would find hardest to see, and the way to see it is to have a baseline before the step rather than after it.
