The honest limit, and it matters more than the taxonomy does. The label may be less decision-relevant than it looks. If he can nap, functions better, and nothing intrudes, then whether a physiologist calls it "reduced tonic arousal" or "mild somnolence" does not change what he should do. The question that actually bears on the dose decision is not "which is it?" but "does it cost me anything?", and that is answered by the unintended-doze count, the night-sleep log, and whether hours disappear. Those are countable. The mechanism label is not. reasoning, not evidence

The seven-item journal

Nightly, 30 seconds: (1) minutes to fall asleep, (2) awakenings, (3) morning fatigue 0–10, (4) daytime sleepiness 0–10, scored separately from 3, (5) vivid dreams present/absent, (6) RLS at bedtime 0–10, (7) pain, scored independently. Plus the two counters above.

Why 3 and 4 must never merge

For an ME/CFS patient they feel similar and mean opposite things: fatigue is the disease, somnolence would be the drug. Collapsing them into one number makes the single most informative signal in this analysis unreadable. reasoning, not evidence — but it follows directly from the AE data

One instrument, two questions

Adding pain as an independent item makes the somnolence tracker and the test of his own CFS hypothesis the same instrument (see Figure 7). Baseline now, at 20mg, before the appointment. Without a baseline the dose test is uninterpretable.

What is not useful

Total sleep time from a consumer wearable. Wearables cannot measure sleep continuity or limb movements reliably. It would be measuring noise and giving it authority. reasoning, not evidence
