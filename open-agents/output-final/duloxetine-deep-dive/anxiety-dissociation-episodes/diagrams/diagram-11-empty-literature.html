The only named dose-transition syndrome, and why it is not yours

There is a described phenomenon at antidepressant dose transitions, and the sentence you would want exists almost verbatim. Arai 2023 calls jitteriness/anxiety syndrome “a recognized adverse effect observed during the initiation or change of dose in antidepressant treatment.” Then look at what the syndrome is made of. Every study that operationalises it uses the same eleven items:

  • anxiety
  • agitation
  • panic attacks
  • insomnia
  • irritability
  • hostility
  • aggressiveness
  • impulsivity
  • akathisia
  • hypomania
  • mania
  • dissociation
  • depersonalisation
  • derealisation

Every item on the list is over-arousal. Not one of them is yours. You reported the opposite pole: dissociated, unable to drive or work. The only described antidepressant dose-transition syndrome excludes your symptom from its definition, so it cannot be the thing that happened to you, and reaching for it anyway would be the same move this page has already had to retract once.

And the quality of even the adjacent evidence

Sinclair 2009 · PMID 19478285 · 107 articles reviewed
4% to 65%
The published incidence range for the syndrome that is described. The same review found no validated rating scales, no robust evidence that incidence differs between drug classes, and concluded that it “remains poorly characterised.” It set out to identify a time course as one of its nine stated aims and reports none. A range spanning sixteen-fold is not an estimate. It is a confession.
Harada 2014 · PMID 25419134 · n=301, prospective
Initiation only
The one prospective study that measured a time course found 7.0% incidence with onset at 7.3 ± 8.07 days. But enrolment required no antidepressant for at least a month beforehand, so it studied starting a drug and contains no dose-increase data at all. The “or change of dose” half of Arai's sentence is an unreferenced background assertion in a three-case report, and the prospective evidence underneath it measured the other half.
⚠ The trap this diagram exists to close

It is tempting to hand you “activation syndrome” as the name for what escitalopram did, because it is a real construct, it is about dose transitions, and it would feel like an answer. It is a category error, and it is the same one this page already made and retracted with non-fearful panic disorder: taking a superficially resonant label from an adjacent literature and giving it to a real person as a description of themselves, when the label's own definition excludes what they described.

Non-fearful panic disorder was absent fear and you have present anxiety. Activation syndrome is a syndrome of over-arousal and you reported under-arousal. Both labels rhyme with you and neither contains you.

So the honest answer to “is this a described thing?” is no. Not sparse. Not studied in the wrong population. Not weakly supported. Nobody has asked the question, so there is no prevalence figure, no time course, and no habituation data anywhere for the thing that cost you two weeks of your life. Your own history is, as far as this project could determine, the best-characterised instance of it in existence. That does not make your account of it wrong. It makes it the only data there is.
