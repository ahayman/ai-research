Two Objects, One Word

Your corrected escitalopram history separates two things this page previously ran together under the word “dissociation.” They behave differently, and the difference that matters is whether they habituate.

What actually happened on escitalopram

“I took Lexapro for almost 6 months. I went from 10 – 20mg over the course of a month, then stayed at 20mg for several months. Both the initial dose and the titration up caused me to lose a week each of functionality. It dissociated me so strongly I couldn't drive or work. But once that was done…the drug faded away and left me exactly where I started.”

No dissociation. No anxiolysis. “Exactly where I started.”

The two things the word has been covering

Your escitalopram dissociation Your own episodes
What set it off A change of dose Sensory overwhelm
How long it ran About a week One to two weeks
Did it habituate? Yes. It faded, and it did not come back across months at an unchanged dose. No. Two to four a month, for decades, and it never faded.
What travelled with it Nothing else. Not anxiolysis either. Severe anxiety, then intense depression, with no negative cognitive content
How many times Twice, both at transitions Hundreds
What ended it Staying on the same dose Nothing. It ran its course.

What the correction does to this page's arguments

Killed
“Inadequate trial” and “wrong dose.” Six months, titrated to a full 20mg, held there for months. No literature can override a dose-and-duration fact. That escape route is closed.
Strengthened
Zero anxiolysis. This is now a clean, fair negative rather than a trial that might not have been given a chance. Every candidate that has to explain the escitalopram failure has a harder job than before, not an easier one.
Forbidden
Treating the drug-induced state as a model of your episodes. One of them habituated in a week. The other did not habituate in twenty years. A shared word is not a shared object.

And the comparison that falls out of it

Your two drug initiationsSERT occupancy stepWhat happened
Escitalopram 0 → 10mg +69.2 points Dissociated. A week of function gone.
Duloxetine 0 → 20mg +71.7 points Mild floatiness. Functional throughout.
Matched within 2.5 occupancy points. Same person, one year apart. Opposite outcomes.
On the tightest available comparison the gap is smaller still: duloxetine 20mg puts thalamic occupancy at 71.3%, escitalopram 10mg at roughly 70%, and those two figures share a tracer, a region, a modality, a single-dose paradigm and a population. Whatever separated the two drugs in you, it was not how much serotonin transporter they occupied.
The escitalopram result is a stronger negative than this project has been treating it as, and it is a weaker analogy. Stronger, because six months at a full dose with nothing to show is a real finding about you rather than a trial that fell short. Weaker, because the state it produced faded on its own within a week and never returned, which is not what your episodes do, and because the two drugs delivered the same transporter engagement and did opposite things. So the escitalopram history has more to say about which explanations survive, and less to say about what your episodes are.

Provenance: the history and the outcomes are your own report, corrected by you and superseding every earlier document here; it is retrospective, unblinded, and about a year old. The occupancy figures are modelled — escitalopram fitted from Klein 2006 (PMID 16955282, single dose, midbrain, n=5 per arm) and duloxetine computed from Takano 2006's published ED50 of 7.9mg (PMID 16506079), both primary, verified. The tracer-matched thalamic pair is Takano 2006 against Arakawa 2016 (PMID 27082864): two laboratories, matched on every axis the method knows how to match on, and still two studies.
