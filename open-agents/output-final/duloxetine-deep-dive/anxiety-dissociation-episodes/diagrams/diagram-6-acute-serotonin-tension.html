Four Lines Pointing the Wrong Way

You asked for mechanism. What the adjacent evidence supplies instead is a problem, and it is the most uncomfortable thing in this deep dive. It is here in the middle of the page rather than filed at the bottom, because that is where this project's errors have always hidden.

The mechanism everyone reaches for first
duloxetine raises serotonin→ serotonin improves sensory gating→ better gating means less overwhelm→ less overwhelm means no episodes
A clean four-step mechanism. The only human evidence on the middle step says the opposite.
The four lines
Oranje 2011PMID 20971512
Escitalopram 15mg · n=20 healthy males · double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover
Verbatim: “Escitalopram significantly decreased P50 suppression and habituation, but had no effect on PPI.” Authors' conclusion: “habituation and sensory gating are disrupted by increased serotonergic activity.”
↓ raising serotonin BREAKS the filter
Jensen 2007PMID 17299503
Escitalopram 10mg · n=18 healthy males · same design, same lab
Escitalopram “significantly delay[ed] habituation of the ASR.” Conclusion: “increased serotonergic activity disrupts habituation.” Habituation is the exact function whose failure produces sensory overwhelm.
↓ raising serotonin SLOWS switching off
Simeon 1995PMID 8570768
m-CPP, a partial serotonin agonist · n=67 · double-blind, placebo-controlled challenge
“Challenge with m-CPP induced depersonalization significantly more than did placebo.” 18% experienced depersonalisation or derealisation.
↓ a serotonin agonist CAUSES dissociation
Maier & Watkins 2005PMID 15893820
The most influential animal model of stress-induced collapse there is
Verbatim: uncontrollable stressors “sensitize serotonergic neurons in the dorsal raphe… and the consequent exaggerated release of serotonin… is at least in part responsible for the behavioral changes that occur.”
↓ serotonin is the EFFECTOR of collapse, not the buffer
And this is not about a different drug class
Duloxetine is nine times more selective for serotonin than for noradrenaline, and at 20mg it sits high on the serotonergic occupancy curve and low on the noradrenergic one. So the four lines above are about the arm of duloxetine doing most of the work at your dose. Two of them use escitalopram, the drug that dissociated you for about a week at each of two dose changes. The tension is not academic. But the read-across has a limit, and it is your own data: your two initiations delivered matched transporter occupancy and produced opposite outcomes, so “both drugs are serotonergic, therefore an escitalopram result transfers” is a weak inference in you specifically.

The only account that reconciles it

The benefit would have to be the adaptation to serotonin, not the serotonin

The acute phase predictsDissociation, no anxiolysis, non-functional. Which is what escitalopram did to you for about a week at each dose change.
The chronic phase predictsEpisodes suppressed. Which is what duloxetine appears to be doing now.

✓ Two virtues

  • Explains the two-to-six week onset delay that every antidepressant shows, which is otherwise a standing embarrassment.
  • Makes a falsifiable prediction: raising the dose above the adapted set-point should transiently re-create the acute state. You ran that experiment twice on escitalopram, unknowingly, and it passed both times. Read the problems column before crediting it.

× Five problems that travel with it

  • ⚠ The virtue it used to lead with is dead, and your corrected history killed it. This account used to explain the escitalopram failure and the duloxetine response with one mechanism, by mapping escitalopram onto the acute phase. But escitalopram had a chronic phase: six months, at a full 20mg, held. The account says that is when the benefit arrives. Nothing arrived. It can only be rescued by making the adaptation drug-specific, which abandons the single mechanism and concedes that the drug difference is doing the work.
  • The confirmed prediction is worth very little. It was confirmed on the wrong drug, retrospectively, in one person who knew the dose had changed. Worse, it is not discriminating: activation folklore, the sensory-gating ladder and ordinary pharmacological intuition all predict that a dose increase transiently worsens things. A prediction its rivals share selects nothing when it comes true.
  • You are at two weeks, and two weeks is early for a chronic adaptation. The account is in tension with your own timeline. Flagged against its own interest.
  • The whole desensitisation family is under sustained attack. Its best-known version has been failing its tests for two decades.
  • Nobody has tested any of this in an autistic adult, in this phenotype, or with duloxetine. The receptor desensitisation timeline in humans is not established either.
Grade: live but wounded, and more wounded than it was. Reasoning, not a mechanism, not evidence.

The limits on the four lines, stated at the claim rather than below it

  1. m-CPP is an agonist; duloxetine is a reuptake inhibitor. An agonist slams a receptor. A reuptake inhibitor raises ambient transmitter and then the system adapts. Not the same pharmacology.
  2. The m-CPP depersonalisation tracked panic, not serotonin per se. The authors report a significant correlation with panic “but not nervousness, anxiety, sadness, depression, or drowsiness.” m-CPP is a reliable panicogen, so the serotonergic step may be upstream of a non-specific route.
  3. The gating studies are n=18 and n=20 healthy males, single acute doses, laboratory startle and EEG paradigms. The leap from a P50 waveform to a crowded festival is enormous and unbridged.
  4. There is a complication inside the same lab. Jensen 2008 (PMID 18000656) at 10mg found P50 suppression intact; Oranje 2011 at 15mg found it disrupted. A 1.5× step flipped the filter. That is a dose ladder rather than a flat claim, and it is a cross-study comparison rather than a within-study dose-response, which is this project's most repeated error. Flagged rather than buried.
  5. All four gating studies come from one group. No independent replication exists. The one independent lab that touched the question ran the opposite manipulation: Mann 2008 (PMID 17895917) found serotonin depletion disrupted PPI but not P50. That is the mirror image, and it refutes any simple monotonic story. An inverted-U with different measures on each limb is the more honest reading.
  6. Maier & Watkins is rats and learned helplessness. Not autism, not sensory overwhelm, not duloxetine, and the model's construct validity for human depression is itself contested.
  7. Nobody has measured whether the acute gating cost persists chronically. Every relevant study is a single acute dose in a drug-naive healthy volunteer. So “am I permanently trading acute sensitivity for aftermath protection?” is not weakly answered. It is unanswered.

Provenance: all four lines are primary, verified (abstracts retrieved; quotations verbatim). The Ki values are primary, verified. The chronic-adaptation account is reasoning, not evidence, and is labelled as a hypothesis because it is one. Your escitalopram history, including the six months at a full dose that breaks the account's leading virtue, is your own report, retrospective and unblinded. Whether any of this applies to you: no evidence exists.
