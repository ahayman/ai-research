Of everything duloxetine was prescribed to do for you, exactly one thing has been tested at 20mg and passed. It is not the pain. It is the anxiety. That is the least hedged sentence in this entire deep dive, so it is worth being precise about what it does and does not buy you.

The trial is Nicolini 2009 primary, verified, PMID 18485261. It enrolled 581 adults with generalised anxiety disorder across four arms: placebo with 170 people, duloxetine 20mg as a genuinely fixed dose with 84, duloxetine 60 to 120mg flexible with 158, and venlafaxine XR with 169. Ten weeks, multi-centre, double-blind. The 20mg arm was not a titration step and it was not a waypoint on the way somewhere else. It existed as a fixed dose for the whole trial, because European regulators asked for it. On the HAM-A total score, that arm separated from placebo at p=0.01 to 0.001. On the HAM-A psychic factor, it separated again, at the same significance. On the HAM-A somatic factor, it did not, at p=0.07 primary, verified, and the abstract is explicit and unambiguous about all three.

This is the only place in duloxetine's entire record where your exact dose has direct, positive, randomised evidence for anything. Low-dose duloxetine has been tested for pain three times, in roughly 350 patients, across two indications, and failed on pain every time, including twice with adequate power primary, verified. There is no other fixed 20mg arm anywhere in the anxiety literature, and none in depression either. One arm, 84 people, one trial, ten weeks, never replicated.

It is also worth resisting the story this invites. The temptation is to read "20mg works for anxiety" as "20mg is the sweet spot, and more would spoil it." Nicolini says the opposite. The higher-dose arm did slightly better, not worse: HAM-A fell 15.3 points versus 14.7, response was 65% versus 60%, remission 44% versus 42% secondary — unverified, because these point estimates come from a review rather than the primary paper, which is paywalled. Every one of those comparisons points the same direction, and it is not the direction that flatters your current dose. So the honest reading is not "20mg is best." It is "20mg cleared the bar, and 60mg cleared it by a little more."

The question nobody asked

Now the caveat that decides how much weight any of this can carry, and it is the reason a whole page is needed rather than a sentence. Non-inferiority of 20mg to 60mg was never tested. Not badly tested, not underpowered, not inconclusive. Never asked.

The proof is arithmetic, and it is exact. Duloxetine ran two nearly identical anxiety registration trials: Hartford 2007 primary, verified, PMID 17414743, with 161 on placebo, 162 on duloxetine 60 to 120mg, and 164 on venlafaxine, and Nicolini 2009, with 170, 158 and 169 in the same three arms plus the 84 on 20mg. Allgulander 2008 primary, verified, PMID 18635722, then pooled the two by prespecified plan to run the formal non-inferiority comparison, and reported 331 on placebo, 320 on duloxetine, 333 on venlafaxine. Add Hartford's arms to Nicolini's and you get 331, 320 and 333. Every arm reconciles to the digit. The 84 people on 20mg appear in none of the totals. The non-inferiority analysis pooled the 60 to 120mg arms and excluded the 20mg arm entirely primary, verified, because the reconciliation is exact and the design is stated in the abstract.

The consequence is a distinction you should carry into the appointment, because it is the kind of thing that gets flattened in a ten-minute conversation. "Beat placebo" and "as good as 60mg" are different claims, and only the first was ever asked. With 84 people against 158, and no comparison performed, "20mg is roughly as good as 60mg for anxiety" is not a finding that was made. It is a gap that has been mistaken for one.

Two more things belong here rather than in the footnotes, because they cut against the reading you might prefer. The first is that the placebo hypothesis does not collapse. Nicolini's placebo arm dropped 11.6 HAM-A points, with a 42% response rate and 20% remission secondary — unverified. The drug adds roughly three points and eighteen percentage points on top of a large placebo effect. That is the ordinary shape of anxiety trial data, and an earlier pass of this project claimed that the 20mg evidence made the placebo explanation for your improvement collapse. It does not. What collapses is only the narrower claim that a 20mg anxiolytic benefit is pharmacologically unsupported. Those are different things, and running them together is how you end up more confident than the evidence permits.

The second is that the meta-analysis, which is the best evidence duloxetine has for anxiety, cannot help you at your dose. Li 2018 primary, verified, PMID 29558528, pooled 8 randomised trials and 2,399 participants and found a response odds ratio of 2.22, with a confidence interval of 1.88 to 2.62, and a remission odds ratio of 1.99, interval 1.66 to 2.39. It is independently funded by the National Natural Science Foundation of China with no competing interests declared, which makes it a rarity in a literature where Eli Lilly sponsored almost everything. But it ran no dose-stratified analysis primary, verified. So it establishes that duloxetine treats generalised anxiety disorder. It does not establish that 20mg does. Nicolini remains the only thing in the world that speaks to your dose, and it is one Lilly trial in which six of the nine authors were Lilly employees or shareholders primary, verified.

The guardrail, stated before you read another word about factor scores

Li 2018 found something else, and it is robust: duloxetine improves the HAM-A psychic anxiety factor and produces no significant difference on the HAM-A somatic factor, across all 8 trials, all doses, 2,399 patients primary, verified. Nicolini's 20mg arm shows the identical split. This is not a 20mg weakness. It is a dose-independent property of the drug, replicated across the whole literature.

It is not about you, and it must not be handed to you as if it were.

The HAM-A "somatic factor" is a list of physical symptom items: muscular tension, sensory symptoms, cardiovascular symptoms, respiratory symptoms, gastrointestinal symptoms, genitourinary symptoms, autonomic symptoms. Palpitations. Sweating. Churning stomach. The HAM-A "psychic factor" is anxious mood, tension, fears, insomnia, cognitive symptoms, depressed mood, and observed behaviour at interview.

Your phenomenology is present anxiety with absent negative thoughts. That is not "somatic anxiety." It is not the somatic axis at all. The HAM-A split is about where the symptom shows up in the body. Your description is about whether the state has propositional content. Those are two different axes, so a drug that moves one of them tells you nothing about the other. The words rhyme; the constructs do not.

This matters more than a definitional quibble, because this project already made exactly this mistake once. An earlier pass found "non-fearful panic disorder" in the literature, noticed that it sounded like your description, and handed it to you as a name for what you have. It was wrong, and it had to be retracted. Grabbing a superficially resonant label from an unrelated measurement literature and giving it to a real person as a description of themselves is a specific failure mode, and mapping you onto the HAM-A somatic factor would be the same failure with a different label. Nobody has measured whether duloxetine's psychic/somatic split has anything to do with your episodes no evidence exists, and the honest answer is that there is no reason to think it does.

What your episodes actually are, and why twenty years of therapy missed them

Here is the fact that carries more diagnostic weight than anything else in this document: your episodes have no negative cognitive content. No negative thoughts. The anxiety is present and severe; the thinking that is supposed to accompany it is absent.

Two consequences follow from that, and both of them are things that already happened to you.

The first is that multiple therapists diagnosed PTSD or cPTSD and never found an index trauma. That is usually read as a failure of the search, and the conclusion drawn is that the trauma is there and buried. Consider the other reading. A trauma diagnosis predicts a memory with content: a proposition, an image, a narrative that the state is about. The search kept coming up empty because the state may not have the shape a trauma memory has. It is not that the content is hidden. It may be that there is no content. Those two situations look identical from the outside and they are completely different problems, and only one of them has a trauma in it reasoning, not evidence.

The second is that cognitive therapies never touched it, and the reason is mechanical rather than a matter of fit or effort or therapeutic alliance. CBT's active ingredient is restructuring a thought. You identify the automatic thought, you test it against evidence, you replace it with a more accurate one, and the feeling follows the thought down. Every step in that chain requires a thought to be there. If the state arrives without cognitive content, then the intervention's working end has nothing to make contact with. It is not that cognitive restructuring was applied poorly. It is that there was no proposition for it to restructure reasoning, not evidence.

That argument is worth keeping, and it is worth being clear about what it is not resting on. An earlier pass supported it by identifying your presentation as non-fearful panic disorder. That diagnostic claim is dead, and it died for three independent reasons, any one of which is sufficient. Panic attacks peak within minutes, and DSM-5 defines them that way; your episodes last one to two weeks, which is a thousand-fold difference in timescale and a different class of event entirely. Non-fearful panic disorder is the absence of fear with somatic panic symptoms present; you report the presence of severe anxiety with the absence of cognitions, and "no fear" and "no negative thoughts" are not the same absence, because you have the affect and those patients do not. And it is defined and measured in cardiology and emergency-department chest-pain presenters, where the correct figure is 32 to 41% of panic disorder patients seeking treatment for chest pain primary, verified, from Fleet 2000, PMID 10906353, a population with no established transfer to you.

Drop the label. Keep the argument. The CBT-mismatch insight never needed non-fearful panic disorder to work, because it follows directly from your own report rather than from anyone else's diagnostic category. If there is no negative cognition, cognitive restructuring has nothing to restructure. That conclusion stands on its own, and it is probably the most immediately useful paragraph in this deep dive, because it retires an explanation you have likely been carrying for years, which is that the therapies failed because you failed at them.

The decoupling, and the tension underneath it

The central observation on duloxetine is not that your episodes stopped. It is stranger and more informative than that.

You report the acute sensory overwhelm as worse on the drug, and the one-to-two-week crash that normally follows as absent. The verified instance is five-plus hours at two crowded festivals, producing two panic attacks with heart rate above 140 bpm at rest. Historically that is a guaranteed crash trigger. There was no crash, and the absence was independently anticipated by your family, who braced for it and watched it not arrive.

Two effects moving in opposite directions, same drug, same person, same day. That shape is doing real work, because it rules things out. It rules out sedation, blunting, and global muting, all of which would move both effects the same way. It also rules out the simplest expectancy account, because expectancy predicts the acute overwhelm would feel better, and it was worse. And a family member's independent expectation of a crash that did not come is not a placebo response in you.

Now the part that is genuinely uncomfortable, and it belongs here rather than in a caveat box. Four verified lines of evidence say that acute serotonin makes your exact problem worse.

Escitalopram at 15mg significantly decreased P50 sensory gating and habituation in healthy volunteers, double-blind and placebo-controlled primary, verified, PMID 20971512. Escitalopram at 10mg significantly delayed habituation of the startle response in the same lab's earlier study primary, verified, PMID 17299503. Habituation is the exact function whose failure produces sensory overwhelm, and the drug in both studies is the one that made you dissociate and go non-functional. Separately, the serotonin agonist m-CPP induced depersonalisation significantly more than placebo in a 67-subject challenge study, with 18% experiencing depersonalisation or derealisation primary, verified, PMID 8570768. And in Maier and Watkins' work on stressor controllability, which is the most influential animal model of stress-induced collapse there is, the mechanism runs through serotonin as the effector: uncontrollable stress sensitises the dorsal raphe, the sensitised raphe releases exaggerated serotonin in response to subsequent input, and that exaggerated release is what produces the behavioural collapse primary, verified, PMID 15893820. The intuitive question was whether serotonin sets a stress-resilience threshold. There is a real, well-developed story about serotonin and stress, and it runs the other way: serotonin is closer to the thing that causes the collapse than the thing that prevents it.

Duloxetine is also serotonergic, and at 20mg it is predominantly serotonergic. Its serotonin transporter affinity is nine times its noradrenaline transporter affinity primary, verified, PMID 11750180, and your dose sits high on the serotonergic occupancy curve and low on the noradrenergic one. So the four lines are not about a different drug class. They are about the arm of duloxetine that is doing most of the work at your dose.

There is exactly one account that reconciles your experience with that evidence, and it is reasoning rather than a finding. If acute serotonin elevation is anxiogenic, dissociogenic and gating-disrupting, then the therapeutic effect cannot be the serotonin. It would have to be the adaptation to the serotonin: a receptor-level change that runs opposite to the drug's acute pharmacology reasoning, not evidence. That account has genuine virtues. It explains your escitalopram failure and your duloxetine response with one mechanism rather than two. It explains why every antidepressant takes weeks rather than hours, which is otherwise a standing embarrassment. And it makes a falsifiable prediction, which is that raising the dose above the adapted set-point should transiently re-create the acute state.

It also has a problem that must travel with it, and it is flagged here against the account's own interest: you are at two weeks, and two weeks is early for a chronic adaptation. The account is in tension with your own timeline. Beyond that, the entire family of "receptors adapt over weeks and that adaptation is the therapeutic mechanism" explanations is under sustained attack, because the best-known version of it, built on 5-HT1A autoreceptors, has been failing its tests for two decades secondary — unverified. Holding the version built on 5-HT2C loosely is the appropriate response.

And underneath all of it sits the flattest fact available: nobody has studied this decoupling in any human, for any drug no evidence exists. Not weakly studied. Not studied in the wrong population. The human evidence explains the acute half. The animal evidence explains the aftermath half. Nothing has both. Your observation is, as far as this project could determine, unreported. That does not make it wrong. It makes it data.

Does it keep working, and how would you know

You asked about durability directly, so here is the number everyone quotes and what it actually is.

The famous figure is that roughly 25% of people on an antidepressant experience "poop-out." It comes from Solomon 2005 primary, verified, PMID 15766292, and the provenance is worth seeing in full. It is observational, not randomised. The cohort is the NIMH Collaborative Depression Study, enrolled between 1978 and 1981. The drugs are tricyclics and MAOIs, because that is what people took in 1980. And the operational definition of tachyphylaxis is recurrence of depression while on maintenance pharmacotherapy, which in an uncontrolled observational cohort cannot distinguish tachyphylaxis from the natural course of a recurring illness, from non-adherence, or from a drug that never worked in the first place reasoning, not evidence, though the reasoning follows directly from the design. Solomon 2005 does not measure how often antidepressants stop working. It measures how often depression comes back in people who are taking one.

The other figure in circulation is a range of 9 to 57% primary, verified. A quantity whose credible range spans six-fold is not an estimate. It is a confession that the field has no measurement instrument.

The most informative single result points somewhere unexpected. Zimmerman and Thongy primary, verified, PMID 17854252, found relapse during continuation in 24.1% of placebo responders versus 7.4% of drug responders. Read that carefully, because it inverts the usual story. If people who responded to a dummy pill lose their response three times as often as people who responded to the drug, then most of what gets called "poop-out" is placebo-response decay, and the phenomenon is real as an experience and largely illusory as a pharmacology. Note also that 24.1% sits remarkably close to Solomon's famous 25%.

The best number for you, and the one that survives the argument that antidepressant relapse trials are contaminated by withdrawal effects in the placebo arm, is this: people who stayed on duloxetine relapsed at 13.7% by 26 weeks in generalised anxiety disorder primary, verified, PMID 18559291, and 14.4% by 52 weeks in depression primary, verified, PMID 19552867. The withdrawal critique attacks the placebo arms of those trials, because those people were taken off a drug. It does not touch the duloxetine arms, because those people were not taken off anything.

That figure of roughly 14% is an upper bound on tachyphylaxis, not a measure of it. It bundles genuine loss of drug effect together with natural illness course breaking through a partially effective drug and with people who stopped taking their tablets. Tachyphylaxis is at most 14% and probably well under it, because that number is everyone who relapsed for any reason at all.

Two absences complete the picture and neither should be softened. No placebo-controlled duloxetine data exists past 12 months, on anyone, for any indication no evidence exists. And duloxetine-specific tachyphylaxis has never been studied no evidence exists. Not "studied and found reassuring." Never measured.

The denominator is exposures, not weeks

The last piece is the one that turns your own knowledge of yourself into a study design, and it follows from something you already know.

Your episodes are not spontaneous. They follow sensory overwhelm. That single fact changes what counts as evidence, because it means the thing being tested is not the passage of time. It is the trigger.

Start with the time-based version, so you can see why it is weak. Treating episodes as a random process at your reported baseline of two to four per month, the probability of seeing zero episodes purely by chance is 13 to 37% at two weeks reasoning, not evidence, from standard Poisson arithmetic that can be checked. That is where you are now. Between one in three and one in seven people whose drug does absolutely nothing would be sitting exactly where you are sitting, seeing exactly what you are seeing, and drawing exactly your conclusion. This is not pessimism. It is the reason not to treat two weeks as informative. The odds fall below 5% at about six weeks and below one in four hundred by three months, so the chance explanation dies somewhere between six weeks and three months.

But notice precisely what that kills. The passage of time kills random fluctuation. It does not touch placebo, regression to the mean, or natural course, because those are not chance. They are systematic effects that produce the identical zero-episode observation. A three-month clean run proves the improvement is real. It says nothing about whether the drug caused it. Those are different questions and waiting only answers the first.

The trigger-conditional version answers the second. If p₀ is your baseline probability of an episode given a significant sensory overload, then surviving k comparable exposures with no episode has probability (1 − p₀)k under the null. If the trigger was near-deterministic for you, two clean exposures is already p<0.05. At p₀ = 0.8 it takes three. At p₀ = 0.5 it takes five. Three to five comparable overloads survived cleanly is roughly a p<0.05 result reasoning, not evidence, and it is a result about the thing that actually matters.

The reason this test is better rather than merely faster is that regression to the mean cannot fake it. Regression explains why a bad stretch is followed by a better stretch. It does not explain why a specific, repeated, previously reliable trigger stopped producing its effect. Two quiet months spent working from home and avoiding crowds is worth almost nothing, because the drug was never tested. One festival day with no crash afterwards is worth more than two uneventful months.

You have one. The festivals were a real exposure and it was survived clean. One is not three to five, and one data point does not settle anything. But it is the highest-value observation you have produced, it is worth strictly more than the two weeks of calendar time it sits inside, and it argues for recording exposures prospectively from now on rather than trying to reconstruct them later. The limits are worth stating at the claim: it requires you to characterise p₀ from memory, which is recall-biased; it requires exposures to be genuinely comparable, which is a judgement call; and deliberately seeking out overload to test a drug carries a real cost and a real risk, so nothing here is suggesting you go looking. The point is that when overloads happen anyway, those are the informative days.

So what you have, assembled, is one real trial at your exact dose that asked the smaller of the two available questions and never asked the larger one; a robust and replicated property of the drug's anxiolytic effect that is not about you and must not be handed to you as a description of yourself; a phenomenology with no cognitive content that plausibly explains two decades of therapeutic near-misses and that nobody has ever studied pharmacologically; a benefit that, if it is real and serotonergic, has to be running against the acute pharmacology of the drug producing it, on a timeline that does not comfortably fit; a durability estimate that is an upper bound on a phenomenon that may not exist as pharmacology at all; and one data point out of the three to five that would make the case. None of that tells you what to do about the dose, and it is not supposed to. What it does is let you walk into the appointment able to say precisely which of your questions the evidence has answered, which it has only appeared to answer, and which nobody has ever asked.

The numbers everyone quotes are broken, and the claim survives anyway

These estimates come from a secondary review rather than the primary paper. The same review reports the changes as "45%" and "55%" decreases respectively, and those percentages are internally inconsistent with the absolute values and with each other. If −14.7 is a 45% decrease then baseline HAM-A was 32.7, and if −15.3 is a 55% decrease then baseline was 27.8. Anxiety registration trials typically enrol at baseline 24 to 26, so both implied baselines are too high and they disagree with one another.

The percentages are not presented here as fact and should not be quoted. The substantive claim survives without them, because on the absolute change, on response, and on remission, all three comparisons independently put the higher dose slightly ahead.

The guardrail, formally

"HAM-A somatic anxiety" is NOT the same construct as "anxiety without negative cognitive content." Do not map them.

The HAM-A somatic factor sorts anxiety symptoms by where in the body they appear. Your description sorts your state by whether it has propositional content. These are orthogonal. A person can have florid somatic anxiety with rich negative cognition, or none of either, or either one without the other. The factor structure of a rating scale designed in 1959 has nothing to say about the second axis, because it does not measure it.

Why this specific error matters here: this project's validation pass caught exactly this failure once already, with non-fearful panic disorder. The error's shape is to take a superficially resonant label from an unrelated measurement literature and hand it to a real person as a description of themselves.   3. The gating studies are 18 and 20 healthy males, single acute doses, laboratory startle and EEG paradigms. The leap from a P50 waveform to a crowded festival is enormous and unbridged reasoning, not evidence.

The only account that reconciles it, and why it is held loosely reasoning, not evidence. If acute serotonin elevation is anxiogenic, dissociogenic and gating-disrupting, and chronic treatment produces adaptation at the relevant receptors, then the acute phase should look like your escitalopram experience, meaning dissociation, no anxiolysis, non-functional; the chronic phase should look like your current experience, meaning episodes suppressed; and the therapeutic effect is not "more serotonin" but the adaptation to more serotonin, a receptor-level change running opposite to the drug's acute pharmacology.

It has three virtues: it explains the escitalopram failure and the duloxetine response with one mechanism; it explains the two-to-six-week onset delay every antidepressant shows; and it makes a falsifiable prediction, which is that a dose increase should re-create the acute state. It has four problems, and they travel with it. The desensitisation timeline in humans is not established secondary — unverified. You are at two weeks, which is early for a chronic adaptation, so the account is in tension with your own timeline reasoning, not evidence, and that is flagged against the account's own interest. The whole desensitisation family is under sustained attack, because the best-known version built on 5-HT1A autoreceptors has failed repeatedly: molecular imaging and postmortem work found no consistent 5-HT1A alterations in depression; 5-HT1A antagonists failed to produce consistent antidepressant efficacy, which the theory predicts they should; and firing rate normalises despite ongoing inhibition rather than because inhibition was removed secondary — unverified. Those critiques are specific to 5-HT1A and do not automatically transfer to a 5-HT2C version, but they should induce real humility about the entire family. And nobody has tested any of this in an autistic adult, in this phenotype, or with duloxetine no evidence exists.

Grade: the best available reasoning. Not a mechanism. Not evidence.

There is also one human study pointing the other way on the adaptation half, and it belongs here. And the crucial qualifier, which is where most n-of-1 reasoning goes wrong: the math kills random fluctuation. It does not touch placebo, regression to the mean, or natural course, because those are not chance. They are systematic effects producing the identical zero-episode observation. A three-month clean run proves the improvement is real. It says nothing about whether the drug caused it.

The trigger-conditional test, which is the good one reasoning, not evidence. This is a study-design argument, not a finding. Your episodes are triggered, so the denominator is exposures rather than time.   • "All changes statistically significant vs placebo" for Nicolini's 20mg arm. False; the somatic factor was p=0.07.
  • "Near-parity with 60–120mg." No comparison was ever performed.
  • The placebo hypothesis "collapses." It does not. Nicolini's placebo arm dropped 11.6 HAM-A points with 42% response.
  • "7 studies / 2,674 participants / RR 1.48 / OR 1.88" as attributed to Li 2018. All four figures wrong. Actual: 8 / 2,399 / OR 2.22 / OR 1.99.
  • The BNST sustained-threat duration story. Timescale off by roughly 1000×.
  • The propranolol / PTSD-prophylaxis parallel. An anti-parallel: propranolol blunts acute reactivity; yours rose above 140 bpm.
  • "HAM-A −14.7 (45%) vs −15.3 (55%)." The absolute values are usable as secondary-source figures. The percentages are internally inconsistent and must not be quoted.
