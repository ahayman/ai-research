What Nobody Has Studied
These are not gaps in the search. They are the state of the field, and they are this project's most honest output. An absence of evidence, correctly identified, is a finding.
Absence 4 · the big one
Nobody has studied this decoupling in any human, for any drug.
A drug that makes acute reactivity worse while preventing a delayed sequela 1–2 weeks later is not a described phenomenon anywhere in the human literature. The human evidence explains the acute half. The animal evidence explains the aftermath half. Nothing has both. This is why the answer is a composite of three literatures rather than a mechanism.
The reason there is no winner
Absence 1
Nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults.
The entire literature is one 3-patient case series. Every statement in this deep dive about how the drug behaves in him is an extrapolation from populations that excluded him.
Absence 2
Nobody has studied the overwhelm-to-crash cycle pharmacologically.
The cycle is described in a primary clinical consensus document, using his own word: “Overload phenomena may trigger a 'crash'.” That it is described is real. That any drug has ever been tested against it is not.
Absence 3
Nobody has studied whether prior dissociation predicts recurrence.
He had a dissociative reaction to escitalopram. Whether that predicts anything at all about a future serotonergic drug is unknown, in either direction. It is a reasonable question with no literature behind it.
Absences 5 and 6 · added by the final research pass
Nobody has measured what duloxetine does to daytime sleep propensity, and nobody has recorded a locus coeruleus neuron on chronic duloxetine in an awake animal.
No MSLT study of duloxetine, or of any SNRI, exists at any dose in any population. And the only chronic-duloxetine locus coeruleus recording is anaesthetised, in a neuropathic pain model. Both bear directly on the nap question.
Absence 7 · added after the reader corrected his history
Nobody has studied antidepressant-induced dissociation.
His escitalopram dissociation tracked dose changes and faded. That specific phenomenon has no literature: narrowed to induced dissociation, four papers exist and none is on topic. There is a named dose-transition syndrome, but its items are all over-arousal, and dissociation is not among them. Reaching for it would repeat a category error the deep dive already had to retract.
What these absences do and do not license. They do not license despair, and they do not license credulity either. An absence means the honest confidence ceiling is “possibly valid,” which is exactly where the reader placed it before the research did. It also means that a null average from an adjacent population is weak evidence about him specifically, given an 8.5-fold spread in individual EC50 on the same drug at the same dose. What they rule out is anyone, including this deep dive, telling him what is happening to him.
The most valuable true thing this chain can tell him is that the obvious mechanism is contradicted by the only evidence that bears on it, and the most attractive analogies are false. Every research pass found real errors in the last, and most failures were misreadings of a real paper or a real paper wrongly discarded. Every PMID in the corpus resolves; no citation is fabricated. Review did catch two invented details of a different kind, though, before they reached him: a quotation with words inserted, and a taper step he never took.
The binding brief named four absences. Two more were added by the research passes, and a seventh after the reader corrected his own history. Category 4 carries all seven with their search-level provenance.
