How the Blunting Claim Collapsed

This one runs against caution, which is exactly why it needs saying. A validation pass in this project called emotional blunting's dose-dependence "one of the strongest arguments against a dose increase." Traced link by link, it does not hold.

The claim as it entered this project

"Emotional blunting is dose-dependent, therefore a dose increase raises the risk, therefore this is an argument against going up."
Stated as established fact by two separate passes. Amplified by a third, which said "it should carry more weight, not less."

Ma H, Cai M, Wang H (2021)

Front Psychiatry 12:792960 · self-described "brief non-systematic" review
"Overall, the occurrence of emotion blunting seems to be dose-related, with lower doses being less likely to induce this symptom." [ref 25]

Masdrakis, Markianos & Baldwin (2023)

Acta Neuropsychiatr · PMID 36644883 · apathy review
Apathy "appears dose-dependent and reversible" — citing only case reports. Concedes: "Most studies are either case reports or internet/telephone surveys."
↓        ↓

Reference [25] is NOT a dose study

Price J, Cole V, Goodwin GM (2009), Br J Psychiatry 195:211–7 · PMID 19721109 · qualitative interview study
Design: 38 individual interviews, 8 in a group interview, 11 validation interviews, 272 web forum posts. Its only dose-relevant text: "Some participants made specific reference to changes in their emotional experiences in relation to changes in medication type or dose."

No dose-response analysis was performed. Dose was never systematically measured or quantitatively analysed.

Hoehn-Saric R, Lipsey JR, McLeod DR (1990)

J Clin Psychopharmacol 10:343–5 · a single case report
One patient, who "noticed a partial improvement in her symptoms when taking fluoxetine 20mg 4 days per week." This is the root. Both reviews trace back here.
↖   Both reviews converge on the same 1990 case report   ↗
Two reviews asserting the same thing from the same source is ONE piece of evidence, not two.
This is textbook citation laundering: repetition across reviews manufacturing the appearance of consensus. The original research and the validator in this project both consumed the laundered version.

And the two reviews contradict each other: Masdrakis says apathy emerges "independently of treatment outcome"; Aşçibaşi (below) found blunting tracked remission. Both cannot be right.

No dose-response study exists — and the closest test points the other way

Searched across OQESA studies, ELEVATE/vortioxetine, PREDDICT, Goodwin, Price, Opbroek 2002, Sansone & Sansone, Fagiolini, and the Jawad scoping review. No dose-stratified or dose-response analysis of antidepressant-induced emotional blunting exists.

Aşçibaşi K, et al. (2020), J Clin Psychopharmacol, PMID 33065720, n=98 MDD. Doses were systematically escalated: "the antidepressant dose was increased in patients with less than a 50% reduction at each visit." OQESA measured at weeks 8 and 16.

↑Doses were RAISED
↓Blunting scores FELL
P < 0.001, t = 5.73

"As the rate of remitted patients is increased, OQESA scores get decreased."

In the one cohort where doses were systematically raised, blunting went DOWN, tracking remission rather than dose. This is confounded with time and remission, so it does not refute dose-dependence. But it is the closest thing to a test that exists, and it does not support it. Compounding it: Goodwin 2017 found blunting correlated with depression severity, raising the live possibility that blunting substantially is a symptom of depression rather than a dose effect — which would invert the clinical logic entirely. [primary, verified]

The verdict

The honest position is not "blunting is dose-dependent, so don't go up."

It is: nobody has ever measured whether blunting is dose-dependent.
That is a reason for monitoring, not for confident prediction in either direction.

A caution built on an untested premise is still an untested premise, and it is not made sound by pointing in the safe direction.

What survives — and it is not nothing

Common46% in Goodwin GM, Price J, De Bodinat C, Laredo J (2017), J Affect Disord 221:31–35, PMID 28628765: 669 depressed patients on treatment plus 150 recovered controls. Broader literature range for blunting: 40–60%. [primary, verified]
ReversibleDose reduction is the standard management, consistently supported by case reports. The clinical practice is real and widespread, which is at least indirect evidence that some dose relationship is genuine. [secondary — unverified]
CaveatsInternet survey, self-selected, cross-sectional, no dose data, confounded by depression itself (blunting is also a symptom of depression). Not a number to bet on. It is the best that exists.
Construct noteThe "20–92%" range circulating in this project's documents is a different construct — it refers to apathy specifically with SSRIs, from a different review whose overall antidepressant-induced apathy range is 5.8–50%. Merging blunting and apathy figures is a construct slip. Do not repeat it.
And the finding that actually matters for you is Goodwin's, and it is not comforting. Verbatim: "There was no difference according to antidepressant agent, though it appeared less frequent with bupropion." SSRIs run 43–47%; bupropion 33% is the lowest. On blunting, duloxetine ≈ escitalopram, at roughly 46% either way. The implicit model — that duloxetine is a different kind of drug from escitalopram and therefore safer on this axis — does not hold here. Whatever made duloxetine different for you, it is not that duloxetine is a low-blunting drug.

Nothing here directs a dose. It is what to understand and what to raise.
