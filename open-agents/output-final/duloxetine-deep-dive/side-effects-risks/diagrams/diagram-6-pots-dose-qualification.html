POTS: Real, Dose-Qualified, and Direction Unknown

This item flip-flopped twice inside this project, so the provenance is stated in full. The mechanism is real. The evidence for it sits four to twelve times above your dose. And for you specifically, the direction of the effect is genuinely unknown, because NET inhibition does two opposite things at once.

First, the correction: an earlier pass called this AI-content-farm sourced. That verdict is overturned.

The claim that duloxetine can produce a POTS-like phenotype is in peer-reviewed primary literature from the Vanderbilt Autonomic Dysfunction Center. [primary, verified]
Mar et al. 2014 (PMID 24227635): "High-dose duloxetine can cause healthy volunteers to develop a POTS-like phenotype with increased orthostatic tachycardia after chronic administration that is likely related to norepinephrine transporter inhibition."
Raj 2013 (PMID 23753844): "Pharmacological NET inhibition can recreate an orthostatic tachycardia phenotype in susceptible healthy volunteer subjects. POTS patients might have less 'tachycardia reserve' and be more susceptible to exaggerated tachycardia with these medications." Its treatment table lists SNRI antidepressants among drugs to withdraw.
2015 Heart Rhythm Society consensus statement (PMID 25980576), Class III, Level B-R: "Drugs that block the norepinephrine reuptake transporter can worsen symptoms in patients with POTS and should not be administered." Class III is the "net harm, do not administer" class. Corroborated by the 2020 Canadian Cardiovascular Society POTS position statement (PMID 32145864).
The earlier pass searched the "recommends-for" list, found nothing, and concluded the "recommends-against" list was empty. It was not.

Both real sources say "high-dose" and "chronic," and the only duloxetine-specific study behind them used 80–240 mg/day in 15 healthy volunteers — four to twelve times your dose. Any text asserting a POTS-like phenotype at 20–60mg is overreaching its own source. This is not a 20mg problem. It is worth a conversation at 60mg.

Note that +2.2 → +16 → +35 bpm across ~30% → ~90% → near-complete occupancy is a coherent dose-response, not a contradiction.

Provenance caveat that must not be dropped: Vincent 2004 (PMID 15184278) could not be retrieved in full by this project. The "POTS-like phenotype" attribution rests on Mar 2014's characterisation of it rather than on the primary itself. One adjacent claim is invalid and should be deleted on sight

"SSRIs are preferred over SNRIs in POTS."

It appears in no guideline. The HRS statement never mentions SSRIs or SNRIs in its POTS section; Raj 2013 never uses the string "SSRI." Its only source is Mar 2014's own speculation that a more selective SSRI "might be better tolerated" with "further studies need to be conducted" — and Mar 2014's own data refute it: in 39 POTS patients, sertraline 50mg vs placebo in crossover produced standing HR and systolic BP that were not significantly different, and symptoms that were worse with sertraline than placebo. [primary, verified]

Why the direction is unknown for you: NET inhibition cuts both ways

↑ Worsens orthostatic TACHYCARDIA
Schroeder 2002 (PMID 11804991), reboxetine 8mg vs placebo, n=18, randomised double-blind crossover: at 75° head-up tilt, HR was 84±3 with placebo and 119±4 with reboxetine (P<0.0001). The paper's title calls it "a human model of orthostatic intolerance."
Green 2013 (PMID 24002370), atomoxetine 40mg in 27 actual POTS patients: standing HR 121±17 vs 105±15 (P=0.001); symptoms worsened (+4.2 vs −3.5, P=0.028).

Coherent with the genetics: a loss-of-function NET mutation causes familial orthostatic tachycardia.

↓ Protects against vasovagal PRE-SYNCOPE
Schroeder 2006 (PMID 16875978), n=51, double-blind randomised crossover: mean tolerated tilt duration 29±2 min with placebo vs 35±1 min with NET inhibition (p=0.001). Odds of premature tilt termination: OR 0.22 (95% CI 0.09–0.55).
And in the 2002 reboxetine study: vasovagal reactions occurred in 9 of 18 subjects on placebo, but only 1 of 18 on reboxetine.

Note: an earlier pass restated that OR as a "78% reduction." An OR of 0.22 is not a 78% risk reduction. Quote the OR.

The duality is demonstrated INSIDE single studies, not across them. The same reboxetine experiment that manufactures a +35 bpm tilt tachycardia also cuts vasovagal reactions from 9/18 to 1/18. Schroeder 2006 reports both a pressor response and increased upright heart rate alongside its protective effect. This is not two literatures disagreeing. It is one drug class doing two opposite things at once, and which one you get depends on which phenotype you have.

And "do you have POTS?" is the wrong question

Garner & Baraniuk 2019 (PMID 31159884), n=39 CFS patients: POTS on formal testing in 6 of 39 (~15%), yet 72% had orthostatic symptoms. The authors' central conclusion, verbatim: "orthostatic tachycardia did not account for OI symptoms in CFS." Separately, van Campen's work finds ~90% of ME/CFS patients show an abnormal cerebral blood flow reduction on tilt, including patients with normal heart rate and blood pressure responses. [primary, verified] / [secondary — unverified]

Most ME/CFS orthostatic intolerance is not POTS. A normal tilt-table heart rate does not rule out orthostatic intolerance in ME/CFS, and that is the most common reason it gets missed. Which of these three you are determines the direction of duloxetine's autonomic effect:

Tachycardic (POTS-type)

HR rise ≥30 bpm sustained without a BP drop. Duloxetine's mechanism is unfavourable. A formal guideline (HRS Class III) says NET blockers should be withdrawn.

Hypotensive / neurally-mediated

Systolic drop ≥20 or diastolic drop ≥10 mmHg within 3 min. NET inhibition may be neutral or even protective (Schroeder 2006).

Symptoms with neither

The most common ME/CFS pattern (per Garner & Baraniuk). Duloxetine's effect is genuinely unknown, and possibly neutral or favourable.

The whole question resolves with ten minutes and a cheap cuff

Nobody has characterised which phenotype you have. An active stand test would: lie down 10 minutes and record HR and BP; stand, and record HR and BP at 1, 3, 5 and 10 minutes, noting symptoms at each point.

Two weeks of baseline before the appointment is achievable, and repeating it after any dose change makes the drug's effect legible rather than inferable. It is the highest-value thing available in the time remaining, and it costs about £25.

Nothing here directs a dose. It is what to understand and what to raise.
