The Only Dose Comparison That Exists, and Why It Cannot Answer His Question

Chalon 2005 is the only study that has ever compared two duloxetine doses on polysomnography. It looks like exactly the answer he wants. It is not, and the reason is structural rather than fixable.

The finding, stated as a finding rather than as a gap. Nobody has ever measured duloxetine's sleep architecture at two doses in patients. Not at his dose range. Not with the schedule held constant. Not in anyone with fibromyalgia or ME/CFS. Nobody has measured it at 20mg at all. This is the honest answer to his question at the architecture level, and it means that anyone who tells him confidently what a dose increase will do to his sleep architecture is filling the gap with intuition.
Three confounds, any one of which is disqualifying:
  1. n = 6 per duloxetine arm. Six people.
  2. Dose is confounded with schedule, irreducibly. The arm that hurt sleep continuity is the only one with an evening dose. These have completely different implications for him, and the study cannot separate them.
  3. Young healthy men, 7 days, at 4–6× his dose. He is a middle-aged autistic adult with ME/CFS and fibromyalgia on 20mg.

The one real signal worth carrying to the appointment, flagged honestly. The arm that reduced sleep continuity, 60mg BID, is the only regimen in the study with an evening dose. That is consistent with "do not take any increment in the evening," and it is equally consistent with "120mg hurts continuity," and n=6 cannot separate them [primary, verified — but the inference is reasoning, not evidence]. Note also that there is no trial of morning versus evening duloxetine dosing with sleep outcomes at all. The standard advice is convention, not evidence. And the claim that "morning dosing reduces insomnia risk by approximately 60%" appears on commercial health sites with no citation and no traceable primary, and should be treated as fabricated until someone produces the study.
