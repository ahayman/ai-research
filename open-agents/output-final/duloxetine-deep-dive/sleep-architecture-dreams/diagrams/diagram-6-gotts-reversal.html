The Reversal: Not Napping May Be Protecting His Sleep

He has wondered whether his inability to nap is causing his poor sleep. The only dataset on napping in ME/CFS points the other way. This is the finding on this page that most deserves his attention, because it runs against his own hypothesis and it is not obvious.

The first thing this changes. He has treated his nap-failure as anomalous. In the only dataset that exists, about one in five ME/CFS patients does not nap at all over a fortnight. That does not explain his experience and it does not make it trivial, but it does mean he is describing something common in his condition rather than unique to him [primary, verified].
The second thing, which is the important one. In the only dataset on napping in ME/CFS, napping is associated with WORSE outcomes, not better: poorer cognitive functioning and more daytime sleepiness. The sign is the opposite of his assumption, and it converges with ordinary sleep medicine, where daytime napping reduces nocturnal sleep pressure and degrades night sleep [reasoning, not evidence]. So it is a live possibility that the thing he wants to fix is the thing that is currently working for him.
Three limits, stated at the claim rather than filed at the bottom:
  1. Gotts measured reported napping, not nap ABILITY. It cannot separate "cannot nap" from "chooses not to nap." His claim is about ability. This study cannot speak to it directly. It is the closest number that exists and it is not the number he needs.
  2. Cross-sectional, so the direction is unresolvable. Napping may cause worse cognition, or worse cognition may cause napping, or a third factor may cause both. This diagram shows a possible reversal, not a demonstrated one.
  3. Confounded by severity. Sicker patients may nap more and think less clearly, with no causal link between the two.
