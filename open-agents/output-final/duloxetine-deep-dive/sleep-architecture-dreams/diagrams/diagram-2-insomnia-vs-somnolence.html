What He Fears, and What the Data Shows

He is braced for insomnia. Across three independent trials and four populations, insomnia is the one sleep effect that does not rise at the step he is considering. The effect that tracks the dose in this range is the other one.

The honest complication, which runs against the reframe. The three rising slopes are all 30→60 steps in trials that had no 20mg arm. In the one trial that actually contains a 20mg arm, somnolence is flat from 20 to 60mg (5.08% → 4.31%) and only rises at 120mg. So the strong claim, "somnolence definitely rises at his specific step," is not supported. The supportable claim is narrower: of the two effects, insomnia is the one that never rises across this range, and somnolence is the one that sometimes does [reasoning, not evidence].

Two corrections to what he will read elsewhere. First, somnolence IS dose-dependent per duloxetine's own FDA label, at 10–11% versus 3% on placebo, and it is listed among the reactions with "a significant dose-dependent relationship in fixed-dose studies." Second, if he encounters a "1.5%" figure for duloxetine somnolence, that is a discontinuation rate, not an incidence rate. It answers a different question and it is roughly seven times too low for this one.
Why this lands where it does. His gain from this drug, in his own words, is waking less tired. Somnolence is daytime sleepiness. It is the one adverse effect that arrives dressed as the thing he is trying to protect, and it reached 41% in the only ME/CFS trial of duloxetine ever conducted. He has also told us his discrimination on exactly this axis is unreliable: "Just feeling 'sleepy' isn't a bother. It's mostly that bone-weary, pain-laden exhaustion..." So the harm signal and the benefit signal both arrive through a channel he has said he cannot read. He is watching the insomnia door. The traffic is at the somnolence door.
