You Are Braced for the Wrong Thing

Insomnia is the effect you fear and it does not move until 120mg. Somnolence is the effect that actually rises with dose, and it lands directly on the disability you are trying to protect.

Why this reframe matters more for you than it would for most people, in your own words:

"There are different types of exhaustion. Just feeling 'sleepy' isn't a bother. It's mostly that bone-weary, pain-laden exhaustion where my brain is filled with so much fog I can barely function."

The consequence is uncomfortable: somnolence may be close to undetectable to you by feel, because the channel you would use to detect it is the channel your ME/CFS already saturates. That is why the instrument advice elsewhere in this deep dive is count events, do not rate feelings: count deliberate naps that succeeded and unintended dozes separately. Benefit looks like the first rising while the second stays at zero. Somnolence looks like the second rising. That axis is volitional and intrusive rather than introspective, so it does not route through the compromised channel.

What this diagram does not settle. The 30→60 somnolence rise comes from an adolescent trial and a Korean adult trial, neither of which is your population, and the fixed 20/60/120 trial found somnolence flat across 20→60 (5.1% to 4.3%) primary, verified. So the honest statement is: somnolence rises somewhere in the 30–60mg region in two trials and did not in a third. The 41% ME/CFS figure is n=29, not stratified by dose, at 60–120mg flexible, so it cannot tell you what 30mg or 60mg alone would do. The largest dataset (Zhou 2023, 216 trials, 64,696 patients) says duloxetine's somnolence curve is a bell/inverted-U that declines at high doses and its insomnia curve is a plateau — which partly disagrees with the within-trial finding that insomnia doubles at 120mg primary, partially verified. On that specific point the within-trial randomized comparison is the stronger design. And the folk version is simply wrong: "low dose sedating, high dose activating" is not supported. The real finding is stranger — duloxetine causes insomnia and somnolence in the same population at the same time (34% and 41% simultaneously in 29 people). These are not opposite ends of one dose axis. They are two different effects happening to different people on the same drug. Finally, on timing: there is no trial of morning versus evening duloxetine dosing with sleep outcomes no evidence exists, the standard advice is convention rather than evidence, and the specific claim that "morning dosing reduces insomnia risk by approximately 60%" appears on commercial health sites with no citation and no traceable primary. Treat it as fabricated until someone produces the study.
