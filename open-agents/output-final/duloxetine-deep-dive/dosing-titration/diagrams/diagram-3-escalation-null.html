The one crack, and it is yours Ruhé 2006 (PMID 17041180) found "some, but limited" evidence for escalation specifically in partial responders after eight weeks. You are a partial responder: real benefit on anxiety and sleep, minimal on pain. That is the one population where the null is softest and it should not be papered over. But Ruhé is a 2006 narrative review superseded on this exact question by Dold's quantitative meta-analysis, which had the RCTs Ruhé lacked and found nothing. Ruhé's own verdict: escalation is "equivocally supported by evidence," and "dose escalation unequivocally increased side-effects." primary abstract only
A citation trap worth knowing about, because it nearly caught this project. The publisher's own page renders the author list as "Dunner DL, Kornstein SG, Greist JH, et al." with Dunner first. PubMed gives Kornstein. Cite Kornstein. This is the second author-order discrepancy caught in this project, and both were introduced by publisher pages rather than by PubMed.
What this diagram does not settle, and the part that cuts against its own tidiness. The familiar background claim that "antidepressant efficacy is flat above minimum doses" rests on Furukawa 2019 and Jakubovski 2016, and those two meta-analyses contradict each other: Furukawa found flat-to-decreasing above 20–40mg fluoxetine equivalents, Jakubovski found a significant increase primary, verified. More decisively, both of them exclude duloxetine primary, verified. Furukawa covers five SSRIs plus venlafaxine and mirtazapine; Jakubovski is SSRIs only. Applying either to your drug is an extrapolation across drug class, which is the move this project has been burned by repeatedly, so it is not made here. And note which drug in Furukawa's dataset looked least flat: venlafaxine, the other SNRI, whose curve keeps rising ("a more modest increase" is not "flat") reasoning, not evidence. What all three positions agree on is narrower and is the part worth keeping: side-effect burden rises with dose in every dataset, and nobody is arguing the efficacy gain from going higher is large. Finally, prophylactic escalation — raising the dose in someone who is currently responding, to protect against future loss of response — has never been studied in anyone, at any dose, for any indication no evidence exists.
