Why occupancy numbers cannot be compared across studies

This is the most common error in this literature, and this project has committed it in both directions. The reason it keeps happening is visible in one chart: the noise sources are all bigger than the effects people try to read out of them.

The region effect, measured inside one study

The same ten men, the same 20mg dose, the same scan, the same drug. Only the brain region differs.

RegionSteady-state SERT occupancy at 20mgIndividual range
Midbrain85.3%78 – 90%
Striatum75.2%59 – 86%
Thalamus74.0%64 – 84%
cross-region mean78.2%—

Computed from Abanades 2011's own per-subject table (PMID 20940733). Baldinger 2014 (PMID 24121201, n=19) confirms the ordering directly: midbrain reads significantly higher than striatum, and thalamus significantly lower.

Eleven points of difference sit between midbrain and thalamus in the same brains on the same afternoon. That is larger than most of the drug effects anyone tries to detect by comparing one study to another. Which is why picking a region, even unconsciously, decides the answer: quote the midbrain and duloxetine looks strong; quote the thalamus and it looks weak. Both numbers are correct. Neither is a fact about the drug.
Sørensen 2022's own instruction, verbatim: "comparison of occupancy data from different brain regions is potentially not meaningful, as SERT is not equally distributed throughout the brain." The operational rule that follows: any occupancy comparison not matched on region, tracer, modality, dosing paradigm (single dose versus steady state) and population is measuring the methods, not the drugs.
