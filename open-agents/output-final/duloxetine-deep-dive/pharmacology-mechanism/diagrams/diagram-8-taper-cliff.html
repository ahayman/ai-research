The same curve, read right to left

A hyperbola does not care which direction you walk it. Equal steps in milligrams are not equal steps in occupancy, and the steepest part of the curve is exactly where the capsules run out.

These decimals are not the claim, and this diagram obeys the rule set by the rest of this page. The individual points on this curve cannot be resolved from one another, so "16 points" and "72 points" are read off a model, not measured. The claim is the ratio: the final step is several times larger than every preceding step combined. That claim depends only on the curvature, and the curvature is the part with two-laboratory replication.
Therefore a weak argument: "going up makes coming down harder." The hard step is the 20 to 0 step, and it is identical whether you arrive at it from 60mg or sit at it now. Going up adds several easy steps in front of a cliff that does not change size. This argument gets made often. It should not carry weight in a dose decision.
Therefore a strong argument: ask the taper question now. The question is one sentence: "Will you write a compounded liquid, or a custom-dose capsule, if I ever need to come off this?" It costs nothing to ask at an appointment you are attending anyway, the answer shapes your options years from now, and it is far easier to ask before you need it than during a taper. Bead-counting is the alternative, and it requires averaging bead counts across several capsules.
Why the floor exists at all: duloxetine is acid-labile, so the pellets are enteric-coated, so they cannot be crushed, chewed or dissolved without stomach acid destroying the drug. That is why the smallest capsule anyone manufactures is 20mg, and why the formulation, not the pharmacology, is what makes the last step hard. Category 11 has the detail. [primary, verified]
