Abanades 2011 did something the other occupancy studies did not: it fitted an EC50 for each person, one at a time. This is the pharmacological answer to why the same drug is transformative for some people and intolerable for others.

The population curve does not describe any individual in it. At the same number of milligrams, these ten men were receiving what is, pharmacologically, a different dose, and the difference is large. Nearly thirty points of occupancy separate the lowest man from the highest. So "you are at 78% occupancy" is not a sentence anyone can say about a person who has not been scanned. Not a doctor, not this page, not any page that quotes a number at you.
Two things follow. First, this is the mechanistic reason a drug's reputation can be so violently bimodal: the variance is real, physical, and measured, not psychological. Second, "start low and see what happens" is not clinical laziness. Given a spread this wide, it is the only defensible way to find an individual's dose, because the population number genuinely does not contain the answer. [primary, verified]
