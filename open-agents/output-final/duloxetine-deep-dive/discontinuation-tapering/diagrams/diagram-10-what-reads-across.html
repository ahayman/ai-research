What Reads Across to Duloxetine, and What Does Not

You have fallen off one of these curves already. That fact does two different jobs, and only one of them is legitimate. Getting the split right is the whole of this page's honesty.

Reads across

The shape of the cliff

Duloxetine's occupancy curve is steep at the bottom and flat at the top, exactly like escitalopram's. Two identical-looking dose reductions cost wildly different amounts depending on where on the curve they happen.

Why it is legitimate: it does not depend on your history at all. It is computed from duloxetine's own published half-maximal dose of 7.9mg, and it reproduces this project's existing "~16" and "~72" figures, which were derived by a different route. The hyperbolic shape is a property of receptor occupancy, not of any one drug: it was fitted across 10 antidepressants, 294 participants and 309 occupancy measures, and duloxetine is one of the ten.

Does not read across

How the cliff will feel

“You already fell off this cliff once, so you know what it will feel like” is the intuitive next step. Your own data refutes it, and refutes it about as cleanly as an n=1 can refute anything.

Why it is not legitimate: those two initiations are matched within 2.5 occupancy points, and within about one point on the tracer-matched comparison. Same brain, one year apart, same transporter engagement, opposite outcomes. So the size of an occupancy change is demonstrably not what decides how you respond, because you ran that experiment on yourself and it came back both ways.

5.8 points bought a week of disability while 9.2 points bought nothing. A smaller step in one direction hurt; a larger step in the other did not. The available resolution is that the two directions run on different mechanisms: descent tracks loss of an adapted-to input, which follows occupancy, while ascent tracks acute surge onto non-saturated targets, which follows concentration. Your 10→20mg step doubled plasma concentration while moving occupancy only 5.8 points. That resolution costs an extra mechanism, so it is less tidy than the story it replaces [reasoning, not evidence].

The trap, stated plainly, because it is the satisfying one

Everything about your history invites the conclusion that you know what duloxetine's cliff will feel like. It is vivid, it is personal, it is the kind of inference that feels like wisdom. It is also the exact move this project has got wrong eight times running, and the evidence against it is the best datum you have supplied.

What survives is worth more than what fails. The taper argument never needed your escitalopram experience: it stands on duloxetine's own ED50 and a review of ten drugs. What your history adds is a demonstration that the arithmetic describes something real that happens to a person, which no systematic review can supply. What it does not add is a forecast. Nobody has a forecast [no evidence exists].
