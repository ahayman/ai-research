A necessary note on brain zaps

The brief electric-shock sensations patients call "brain zaps" are what duloxetine's discontinuation reputation is built on, and they are real and widely described in patient reports. But the closest verified figure in duloxetine's own trial data is paraesthesia at 2.9%, and trial adverse-event coding was never designed to capture the phenomenon the way patients describe it. Do not read 2.9% as a brain-zap incidence rate. Read it as evidence that the trials were not asking the right question. The phenomenon is well-attested by patient report and poorly quantified by the trial literature.

The placebo column is the part worth carrying away. Roughly half of what gets called withdrawal in these datasets happened to people who stopped a dummy pill, which is the single best explanation of why the wider incidence dispute exists at all. It does not mean the symptoms are imaginary. It means that stopping a drug you expected to need is itself an event, and that the drug's pharmacology accounts for about half of what follows.
