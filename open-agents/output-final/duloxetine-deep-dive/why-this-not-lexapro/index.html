The experiment you ran without knowing

Here is what you supplied. Escitalopram was almost six months, not a few weeks. You went 10mg to 20mg over a month, then held 20mg for months. The initial dose cost you about a week of function and the titration cost you about another, and in both cases it dissociated you so badly you could not drive or work. Then it faded, and left you exactly where you started. Zero benefit at any dose, ever.

Put that next to duloxetine, and the two together are the strongest finding in this project.

Both of your starting doses land in the same place on the only measurements anyone has ever taken. In the striatum, escitalopram 10mg reads 73 ± 4% serotonin transporter occupancy and duloxetine 20mg reads 75 ± 8%, and both of those numbers come from the same tracer, the same scanning method, ten subjects each, and the same steady-state dosing (Sørensen 2022 Table 2, PMID 34548628, collating Baldinger 2014 and Abanades 2011) primary, verified — the table was retrieved and read in this pass. In the thalamus, escitalopram 10mg reads 60 to 75% and duloxetine 20mg reads 71 to 74%, so duloxetine's entire thalamic band sits inside escitalopram's primary, verified. In every brain region where both drugs have ever been measured, their occupancies overlap, and nobody can demonstrate a difference. One of them dissociated you for a week. The other produced mild floatiness.

Same brain. Same serotonin transporter engagement, as far as anyone can measure it. Opposite outcomes. That is not an inference and it is not a hypothesis. It is what happened, and it establishes the thing you had already reasoned your way to from first principles before any of this research found it: serotonin transporter occupancy is not the variable that explains what happened to you. You wrote that the drugs “simply work differently, and that alone is enough to explain the variation,” and the evidence for it was sitting in the corpus the whole time, unassembled, waiting for your correction to make it visible.

Now the caveat, because it is load-bearing and it is a correction to the brief that commissioned this page. The instructions I was given said your two initiations were “matched within 2.5 occupancy points,” and matched “within about 1 point” on thalamic data. Both of those overstate it, and I am not going to use either. The 2.5-point figure compares a number modelled from a SPECT scan of the midbrain in Vienna against a number modelled from a PET scan of the thalamus in Chiba, and this page's own Deep Dive shows that changing only the scanner moves occupancy estimates by 23 to 61 points primary, verified. You cannot establish a 2.5-point agreement inside a 23-point noise band any more than you can extract a 10-point difference from it, and the second is precisely why the old “less serotonin” story died. The 1-point thalamic figure is worse: it comes from a study that names no brain region and reports no escitalopram-10mg-specific value at all, only a 69.1 to 77.9% range covering three different drug groups at once (Arakawa 2016, PMID 27082864, abstract retrieved verbatim this pass) primary, verified. That exact inference is on this project's dead list, and the brief resurrected it.

The finding does not need the false precision, and it is stronger without it. What the evidence supports is not “the two exposures were equal to within a point.” It is “nobody can show they differed, in any region where anyone has looked, and your outcomes differed categorically anyway.” An argument built on a precise match invites someone to attack the precision. An argument built on an overlap that nobody can resolve cannot be attacked that way, because it is claiming less and it needs less.

The corrected history also kills a candidate outright, and it is the dullest and most persistent one. Every earlier version of this page carried “wrong dose, or an inadequate trial” as a live explanation, on the reasoning that you stopped early and never reached whatever slow adaptation the benefit requires. Six months at a full 20mg is not an inadequate trial by any standard anyone uses. It was clean, it was fair, it was long, it was fully dosed, and it did nothing. That makes the escitalopram result stronger, not weaker. It is not “he might not have given it a chance.” It is “the drug does not work for him,” and every candidate that has to explain the escitalopram failure now has a harder job rather than an easier one.

And it changes what the escitalopram dissociation actually was, which every document in this project has had wrong. The corpus describes a persistent drug-induced state. It was not persistent. It was roughly a week at each dose change, and then it faded, and months at a steady 20mg produced neither dissociation nor benefit. A transient, transition-linked state that habituates is a different object from a steady adverse condition, and it is also plausibly a different object from your native episodes, which last one to two weeks and do not habituate reasoning, not evidence. Something about the transition did the work, not the level.

Nobody has studied that. There is a named syndrome at antidepressant dose changes, called activation or jitteriness syndrome, and it has an eleven-item symptom list that is identical in every study that operationalises it: anxiety, agitation, panic attacks, insomnia, irritability, hostility, aggressiveness, impulsivity, akathisia, hypomania, mania (Harada 2008, PMID 18781664) primary abstract only. Dissociation is not on that list. Depersonalisation is not on that list. It is a syndrome of over-arousal, and you report the opposite pole. Reaching for it to explain you would repeat this project's signature error, which is mapping your experience onto a construct that excludes it. And the literature on antidepressant-induced dissociation specifically is not thin. It is empty. Forty-four PubMed hits, mostly burnout studies false-matching on an unrelated subscale; narrowed properly, four hits, none on topic no evidence exists. No prevalence figure. No time course. No habituation data. Your single most disabling drug reaction is a phenomenon the literature has never named.

There is a temptation here that I want to defuse rather than indulge. An earlier pass predicted, in writing, before your correction arrived, that a dose increase should transiently re-open the acute window. Your history confirms it, twice. That is worth much less than it looks. It was a prediction about duloxetine confirmed by escitalopram, which assumes the very drug-difference this page exists to interrogate; it is n=1, retrospective, unblinded, on about a year of recall; and most importantly it is not discriminating. Activation folklore predicts it, the gating ladder predicts it, and ordinary pharmacological intuition predicts it. A prediction that all the rival theories share selects nothing when it comes true. It must not move any candidate's ranking, and it does not.

Three older explanations are in the graveyard, and how they died matters more than that they died. The intuitive one, the one the old page title encoded, was that duloxetine adds norepinephrine and the norepinephrine is the difference. That is undercut by the drug's own binding data: 0.8 nM for the serotonin transporter against 7.5 nM for the norepinephrine transporter, a ratio of 9 (Bymaster 2001, PMID 11750180) primary, verified. Lower means tighter, so duloxetine is roughly nine times more selective for serotonin, and at your dose the imaging agrees with the chemistry. At 20mg, duloxetine is predominantly a serotonergic drug. The second was that maybe you needed less serotonin, built on a 10-point gap between two numbers that came from different scanners, tracers, regions, dosing schedules, sample sizes and continents, with the two biggest confounds both pushing in the hypothesis's favour. The third was that 20mg is simply sub-therapeutic, built on an 80% line that is anchored to where five other drugs' minimum approved doses happen to land in the striatum, and is not a measurement of who gets better primary, verified. Where that line has actually been tested in anxiety it failed outright: patients sat at an average of 78% occupancy and there was no correlation between occupancy and improvement primary, verified.

All four deaths are the kind that count, and this is the distinction that makes the rest of the page short enough to be useful. You made the argument yourself and you were right about it: every measured effect has some subpopulation that does not show it and some that shows its opposite, so a mechanism that merely failed to reach significance on a population average is not dead at all, because a null average is perfectly compatible with a large effect in one person. But a mechanism built on a measurement artifact, or contradicted by a binding constant, or refuted by a fact about your own dosing history, is gone regardless of how variable people are. No amount of heterogeneity revives the second kind. The SNRI story died on chemistry that does not care about populations. The “less serotonin” story and its mirror-image “parity” correction both died because the numbers being compared were never comparable. The sub-therapeutic story died on a threshold that was never a threshold. The inadequate-trial story died on your calendar. None of them is waiting quietly for the right subgroup.

What is left is a better question with live candidates and no winner, and I am going to rate them rather than pick one, because you asked for that and because this chain flip-flopped nine times trying to do the opposite, which is about as much evidence for your position as a research process can generate against itself.

Your “slight difference, outsized effect” intuition is vindicated, and on an axis you were not aiming at. One Copenhagen group ran escitalopram in healthy volunteers, double-blind, placebo-controlled, crossover. At 10mg, P50 sensory gating was intact (Jensen 2008, PMID 18000656, n=21) primary, verified. At 15mg, same lab, same paradigm, same population, same design, and framed by the authors as a deliberate dose extension of their own prior study, gating was significantly disrupted (Oranje 2011, PMID 20971512, n=20) primary, verified. A 1.5-fold dose step flipped the human sensory filter. Convert that dose step to occupancy and it is roughly 3.7 percentage points reasoning, not evidence: the occupancy is modelled, the gating results are primary. A pharmacologically tiny step, a categorical functional change, in humans, blind, placebo-controlled, in your own drug. The caveat is that the number is soft and I will not pretend otherwise: the curve is fitted to a different lab's SPECT data in a different brain region, Jensen's and Oranje's volunteers were never scanned at all, and the fit misses the one observation held back to check it by 5.2 points, which is larger than the 3.7-point step the argument rests on reasoning, not evidence. What survives all of that is qualitative and it is enough: on any version of the curve, a 1.5× dose step is small, and it flipped the filter.

That has a corollary the brief overstated and I will state properly. The brief says this proves the gating effect “cannot be a SERT-occupancy effect.” It does not prove that. What it forces is a choice: either the filter is exquisitely sensitive to occupancy in a range where nothing else is, or the effect runs through something that is not saturated and still scales with concentration while occupancy has flattened out. The second is more parsimonious, and either way occupancy is the wrong axis reasoning, not evidence. Which means your gating ladder and your within-person control are the same finding arriving twice from different directions, and that convergence is real rather than manufactured.

Your own candidate, regional distribution, is live, well-formed, and untestable, and those three things are not the same as unfalsifiable. You proposed that the drugs might differ by little more than one concentrating more in one part of the brain than another, and still produce an outsized effect. That is the explanatory structure of a published human PET study. Kim 2017 found escitalopram's occupancy significantly higher in the dorsal raphe than in the putamen (p<0.001), with genuinely different curve shapes in the two regions, and concluded that this regional gradient may explain the delayed onset of action of SSRIs by modulating autoreceptor function (PMID 27557550, n=12) primary, verified. That is your move, applied to a different SSRI mystery, in a peer-reviewed journal. And Baldinger 2014 states your premise almost verbatim as established preclinical fact: “a regionally varying SERT activity and antidepressant drug concentration” (PMID 24121201, n=19) primary, verified. The amplifier is real and named: the raphe holds the autoreceptors, so a gradient at one small nucleus can lower serotonin release across the entire projection field, which means a small regional difference can produce a large and possibly opposite-signed effect. That is not hand-waving. It is the published explanation for why every SSRI takes weeks to work.

And it cannot be tested, for a reason that is a fact about the literature rather than about your reasoning. Escitalopram has been imaged in fourteen brain regions. Duloxetine has been imaged in three: thalamus, midbrain, striatum primary, verified — Sørensen Table 2, read this pass. Every region the dissociation literature implicates, which is the insula, the anterior cingulate and the prefrontal cortex, has never been measured for duloxetine at any dose, in twenty years primary, verified. The three regions that do overlap show parity. So the honest verdict is that your hypothesis is not refuted, not testable with existing data, unfalsifiable as you state it (fourteen regions times two directions is twenty-eight available post-hoc stories), and sharply falsifiable the moment it is made specific, at which point it needs one PET scan that nobody has commissioned. That is an unusual and respectable place for a hypothesis to be, and I am not going to manufacture support for it because you proposed it. Three things run against it and you should have them. It predicts nothing you can observe in your diary. Baldinger runs against its intuitive version, because escitalopram occupies the amygdala and subgenual cingulate more than average, so “escitalopram never reached the emotional regions” is not a story available to you primary, verified. And the one regional comparison that looks favourable, midbrain 85% against 75%, is a cross-modality artifact of exactly the kind on the dead list.

The candidate the old page rated as real-but-unconnected has to be demoted hard, and this is the largest single change to the ratings. Escitalopram binds an allosteric site on the transporter that slows its own dissociation from the main site, and this project has repeatedly called that mechanism untested, surviving by never having been examined. It has been examined. Twice, in vivo, with purpose-built knockin mice, by two separate groups, and it largely failed both times. Murray 2016 built an allosteric-null mouse and found no measurable effect on hippocampal serotonin (p=0.297) and none in the tail suspension test, concluding the site “may not produce meaningful in vivo effects” (PMID 26621784, all three authors at Emory, no manufacturer involvement) primary, verified. Jacobsen 2014 abolished the site genetically in humanised mice at clinically relevant plasma levels and found escitalopram's effect “unaffected by loss of the allosteric site” (PMID 24810106) primary, verified. And Jacobsen's conflict-of-interest statement reads “ALP, MC and CS are employees of Lundbeck Research, USA. MGC has consulted for Lundbeck and received compensation” primary, verified. Lundbeck makes escitalopram, and the paper concludes against the manufacturer's own proprietary differentiator, which cuts in favour of its credibility rather than against it. The conflict-of-interest disclosure still belongs on the positive allosteric literature, which is substantially a manufacturer research programme and whose standard review opens by asserting escitalopram's superiority primary, verified. And the popular claim that escitalopram is the only drug with that site is simply wrong: the site belongs to the transporter, and R-citalopram modulates it too primary, verified. What is escitalopram-specific is the exploitation, not the possession.

But I have to report the finding that cuts against my own demotion, because omitting it would be the exact selective reading this project keeps committing. Murray is not the clean negative the brief describes. It reports three results, and one of them is positive and statistically significant: in marble burying, removing the allosteric site meant a ten-fold larger dose of escitalopram was needed for the same effect (p=0.023) primary, verified. A ten-fold potency shift is not “essentially nothing.” Murray's own conclusion has two halves, and the second is “there may be a regional specificity in the role of the allosteric site.” So the candidate that looks most disconfirmed and the candidate you proposed yourself point at each other, which is either a real convergence or a coincidence, and one mouse assay cannot tell you which. Jacobsen's allosteric test was also a secondary objective rather than what the study was powered for, and Murray's knockin protein expressed at only 10 to 30% of normal, which is a genuine power confound primary, verified. These are mouse studies with mouse-grade objections, so by this page's own rule the candidate is wounded rather than dead. It moves to the bottom of the live list because it has been tested and mostly failed, while your regional candidate has not been tested at all.

Two candidates survive largely as they were. Acute serotonin as the effector rather than the buffer explains your Lexapro experience better than anything else on the page and fails on the difference between the drugs, because duloxetine is also predominantly serotonergic at your dose and so should have done the same thing reasoning, not evidence. And individual variation is its own answer: Abanades fitted individual concentration-response values across ten men on identical doses of the same drug under an identical protocol and found an 8.5-fold spread (PMID 20940733) primary, verified. The population curve does not describe any individual in it. That is the quantitative backbone of your own argument, it is why two similar drugs diverging wildly in one person is the expected observation rather than the anomalous one, and it is why “try the next one and see” is correct clinical practice rather than laziness. Note its cost, though: if it is the answer, there is no mechanism here to find, only a distribution to act on, and a hypothesis that cannot lose is not doing much work.

And then there is what nobody knows, which on this page is not a footnote but a substantial fraction of the answer. Nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults, beyond one three-patient case series. Nobody has studied whether a previous drug-induced dissociation predicts the next one, so your escitalopram history is not a risk factor with a number attached; it is an anecdote with an n of one, and it happens to be yours no evidence exists. Nobody has studied antidepressant-dose-change-induced dissociation in anyone no evidence exists. Nobody has imaged duloxetine outside three brain regions in twenty years, which is the single fact that makes your own hypothesis unanswerable primary, verified. And the systematic review this page leans on hardest says so itself, in its own limitations: “it is therefore possible that the reported occupancy measures do not reflect those areas which are most important for patients, clinically. Whether blocking of SERT in some brain regions is of particular importance regarding treatment effect, adverse events, or withdrawal symptoms remains unresolved” primary, verified. Your dissociation is an adverse event. The review is not saying you are right. It is saying nobody has looked.

So the shape of the honest answer is this. Your corrected history gave this page a within-person, occupancy-overlapping, opposite-outcome comparison, and that is the closest thing to a controlled experiment the project contains, which settles your premise as fact rather than conjecture: whatever separated the two drugs, it was not how much serotonin transporter they occupied. Four explanations died, and none of them died of a null result in some other population, which is the death that would have left them alive for you. What remains is a list rather than a verdict, and the pattern inside the list is the finding rather than a failure to reach one: the candidate that fits you most precisely has the thinnest evidence, the candidate with the best evidence explains your divergence by explaining that divergences are ordinary, the candidate you proposed yourself is the only one whose premise is established rather than assumed and it predicts nothing you can check, and the candidate that was rated real-but-unconnected turns out to have been tested and to have mostly failed. You asked for mechanism rather than prediction and candidates rather than verdicts, and that is what this looks like when it is done properly. Nothing here tells you what to do about a dose, and nothing here could.

The within-person control

The claim: escitalopram 10mg and duloxetine 20mg put him at serotonin transporter occupancies that nobody can distinguish, in every region where both drugs have been measured, and the two produced categorically different outcomes in the same person a year apart. Therefore SERT occupancy does not explain what happened to him.

The region-matched comparison, extracted directly from Sørensen 2022's Table 2 (PMID 34548628, PMC8960396, full text retrieved and the table read in this pass) primary, verified:

Striatum73 ± 4% (n=10)75 ± 8% (n=10)No
Thalamus60 ± 3 – 75 ± 4% (n=18)71 ± 5 – 74 ± 7% (n=13)No
Midbrain64 ± 6 – 81 ± 5% (n=20)85 ± 4% (n=10)Void: cross-modality

The striatal row is the best comparison this project has ever had, and no prior pass made it. Escitalopram's 73 ± 4% is Baldinger 2014; duloxetine's 75 ± 8% is Abanades 2011. Both are [11C]DASB. Both are PET. Both are the striatum. Both are n=10. Both are multiple-dose rather than single-dose. That is five matched axes, and it requires no inference of any kind: the numbers are printed in the same column of the same table.

What is not matched, stated plainly: different laboratories (Vienna and London) and different populations (nineteen depressed patients versus ten healthy men) reasoning, not evidence. It is still two studies. But 73 ± 4 against 75 ± 8 does not become distinguishable under any handling of those two mismatches, and the direction of the trivial 2-point difference is meaningless at those standard deviations.

The midbrain row looks tempting and is worthless. Duloxetine's 85% is PET with [11C]DASB; escitalopram's midbrain range includes Klein's SPECT with [123I]ADAM. Different tracer, different modality. The modality effect alone is 23 to 61 points, which is six times the apparent gap primary, verified. This is the identical structure of the “escitalopram less serotonin” claim that is already on the dead list, and it is on the dead list for exactly this reason.

What the brief got wrong, and it is the most important correction in this pass.

The brief commissioning this page states the control as “matched within 2.5 occupancy points,” and “within ~1 point on tracer-matched thalamic data (71.3% vs ~70%).” Neither is usable.

The 2.5-point figure compares 69.2% (modelled from Klein 2006, which is SPECT, [123I]ADAM, midbrain, Vienna) against 71.7% (modelled from Takano's ED50, which is PET, [11C]DASB, thalamus, Chiba). That is cross-modality, cross-region and cross-laboratory, which is three of the seven axes this page spends a whole section demolishing. A 2.5-point agreement inside a 23-point noise band establishes nothing. The blade cuts both ways: you cannot extract a small difference from large noise, and you cannot establish a small sameness from it either. The first half of that is why “less serotonin” is dead. The second half has been overlooked, and the brief overlooked it.

The 1-point thalamic figure is worse: it resurrects a dead-list item. It traces to supplement 8's table, which assigns Arakawa 2016 a region (“thalamus”) and an escitalopram-10mg value (“~69–70%”). Arakawa's abstract, retrieved verbatim this pass, contains neither. It names no brain region anywhere, and its only occupancy figure is “Escitalopram and sertraline showed high occupancies of 69.1-77.9% at 4h”, which covers three groups at four subjects each: escitalopram 10mg, escitalopram 20mg, and sertraline 50mg primary, verified. Taking the bottom of that range and calling it escitalopram 10mg is precisely the inference the dead list retired, in both directions. The tilde in “~69–70%” is the tell.

The finding survives the corrections and is stronger for them. It never needed a precision match. It needed an overlap that nobody can resolve, and it has one in every region where the question has been asked.

Why the within-person design is the right one here, and this is not a consolation prize. Abanades' 8.5-fold between-person spread in concentration-response is exactly what makes population comparisons unable to speak about him primary, verified. His own body is the only control that does not suffer from it. The old version of this page reached the same conclusion by cross-study comparison and had to hedge it as something the overlapping ranges “permit.” His history upgrades it from a permission to a demonstration.

The remaining honest caveat, which must travel: the escitalopram side is retrospective and about a year old, he was not blind and neither was anyone else, and nobody has ever scanned him, so the occupancies are population figures applied to a man whom this page's own strongest candidate says population figures do not describe reasoning, not evidence. That last point is a genuine tension and it is worth naming rather than hiding. It does not dissolve the argument, because the argument needs only that no defensible difference exists, and individual variation makes a defensible difference less available rather than more. But it is a tension.

The corrected history: an adequate trial, and a transient

In his words: “I took Lexapro for almost 6 months. I went from 10 - 20mg over the course of a month, then stayed at 20mg for several months. Both the initial dose and the titration up caused me to lose a week each of functionality. It dissociated me so strongly I couldn't drive or work. But once that was done...the drug faded away and left me exactly where I started.”

Four things follow, and they supersede every document in this corpus.

  1. “Inadequate trial” and “wrong dose” are dead. Six months, titrated to a full 20mg, held for months reader report, and it is definitive: no literature overrides a dose-duration fact. This was carried as a live candidate on every prior version of this page and it is now removed rather than downgraded.
  2. The negative is stronger, not weaker. A six-month full-dose trial with zero anxiolysis is a strong individual result. Every candidate that must explain the escitalopram failure now has a harder job.
  3. The dissociation was transient and transition-linked, not a steady state. About a week at 0→10, about a week at 10→20, then it faded, and months at steady 20mg produced neither dissociation nor benefit. A steady-state account cannot produce that shape. Something about the transition did the work.
  4. It is plausibly a different object from his native episodes. His native episodes run one to two weeks and do not habituate. His escitalopram dissociation ran about a week and did habituate reasoning, not evidence. Category 5 currently treats them as the same thing and should not.

The literature on his symptom is empty, and this is one of the most valuable claims on the page.

A dose-transition syndrome is described, and the sentence exists verbatim: “Jitteriness/anxiety syndrome is a recognized adverse effect observed during the initiation or change of dose in antidepressant treatment” (Arai 2023, PMID 38868737) primary abstract only.

His symptom is not in it. The syndrome's eleven-item list is identical in every study that operationalises it: “anxiety, agitation, panic attacks, insomnia, irritability, hostility, aggressiveness, impulsivity, akathisia, hypomania, and mania” (Harada 2008, PMID 18781664; identical list in the prospective replication, Harada 2014, PMID 25419134) primary abstract only. Dissociation is not on it. Depersonalisation is not on it. Derealisation is not on it. It is a syndrome of over-arousal and he reports the opposite pole.

And the syndrome itself is poorly characterised. Its systematic review (Sinclair 2009, PMID 19478285, 107 articles) found “no validated rating scales,” incidence “varied widely from 4 to 65%,” and concluded it “remains poorly characterised” primary abstract only. A 4-to-65% range is a confession, not an estimate. The one prospective study that measured a time course enrolled only antidepressant-naive patients, so it studied initiation only and contains no dose-increase data at all (Harada 2014) primary, verified. The “or change of dose” half of the claim is asserted, not measured.

Antidepressant-induced dissociation specifically: 44 PubMed hits, mostly burnout studies false-matching on the Maslach Inventory's unrelated “depersonalization” subscale. Narrowed properly: 4 hits, none on topic no evidence exists. No prevalence. No time course. No habituation data. Nobody has asked the question.

The confirmed prediction, and why it earns nothing. Supplement 4 predicted in writing, before the correction arrived, that “a dose increase should transiently re-open the acute window.” He ran that experiment twice, unknowingly, and it passed both times. Treat this as the chain's most suspicious moment rather than its most triumphant. It is (a) a prediction about duloxetine confirmed by escitalopram, which is close to circular because the drug-difference is the very question; (b) n=1, retrospective, unblinded, about a year of recall; (c) not discriminating, because activation folklore, the gating ladder and plain pharmacological intuition all predict the same thing. A prediction its rivals share selects nothing. It moves no ranking.

The graveyard: four explanations and how each died

The distinction that shortens the list is between being killed by heterogeneity and being killed by logic, measurement, or a fact about him. A null average is compatible with a large individual effect, so a mechanism that failed to show up on a population mean is still a candidate. A mechanism built on a measurement error, or contradicted by a binding constant, or refuted by his calendar, is gone regardless of how variable people are. All four of this page's dead explanations died the second way.

Death 1: “Duloxetine adds norepinephrine, that's the difference.”

Killed by the binding constants. Bymaster 2001 (PMID 11750180) reports it verbatim: “Duloxetine inhibited binding to the human NE and 5-HT transporters with K(i) values of 7.5 and 0.8 nM, respectively, and with a K(i) ratio of 9” primary, verified. Lower Ki means higher affinity, so duloxetine is nine times more selective for the serotonin transporter. NET occupancy at 20mg is about 30% primary, verified.

This does not depend on any population average. The molecule binds what it binds. The rescue is that 30% NET occupancy might be doing decisive work, which is possible, but no NET occupancy threshold exists for any clinical effect in any indication no evidence exists, so the rescue is unfalsifiable rather than supported. The honest status of the noradrenergic contribution at 20mg is “unknown and not dismissible on occupancy grounds,” which is different from “the explanation.” It remains the most obvious real pharmacological difference between the two drugs, and it is untested against his phenotype no evidence exists.

Death 2: “So maybe he needed LESS serotonin.”

Killed by cross-study artifact. The comparison was duloxetine 20mg at 71.3% against escitalopram 10mg at 81.5%, and every methodological axis differs. Two decisive facts:

  1. The artifact ran in the hypothesis's own favour. Test 1, fully independent, no manufacturer involvement. Three of the authors are Lundbeck employees, the senior author consulted for Lundbeck, and the paper concludes against escitalopram's proprietary differentiating mechanism. PMID 27665061 on the same site is also Lundbeck. The positive allosteric literature is substantially a manufacturer research programme, and this project applies exactly that standard to an industry-funded pregabalin meta-analysis elsewhere. The finding stands.

One popular framing is simply wrong. It is often said escitalopram is the only drug with that site. The site belongs to the transporter, and the same literature reports that R-citalopram, the non-therapeutic enantiomer, is also an allosteric modulator of SERT primary, verified. What is escitalopram-specific is the therapeutic exploitation of the site, not possession of it.

The honest re-rating. These are mouse studies with a genuine expression confound and one significant positive, so by this page's own rule (heterogeneity-grade objections do not kill) candidate 5 is not dead. But it is no longer “untested and surviving by never having been examined.” It is tested, twice, in vivo, and mostly negative, which is a categorically different epistemic position. It moves from the middle of the live list to the bottom. And it retains the one thing nothing else has: it is specifically and only about escitalopram, which is exactly the shape the question needs. It is a mechanism that would explain everything if it worked, and the best available evidence says it mostly does not.

Candidate 6: SSRI-induced emotional blunting  ·  Fit: LOW, and the reason it is low is instructive  ·  Evidence: reasonable on prevalence, absent on everything the candidate needs

His escitalopram experience superficially reads like a textbook description of antidepressant-induced emotional blunting, and this is the explanation most people reach for first. It is rated low because it does not discriminate between the two drugs, which is the only thing this page's question requires.

Goodwin 2017 (PMID 28628765), the largest survey: “Six hundred and sixty nine depressed patients on treatment and 150 recovered (formerly depressed) controls... The rate of emotional blunting in treated depressed patients was 46%... There was no difference according to antidepressant agent, though it appeared less frequent with bupropion” primary, verified.

If blunting does not differ between agents, it cannot explain a difference between agents. Whatever made duloxetine different for him, the survey data say it is not that duloxetine is a low-blunting drug.

Two further corrections that must travel with any use of this literature:

  • “Emotional blunting is dose-dependent” is not established. No dose-stratified or dose-response analysis exists no evidence exists. The claim propagates across reviews from a shared root: a 1990 case report, via a 2009 qualitative interview study that never measured dose. The one cohort in which doses were systematically raised found blunting fell: Aşçibaşi 2020 (PMID 33065720, n=98), OQESA scores decreased from week 8 to week 16 (P<0.001) while doses were escalated primary, verified. That is confounded with time and remission, so it does not refute dose-dependence; it is simply the closest thing to a test that exists and it does not support it.
  • The prevalence figure “40-60%, range 20-92%” merges two constructs. 40-60% is emotional blunting; 20-92% is apathy specifically with SSRIs, from a different review whose overall antidepressant-apathy range is 5.8-50% primary, verified. Do not quote the merged range.
