The Corrected History

He supplied a corrected escitalopram history, and it changes this page more than any other. It kills a candidate outright, and it changes what the dissociation actually was in every document in this project.

“I took Lexapro for almost 6 months. I went from 10 - 20mg over the course of a month, then stayed at 20mg for several months. Both the initial dose and the titration up caused me to lose a week each of functionality. It dissociated me so strongly I couldn't drive or work. But once that was done...the drug faded away and left me exactly where I started.”

What the correction does to this page

“Wrong dose, or an inadequate trial”

DEAD. Six months. Titrated to a full 20mg over a month. Held for months. That is an adequate trial by any standard anyone uses. It was carried as a live candidate on every prior build of this page, rated “unfalsifiable now.” It was not unfalsifiable. It was falsified, by a fact that was always available and that nobody asked for. reader report — definitive; no literature overrides a dose-duration fact

“A persistent drug-induced dissociation”

WRONG SHAPE, and the shape is the whole point. Every document in this corpus describes a steady state. It was about a week at each dose change, and then it faded, and months at a steady 20mg produced neither dissociation nor benefit. A steady-state account cannot produce that. Something about the transition did the work, not the level. reader report

The negative got stronger, not weaker. A six-month full-dose trial with zero anxiolysis is a strong individual result. It is not “he might not have given it a chance.” It is “the drug does not work for him,” and every candidate that has to explain the escitalopram failure now has a harder job rather than an easier one.

And nobody has studied the thing that happened to him

The questionWhat exists
Is transient dissociation at a dose change a described phenomenon? A dose-transition syndrome is described: activation / jitteriness syndrome, “observed during the initiation or change of dose” (Arai 2023, PMID 38868737). His symptom is not in it. primary abstract only
What is on that syndrome's symptom list? The same eleven items in every study that operationalises it: anxiety, agitation, panic attacks, insomnia, irritability, hostility, aggressiveness, impulsivity, akathisia, hypomania, mania (Harada 2008, PMID 18781664). Dissociation is not on it. Depersonalisation is not on it. Derealisation is not on it. It is a syndrome of over-arousal, and he reports the opposite pole. primary abstract only
How well characterised is even that syndrome? Its systematic review (Sinclair 2009, PMID 19478285, 107 articles): “no validated rating scales,” incidence “varied widely from 4 to 65%,” and it “remains poorly characterised.” A 4-to-65% range is a confession, not an estimate. primary abstract only
Has anyone measured the dose-change half? No. The one prospective study with a time course required patients to be antidepressant-naive, so it studied initiation only and contains no dose-increase data at all (Harada 2014, PMID 25419134, n=301). The “or change of dose” half is asserted, not measured. primary, verified
Is there a literature on antidepressant-induced dissociation? It is empty. 44 PubMed hits, mostly burnout studies false-matching on the Maslach Inventory's unrelated “depersonalization” subscale. Narrowed properly: 4 hits, none on topic. No prevalence. No time course. No habituation data. no evidence exists

The confirmed prediction, and why it earns nothing

An earlier pass predicted in writing, before this correction arrived, that a dose increase should transiently re-open the acute window. He ran that experiment twice, unknowingly, and it passed both times.

Treat this as the chain's most suspicious moment rather than its most triumphant. It is a prediction about duloxetine confirmed by escitalopram, which is close to circular because the drug-difference is the very question this page exists to ask. It is n=1, retrospective, unblinded, on about a year of recall. And it is not discriminating: activation folklore predicts it, the gating ladder predicts it, and plain pharmacological intuition predicts it.

A prediction that all its rivals share selects nothing when it comes true. It moves no ranking, and it has not. reasoning, not evidence
