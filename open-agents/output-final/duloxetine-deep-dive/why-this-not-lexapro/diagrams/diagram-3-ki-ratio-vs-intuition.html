The Ki Ratio Against the Intuition

Why "the SNRI adds norepinephrine" cannot be the explanation, read off the drug's own binding constants.

What the old page title assumed

"Duloxetine is an SNRI and escitalopram is an SSRI. Duloxetine adds norepinephrine. The norepinephrine is the difference."

What the binding data say

Duloxetine binds the serotonin transporter roughly nine times more tightly than the norepinephrine transporter.

Ki, so the bar you want is the short one. Lower Ki means tighter binding.

The bar chart is the trap. Longer looks like more, and here longer means less. This is the single most inverted-in-summary fact in duloxetine's pharmacology, and an inverted Ki summary was caught circulating in secondary sources during this project.
9× more selective for serotonin
Bymaster 2001 states the ratio outright: "K(i) values of 7.5 and 0.8 nM, respectively, and with a K(i) ratio of 9."

The conclusion, and it survived every pass of this project: at 20mg, duloxetine is predominantly a serotonergic drug. Whatever worked, it probably was not the SNRI part. That is why this page was retitled.

The one rescue, stated fairly. 30% NET occupancy is not zero, and it could in principle be doing decisive work. But note what that rescue requires: no NET occupancy threshold exists for any clinical effect in any indication no evidence exists. So "the norepinephrine is enough" is unfalsifiable rather than supported. The honest status of the noradrenergic contribution at 20mg is "unknown, and not dismissible on occupancy grounds," which is a different thing from "the explanation."
