More gets in, less gets screened out, and nothing switches off. That is not a metaphor for sensory overwhelm. It is an electrophysiological description of one. reasoning, not evidence — the synthesis across the three rows is an interpretation; every individual row is primary and verified.

And the left-hand column is milder in exactly one specific way: the filter still works.

Why this is a candidate for him. His episodes are triggered by sensory overwhelm. Escitalopram gave him dissociation, non-functionality, and zero anxiolysis. A drug that measurably degrades the sensory filter is a candidate explanation for why that drug made things worse rather than better.

One thing no prior build quoted, from the paper usually cited as the null. Jensen 2008 verbatim: "Escitalopram did not affect P50 suppression but was found to increase P50 amplitude to the first (or conditioning) stimulus." At 10mg the filter held, and the response to the first click got bigger. That is "detects more," in the same paradigm, from the study on the intact side of the flip. primary, verified

It is what this project's own best-fitting candidate measured, and nobody noticed because nobody had put the two literatures on the same axis. reasoning, not evidence — the occupancy is modelled; the gating outcomes are primary

⚠ Four reasons the number is soft. The brief that commissioned this diagram states none of them.
  1. The fit misses its own held-out point by more than the step. Model 69.2% at 10mg against Klein's observed 64%: a residual of 5.2 points, which is larger than the 3.7-point step the argument rests on.
  2. Klein publishes its own escitalopram Emax and it is not the fitted one. Verbatim: "Emax was slightly higher after administration of citalopram (84%) than escitalopram (79%)." Refitting at Emax=79% gives a step of 2.4 to 3.3 points depending on the anchor. The honest range is roughly 2 to 4. primary, verified
  3. Jensen's and Oranje's volunteers were never scanned. The occupancies are imported from a different lab's subjects, using a different modality (SPECT) in a different region (midbrain). Nobody has measured occupancy in anyone who did a P50 experiment.
  4. Klein's observed data is the wrong shape for the model. 5→10mg is +4 points across a doubling; 10→20mg is +11 across the next. A hyperbola predicts the opposite. At n=5/arm with ±6 SD nothing resolves, but the model is being asked to do work the data does not clearly support.

What survives is qualitative and it is enough: on any version of the curve, a 1.5× dose step is small, and it flipped the filter.

The corollary, stated properly rather than as the brief states it

The brief says this proves "the gating effect cannot be a SERT-occupancy effect." It does not prove that. SERT is near its plateau at 10mg, so if a step of a few points flips the filter, then either the filter is exquisitely occupancy-sensitive in a range where nothing else is, or the effect runs through something unsaturated that still scales with concentration after occupancy has flattened. The second is more parsimonious. Either way, occupancy is the wrong axis. reasoning, not evidence

Which is the same conclusion the within-person control reaches from the other direction (diagram 1). Two arrivals at one finding is worth more than one, and it is also exactly when to check they are independent. They are: one is his own history, one is a Danish EEG lab, and neither knows about the other.

The caveats, stated at the claim and not below it

  1. This is a cross-study comparison presented as a dose-response. Two studies laid side by side, not one dose-response experiment. This project's single most repeated error is exactly that, and this candidate commits it. The mitigation is real but partial: same lab, paradigm, population, design, consecutive, author-framed as an extension. That is about as clean as cross-study gets, and it is still not within-study.
  2. No independent replication, and here that claim is true. All the escitalopram studies come from one group. Only three papers on serotonin and P50 gating in healthy volunteers exist in total: Jensen 2008 (raised serotonin), Oranje 2011 (raised serotonin), Mann 2008 (depleted it). Two labs, one of which never raised serotonin. primary, verified by enumeration
  3. The one independent lab got a mirror image, not a replication. See below.
  4. Every study is a single acute dose in a drug-naive healthy volunteer. A search specifically for chronic-dosing gating studies found none, so whether an acute gating cost persists is unanswered in the literature. no evidence exists ⚠ His corrected history is now the only datum on this in existence, and it changes the reading of this caveat. He reports the escitalopram cost faded after about a week at each dose change. A single acute dose in a drug-naive volunteer turns out to be a model of the first day of a dose change, which is exactly when he says the cost lands. So the studies' chief limitation is a match to the phenomenon's actual timescale rather than a mismatch. That is a genuine strengthening of this candidate, and it rests entirely on n=1, retrospective, unblinded, and on the wrong drug. reasoning, not evidence
  5. Escitalopram is not duloxetine, and n≈20 healthy males in an EEG lab is not him at a festival. The leap is enormous and nobody has bridged it. reasoning, not evidence
Caveat 3 in full, because it matters. Mann 2008 (PMID 17895917, Monash, n=16, double-blind placebo-controlled crossover): "selective serotonin depletion significantly disrupted PPI, but not P50 suppression." primary, verified

So lowering serotonin breaks PPI and spares P50. Raising serotonin breaks P50 and spares PPI. These are mirror images, and they do not describe a single dial. Mann does not replicate Oranje, because it never raised serotonin. It corroborates that serotonin modulates gating, and it refutes any simple "more serotonin equals worse gating" monotonic story. An inverted-U with a different measure on each limb is a more honest reading. reasoning, not evidence
