The whole top edge runs the wrong way

Everything that fits him well is on the left. The sensory gating ladder describes his experience most precisely and rests on one laboratory, three papers in existence, two studies laid side by side rather than one dose-response experiment, and not a single chronic dose administered to anyone. His own regional candidate has the best-established premise on the page, replicated across two laboratories, and zero data applying it to him or to duloxetine. The allosteric site has exactly the right shape, being the only candidate that is specifically and only about escitalopram, and it sits at the far left because it has been tested twice in vivo and mostly failed.

The one candidate on the right explains his divergence by saying that divergences of this size are ordinary. It is well measured, it is his own argument, and it removes the need for a mechanism rather than supplying one.

These do not combine into a verdict. They are not even competing for the same job.

Look at what is in the top-right quadrant

The top-right is where an answer would live: something that both fits him and is well evidenced. The only thing in it is a candidate whose content is "there is no specific answer here, only a distribution."

That is not a rhetorical trick. It is the actual state of the evidence, and it has a practical consequence: his own tracking is not a poor substitute for the literature on this question. It is the only measurement that exists. Nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults. Nobody has studied whether a previous drug-induced dissociation predicts the next one. Nobody has imaged duloxetine outside three brain regions in twenty years.

The scatter has no winner, and that is the output.
