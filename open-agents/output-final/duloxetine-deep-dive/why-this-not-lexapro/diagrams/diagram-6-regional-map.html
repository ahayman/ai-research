⚠ The “59 to 88%” figure has been mischaracterised throughout this project, and the correction matters because his own candidate is on the other end of it.

Every prior build of this page called escitalopram 10mg's 59–88% span a between-study range and read it as noise. Look at the chart. The bottom of the range is the insula, the hippocampus and the putamen. The top is the amygdala. It is not a study-to-study spread. It is a map. primary, verified

The span is regional structure and measurement noise confounded, and they cannot be separated, because the extreme regions are also the worst-measured ones: hippocampus ±23, temporal cortex ±23, insula ±15. Low-SERT cortical regions are where [11C]DASB has its poorest signal-to-noise. Both readings are partly right and neither is clean.

The conclusion is unaffected and the reason changes completely. Retiring the cross-study comparison never needed “it's all noise.” It needed “you cannot compare a midbrain number to a thalamus number,” which is a claim about regional structure, and the structure is real. So the same fact that kills the comparison is the fact his candidate is built on, and the previous build used “it's all noise” to dismiss the very heterogeneity he is now proposing as signal.

The three regions duloxetine has been measured in are not the regions dissociation lives in

The functional-imaging literature on depersonalisation and derealisation converges on the prefrontal cortex (Modesti 2022, PMID 36143190), the insula (Jia 2025, PMID 39935045; and the dissociative subtype of PTSD separates from PTSD on insula subregion connectivity with 80.4% accuracy, Harricharan 2020, PMID 31502268), and the anterior cingulate (Jia 2025). primary abstract only

Every one of those regions is marked “never measured” in the chart above. Duloxetine has been imaged in the thalamus, the midbrain and the striatum, and nowhere else, at any dose, in any study. The two regions where a clean comparison is possible show parity. The regions that would settle the question have never been looked at. primary, verified

“comparison of occupancy data from different brain regions is potentially not meaningful, as SERT is not equally distributed throughout the brain. It is therefore possible that the reported occupancy measures do not reflect those areas which are most important for patients, clinically. Whether blocking of SERT in some brain regions is of particular importance regarding treatment effect, adverse events, or withdrawal symptoms remains unresolved.”
Sørensen 2022, PMID 34548628 — from the review's own limitations section primary, verified — retrieved this pass

His dissociation is an adverse event. The systematic review this page leans on hardest says the question is open, without being asked, in its own limitations. It is not saying he is right. It is saying nobody has looked, and that is the strongest thing that can honestly be said for this candidate.

⚠ And here is the finding that runs against the candidate, from the same source. It is included because hunting the disconfirming study is this page's method.

Sørensen, verbatim: “For desvenlafaxine and escitalopram, the relationship between dose and occupancy appeared relatively consistent across brain regions; only for escitalopram, the plateau appeared to be reached at a slightly lower occupancy in the putamen, compared with other brain regions.” primary, verified

That is in direct tension with Kim 2017, which found escitalopram's occupancy significantly higher in the dorsal raphe than the putamen at p<0.001, with different curve shapes in the two regions (Hill 0.817 against 0.459). Both flag the putamen as the low region, so they agree on direction; they disagree on whether it matters. Kim is a dedicated serial-scan PK-PD study and is better powered for the question than Sørensen's visual read of overlay plots across heterogeneous studies. reasoning, not evidence But “relatively consistent” is the review's own summary of escitalopram's regional behaviour, and it belongs here rather than in a footnote.

Live. Well-formed. Untestable. Not unfalsifiable, and the difference is the whole verdict.

Not refuted — nothing contradicts it. Unfalsifiable as he states it — “one drug concentrating more somewhere” names no region and no direction, and 14 regions times 2 directions is 28 available post-hoc stories. A hypothesis that cannot lose is not doing work.

Sharply falsifiable the moment it is made specific. For example: “duloxetine's dorsal-raphe-to-striatum occupancy ratio is lower than escitalopram's at equivalent exposure, therefore less autoreceptor brake, therefore net projection-field serotonin differs in direction rather than degree.” That version names the region, the direction and the mechanism, and predicts a number. It needs one PET scan. Duloxetine has never been imaged in the dorsal raphe.

Two more things against its own interest. Baldinger runs against the intuitive version: escitalopram occupies the amygdala (88 ± 9%) and accumbens (81 ± 4%) more than average, so “escitalopram never reached the emotional regions” is not a story available to him. And it predicts nothing he can check: no diary entry, no sleep measure, no festival bears on it.

He asked whether this is answerable and wrote “perhaps this is not something we can answer.” He is right, and right for a specific and citable reason: duloxetine has been imaged in three brain regions in twenty years, and none of them is one where dissociation lives.
