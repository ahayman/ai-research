You wrote the conclusion of this project before it managed to reach one. Partway through the research, you told the chain of agents assembling it that it was solving the wrong problem, and you were right.

“Every study will have counter-evidence since for every measured effect, there will be some population that doesn't experience that effect, and even some population that may experience its opposite... Any reasonable hypothesis will always find a counter example in the population. That doesn't prove the hypothesis wrong; it just proves the hypothesis wrong for some of the population. The problem, to my mind, is you're trying to find a single explanation that can't be disproved instead of compiling a list of possibly valid explanations.”

“I think you've found several possibly valid mechanisms... And I don't think it's possible to get any more certain than 'possibly valid'. We simply do not understand the brain well enough to go beyond that.”

Eight research passes went into this deep dive, and every single one of them found real errors in the pass before it. A mechanism would get crowned, the next pass would kill it, and the pass after that would commit the same methodological sin in reverse. That is not a chain converging on truth. It is alternating over-correction, and what it produced was a sequence of obituaries rather than an answer. So the finding this whole hub is built on is the one you named yourself: no single mechanism explains you. That is not a hedge and it is not a failure of the search. It is what evidence looks like once you stop requiring it to name a winner.

There is a number underneath that conclusion, and it carries more weight than any mechanism sitting on top of it. A GSK group at Hammersmith gave duloxetine to ten people and fitted an individual EC50 for each one of them. Same drug, same dose, same protocol, same lab. The fitted values ranged across an 8.5-fold spread, from 0.54 to 4.61 ng/mL [primary, verified].

8.5×
Ten people. One drug. One dose. And eight and a half times the difference in how much of it it took to do the same job. The population curve does not describe any individual inside it, and that includes you.

Read what that does to every population number on this site. The dose-response curve printed in the label is an average across people who differ from each other by 8.5-fold, so it is a description of a crowd rather than of a person. Three things follow directly. It is why forums about this drug read like a shouting match between people who are all telling the truth. It is why “try it and see” is correct clinical practice rather than laziness or evasion. And it is why your escitalopram result and your duloxetine result pointing in opposite directions is expected rather than anomalous, because the same drug at the same dose already produces an 8.5-fold range in ten healthy volunteers before anyone changes the molecule.

The fact everything here orbits

You spent five hours or more at two crowded festivals. You had two panic attacks, with your heart rate above 140 at rest. Historically that is a guaranteed trigger, because one day at a theme park has reliably bought you a one to two week episode: dissociation, then severe anxiety, then intense depression, with no negative thoughts attached to any of it.

The acute overwhelm was worse than it used to be. The crash never came.

Your family braced for it and it did not arrive, and that matters more than it might appear, because it means the absence was anticipated independently rather than noticed by you afterwards. So there are two effects, moving in opposite directions, from one drug, in one person, on one day.

That shape does real work. It rules out sedation, because sedation cannot sharpen the acute experience while removing the aftermath. It rules out emotional blunting, because blunting mutes globally and one of these two things got louder. It rules out every global-muting story for the same reason, since a single dial cannot move two readings in opposite directions. And it is not the shape expectancy predicts either, because expectancy predicts the acute experience would feel better, and it felt worse. The sign is wrong.

So the question this project is actually chasing is not why the episodes stopped. It is why the overwhelm is decoupled from the crash.

Three literatures, and nobody has joined them

The best available account of that decoupling is not one thing, and you are entitled to see it stated that way rather than dressed up as a theory.

The acute half has exactly one candidate that predicts it. In a single Copenhagen lab, escitalopram at 10mg left P50 sensory gating intact, and escitalopram at 15mg disrupted it, with the same paradigm, the same design, and the same population [primary, verified]. A 1.5-fold dose step flipped the filter. Mismatch negativity was raised at both doses, so the phenotype is: detects more, filters less, stops responding more slowly. That is a fair sketch of a festival getting worse. It is human and it is dose-graded, which are the two things almost nothing else here is. It is also a single lab, every one of the four studies is a single acute dose in a drug-naive volunteer, so whether the cost persists across weeks is simply unmeasured, and the leap from a P50 waveform to a crowd is enormous and unbridged.

The crash half has exactly one candidate whose native timescale is your timescale. In Chattarji's rats, one two-hour stressor produces amygdala spine growth and anxiety ten days later, explicitly dissociable from the acute response. In Musazzi's, chronic antidepressants at fourteen days, venlafaxine among them, completely prevent the stress glutamate surge while corticosterone still rises normally [primary, verified]. That is the shape of your report: the stressor lands, and the sequela does not. But it is animals, the join between those two labs has never been tested, the regions do not even match, since Musazzi measured prefrontal cortex while Chattarji's effect is in the amygdala, Chattarji's vehicle gavage was also protective, and it predicts your acute experience would be intact when you report it got worse.

The sleep half has two candidates, and the better one is dull. Duloxetine at a fixed 20mg beat placebo on anxiety in an actual randomised trial, which nothing else on the list can say at your dose [primary, verified], and fewer night-time awakenings would explain the vivid dreams and the reduced morning fatigue with a single mechanism. Its own worst problem is that escitalopram gave you no anxiolysis whatsoever, so it cannot explain why the previous antidepressant did nothing.

None of those three literatures has ever been connected to either of the other two, and none of them has been tested in a person resembling you. So the composite is the answer. Presenting it as a unified theory would be the precise error that eight passes kept making.

What nobody has studied

The evidence-absences are the most honest thing this project produced, and they should not be softened into caveats. Nobody has studied duloxetine in autistic adults, because the entire literature is one three-patient case series. Nobody has studied the overwhelm-to-crash cycle pharmacologically, although the cycle itself is described in a primary clinical consensus document using your own word for it. Nobody has studied whether a prior dissociative reaction predicts a later one, in either direction. And the largest of them: nobody has studied this decoupling in any human, for any drug. The final research pass added two more, because no MSLT study of duloxetine or of any SNRI exists at any dose, and nobody has ever recorded a locus coeruleus neuron on chronic duloxetine in an awake animal.

An absence is not a negative, and the difference is load-bearing here. It means the honest ceiling on confidence is “possibly valid,” which is exactly where you put it before the research did. It also means a null average borrowed from an adjacent population is weak evidence about you specifically, given that 8.5-fold spread. What the absences genuinely rule out is anyone, this deep dive included, telling you what is happening to you.

The dose question, and why this page will not answer it

The two effects you care about sit on different dose curves. That is the entire decision, and nearly everything else is commentary.

Pain has a threshold at roughly 40 to 60mg rather than a slope, and you are underneath it. Below 40mg duloxetine is 0-for-3 on pain across about 350 patients, and twice those trials were adequately powered, so the usual “underpowered” excuse is unavailable. Cochrane's own authors write that doses of 60mg and 120mg daily are efficacious and lower daily doses are not [primary, verified]. But the payoff at 60mg is modest and the honest figure needs its own caveat carried with it: the fibromyalgia-specific number is NNTB 11, the evidence is graded LOW, and the review is Lilly-funded. Its “no clinically relevant benefit” verdict is quotable but it should not travel unqualified, because that same review missed its own relevance cutoff by a single point and calls NNTB 10 clinically relevant. Eleven versus ten is not a fact about your body.

The antidepressant effect is flat, and that one is not close. Non-remitters at 60mg were randomised either to stay or to escalate to 120mg, and remission came out at 30.0% versus 30.5% [primary, verified]. The gain was time, not milligrams. A meta-analysis of antidepressant dose escalation puts the effect at Hedges g = −0.04, with significantly more dropouts from side effects on the higher dose. That says nothing about pain, because pain is the curve with the threshold, and conflating them is how this gets muddled.

On sleep, the verified answer is narrower than the worry. In the only duloxetine trial with posted results containing a fixed 20mg arm, insomnia actually falls across your step, from 10.17% to 8.62% (p=0.78). Somnolence is flat, 5.08% to 4.31% (p=1.00). Fatigue is the only thing that moves, and the 20 to 60mg contrast there is unresolved: 0/59 versus 7/116, Fisher p=0.0969, because the 20mg arm was 59 people with zero events. What is solid in that same trial is 60mg against placebo on fatigue, 0/117 versus 7/116, p=0.0069. So the honest summary is that the direct comparison you need has never been adequately powered, and you are braced for insomnia when the dose-linked signal in the wider literature is somnolence.

This page does not recommend a dose, and there is a specific reason to distrust it if it did. Four of the five corrections issued against this project's own brief all pushed in the same direction, toward “don't increase.” That bias is documented inside the corpus. It is evidence about the chain that assembled the research; it is not evidence about your dose. A hub that quietly inherited it would be committing the exact failure eight passes have been learning to catch. So this page does not recommend staying at 20mg, and it does not recommend going up. Category 12 prepares the conversation. It does not pre-empt it.

There is one thing worth understanding before that conversation, because it is structural rather than advisory. Your own hypothesis, that the crashes aggravate your ME/CFS and fibromyalgia and that preventing them should improve pain slowly, and the standard model, that pain needs the noradrenergic threshold, make different predictions now and identical predictions at 60mg. If pain improves at 60mg, both models are satisfied, and you will never learn which was right. The 20mg window is the only clean test of your hypothesis that will ever exist. That is not an argument for staying, since information is not the only thing worth having and pain matters to you too. It is an argument for knowing what the choice costs, and for noticing that it has a cost on both sides rather than one.

Four things worth doing before the appointment

Ask what your transferrin saturation is. You started oral iron at ferritin around 70, which is correct, because the IRLSSG threshold is 75 or below. But iron is contraindicated above 45% transferrin saturation, and ferritin cannot tell you what your saturation is. Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant, so with fibromyalgia and ME/CFS it can read falsely high, which makes the one number you have the least reliable one to lean on. This is a full morning fasting panel, and the real-world failure is not ordering it. It is not acting on it.

Ask whether he will write a compounded liquid when the time comes. The taper cliff sits below 20mg, not above it, and there is no capsule underneath 20mg, so you are already standing on it. Going up does not put you there. That means “going up makes coming down harder” is a weak argument and you should not be persuaded by it. But asking the question now, while it is still hypothetical and costs nothing, is a strong move, because the answer shapes what your options look like later.

Try to nap, and run the full twenty minutes. You have been running an aborted version of a standard sleep test your whole life, since the protocol allows twenty minutes and you quit at ten to twenty. Your lifelong “hard zero” may in fact be a latency of eighteen minutes that you have never once let run. A number can move; a “no” cannot. Do it at 20mg, before anything changes, because that window closes at the dose change and does not reopen. Know the asymmetry before you start: a clean repeatable success is genuinely informative, while a failure teaches you almost nothing, because duloxetine improved your sleep, which lowers sleep drive, which makes napping harder. The drug pushes the outcome both ways at once, so a null is compatible with both hypotheses. Run it anyway, but do not read a null as a refutation.

Notice the timing. Your appointment lands at roughly four weeks on the drug, and most of the evidence that would inform it arrives afterwards. Iron takes about twelve weeks to act. The crash-protection question is counted in exposures rather than in weeks. Against a baseline of episodes several times a month, four weeks is a thin observation window. So the decision has to be made on less information than it deserves, and that is a fact about the calendar rather than a failure of preparation.

What all of this adds up to is smaller and more useful than a verdict. The drug is doing something real, because the acute effect went the wrong way for a placebo and the absent crash was called in advance by people other than you. Nobody can tell you what that something is, since the honest account is three unjoined literatures and six things nobody has studied, and the field's own numbers say a population curve was never going to describe you anyway. Both of the effects you value are real, and they sit on different dose curves, which is why the decision in front of you is a genuine trade rather than a puzzle with a hidden answer. So the useful posture is the one you arrived with: hold the candidates as candidates, watch the things that would falsify each of them, ask the two questions that are time-sensitive, run the nap while the window is open, and let the dose be a decision you and your doctor make rather than one this research pretends to have made for you.

Reading the provenance tags

These appear inline, inside the sentence, on every load-bearing claim across all twelve pages, and never in a footnote. That is deliberate: this chain's signature failure was filing caveats in appendices while headlines spent unearned certainty.

[primary, verified] The paper was retrieved and the claim is in it. PMIDs were checked against NCBI directly, because a resolving PMID is not verification.
[secondary — unverified] A review asserts it and the primary was never reached. Two AI-generated sources and two publisher pages with reversed author order were caught in this corpus.
[reasoning, not evidence] An inference. No study says this. The premises may be verified while the join is not.
[no evidence exists] Searched, and nothing found. This is a finding rather than a gap.
