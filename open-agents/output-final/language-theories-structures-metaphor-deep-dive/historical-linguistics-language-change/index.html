Language: Theories, Structures & Metaphor

Historical Linguistics and Language Change

How Languages Transform -- and Why They All Transform the Same Way

Validated Research: Of 54 distinct factual claims examined, 49 confirmed, 5 rated uncertain (precise attributions / scholarly nuance), 0 invalid. Minor corrections applied. Research reliability rated 9/10.

Key Takeaways

  1. Language change is systematic, not random. Sound changes follow exceptionless laws; semantic changes follow predictable directional patterns; grammaticalization follows universal pathways.
  2. The same changes happen independently in unrelated languages. The same sound shifts, grammaticalization pathways, and metaphorical extensions recur across language families with no contact -- pointing to deep constraints in human cognition.
  3. Metaphor is the engine of abstraction in language. Virtually all abstract vocabulary derives historically from concrete, bodily, physical vocabulary through metaphorical extension. "Comprehend" comes from Latin "to seize." This is not poetry; it is the mechanism.
  4. Grammaticalization is metaphor fossilized into grammar. When "go" becomes a future tense marker, or "head" becomes "on top of," metaphor is constructing language itself.
  5. Languages are related in families, like biological species. The comparative method reconstructs ancestral languages and traces divergence through time -- within a window of roughly 8,000-10,000 years.

1. Overview

Languages change. No language spoken today sounds the way it did a thousand years ago. The question is not whether languages change, but how -- and the answer turns out to be one of the most pattern-rich domains in all of science.

Historical linguisticsThe study of language change over time and the reconstruction of ancestral languages through systematic comparison. is the study of language change over time -- the discipline that figured out how to reconstruct languages no one has heard for five thousand years and to prove that English and Hindi are distant cousins. What makes it especially relevant to this deep dive is a discovery that connects it directly to the theory of conceptual metaphor: the same patterns of change happen independently, again and again, in unrelated languages across the world. The same sounds shift in the same directions. The same kinds of words become the same kinds of grammar. And the engine driving most of these changes is metaphor.

2. The Comparative Method: Reading the Past from the Present

The Core Idea

The comparative methodThe technique of comparing systematic sound correspondences across related languages to reconstruct their common ancestor (proto-language). is the foundational technique of historical linguistics. Its logic is elegant: if two or more languages share systematic, recurring correspondences in their sounds, words, and grammar that cannot be explained by chance or borrowing, then those languages must descend from a common ancestor. By cataloging those correspondences, linguists can work backward to reconstruct the ancestral language -- a proto-languageA hypothetical reconstructed ancestor from which a group of related languages descended. Marked with an asterisk (*). -- that no one alive has ever heard.

Reconstructed forms are written with an asterisk (*) to mark them as hypothetical. When you see a form like PIE *pater-, it means "we have never found this word written down or heard it spoken, but the evidence from the daughter languages tells us it must have existed."

How It Works

The method proceeds through four stages:

Step 1 -- Assemble cognatesWords in different languages that share a common etymological origin. English "father" and Latin "pater" are cognates.. Cognates are words in different languages that descend from the same ancestral word. Latin pater, Greek pater, Sanskrit pitar, Gothic fadar, and English father all descend from PIE *ph2ter-.

Step 2 -- Establish sound correspondencesA systematic, recurring pattern of sound relationships between related languages (e.g., Latin p- regularly corresponds to English f-).. Genuine genetic relationships produce regular, systematic patterns. Wherever Latin has initial p-, English regularly has f-: pater/father, piscis/fish, pes/foot, plenus/full. One pair could be coincidence. Four pairs in the same pattern cannot be -- and this pattern holds across hundreds of additional word pairs.

Step 3 -- Reconstruct proto-forms. Using the correspondence sets, determine what the ancestral sound must have been and how it changed differently in each daughter language.

Step 4 -- Formulate sound laws. State the sequence of regular changes as explicit rules that must account for every cognate -- no unexplained exceptions permitted.

What Reconstruction Reveals

The reconstructed PIE lexicon tells us about culture through vocabulary. PIE had words for *kwekwlos ("wheel"), *h1ekwos ("horse"), *yugom ("yoke"), and *h2eyos ("copper/bronze") -- but no word for "iron." This places PIE speakers in a late Neolithic to early Bronze Age context, approximately 4500-2500 BCE. You can date a dead language by what its speakers had words for.

The Time Barrier

Beyond roughly 8,000-10,000 years, the signal of common ancestry degrades to the point where reliable reconstruction becomes impossible. This is why proposed "macrofamilies" like Nostratic and the speculative Proto-World hypothesis remain deeply controversial.

3. Sound Change: The Engine of Divergence

Sound change is the primary mechanism by which a single language splits into multiple descendants. When communities separate, their speech drifts in different directions. Over centuries, the accumulated changes produce dialects, then mutually unintelligible languages, then entire language familiesA group of languages descended from a common ancestor (proto-language), related by regular sound correspondences and shared innovations..

The Neogrammarian Hypothesis

The Neogrammarians (Junggrammatiker), a group of scholars at the University of Leipzig around 1875, established the foundational principle. August Leskien's formulation was bold and testable:

"Sound laws admit no exceptions."

When a sound change occurs, it affects every word containing the relevant sound in the relevant environment. If PIE *p became Proto-Germanic *f at the beginning of words, it did so in every single word that started with *p. Not most words. Every word.

When apparent exceptions appear, they must be explained by another regular sound law operating in a different environment, by analogyThe process by which speakers regularize irregular forms by extending dominant patterns. "Helped" replacing "holp" is an example. (speakers extending dominant patterns), by dialect borrowing, or by sporadic changes (genuinely rare). The regularity of sound change is what makes reconstruction possible.

Grimm's Law: The Discovery That Started It All

Grimm's LawThe systematic sound shift by which PIE stop consonants changed in Proto-Germanic: voiceless stops became fricatives, voiced stops became voiceless stops, aspirated stops became voiced stops. (the First Germanic Consonant Shift) was the first sound change described as a systematic process. Friedrich von Schlegel noticed the Latin p / Germanic f correspondence in 1806; Rasmus Rask systematized the consonant correspondences between Germanic and Greek in 1818; Jacob Grimm formalized the full pattern in 1822.

The shift consists of three phases that form a chain shiftA series of linked sound changes in which the movement of one sound triggers the movement of others, as in Grimm's Law and the Great Vowel Shift. -- each phase pushes sounds into the space vacated by the previous one:

Everything moves one step around a cycle. Voiceless stops become fricatives; voiced stops fill the gap; aspirated stops fill the next gap. This is a push chain: each change forces the next. The significance extends far beyond the specific content -- it demonstrated that sound change is systematic rather than accidental, creating the entire field of historical phonology.

Verner's Law: The Exception That Proved the Rule

Apparent exceptions to Grimm's Law -- cases where PIE voiceless stops became voiced fricatives instead of the expected voiceless ones -- were solved by Karl Verner in 1875. He discovered that voicing occurred when the preceding syllable was unstressed in PIE. Since PIE had mobile stress but Proto-Germanic later fixed stress on the initial syllable, the conditioning environment was obscured. You had to look at Sanskrit and Greek, which preserved the old stress patterns, to see the rule.

What looked like chaos was deeper order. The "exceptions" followed their own perfectly regular rule. The problem was not irregularity -- the conditioning environment had been erased by a subsequent change.

The Great Vowel Shift: When English Stopped Sounding Like English

The Great Vowel Shift (GVS) was a series of dramatic, parallel changes in the long vowels of English, occurring roughly from the 15th to the 18th century. The term was coined by the Danish linguist Otto Jespersen (1860-1943). The GVS is the boundary between Middle English (which sounded roughly like modern Italian or Spanish to our ears) and Modern English.

The GVS is the primary reason English spelling appears so chaotic. Spellings were largely standardized during the Middle English period, before the pronunciation shifted. English spelling is not irrational -- it is a fossil record of an older pronunciation system, preserved in amber while the living language moved on.

Chain Shifts: The Pattern Behind the Pattern

Both Grimm's Law and the Great Vowel Shift are examples of chain shifts -- sets of linked changes where the movement of one sound triggers the movement of others. There are two types:

  • Pull chains (drag chains): One sound moves, leaving a gap. Another sound moves to fill the gap, creating a new gap, and so on.
  • Push chains: One sound encroaches on the territory of another, forcing the second to move to avoid merging. The first change "pushes" subsequent changes.

Whether the GVS was a push chain or a pull chain is still debated. But the critical point is the existence of the pattern itself: sounds do not change in isolation. They change as part of systems. Sound systems behave like ecosystems.

The Recurring Types of Sound Change

Beyond the dramatic chain shifts, the same kinds of sound changes recur independently across unrelated languages:

  • Assimilation: A sound becomes more like its neighbor. Latin in- + possibilis became impossibilis because /n/ assimilated to /m/ before /p/.
  • DissimilationA sound change in which a sound becomes less similar to a nearby similar sound. Latin peregrinus became English pilgrim (first /r/ becomes /l/).: A sound becomes less like a nearby similar sound. Latin peregrinus became English pilgrim, with the first /r/ dissimilating to /l/.
  • Deletion (elision): Loss of a sound, especially in unstressed positions. Latin calidu became Spanish caldo.
  • EpenthesisThe insertion of a sound into a word, often to ease articulatory transitions. Latin humilis became English humble (insertion of /b/).: Insertion of a sound to ease transitions. Latin humilis became English humble through insertion of /b/ between /m/ and /l/.
  • MetathesisA sound change in which sounds switch positions within a word. Old English thridda became Modern English third.: Sounds switch positions. Old English thridda became Modern English third.
  • LenitionThe "weakening" of a consonant, typically from a stop to a fricative, or from voiceless to voiced.: Consonant weakening. Latin vita became Spanish vida, with voiceless /t/ becoming voiced /d/.

These categories recur because they are driven by the physics of articulation and the constraints of human perception. Articulatory ease, perceptual distinctiveness, and the need to maintain contrast are universal -- so the same kinds of changes happen everywhere.

Sturtevant's Paradox: The Deep Irony

A profound irony lies at the heart of historical linguistics, formulated by Edgar H. Sturtevant in 1947:

"Sound change is regular but produces irregularity; analogy is irregular but produces regularity."

Regular sound change, applied blindly to every word that meets its conditions, disrupts previously transparent patterns. Analogy works in reverse: speakers regularize irregular forms by extending the dominant pattern. English "helped" replaced the older form "holp" by analogy with the regular past tense pattern.

The irregular verbs that survive in modern languages (was/were, go/went, be/am/is/are) are typically the most frequently used. They are heard so often that speakers memorize them individually, which protects them from analogical regularization. This is why the irregular verbs in every language are concentrated among the most basic, everyday words.

4. Grammaticalization: When Words Become Grammar

The Core Process

GrammaticalizationThe process by which content words gradually become grammatical function words, clitics, and ultimately inflectional affixes. is the process by which ordinary content words -- words with concrete, lexical meanings -- gradually transform into grammatical function words, then into clitics, and ultimately into inflectional affixes. Hopper and Traugott (2003) formalized this as the cline of grammaticalization:

content word → grammatical word → clitic → inflectional affix

This cline operates both as a snapshot (different forms at different stages coexist) and as a historical trajectory. The process involves four concurrent changes:

  • Semantic bleachingLoss of concrete lexical meaning during grammaticalization, as a form shifts from specific content to abstract grammatical function.: The concrete lexical meaning fades. A word that once meant something specific comes to mean something abstract and functional.
  • Phonetic erosionReduction in the phonological substance of a form during grammaticalization (e.g., "going to" becomes "gonna").: The word shrinks. It loses syllables, stress, and phonetic substance.
  • DecategorializationLoss of the morphosyntactic properties of a word's original class during grammaticalization (e.g., a noun losing its ability to be pluralized as it becomes a preposition).: The word loses the grammatical properties of its original word class.
  • ObligatorificationThe process during grammaticalization by which a form becomes increasingly obligatory in its grammatical context.: The form becomes increasingly obligatory.

The Textbook Example: "Going to"

The verb phrase "be going to" originally described literal physical movement toward a destination: "I am going to market to buy fish." Over centuries, the construction was reanalyzed as a marker of future intention: "I am going to study tonight." No physical movement implied. The spatial meaning has been bleached away; what remains is futurity.

Phonetic erosion reduced the form further: going to → gonna → 'mma ("I'mma do it"). Each reduction moves the form further along the cline.

This grammaticalization is not a quirk of English. The path motion verb → purposive → future tense marker is one of the most well-documented cross-linguistic pathways. Constructions involving "go" and "come" have independently become future markers in dozens of unrelated languages, including French (aller + infinitive), many West African languages, and numerous others. The same metaphor -- future time is a destination you move toward -- produces the same grammar, independently, across the world.

Other English examples:

  • "Will": Old English willan meant "to want, to desire." Over centuries it grammaticalized into the future auxiliary will, losing its volitional meaning. The phonetic reduction to 'll ("I'll go") represents further erosion along the cline.
  • "Have" (perfect aspect): The possessive verb "have" grammaticalized into the perfect aspect marker. Originally "I have a letter written" meant "I possess a letter that has been written." Reanalysis produced "I have written a letter," where "have" is an auxiliary.

This Is Metaphor in Action

Grammaticalization is not just a mechanical process of erosion and reduction. At every step, it is driven by metaphorical extension from concrete to abstract domains.

When "go" (a verb of physical motion through space) becomes a future tense marker, the underlying conceptual metaphor is TIME IS SPACE. When "will" (a verb of desire/volition) becomes a future marker, the underlying metaphor is INTENTION IS FUTURITY.

The body-part-to-spatial-preposition pathway is particularly striking:

The underlying metaphor is THE WORLD IS A BODY -- the spatial structure of the environment is understood through the template of the human body. This metaphor is not cultural -- it recurs independently across unrelated language families on every inhabited continent.

The Unidirectionality Hypothesis

A central finding about grammaticalization is that the process is unidirectionalThe principle that grammaticalization and semantic change consistently move from concrete to abstract, from lexical to grammatical, and from free to bound -- but not in reverse.: forms move from lexical to grammatical, from concrete to abstract, from freer to more bound -- but not in reverse. Antoine Meillet first articulated this in 1912, and it has been overwhelmingly confirmed.

Content words become function words. Function words become affixes. Affixes erode further and disappear. But affixes do not spontaneously become independent words. Grammar does not degrammaticalize.

5. Semantic Change: How Words Shift Meaning Through Metaphor

The Six Types of Semantic Change

Words change their meanings over time through several well-documented processes:

Broadening (generalization): A word's meaning expands from specific to general.

  • Old English docga: "a specific powerful breed" → Modern English dog: any canine
  • Latin arripare: "to arrive at the shore" → English arrive: to reach any destination
  • Bird originally meant "young bird, chick" → any bird

NarrowingSemantic change in which a word's meaning becomes more specific. "Deer" (from "any animal" to a specific animal family). (specialization): A word's meaning contracts from general to specific.

  • Old English deor: "any animal" → Modern English deer: a specific family
  • Old English mete: "food" → Modern English meat: flesh of animals (original meaning survives in sweetmeat)
  • Starve originally meant "to die" (of any cause) → "to die of hunger"

AmeliorationSemantic change in which a word's meaning shifts in a more positive direction. "Nice" (from "ignorant" to "pleasant") is a classic example.: A word's meaning shifts more positively.

  • Latin nescius "ignorant" → English nice (through "foolish" → "delicate" → "pleasant" -- a journey of eight centuries)
  • Knight: "boy, servant" → "noble warrior"

PejorationSemantic change in which a word's meaning shifts in a more negative direction. "Silly" (from "blessed" to "foolish").: A word's meaning shifts more negatively.

  • Old English selig "blessed, happy" → Modern English silly (through "innocent" → "harmless" → "foolish")
  • Villain: "farm laborer" (Latin villanus) → "wicked person"
  • Knave: "boy, servant" → "dishonest person"

Metaphor: Meaning change based on perceived similarity.

  • Broadcast: "to cast seeds widely" → "to transmit signals"
  • Web: "spider's silk" → "interconnected network" → "the World Wide Web"
  • Grasp: "to seize physically" → "to understand"

Metonymy: Meaning change based on association or contiguity.

  • Crown: "royal headgear" → "the monarchy itself"
  • Tongue: "organ of speech" → "language"

Sweetser's Three Domains: The Architecture of Semantic Change

Eve Sweetser's landmark work From Etymology to Pragmatics (1990) demonstrated that semantic change follows systematic metaphorical patterns. Her central insight was that word meanings extend predictably across three domains:

  1. Sociophysical domain: The external, physical world of objects, actions, and social forces
  2. Mental/epistemic domain: The internal world of knowledge, belief, and reasoning
  3. Speech act domain: The world of conversational interaction and communication

The critical finding: meanings consistently flow from domain 1 to domain 2 to domain 3 (concrete to abstract), almost never in reverse.

Perception verbs illustrate the pattern: See (physical vision, domain 1) → "understand" (domain 2) → "see to it that" (speech act, domain 3). This recurs across Indo-European and beyond.

Modal verbs follow the same trajectory: Must (physical obligation, domain 1) → epistemic necessity ("it must be raining" = logical conclusion, domain 2). The direction is always obligation → logical necessity, never reverse.

Conjunctions show it too: Since (temporal, domain 1) → causal ("since you asked," domain 2). While (temporal, domain 1) → concessive ("while I agree...," domain 2).

Body-to-Mind Mappings: Where Abstract Vocabulary Comes From

One of the most striking findings of historical semantics is how pervasively abstract vocabulary derives from concrete, bodily experience. As Giambattista Vico observed: "Words are carried over from bodies and from the properties of bodies to express the things of the mind and spirit."

Understanding is physical grasping: Comprehend from Latin comprehendere "to seize"; grasp an idea; catch the meaning; get the point; apprehend from Latin apprehendere "to seize."

Knowing is seeing: I see = I understand; insight = seeing inward; illuminate a topic; clear or obscure; perspective, point of view, outlook.

Obeying is hearing: Obey from Latin obedire (ob- "toward" + audire "to hear"). The same pattern in Greek: hypakouō "obey" from hypo + akouō "hear."

Knowing is holding: Contain knowledge; retain information; hold a belief; an idea is in one's mind.

These are systematic, cross-linguistic patterns. Languages that have never been in contact independently develop the same metaphorical equations. This reveals something fundamental: our entire vocabulary for mental life is built on metaphorical extensions from physical experience. Every time you say "I see what you mean" or "Let me grasp that concept," you are using a fossil metaphor -- a metaphorical extension so deeply embedded it feels literal.

Proto-Indo-European: The Model Case

Proto-Indo-European is the most extensively reconstructed proto-language. The reconstruction reveals:

Phonology: Three series of stop consonants, three "laryngeal" consonants (*h1, *h2, *h3), and a system of ablautSystematic vowel alternation within a word root that serves grammatical functions. English preserves traces in sing/sang/sung, drive/drove/driven. -- systematic vowel alternation that served grammatical functions.

Morphology: Eight cases for nouns, three genders, three numbers, and a complex verb system with aspect, mood, and voice distinctions.

Homeland: The Kurgan hypothesis (Gimbutas, 1956) places PIE speakers in the Pontic-Caspian steppes around 4500-2500 BCE. The Anatolian hypothesis (Renfrew, 1987) proposes an earlier Anatolian origin. Recent ancient DNA evidence has increasingly supported the steppe hypothesis.

How to Tell Genetic Relationship from Contact

  • Core vocabulary resistance: Basic vocabulary (body parts, kinship, numerals, pronouns) strongly resists borrowing. The Swadesh listA standardized list of approximately 100-200 basic concepts (body parts, kinship terms, numerals, pronouns) that tend to resist borrowing and are reliable indicators of genetic relationship. formalizes ~100-200 such concepts.
  • Shared innovations: Only novel changes shared by a subset of languages prove subgrouping. Shared retentions do not.
  • Systematic sound correspondences: Genuine relationships produce regular, predictable patterns across hundreds of word pairs.
  • Grammatical morphology: Inflectional morphology is rarely borrowed. Shared patterns are strong evidence.

When Languages Meet: Contact, Pidgins, and Sprachbunds

SprachbundsA group of geographically proximate languages that share structural features due to prolonged contact rather than common ancestry (also called a "linguistic area"). (linguistic areas) are perhaps the most fascinating outcome: geographically proximate languages that share structural features through prolonged contact, regardless of genetic affiliation.

The Balkan Sprachbund is the classic example. Albanian, Romanian, Bulgarian, Macedonian, Greek, and Balkan Turkish share postposed definite articles, avoidance of the infinitive, and merger of genitive and dative cases -- despite belonging to different branches (and Turkish being non-IE entirely).

The South Asian Linguistic Area (Emeneau, 1956) shows similar convergence between genetically unrelated Indo-Aryan and Dravidian languages: retroflex consonants, SOV word order, echo word formation.

The Deep-Time Question

Attempts to push beyond established families into "macrofamilies" remain deeply controversial. The Nostratic hypothesis (Pedersen, 1903; Illich-Svitych, 1960s) posits a super-family with Proto-Nostratic spoken around 15,000-12,000 BCE. The even more speculative Proto-World hypothesis proposes a single ancestor for all human languages.

The fundamental problem is the time barrier. At such depths, the comparative method cannot distinguish genuine cognates from chance resemblances. Most historical linguists consider these hypotheses untestable with current methods -- though whether all human languages share a common origin remains one of the great open problems in the field.

7. Universal Patterns: Why Languages Change the Same Way

The Central Mystery

Here is the fact that ties everything together: the same kinds of changes happen independently in unrelated languages, across every inhabited continent, throughout recorded history. This is not coincidence. It is a pattern, and it demands explanation.

Recurrent Sound Changes

  • Lenition (consonant weakening between vowels) occurs independently in Romance, Celtic, Japanese, and many other families. Physics favors weakening between vowels.
  • Final consonant deletion or devoicing occurs in German, Russian, Turkish, Thai, and many others.
  • Vowel reduction in unstressed syllables has occurred independently in English, Russian, Portuguese, and many others.
  • Palatalization -- velar/dental consonants becoming palatal before front vowels -- occurred independently in Romance, Slavic, Bantu, and many others.

The Directionality of Semantic Change

Perhaps the most profound pattern is the consistent directionality across all documented languages:

  1. Spatial → Temporal: Space words become time words. "Before" (in front of) → "before" (earlier than). This never reverses.
  2. Physical → Mental: "Grasp" (seize) → "grasp" (understand). "See" (perceive) → "see" (know). This never reverses.
  3. Concrete → Abstract: Concrete vocabulary provides the raw material for abstract vocabulary.
  4. External → Internal: Words for external perception extend to internal cognition. Physical force extends to social obligation, then logical necessity.

These directional patterns are near-exceptionless rules, confirmed across thousands of cases in hundreds of languages. They all point in the same direction: from the body outward, from the concrete upward, from the physical inward.

What the Patterns Mean

The recurring patterns reveal that historical linguistics and cognitive linguistics are studying the same phenomenon from different temporal perspectives:

  • Cognitive linguistics (Lakoff, Johnson, Sweetser) shows that our conceptual system is structured by metaphorical mappings -- as a snapshot of the present.
  • Historical linguistics shows that these same mappings drive the creation of abstract vocabulary and grammar -- as a movie of the past.

Grammaticalization is the diachronic process that creates the synchronic structures cognitive linguists study. The two perspectives converge on a single insight:

Metaphor is not decoration on language. It is the mechanism by which language grows.

Every abstract word you use today -- comprehend, insight, perspective, consider, reflect, contain, retain, grasp, illuminate, clear, hold -- was once a concrete, physical word that underwent metaphorical extension. The history of every language is, at its deepest level, a history of metaphors becoming so entrenched that we forget they were metaphors at all.

Reduction in the phonological substance of a form during grammaticalization (e.g., "going to" → "gonna").

Pidgin

A simplified contact language used for limited purposes (typically trade), combining elements of multiple source languages. Not anyone's native language.

Proto-language

A hypothetical reconstructed ancestor from which a group of related languages descended. Marked with an asterisk (*).

Pull chain (drag chain)

A chain shift in which one sound moves from its position, leaving a gap that attracts another sound to fill it.

Push chain

A chain shift in which one sound encroaches on the territory of another, forcing the second to move.

Semantic bleaching

Loss of concrete lexical meaning during grammaticalization.

Sound correspondence

A systematic, recurring pattern of sound relationships between related languages (e.g., Latin p- regularly corresponds to English f-).

Sprachbund (linguistic area)

A group of geographically proximate languages that share structural features due to prolonged contact rather than common ancestry.

Sturtevant's Paradox

The observation that sound change is regular but produces irregularity, while analogy is irregular but produces regularity.

Swadesh list

A standardized list of approximately 100-200 basic concepts that tend to resist borrowing and are reliable indicators of genetic relationship.

Unidirectionality

The principle that grammaticalization and semantic change consistently move from concrete to abstract, from lexical to grammatical, and from free to bound -- but not in reverse.

Verner's Law

The discovery that apparent exceptions to Grimm's Law were conditioned by PIE stress placement: voiceless fricatives became voiced when the preceding syllable was unstressed.
