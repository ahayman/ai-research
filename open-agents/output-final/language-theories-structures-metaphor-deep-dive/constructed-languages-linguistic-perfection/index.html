Constructed Languages and the Dream of Linguistic Perfection

How four centuries of attempts to build a better language proved that natural language was already optimized

The Recurring Dream

For as long as humans have used language, some have looked at it and thought: we can do better. Words are ambiguous. Grammar is irregular. Spelling is arbitrary. Vocabulary is bloated with synonyms. Surely a rational species could design a language free of these defects—one that maps cleanly onto reality, that anyone could learn, that leaves no room for misunderstanding.

This dream has been pursued seriously for at least four hundred years, by philosophers, logicians, idealists, linguists, and novelists. Each attempt has been brilliant, internally coherent, and ultimately defeated by the same adversary: the actual requirements of human communication.

Core Pattern

The "flaw" the designer removes turns out to be a feature. Every time. Ambiguity is compression. Irregularity is processing speed. Redundancy is error correction. Polysemy is cognitive efficiency. Cultural specificity is the difference between a living language and a dead notation.

The history of constructed languages is, paradoxically, the strongest argument for the design intelligence of natural languages. Natural languages are not poorly made. They are optimized for constraints—limited working memory, noisy channels, embodied cognition, social embeddedness, the need for historical change—that every rational designer has systematically underestimated.

The Cycle That Repeats

  1. A designer identifies a "flaw" in natural language (ambiguity, irregularity, cultural bias).
  2. The designer builds a language that eliminates the flaw.
  3. The language either fails to attract speakers or begins to attract them.
  4. If speakers arrive, they reintroduce the "flaw"—because it serves an essential communicative function.
  5. The language either evolves naturally (regaining its "flaws") or remains a theoretical curiosity.
Timeline of Constructed Languages Nine centuries of linguistic engineering, from Hildegard von Bingen's mystical Lingua Ignota (c. 1150) through the Scientific Revolution's philosophical languages, the 19th-century auxiliary movement, mid-20th-century logical experiments, Tolkien's artistic conlangs, and the modern community-driven era.

The 17th-Century Philosophical Languages

The Scientific Revolution's Demand for Precision

The first serious wave of language construction emerged from the Scientific Revolution. The Royal Society of London, constituted in 1660 and chartered in 1662 with the motto Nullius in verba ("take nobody's word for it"), embodied a radical conviction: knowledge should be verifiable, communicable, and systematic. The existing languages of Europe seemed inadequate. What was needed was a language designed from scratch to represent reality itself.

Lodwick, Dalgarno, and the First Attempts

Francis Lodwick (1619–1694), a London merchant, proposed the first system in A Common Writing (1647). George Dalgarno, a Scottish teacher, produced the first fully elaborated universal language in Ars Signorum (1661), classifying concepts into hierarchical categories with systematic symbols based on their taxonomic position.

Wilkins' Grand Taxonomy

The most ambitious attempt came from John Wilkins, a founding member of the Royal Society. His Essay towards a Real Character and a Philosophical Language (1668) tried to classify all human knowledge into a single hierarchical system, then assign each concept a symbol encoding its position:

  • 40 Main Genera: all concepts divided into forty top-level categories, each assigned a two-letter syllable
  • Differences: each genus subdivided further, adding a third letter
  • Species: further subdivision, adding a fourth letter

So Zi identifies "beasts" (mammals). Zit narrows to "rapacious beasts of the dog kind." Zita specifies dogs. The word tells you what it means. If you know the system, you can decode any word without a dictionary.

Wilkins' Taxonomic Real Character Wilkins' hierarchical classification: 40 genera subdivided by differences and species. Each concept's symbol encodes its taxonomic position. The fatal flaw: nature does not divide into clean, non-overlapping categories.

Why It Failed

The taxonomy was unstable. Wilkins' 40 genera reflected 17th-century assumptions about how knowledge should be organized. But knowledge does not hold still. A language whose words encode their position in a fixed taxonomy becomes obsolete every time the taxonomy changes—and taxonomies always change.

The categories were culturally embedded. Every classification system encodes the worldview of its creator. The Dewey Decimal System devotes eight of its nine religion subcategories to Christianity. Wilkins' genera encoded a 17th-century English Protestant worldview. The dream of culturally neutral classification is a contradiction in terms.

The system could not handle polysemy. Natural language words carry multiple related meanings—"run" can mean to jog, to operate, to flow, to manage. This is not a defect; it is a feature of efficient communication. Wilkins' one-concept-one-word system treated each meaning as requiring its own unique address.

Leibniz: Reducing Thought to Calculation

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716) took the philosophical language dream to its most radical conclusion. Where Wilkins wanted a universal vocabulary, Leibniz wanted a universal reasoning engine:

  1. Characteristica Universalis: a universal notation for recording any item of knowledge formally and systematically
  2. Calculus Ratiocinator: a method for manipulating encoded knowledge computationally, revealing logical consequences
"The only way to rectify our reasonings is to make them as tangible as those of the Mathematicians, so that we can find our error at a glance, and when there are disputes among persons, we can simply say: Let us calculate, without further ado, to see who is right." — Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz

Leibniz never completed the project. It required "an analysis brought to an end and deepened down to the ground"—a complete decomposition of all concepts into primitives. But concepts are not atoms. They shade into each other, depend on context, and shift meaning with use.

Prophetic Legacy

Despite failure as a completed project, Leibniz's vision did come true within the restricted domain of formal reasoning. Programming languages, symbolic logic, and computational reasoning are all descendants of the calculus ratiocinator. Leibniz was right about formal reasoning and wrong about natural language—because they operate under fundamentally different constraints.

Borges, Eco, and the Impossibility of Fixed Categories

In 1942, Jorge Luis Borges published "The Analytical Language of John Wilkins," delivering the most devastating critique of the philosophical language tradition. To expose the arbitrariness of any attempt to categorize the world, he invented a fictional Chinese encyclopedia dividing animals into:

(a) those that belong to the Emperor, (b) embalmed ones, (c) those that are trained, (d) suckling pigs, (e) mermaids, (f) fabulous ones, (g) stray dogs, (h) those that are included in this classification, (i) those that tremble as if they were mad, (j) innumerable ones, (k) those drawn with a very fine camel's hair brush, (l) others, (m) those that have just broken a flower vase, (n) those that resemble flies from a distance.

This list is absurd—in exactly the same way Wilkins' taxonomy is absurd. The only difference is that Wilkins' categories feel natural because they reflect our own cultural assumptions. Borges' conclusion: "there is no description of the universe that isn't arbitrary and conjectural."

Michel Foucault opened The Order of Things (1966) with this passage. Umberto Eco's The Search for the Perfect Language (1993/1995) provided the definitive historical survey, arguing that every attempt has failed because language actively constructs the categories through which we understand the world.

Deepest Pattern

You cannot build a perfect language because you cannot build a perfect taxonomy, and you cannot build a perfect taxonomy because knowledge itself is not taxonomic. Concepts overlap, shade into each other, depend on context, and change over time. A language that freezes categories freezes understanding.

International Auxiliary Languages

Volapük: Explosive Rise, Catastrophic Fall

Johann Martin Schleyer constructed Volapük in 1879–1880. Growth was astonishing: by 1889, 283 clubs, 25 periodicals, 316 textbooks in 25 languages, nearly a million claimed adherents. But its vocabulary was virtually unrecognizable ("lol" from English "rose," "nim" from "animal"), and Schleyer treated the language as personal property. When the Volapük Academy proposed reforms, Schleyer vetoed them. The schism destroyed the movement. Many clubs simply converted to Esperanto.

Key Lesson

A constructed language cannot survive if its creator treats it as personal property. Language requires community ownership to evolve and survive—a principle equally true of natural languages, where prescriptive authorities can slow change but never prevent it.

Esperanto: The Most Instructive Success

L. L. Zamenhof (1859–1917) created Esperanto in 1887, growing up in multilingual Bialystok where Russians, Poles, Germans, and Jews lived in friction. His design decisions were shrewd:

Complete regularity. Zero exceptions: -o for nouns, -a for adjectives, -i for infinitives, -e for adverbs, -j for plural, -n for accusative.

Productive morphology. A small set of roots generates large vocabulary through systematic affixation: mal-san-ul-ej-o = opposite-health-person-place-noun = "hospital."

Community ownership. Unlike Schleyer, Zamenhof claimed no proprietary rights.

Esperanto Morphology: Word-Building System Esperanto builds words from invariant roots + regular affixes + mandatory part-of-speech endings. Approximately 900 roots combined with ~50 affixes and 4 endings generate tens of thousands of words.

Esperanto is the world's most widely spoken constructed auxiliary language: ~100,000 active speakers, ~1,000–2,000 native speakers (denaskuloj), communities in over 100 countries, an active literary tradition, and a popular Duolingo course. Yet it also demonstrates the limits of designed perfection:

  • European bias: Nearly all vocabulary comes from Indo-European languages, making it significantly easier for European speakers than for speakers of Chinese, Japanese, or Arabic.
  • Gender asymmetry: All animate nouns defaulted to male unless given the suffix -in, baking a specific cultural assumption into the language's foundations.
  • Historical persecution: Hitler's Mein Kampf identified Esperanto as a tool of "international Jewry." The language was forbidden in Germany in 1935. Members of the Zamenhof family were murdered during the Holocaust.

The Schematic-Naturalistic Tension

Other auxiliary languages reveal a fundamental design tension. Ido (c. 1907) reformed Esperanto to be more systematic. Interlingua (published 1951) took the opposite approach—designed to look like a Romance language, immediately readable for Romance speakers. Novial (Jespersen, 1928) split the difference but disappeared after its creator's death in 1943.

Impossible Choice

Every auxiliary language faces the same impossible choice between schematic design (systematic for speakers of any language) and naturalistic design (familiar for speakers of source languages). There is no culturally neutral starting point for a language. The dream of universal neutrality requires a perspective from nowhere, and humans do not have perspectives from nowhere.

Logical Languages: Ambiguity Is Compression

Loglan and the Sapir-Whorf Experiment

In 1955, James Cooke Brown began developing Loglan to test the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis—the idea that language structure constrains what you can think. Features included grammar based on predicate logic, complete unambiguity (each sentence parseable in exactly one way), and vocabulary algorithmically blended from widely spoken languages.

Lojban: The Open-Source Fork

History repeated the Volapük pattern. Disputes over intellectual property led to a fork: the Logical Language Group began developing Lojban in 1987 as a free alternative. Lojban achieves something remarkable—a formally parseable grammar defined by a Parsing Expression Grammar that a computer can process. Every well-formed sentence has exactly one parse.

Root words (gismu) are algorithmically derived from six languages: Mandarin, English, Hindi, Spanish, Russian, and Arabic. The language includes systematic attitudinal indicators—precise markers for emotions far more fine-grained than natural language interjections.

The Cost of Removing Ambiguity

Lojban definitively proves that it is possible to create a genuinely unambiguous language—and the cost is enormous. Sentences are often significantly longer than natural language equivalents, because disambiguation requires explicitly encoding information that context would normally provide for free.

Information Theory

Ambiguity is not laziness. It is compression. Natural language is a lossy compression algorithm. Ambiguity is the compression technique. Context is the decoder. Removing ambiguity is like transmitting uncompressed data—technically possible, but wasteful of bandwidth. Human communication channels have limited bandwidth, so compression through ambiguity is not optional. It is a design requirement.

Artistic Languages: Embracing the "Flaws"

Tolkien: Irregularity as Signal

J.R.R. Tolkien's languages represent a fundamentally different approach. Where Wilkins, Zamenhof, and Brown tried to make language more rational, Tolkien aimed to make it more natural—to create languages that looked and felt as though they had evolved over centuries, with all the irregularity that implies.

Tolkien was a professional philologist who understood what other designers did not: real languages are historical objects. Their irregularities are not noise—they are the fossils of past regularities. English "go/went" preserves the history of a merged verb. A language without such fossils is artificial in the most sterile sense.

He began working on Quenya around 1910, inspired by Finnish morphology. Welsh phonology shaped Sindarin. He designed the proto-language Primitive Quendian and constructed all Elvish dialects to believably descend from this single ancestor, just as Romance languages descend from Latin. ("The invention of languages is the foundation. The 'stories' were made rather to provide a world for the languages than the reverse.")

Klingon: From Alien to Natural

Marc Okrand created Klingon for Star Trek III (1984), deliberately designing it to sound "alien" using features from Native American and Southeast Asian languages. What happened next demonstrated something powerful: a community arose and expanded beyond the fictional context. Speakers translated Hamlet, composed original literature, and needed words for bicycles, pineapples, and the coronavirus. A language designed to be alien became, through use, increasingly natural.

Toki Pona: The Opposite Experiment

Sonja Lang's Toki Pona (2001), inspired partly by Taoist philosophy, limits itself to approximately 120–137 words and just 14 phonemes. Where Wilkins tried to give every concept its own word, Toki Pona forces speakers to combine simple elements: tomo tawa ("moving building") = car.

Toki Pona reveals two things simultaneously. First, natural languages' large vocabularies are not excessive—they enable precision that minimal-vocabulary speakers must painstakingly reconstruct. Second, communication is possible with remarkably few building blocks when speakers share context and negotiate meaning.

The Professionalization of Conlanging

David J. Peterson won a contest to create Dothraki for HBO's Game of Thrones with a 303-page proposal. He has since created over 40 languages for film and television. Peterson's work represents a striking shift: the goal is no longer to improve on natural language but to simulate its properties—creating fictional languages that feel authentic precisely because they incorporate the irregularity and cultural specificity that earlier designers tried to eliminate.

The Perfection Paradox Cycle Every constructed language encounters the same cycle: identify a natural-language "flaw," engineer it away, discover the "flaw" served an essential function, watch the language fail or adapt. This cycle has repeated for 400 years.

What Constructed Languages Reveal About Natural Language

Redundancy Is Error Correction

Constructed language designers consistently treated redundancy as waste. But the big visual and phonological difference between "I am" and "he is" makes them easy to tell apart quickly, despite being "irregular." Redundancy provides error correction: if you mishear one word, redundant information elsewhere lets you recover the meaning. Only common verbs "should" be irregular—and in most natural languages, they are. "To be" is the most common verb and the most irregular. This is strategic: frequent forms can be short, distinctive, and irregular because they are learned by sheer repetition.

Irregularity Is Inevitable

When children acquire Esperanto as a first language (the ~1,000–2,000 denaskuloj), they show the same patterns seen in natural language development: loss of the accusative marker -n, phonological reduction, attrition of the tense-aspect system, and pronominal cliticization. The sound ĥ has nearly disappeared community-wide. These changes parallel natural creolization, demonstrating that linguistic irregularity is not a design flaw that can be engineered away. It emerges inevitably from the process of language use by real human beings.

Ambiguity Enables Efficiency

Natural language ambiguity rarely leads to miscommunication—context resolves most cases effortlessly. The alternative (a unique word for every concept) would require a vastly larger vocabulary, imposing a much heavier burden on memory and learning. Polysemy allows natural languages to cover enormous conceptual territory with manageable vocabulary sizes. Lojban proves this by demonstration: removing ambiguity forces explicit encoding of context, making sentences substantially longer.

Community Overrides Design

Every constructed language that gains real speakers begins to evolve in unintended ways:

  • Volapük: Schleyer's control destroyed the community entirely
  • Esperanto: despite Zamenhof's regular design, has evolved phonologically, morphologically, and syntactically
  • Klingon: designed for alien warriors, expanded to cover mundane human life
  • Toki Pona: designed with ~120 words, the community developed conventions requiring an expanded dictionary
  • Loglan/Lojban: forked due to the same proprietary control issue that killed Volapük
Universal Pattern

Language belongs to its speakers, not its creator. Design intent is a starting condition, not a permanent constraint. Prescriptive grammarians have fought losing battles against language change for centuries; constructed languages demonstrate this with particular clarity because the original intent is documented and deviation from it is measurable.

Language Cannot Be Separated from Culture

Wilkins' taxonomy encoded 17th-century English assumptions. Esperanto's vocabulary reflects European structures despite claims of universality. Even Lojban's "neutral" vocabulary reflects the choice of which six source languages to use. Tolkien understood this most clearly: he built entire cultures to go with his languages, because languages without cultures are abstractions without life. A language without cultural context is a notation, not a language.

The Paradox

Every "flaw" in natural language, when examined carefully, turns out to be a solution to a problem the designer did not know existed. Natural languages are brilliantly optimized for constraints—limited memory, noisy channels, embodied cognition, social embeddedness—that rational designers consistently fail to account for. The history of constructed languages is a 400-year empirical demonstration that natural language features are functional.

Why Natural Language "Flaws" Are Necessary Four features conlang designers repeatedly eliminate—ambiguity, irregularity, redundancy, polysemy—each serve essential communicative functions optimized by millennia of human use.

Connections and Implications

For Linguistic Theory

Constructed languages provide controlled experiments in language design. When a feature is deliberately removed and the resulting language fails or changes, we learn something about why natural languages have that feature—evidence that observational linguistics alone cannot provide.

For the Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis

Loglan was designed to test whether language constrains thought. Toki Pona was designed to induce positive thinking. Lojban's community reports cognitive effects. Yet none has produced rigorous evidence for strong linguistic relativity. A 2023 PNAS study found that constructed languages are processed by the same brain mechanisms as natural languages—suggesting the medium matters less than the cognitive architecture processing it.

For Sign Language

Manually Coded English (MCE) and Signed Exact English (SEE) were constructed systems that attempted to represent English grammar in sign. They failed to displace natural sign languages (like ASL) for the same reasons philosophical languages failed: the "imperfections" of natural sign languages are adaptations to the visual-manual modality, not defects to be corrected.

For Artificial Intelligence

Leibniz's dream of reducing thought to calculation has been partially realized in programming languages and AI. But the boundary remains: formal systems handle formal reasoning well and natural language poorly. Large language models inherit natural language's properties, including its ambiguity and context-dependence. Whether AI can truly "understand" language may be a question about whether these features are bugs or features.

Notable Omissions

  • Ithkuil (John Quijada, 2004/2011): designed for extreme brevity and precision, considered essentially unlearnable—a stark illustration of the perfection paradox
  • Blissymbols (Charles K. Bliss, 1949): an ideographic writing system that found unexpected application in augmentative communication for people with disabilities
  • Laadan (Suzette Haden Elgin, 1982): a feminist constructed language testing whether natural languages are inadequate for expressing women's perceptions

Glossary

A posteriori language
Constructed language derived from existing natural languages (e.g., Esperanto, Interlingua)
A priori language
Constructed language designed from scratch, without derivation from existing languages (e.g., Wilkins' Real Character, Toki Pona)
Agglutinative
Grammar where words are formed by stringing together morphemes, each with a single clear meaning (e.g., Esperanto: mal-san-ul-ej-o = hospital)
Bridi
In Lojban, a predication—the fundamental unit composed of a selbri (predicate) and its sumti (arguments)
Calculus ratiocinator
Leibniz's proposed system for mechanically manipulating symbolically encoded knowledge to derive logical conclusions
Characteristica universalis
Leibniz's proposed universal notation for recording any item of knowledge formally and systematically
Conlang
Short for "constructed language"—any language deliberately designed rather than having evolved naturally
Creolization
The process by which a pidgin develops into a full natural language when acquired as a first language by a new generation
Denaskuloj
Esperanto term for native speakers—people who acquired Esperanto from birth (~1,000–2,000 worldwide)
Gismu
In Lojban, root words algorithmically derived from six major natural languages (~1,350 total)
Philosophical language
17th-century artificial language classifying all concepts hierarchically, with word structure encoding taxonomic position
Polysemy
A single word having multiple related meanings—a feature of efficient communication, not a defect
Sapir-Whorf hypothesis
The idea that language structure influences or constrains thought. Strong version (determines) largely rejected; weak version (influences) has empirical support
