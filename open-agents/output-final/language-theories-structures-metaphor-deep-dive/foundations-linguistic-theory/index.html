Foundations of Linguistic Theory From Structuralism to Cognitive Linguistics

How a century of competing frameworks shaped our understanding of what language is

Validation Status

1. Why Linguistic Theory Matters

Every time you speak a sentence, you perform one of the most complex cognitive acts known to science. You select from tens of thousands of stored words, arrange them according to intricate structural rules you have never been taught explicitly, and produce an utterance that another person — who has also never been taught those rules — understands immediately. You do this multiple times per minute, effortlessly, and you have been doing it since roughly age four.

The question of how this works — what language actually is, where its structure comes from, and how children acquire it — has driven more than a century of theoretical debate. That debate is not merely academic. The answer determines how we teach languages, how we build translation systems, how we diagnose language disorders, and how we understand the relationship between language and thought.

What makes this field particularly satisfying to study systematically is that it is structured by a set of recurring oppositions — binary tensions that reappear in every era, often under different names but with the same underlying logic. These oppositions form the skeleton of the entire field:

2. Saussure's Structuralism: Language as a System of Differences

The Founding Insight

Ferdinand de Saussure (1857–1913), a Swiss linguist, never published the work that made him famous. His Course in General Linguistics appeared in 1916, three years after his death, compiled by his students Charles Bally and Albert Sechehaye (with the collaboration of Albert Riedlinger) from lecture notes taken at the University of Geneva between 1906 and 1911.

Before Saussure, linguistics was primarily historical: scholars traced how languages evolved over centuries, comparing Latin to French to Italian, reconstructing proto-languages, cataloguing sound changes. Saussure's revolution was to insist that language could and should be studied as a synchronic system — a snapshot of its structure at a single moment in time, analyzed on its own terms.

The Linguistic Sign

Saussure argued that the basic unit of language is the signThe union of a signifier (sound-image) and signified (concept), forming the basic unit of language in Saussure's theory., which consists of two inseparable components:

  • Signifier (signifiant): The sound pattern — not the physical sound wave itself, but the psychological impression of the sound. A mental image of sound.
  • Signified (signifié): The concept associated with that sound pattern — not the physical object in the world, but the mental idea.

The sign is the union of these two, "like two sides of a sheet of paper." You cannot separate one from the other.

The relationship between signifier and signified is arbitrary. There is no natural reason why the sound pattern "tree" corresponds to the concept of a tree. Evidence: different languages use entirely different sounds for the same concept (English "tree," French "arbre," German "Baum," Japanese "ki"). Saussure distinguished between absolute arbitrariness (most vocabulary — no motivation at all) and relative arbitrariness (partial motivation — "nineteen" is motivated by "nine" and "ten," while "twenty" is fully arbitrary).

Langue vs. Parole

Saussure drew a distinction that set the terms for every debate that followed:

  • LangueThe abstract, systematic code shared by a speech community. Social, collective, and stable. What linguists should study, according to Saussure.: The abstract, systematic code shared by a speech community. It is social, collective, and stable. Langue is what linguists should study.
  • ParoleThe actual utterances produced by individual speakers. Variable, messy, and subject to errors, hesitations, and personal idiosyncrasies.: The actual utterances produced by individual speakers. It is variable, messy, and subject to errors, hesitations, and personal idiosyncrasies.

This is the first instance of the system vs. use opposition. Saussure chose the system side: parole is data, but langue is the proper object of science.

Language as a System of Differences

Saussure's deepest insight: signs have no positive content of their own. They gain their value entirely through their differences from other signs in the system. The word "cat" means what it means partly because it is not "bat," not "cap," not "car," not "dog." Meaning is relational, not intrinsic.

This operates along two axes:

  • Paradigmatic relationsRelations of substitution between elements that could fill the same slot in a structure. The vertical axis of choice. (vertical): The set of elements that could substitute for each other in a given slot. In "The ___ sat on the mat," the paradigmatic set includes "cat," "dog," "bird," and so on.
  • Syntagmatic relationsSequential, combinatorial relations between elements in a chain. The horizontal axis of combination. (horizontal): The sequential combinations of elements in a chain. In "The cat sat on the mat," each word's value is partly determined by what precedes and follows it.

Structuralism After Saussure

Saussure's framework branched into several schools, each emphasizing different aspects:

The Prague School (1920s–1930s)

Vilém Mathesius, Roman Jakobson, and Nikolai Trubetzkoy combined structuralism with functionalism. Trubetzkoy's Principles of Phonology (1939, published posthumously) formalized how speech sounds are defined by their oppositions. Jakobson extended this into binary distinctive features, a system where every speech sound is characterized by a set of yes/no values (voiced/voiceless, nasal/oral, etc.).

American Structuralism (1930s–1950s)

Leonard Bloomfield's Language (1933) established a rigorously empirical, behaviorist-leaning approach. Bloomfield insisted on distributional analysis: classify linguistic units by the environments in which they occur, not by appeals to meaning or intuition. His student Zellig Harris further formalized these methods. Harris's own student, Noam Chomsky, would overturn the entire paradigm.

The Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis

Edward Sapir (1884–1939) and Benjamin Lee Whorf (1897–1941) proposed that language shapes thought. The strong version (language determines thought) is largely rejected. The weak version (language influences certain cognitive processes, such as color perception and spatial reasoning) has received substantial empirical support.

Pattern

Each branch of structuralism pushes toward a different pole of the key oppositions. Prague School: functional. American Structuralism: formal and empirical. Sapir-Whorf: social and cognitive.

3. Chomsky's Generative Revolution: Language as Biological Endowment

The 1957 Paradigm Shift

In 1957, Noam Chomsky (b. 1928) published Syntactic Structures, a slim book that challenged both American structuralism and behaviorist psychology simultaneously. Chomsky argued three things:

  1. Language is not a set of habits learned through conditioning.
  2. Language is not adequately described by cataloguing surface patterns.
  3. Language is a generative system — a finite set of rules capable of producing an infinite number of well-formed sentences.

This inverted multiple oppositions at once. Where Bloomfield had treated language as behavior to be observed, Chomsky treated it as mental structure to be modeled. Where structuralists had catalogued surface patterns, Chomsky proposed abstract underlying rules. Where behaviorists located language in the environment, Chomsky located it in the biology of the individual mind.

Universal Grammar

Chomsky's central theoretical claim: all humans are born with an innate Universal Grammar (UG)The hypothesized innate biological endowment that constrains which forms human languages can take. Not a grammar of any specific language, but a framework of principles and parameters. — a biological endowment that constrains which forms human languages can take. UG is not a grammar of any specific language but a framework of principles and parameters:

  • Principles: Universal constraints that hold across all languages (e.g., every clause has a head; certain structural dependencies cannot cross certain boundaries).
  • Parameters: Binary switches that vary across languages. The head-directionality parameter, for example, determines whether a language places the head before its complement (English: "eat the apple") or after (Japanese: "ringo-o taberu" — "apple eat").

Deep Structure and Surface Structure

In the Standard Theory (1965, Aspects of the Theory of Syntax), Chomsky proposed that every sentence has two levels of representation:

  • Deep structureThe abstract, underlying representation that captures basic grammatical relationships and meaning. Generated by phrase structure rules.: The abstract, underlying representation that captures basic grammatical relationships and meaning.
  • Surface structure: The actual linear order of words as spoken or written.

Transformational rulesRules that map deep structures to surface structures by moving, inserting, or deleting elements. E.g., wh-movement, passivization, do-support. map deep structures to surface structures. They can move elements (wh-movement: "You saw what" becomes "What did you see?"), insert elements (do-support), or restructure them (passivization: "The cat chased the mouse" becomes "The mouse was chased by the cat").

Competence vs. Performance

Chomsky distinguished:

  • CompetenceA speaker's unconscious, internalized knowledge of their language -- the mental grammar. Contrasts with performance. Individual and psychological, unlike Saussure's social langue.: A speaker's unconscious, internalized knowledge of their language — the mental grammar that allows them to judge grammaticality, produce novel sentences, and understand sentences they have never heard before.
  • Performance: The actual use of language in real situations, affected by memory limitations, fatigue, distractions, and errors.

This parallels Saussure's langue/parole distinction, but with a critical difference. Saussure's langue is social — a shared convention of a speech community. Chomsky's competence is individual — a psychological reality inside a single mind. Same opposition (system vs. use), but the system itself has moved from the social to the individual pole.

The Poverty of the Stimulus

This is one of the most debated arguments in cognitive science, and it is the linchpin of the nativist position:

  1. Children acquire complex grammatical knowledge rapidly, typically by age 4–5.
  2. The linguistic input they receive is impoverished: it contains no explicit instruction about what is ungrammatical (only "positive evidence"), includes speech errors and fragments, and does not exemplify many abstract principles children come to know.
  3. Therefore, children must bring innate, language-specific knowledge to the task. That knowledge is Universal Grammar.

Supporting evidence includes the phenomenon of homesign: deaf children who are not exposed to sign language often spontaneously develop gestural communication systems with grammatical structure.

Counterarguments: Philosopher Fiona Cowie has argued the poverty of the stimulus fails on both empirical and conceptual grounds. Usage-based theorists argue that children's input is richer than assumed and that general cognitive abilities are sufficient. This debate continues.

The Minimalist Program (1993+)

The Minimalist Program represents a radical simplification:

  • The core claim: the language faculty is "optimal in its design" — it contains only what is strictly necessary.
  • MergeThe fundamental structure-building operation in the Minimalist Program. Takes two syntactic objects and combines them into a new, larger object. External Merge combines lexical items; Internal Merge moves elements. replaces the elaborate system of transformations. Merge is a single operation: take two syntactic objects and combine them into a new, larger object.
  • In a landmark 2002 paper in Science, Hauser, Chomsky, and Fitch hypothesized that the Faculty of Language in the Narrow sense (FLN) may contain only recursion: the ability to embed structures within structures indefinitely.

4. The Chomsky-Skinner Debate: A Pivotal Moment

Two Books in 1957

By coincidence, 1957 saw the publication of two books that embodied opposite poles of the nature/nurture axis:

  • B.F. Skinner's Verbal Behavior: An attempt to explain language entirely through behaviorist principles of operant conditioning.
  • Chomsky's Syntactic Structures: An argument that language requires positing innate mental rules.

Skinner defined verbal behavior as "behavior reinforced through the mediation of other persons" and proposed six functional categories of verbal operantsFunctional units of verbal behavior defined by their controlling variables in Skinner's framework. Include mands (requests), tacts (labels), echoics (imitation), intraverbals (conversation), textuals (reading), and autoclitics (grammar).:

Chomsky's 1959 Review

Chomsky's review of Verbal Behavior, published in the journal Language (vol. 35, pp. 26–58), became arguably more famous and influential than the book it reviewed. The review advanced four main arguments:

  1. Stimulus control is vacuous: Skinner's concepts of "stimulus" and "response" become so broad when applied to verbal behavior that they lose scientific precision.
  2. The creativity problem: Humans routinely produce and understand sentences they have never heard before. Stimulus-response associations cannot account for this.
  3. The structure problem: Behaviorism treats language as a linear chain. But sentences have hierarchical structure.
  4. The acquisition problem: Children acquire language too quickly, too uniformly, and with too little explicit instruction to be explained by conditioning alone.

The Cognitive Revolution

Chomsky's review is widely credited as a catalyst for the cognitive revolution in psychology, though the foundations of behaviorism were already weakening by 1959. The review helped establish that mental representations and internal rules are legitimate objects of scientific study, that language cannot be reduced to behavior, and that the mind is not a blank slate.

The Counter-Narrative

The standard telling of this debate presents it as a one-sided victory. The fuller picture is more nuanced:

  • Kenneth MacCorquodale published a detailed defense of Skinner's position in 1970, arguing that Chomsky had mischaracterized many of Skinner's claims.
  • Some scholars argue that Chomsky's review succeeded more as rhetoric than as rigorous engagement with Skinner's actual framework.
  • Modern applied behavior analysis (ABA) continues to use Skinner's verbal operant categories productively, particularly in clinical contexts.
  • The either/or framing (nature vs. nurture, rules vs. associations) has largely given way to interactionist perspectives that reject the dichotomy.
Pattern

A debate that appeared settled was not as settled as the standard narrative suggests. This is itself a recurring pattern in the field — apparent resolutions that turn out to be temporary.

5. Cognitive Linguistics: The Counter-Revolution

Origins

Cognitive linguistics emerged in the late 1970s and early 1980s from scholars who had been trained in generative grammar and rejected its foundational assumptions. The movement inverted several of Chomsky's core commitments:

Chomsky's PositionCognitive Linguistics Position
Syntax is autonomous (independent of meaning)All grammar is meaningful
Language uses a dedicated innate module (UG)Language uses general cognitive abilities
Competence (idealized system) is the proper objectUsage (actual language experience) is primary
Grammar is formal computationGrammar is embodied conceptualization

Three founding figures, all born within a year of each other, drove the movement:

George Lakoff (b. 1941) — Originally one of the "generative semanticists," a group that included Haj Ross, Paul Postal, and James McCawley. They called themselves "The Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse." After the "linguistics wars" of the 1960s–70s, Lakoff turned to metaphor theory and embodied cognition. His 1987 book Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things became a foundational text.

Ronald Langacker (b. 1942) — Developed Cognitive Grammar (originally called "Space Grammar") beginning in the mid-to-late 1970s. His two-volume Foundations of Cognitive Grammar (1987, 1991) provided the formal architecture.

Leonard Talmy (b. 1942) — Developed cognitive semantics, focusing on how language structures conceptual content. His two-volume Toward a Cognitive Semantics (2000) systematized concepts including force dynamics, figure/ground organization, and motion event framing.

Core Commitments

Cognitive linguistics rests on five linked commitments, each one a direct rejection of a Chomskyan assumption:

  1. No autonomy of syntax. Grammar is not a self-contained formal system independent of meaning. Every grammatical structure carries meaning.
  2. No Universal Grammar module. There is no language-specific innate module. Language draws on general cognitive abilities: categorization, attention, memory, analogy, pattern recognition.
  3. Language is embodiedThe principle that cognitive and linguistic categories are grounded in bodily experience. Abstract concepts are understood through metaphorical extension from concrete, physical experience.. Linguistic meaning is grounded in bodily experience. We understand abstract concepts through metaphorical extension from concrete, physical experience.
  4. Semantics drives grammar. Meaning is primary. Grammatical form reflects conceptual structure.
  5. Usage-basedThe view that linguistic knowledge is built from actual experience with language, not from innate specification. Grammar emerges from patterns in language use.. Linguistic knowledge emerges from actual language use, not from an innate grammar merely "triggered" by input.

Lakoff: Conceptual Metaphor Theory

George Lakoff, with philosopher Mark Johnson, argued in Metaphors We Live By (1980) that metaphor is not a literary ornament but a fundamental cognitive mechanism. We systematically understand abstract domains in terms of concrete, experiential ones:

  • ARGUMENT IS WAR: "He attacked my position," "She shot down my argument"
  • TIME IS MONEY: "Don't waste my time," "I spent three hours"
  • IDEAS ARE OBJECTS: "Let me give you that idea," "I can't grasp the concept"
  • LIFE IS A JOURNEY: "She's at a crossroads," "We've come a long way"

These are not just linguistic conventions. Lakoff and Johnson's claim is that they structure thought itself — that the metaphors embedded in language shape how we reason about abstract domains.

Talmy: Cognitive Semantics

Leonard Talmy's work addresses how language packages conceptual content:

  • Force dynamicsTalmy's framework for how entities interact with respect to force -- pushing, resisting, blocking, overcoming blockage. Extends across physical, psychological, social, and inferential domains.: A generalized framework for how entities interact with respect to force — pushing, resisting, blocking, overcoming blockage, helping, letting, hindering. This extends across physical, psychological, social, and inferential domains.
  • Figure and ground: Adapted from Gestalt psychology. In spatial language, the Figure is the entity being located (often smaller, more mobile) and the Ground is the reference entity (often larger, more stationary).
  • Motion event typology: Languages differ systematically in how they package motion event components. English typically encodes manner in the verb and path in particles ("run out," "swim across" — satellite-framed). Spanish typically encodes path in the verb ("salir corriendo" = "exit running" — verb-framed).
  • Fictive motion: Figurative expressions describing static scenes with motion language: "The road runs along the coast."

Adele Goldberg's key insight (1995): syntactic patterns are themselves meaningful, independent of the words that fill them. The ditransitive construction means "transfer" even when combined with verbs that do not inherently mean transfer. "She sneezed the napkin off the table" uses the caused-motion construction to give "sneeze" a causal meaning it does not have in isolation. The construction carries the meaning; the verb is recruited into it.

Usage-Based Acquisition

Michael Tomasello's Constructing a Language (2003) synthesizes cognitive linguistics with developmental psychology, proposing that children learn language through two general cognitive capacities:

  • Intention-reading: Understanding the communicative goals of others through shared attention.
  • Pattern-finding: Extracting regularities from input through categorization, analogy, and statistical learning.

Children's early language is "item-based" — tied to specific words and phrases they have heard. Only gradually do they abstract more general patterns. There is no need for a language-specific innate module.

6. Connectionism and Emergentism: The Latest Challenge

Core Ideas

Connectionism (also called Parallel Distributed Processing, or PDP) and emergentismThe view that linguistic structure emerges from the interaction of simpler, non-linguistic processes. Complex, apparently rule-governed behavior arises without explicit rules being represented anywhere. represent approaches that model language as an emergent property of neural-network-like computation:

  • Language knowledge is not stored as discrete rules but as patterns of connection strengths between simple processing units.
  • Learning consists of adjusting connection weights based on statistical regularities in the input.
  • Complex, apparently rule-governed behavior emerges from the interaction of many simple processes without explicit rules being represented anywhere.

The Past Tense Debate (1986)

Rumelhart and McClelland (1986) built a neural network that learned to produce English past tense forms from verb stems. The model contained no explicit rules — only units representing phonetic features connected by weighted links. The model mimicked children's developmental stages: first producing correct irregular forms ("went," "came"), then over-regularizing ("goed," "comed"), then eventually mastering both patterns.

The provocative claim: "Linguistic rules may be merely convenient approximate fictions."

Pinker and Prince (1988) published a detailed critique: the model could not represent certain words correctly, could learn "rules" found in no human language, could not properly distinguish regular from irregular morphology, and failed on fundamental aspects of its assigned task. Despite these flaws, the model shifted the field's attention toward the role of statistical structure in language.

The Competition Model

Elizabeth Bates and Brian MacWhinney developed a functionalist, connectionist model of language processing (1982 onward): language processing involves competition among multiple cues — word order, case marking, agreement, animacy, stress — that point to different interpretations. Different languages weight these cues differently. Bilingual speakers show cue competition between the weightings of their two languages.

Statistical Learning in Infants

Research by Saffran, Aslin, and Newport (1996) demonstrated that 8-month-old infants can segment continuous speech into words based solely on transitional probabilities between syllables. This provided striking evidence that domain-general statistical learning mechanisms are available from very early in development.

Modern views increasingly recognize that both rule-like behavior and statistical sensitivity are real, and that the debate may have been structured around a false dichotomy. The success of large language models since the late 2010s has reinvigorated connectionist arguments, demonstrating that statistical models trained on large corpora can produce fluent, grammatically structured language — raising fresh questions about whether symbolic rules are necessary at all.

7. The Missing Piece: Systemic Functional Linguistics

No survey of linguistic theory is complete without acknowledging M.A.K. Halliday's Systemic Functional Linguistics (SFL). Halliday (1925–2018) developed a comprehensive functional theory of language starting in the 1960s that treats language as a social semiotic system — a resource for making meaning in social contexts.

SFL identifies three simultaneous metafunctions in every utterance:

  • Ideational: Language represents experience (what is being talked about).
  • Interpersonal: Language enacts social relationships (who is talking to whom, with what authority).
  • Textual: Language creates coherent messages (how information is organized and flows).

SFL occupies a distinctive position on the key oppositions: it is functional (not formal), social (not individual), usage-oriented (not system-abstracted), and meaning-driven (not syntax-autonomous). It has had enormous influence in applied linguistics, discourse analysis, education, and computational linguistics, particularly outside the United States.

8. The Recurring Patterns

The Pendulum

There is a discernible historical oscillation in what the field takes language to be:

  1. A social system (Saussure: language as shared convention)
  2. Observable behavior (Bloomfield/Skinner: language as stimulus-response)
  3. An individual mental organ (Chomsky: language as innate grammar)
  4. Embodied cognition (Cognitive Linguistics: language as grounded experience)
  5. Emergent network dynamics (Connectionism: language as statistical pattern)

Each paradigm shift has been partly a reaction against the perceived failures of its predecessor. Each one inverts at least one of the key oppositions.

What Connects Every Theory

Despite deep disagreements, several themes recur across all frameworks:

Structure beyond the surface. Every theory acknowledges that language has internal organization beyond surface sequences. Saussure's system of differences, Chomsky's deep structure, Langacker's symbolic units, and connectionist hidden layers all grapple with the non-obvious organization of language.

The infinite from the finite. How does a finite system (brain, grammar, vocabulary, network) produce and comprehend an infinite range of expressions? Saussure saw this in paradigmatic and syntagmatic combination; Chomsky in recursive rule application; Construction Grammar in productive constructions; connectionism in generalization from training data.

The acquisition puzzle. Every theory must explain how children learn language. The disagreements are about mechanism (innate module vs. general cognition vs. statistical learning), not about the remarkable nature of the achievement.

Form-meaning pairing. From Saussure's signifier/signified to Langacker's symbolic units to Goldberg's constructions, the idea that language fundamentally pairs form with meaning has never been abandoned — only its implementation differs.

The tension between system and use. Saussure's langue/parole, Chomsky's competence/performance, the usage-based emphasis on actual utterances — every framework must negotiate between the idealized system and the messy reality of language in action.
