Metaphor Across Languages & Writing Systems The Grand Convergence

Every language on Earth uses metaphor. No language on Earth uses it the same way.

Lakoff & Johnson (1980) · Kovecses (2005) · Sweetser (1990) · Grady (1997) · Nunez & Sweetser (2006) · Boroditsky (2001)

Validation Status

This explanation draws on independently validated research (confidence score 8.5/10, 49 of 55 claims fully confirmed, 5 confirmed with nuance needed, 1 partially corrected). All corrections have been incorporated, including adjustments to Chinese character etymology, scholarly debates, English onomatopoeia counts, and neuroanatomical specifics of script processing.

1. The Master Pattern

This is the synthesis — the place where everything converges. If you have been following this deep dive through linguistic theory, structural typology, writing systems, and conceptual metaphor, this is where those threads weave together into a single pattern.

The pattern is this: embodied metaphors are universal at the generic level, but each language’s structural machinery provides unique scaffolding for how those metaphors are constructed, elaborated, and transmitted.

That is the sentence. The rest of this explanation is an extended demonstration of why it holds.

Here is what it means, broken into its two halves:

The universal half. All humans have bodies. All bodies interact with gravity, temperature, light, texture, pressure, and movement. These interactions generate a shared stock of conceptual mappingsSystematic correspondences between a concrete source domain (body, space, physical force) and an abstract target domain (time, emotion, understanding). The cognitive infrastructure of metaphor.: MORE IS UP, KNOWING IS SEEING, AFFECTION IS WARMTH, ANGER IS HEAT. These mappings appear across languages, across cultures, across writing systems. They are the cognitive bedrock — the deep substrate that makes cross-cultural communication possible at all.

The culturally specific half. But no language delivers these mappings raw. Every language has structural machinery — morphology, writing systems, phonology, classifiers, honorific systems, etymological layers — and that machinery shapes which metaphors are visible, which are extended, which are elaborated, which are built into grammar, and which are left implicit. Chinese makes metaphor visible through radical composition. Arabic weaves metaphor through root-pattern morphology. Indo-European languages fossilize metaphor in etymology. Japanese layers metaphor across multiple scripts and sound-symbolic systems.

The result is that metaphor operates at two simultaneous levels in every language: the universal embodied foundation and the culture-specific structural scaffolding. Neither level alone tells the full story. Together, they explain why a Chinese speaker and an English speaker can both understand “anger is hot” while constructing that understanding through entirely different linguistic architectures.

Key Insight

The body provides the foundation; culture builds the house. A finite set of embodied experiences generates the raw material for metaphor. The structural machinery of each language determines which metaphors are transparent, which are buried, which are obligatory, and which are absent.

2. Universal vs. Culture-Specific Metaphors: The Kovecses Framework

The Embodied Universals

Start with what is shared. Lakoff and Johnson’s Conceptual Metaphor TheoryThe theory (Lakoff & Johnson, 1980) that metaphor is not linguistic decoration but a fundamental cognitive mechanism: abstract concepts are understood through systematic mappings from concrete, bodily experience. (1980) proposed that many basic metaphors arise from correlations between bodily experiences that all humans undergo:

  • MORE IS UP / LESS IS DOWN: Adding objects to a pile makes the level rise. “Prices are up.”
  • HAPPY IS UP / SAD IS DOWN: An upright posture correlates with positive emotional states. “I’m feeling up today.”
  • KNOWING IS SEEING: Vision provides the most reliable information. “I see what you mean.”
  • AFFECTION IS WARMTH: Being held by a caregiver is simultaneously warm and affectionate. “A warm welcome.”
  • ANGER IS HEAT: Physiological arousal during anger includes increased body temperature. “He was boiling with rage.”

Joseph Grady (1997) called these primary metaphorsConceptual mappings directly grounded in universal bodily experience (Grady, 1997). The atomic building blocks from which complex metaphors like LOVE IS A JOURNEY are assembled. — conceptual mappings directly grounded in universal bodily experience. Complex metaphors are built by composing primary ones: LOVE IS A JOURNEY is assembled from RELATIONSHIP PROGRESS IS MOTION + PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS + DIFFICULTIES ARE OBSTACLES.

Neuroimaging research supports this account: comprehending metaphors activates sensorimotor brain regions even when the language is purely abstract. When you process “grasp the concept,” motor areas associated with physical grasping show activation. However, this effect is strongest for novel or unfamiliar metaphors. As metaphors become conventionalized (“dead”), their sensorimotor activation diminishes. The body’s fingerprint on metaphor fades with familiarity, but it never disappears entirely.

Kovecses’s Distinction: Generic Level vs. Specific Level

Zoltan Kovecses, in Metaphor in Culture: Universality and Variation (2005), provided the framework that resolves the universality-vs-relativity tension:

Generic-level metaphorsConceptual metaphors operating at a high level of abstraction that tend to be universal across cultures (e.g., ANGER IS HEAT). Derived from shared embodied experience. tend toward universality because they derive from universal embodied experience. ANGER IS A HOT FLUID IN A CONTAINER appears across English, Hungarian, Chinese, Japanese, and Zulu.

Specific-level metaphorsCulturally particular elaborations of generic-level metaphors. While ANGER IS HEAT is universal, English locates anger in the head/face, Chinese in the liver, and Japanese in the belly. are where cultural variation enters. While the generic mapping may be shared, its elaboration diverges:

  • English has HAPPINESS IS BEING OFF THE GROUND (“cloud nine,” “walking on air”) — individual transcendence.
  • Chinese has HAPPINESS IS FLOWERS IN THE HEART (心花怒放, xin hua nu fang) — internal organic flourishing.
  • Both share HAPPY IS UP at the generic level. The specific imagery is culturally shaped.

Kovecses identified five dimensions of cross-cultural variation:

  1. Range: Which source domains a culture uses for a given target.
  2. Elaboration: How deeply a shared metaphor is developed.
  3. Emphasis: Which aspects of a shared metaphor a culture foregrounds.
  4. Specificity: The concrete expressions filling out a generic-level mapping.
  5. Alternative metaphors: Conceptual metaphors that exist in one culture but not another.

Figure 1: Kovecses’s three-tier framework resolves the universality-vs-relativity tension. Generic-level universals (ANGER IS HEAT, HAPPY IS UP) derive from shared embodied experience. Cultural elaboration shapes body loci, source domains, and emphasis. Language-specific expressions sit at the base. The ANGER IS HEAT case study shows four cultures sharing the generic metaphor but locating anger in head (English), liver (Chinese), belly (Japanese), and heart (Zulu).

3. Chinese: Metaphor You Can See

Chinese offers the most visually transparent metaphorical system of any major language. Because Chinese characters (hanzi) are built from semantic components called radicalsRecurring semantic components in Chinese characters (部首, bushou) that indicate the general meaning category. The water radical 氵 appears in liquid-related characters; the heart radical 心/忧 in emotion and thought characters., and because Chinese forms new words by compounding free morphemes rather than by inflection, the conceptual relationships that other languages bury in etymology remain visible on the surface.

Radical Composition as Visual Metaphor

Chinese characters include a class called compound ideographsChinese characters (会意, huiyi) whose meaning emerges from the semantic interaction of their visual parts. Example: 明 = 日 (sun) + 月 (moon) = bright. (会意, huiyi) — characters whose meaning emerges from the semantic interaction of their visual parts. These are metaphors you can see.

明 (ming, “bright”) combines 日 (sun) and 月 (moon). The character presents brightness as the co-presence of the two great illuminators. A validation note: in the original oracle bone script, the character was 朋, combining 囧 (a window shape) with 月 (moon) — moonlight shining through a window. The 囧 component was later reanalyzed as 日 (sun). What we see today is a later reinterpretation — the character has been re-metaphorized over time.

The pattern extends through the character system:

  • 休 (xiu, “rest”): A person 亹 leaning against a tree 木. Rest is bodily repose against a natural support.
  • 信 (xin, “trust”): A person 亹 beside speech 言. Trust is a person standing by their word.
  • 森 (sen, “forest”): Three trees 木 stacked. Forest is the multiplication of trees.
  • 好 (hao, “good”): A woman 女 with a child 子. Multiple scholarly readings coexist: “maternal care is good,” “bearing children is fortune,” “mutual affection.”

Radicals as Semantic Taxonomies

The radical system creates semantic fields — entire domains of meaning organized around a single visual element. The water radical 氵 appears in approximately 15% of all characters in the dictionary. When you see 氵, the meaning almost certainly involves liquid, flow, washing, or dissolving: 河 (river), 海 (sea), 湖 (lake), 洗 (wash), 泪 (tears).

The radical also traces metaphorical extension paths. The character 洋 (yang) originally meant “ocean” but extended to “foreign” — because the sea was associated with what lay beyond, encoding a metaphorical chain: water, ocean, beyond, foreign.

The heart radical 心/忧 organizes a vast semantic field of emotions, thoughts, and mental states: 想 (think), 怒 (anger), 悲 (sad), 情 (feeling), 恐 (fear), 慈 (compassion). This encodes the foundational Chinese metaphor: THE HEART IS THE SEAT OF BOTH THOUGHT AND FEELING — unlike English, which separates “heart” (emotion) from “mind” (cognition).

Compounding as Transparent Metaphor

Because Chinese is predominantly an isolating language, its word-formation process is itself a continuous act of metaphorical construction:

  • 电脑 (diannao, “computer”) = electric + brain. A computer is an “electric brain.”
  • 火车 (huoche, “train”) = fire + vehicle. A train is a “fire vehicle,” preserving the steam-power metaphor.
  • 电话 (dianhua, “telephone”) = electric + speech. “Electric speech.”
  • 手机 (shouji, “mobile phone”) = hand + machine. A “hand machine.”

These compounds are transparent in a way that English borrowings from Greek and Latin are not. An English speaker who says “telephone” (tele + phone = “far voice”) needs classical knowledge to decode the metaphor. A Chinese speaker seeing 电话 can immediately parse the conceptual structure.

Classifiers as Metaphorical Categorization

Chinese uses classifiersGrammatical particles (量词, liangci) required in Chinese between numbers and nouns, categorizing objects by perceived properties such as shape, function, or material. A built-in metaphorical taxonomy. — grammatical particles required between numbers and nouns that categorize objects by perceived properties:

  • 条 (tiao): For long, thin, flexible things — rivers, fish, roads, snakes, and news items. Using 条 for news implicitly applies the metaphor NEWS IS A THREAD.
  • 张 (zhang): For flat, sheet-like things — paper, tables, beds, and faces. A face is categorized as a flat surface.
  • 口 (kou, “mouth”): For people in a household. The metaphor PEOPLE ARE MOUTHS TO FEED is built into the grammar.

Every time a Chinese speaker counts or quantifies a noun, they simultaneously perform a metaphorical categorization. Metaphor is not optional decoration. It is grammatically required.

4. Arabic: Metaphor Radiating from the Root

If Chinese makes metaphor visible through spatial composition, Arabic makes metaphor productive through temporal radiation. The Arabic root-pattern system generates entire families of related meanings from a single three-consonant skeleton, and the relationships between those meanings are themselves metaphorical mappings.

The Trilateral Root as Metaphorical Network

Arabic morphology is built on trilateral rootsThree-consonant skeletons in Arabic morphology carrying core semantic meaning. Different vowel patterns and affixes (the wazn system) generate families of related words from a single root. — three-consonant skeletons carrying core semantic meaning. Different vowel patterns, prefixes, and suffixes (the wazn system) are applied to the root to generate related words.

K-T-B (ك-ت-ب) — the “writing” root

The extension from “physical act of inscribing” to maktub (“fate, destiny”) encodes the theological metaphor DESTINY IS A TEXT WRITTEN BY GOD — a mapping so embedded in the morphological system that it feels like a natural fact of the word rather than a conceptual leap.

The root S-L-M (س-ل-م) radiates from “wholeness, intactness” to: سَلَام salam (“peace” — being unbroken), إِسْلَام islam (“submission” — entrusting wholeness to God), مُسْلِم muslim (“one who submits”), سَلِيم salim (“sound, safe”). Every word is a metaphorical extension of the core concept, and the root system preserves the genealogy.

The Wazn System: Patterns as Metaphorical Operators

The waznThe Arabic morphological pattern/template system (وزن). Approximately 15 verb forms, each applying a specific semantic operation (intensification, causation, reciprocity, reflexivity, seeking) to a trilateral root. system multiplies the root’s metaphorical potential. Each pattern is a semantic operator — a systematic transformation applied to any root:

  • Form I (fa’ala): Basic meaning — كَتَبَ “he wrote”
  • Form II (fa’’ala): Intensification or causation — كَتَّبَ “he made someone write”
  • Form III (faa’ala): Reciprocal action — كَاتَبَ “he corresponded with”
  • Form VI (tafaa’ala): Mutual reciprocal — تَكَاتَبَ “they corresponded with each other”
  • Form X (istaf’ala): Seeking — اِسْتَكْتَبَ “he asked someone to write”

This is fundamentally different from how English or Chinese extend meaning. Arabic transforms a root from within, using the pattern as a conceptual lens. The consonants stay; the vowels shift; the meaning transforms.

Balaagha: The Arabic Science of Metaphor

Arabic developed one of the world’s most sophisticated theoretical traditions for analyzing metaphor. The discipline of balaaghaThe Arabic science of rhetoric and eloquence (البلاغة), developed between the 11th and 13th centuries CE. Divided into semantics (ilm al-ma’ani), figurative speech (ilm al-bayan), and rhetorical embellishment (ilm al-badi’). matured between the 11th and 13th centuries CE into a formal analytical science. Two foundational figures — Abd al-Qahir al-Jurjani (d. 1078 CE) and Al-Sakkaki (d. 1229 CE) — established frameworks that anticipate modern Conceptual Metaphor Theory by nearly a millennium.

The Arabic analysis of isti’ara (metaphor) distinguishes between explicit metaphor, implicit metaphor, and dead metaphor — a taxonomy strikingly parallel to modern linguistics. The richness of this tradition reflects the centrality of figurative language in Arabic culture. Arabic did not just use metaphor prolifically; it built a science of metaphor.

5. Indo-European Etymology: The Body-to-Mind Highway

If Chinese makes metaphor visible on the page and Arabic radiates metaphor through morphological patterns, Indo-European languages fossilize metaphor in etymology. The metaphorical origins are invisible to casual speakers but recoverable through linguistic archaeology.

Sweetser’s Hierarchy: Senses as Cognitive Metaphors

Eve Sweetser’s From Etymology to Pragmatics (1990) demonstrated that across Indo-European languages, physical sensory vocabulary has been systematically extended to cognitive domains — and always in the same directions. This is the MIND-AS-BODY metaphorSweetser’s (1990) term for the systematic pattern across Indo-European languages in which physical/sensory vocabulary is extended to cognitive/emotional domains. Vision maps to knowledge, hearing to obedience, touch to emotion, taste to preference, smell to suspicion.:

SensePhysical DomainCognitive DomainExamples
VisionSeeingKnowledge“I see what you mean.” Latin videre → “evidence”
HearingListeningObedienceLatin oboedire (“to obey”) = ob-audire (“to hear toward”)
TouchPhysical contactEmotion“I was touched.” “Emotion” = Latin e-movere
TasteGustatoryJudgment“In bad taste.” Latin sapere = taste / be wise
SmellOlfactorySuspicion“Something smells fishy.” “A nose for trouble.”

This hierarchy reflects the reliability and controllability of each sense. Vision provides the most reliable information, so it maps to knowledge. Smell is passive and associated with detecting hidden things, so it maps to suspicion. The direction is always: physical to mental, concrete to abstract. The reverse direction almost never occurs as a productive historical process.

The Grasp-Comprehend Nexus

Latin comprehendere = com- (“completely”) + prehendere (“to seize”). The original meaning was purely physical: to grab something with your hands. The shift to “to understand” encodes UNDERSTANDING IS PHYSICAL GRASPING.

This mapping recurs independently across the family:

  • English “grasp”: Physical grasping → “to grasp an idea”
  • German begreifen: “To seize” → “to comprehend”
  • Italian capire: From Latin capere (“to take, catch”) → “to understand”
  • English “perceive”: From Latin per-cipere (“to take completely”)

These are all dead metaphorsFigures of speech whose original figurative imagery has been forgotten through extensive use. The word is processed as having a single, literal meaning. “Comprehend” (from “seize completely”) is a dead metaphor. The fossil record of past conceptual mapping. — figures of speech that have lost their original imagery. But the dead metaphor is not truly dead. It is a fossil. By excavating etymology, we reconstruct the metaphorical thinking of speakers who lived thousands of years ago.

6. Japanese: Metaphor in Layers

Japanese does not do metaphor through a single mechanism. It layers metaphorical systems on top of each other — kanji semantics, mimetic sound symbolism, body-part emotion mapping, and grammaticalized social metaphor — creating the most multi-layered metaphorical landscape of any major language.

Multi-Script as Multi-Register Metaphor

Japanese deploys three scripts simultaneously: kanji (Chinese logographs), hiragana (native syllabary), and katakana (foreign loanword syllabary). Each script carries different metaphorical registers.

Kanji have dual readings: Chinese-derived (on’yomi) and native Japanese (kun’yomi). The character 心 can be read as shin (Chinese-derived, used in compounds like 心理 shinri, “psychology”) or kokoro (native Japanese). The word kokoro carries specifically Japanese cultural connotations — the holistic seat of emotion-and-thought unified. One character, two metaphorical traditions, accessible simultaneously.

The Japanese Body Map: Hara, Kokoro, Ki, Mushi

Japanese distributes emotion metaphors across a distinctive set of body-concept centers that differ significantly from both English and Chinese:

KokoroJapanese for “heart/mind/spirit” (心). A holistic concept unifying emotion and cognition in the chest, with no precise English equivalent. Unlike English “heart” (emotion only). (心, “heart/mind/spirit”): Located in the chest. Unlike English, which separates “heart” (emotion) from “mind” (cognition), kokoro embraces both — a holistic core where feeling and thought are unified.

HaraThe Japanese concept of the belly (腹) as the seat of thinking, intention, character, and moral judgment. Central to Japanese emotion metaphors. No English equivalent beyond the weak “gut feeling.” (腹, “belly”): The seat of intention, character, and moral judgment:

  • 腹が黒い (hara ga kuroi, “black belly”) — scheming, malicious
  • 腹を割る (hara o waru, “split the belly”) — to speak frankly
  • 腹が立つ (hara ga tatsu, “belly stands up”) — to become angry

Ki (気, “breath/vital energy”): Used for mood, temperament, and mental activity: 気分 (kibun, mood), 気持ち (kimochi, feeling), 元気 (genki, health/vigor).

Mushi (虫, “worm/insect”): An entity imagined to live inside the belly that influences behavior: 虫が好かない (mushi ga sukanai, “the worm doesn’t like it”) — instinctive dislike.

Figure 5: Japanese distributes emotion metaphors across a four-system body map: kokoro (heart-mind-spirit) unifies emotion and thought; hara (belly) governs intention and moral character; ki (vital energy) flows as mood and temperament; mushi (an imagined insect) triggers instinctive reactions. The English system, shown for comparison, is far less differentiated.

Mimetics: Sound as Sensory Metaphor

Japanese has over 4,500 recognized onomatopoeia and mimetic wordsWords that represent non-auditory sensory experiences through sound symbolism. Japanese has over 4,500, covering states, emotions, textures, and manners of action — a form of cross-modal metaphor. — far exceeding English’s several hundred to roughly 1,500. Three categories represent a form of cross-modal metaphor with no equivalent in English:

  • Gitaigo (擬態語): States and conditions — キラキラ (kirakira, sparkling)
  • Gijogo (擬情語): Emotional states — ワクワク (wakuwaku, excited)
  • Giyogo (擬容語): Manner of action — ノロノロ (noronoro, sluggishly)

When Japanese speakers say ピカピカ (pikapika) for something shiny, sharp, bright sounds (p, k, i) metaphorically encode sharp, bright visual qualities. When they say ヌルヌル (nurunuru) for something slimy, soft rounded sounds (n, r, u) encode tactile qualities. This is sound-to-sensation metaphor used constantly in everyday speech.

Keigo: Social Hierarchy as Spatial Metaphor

Japanese honorific speech (keigoThe Japanese honorific speech system (敬語). Sonkeigo elevates the listener, kenjogo lowers the speaker, and teineigo creates horizontal distance. Encodes RESPECT IS VERTICAL DISTANCE as grammar., 敬語) encodes social relationships through grammaticalized spatial metaphor:

  • Sonkeigo (尊敬語): Elevates the listener with special verb forms that metaphorically “raise” the other person.
  • Kenjogo (謙譲語): Lowers the speaker with verb forms that metaphorically diminish the speaker’s actions.
  • Teineigo (丁寧語): Creates horizontal distance through polite verb endings.

The underlying metaphor is spatial: RESPECT IS VERTICAL DISTANCE and POLITENESS IS HORIZONTAL DISTANCE. Every verb conjugation in keigo is a metaphorical claim about social positioning. Failing to use appropriate keigo is a social violation — the metaphor is built into the grammar so deeply that a Japanese speaker cannot form a sentence without it.

7. Cross-Cultural Time Metaphors: Where Space Meets Time

Time is invisible, intangible, and abstract. Every language on Earth uses spatial metaphors to talk about it. But the specific spatial mapping varies dramatically — and this variation provides the clearest evidence that universal embodied foundations are filtered through culture-specific conceptual frameworks.

English maps time along the horizontal axis with the future ahead: “Looking forward to the meeting.” “Put it behind you.”

Mandarin Chinese uses both a vertical and a horizontal axis: 上个月 (shanggeyue, “upper month” = last month) and 下个月 (xiageyue, “lower month” = next month) for the vertical; 前天 (qiantian, “front day” = day before yesterday) for the horizontal.

Aymara: The Future Is Behind You

The AymaraAn Andean language that reverses the dominant future=ahead temporal metaphor. Past is “in front” (visible/known) and future is “behind” (unseen/unknown), grounded in epistemological logic. Documented by Nunez & Sweetser (2006). language presents the most dramatic departure. Nunez and Sweetser (2006) documented that nayra (“eye,” “front,” “sight”) means “past”, while qhipa (“back,” “behind”) means “future.”

The logic is elegant: the past is in front because it is visible — you can see it, you know it. The future is behind because it is unseen. In Aymara, which grammatically requires speakers to mark whether they have direct evidence for claims (evidentialityA grammatical feature requiring speakers to mark the source of their knowledge (direct experience, hearsay, inference). Present in Aymara and relevant to its past-is-in-front temporal metaphor.), this mapping makes perfect epistemological sense.

Kuuk Thaayorre: Time Follows the Sun

Kuuk Thaayorre speakers use absolute cardinal directions (north, south, east, west) rather than body-relative directions. This absolute frame extends to time: temporal sequences are arranged from east to west — following the sun’s path — regardless of which direction the speaker faces. This is TIME IS THE SUN’S PATH — time anchored to astronomical reality rather than to the speaker’s body.

Figure 6: Four languages, four fundamentally different spatial-temporal metaphor systems. English maps future=ahead. Mandarin uses both vertical (earlier=up) and horizontal axes. Aymara reverses the dominant pattern, placing the known past in front and the unseen future behind. Kuuk Thaayorre aligns time with the sun’s east-to-west path. All use space for time — none use the same mapping.

Key Insight

All four languages use space to structure time (universal). But the specific spatial axis and the logic connecting space to time differ based on what each culture treats as the salient relationship — bodily forward motion, vertical ordering, epistemological visibility, or astronomical observation.

8. Script and Metaphorical Thinking

The script relativity hypothesisThe proposal that writing systems (not just spoken languages) influence cognition. Evidence supports a weak version: script-specific effects on attention during reading, with unclear extension to general cognition. extends the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis from spoken language to written language: the writing system may influence cognitive processing.

Logographic readers (Chinese, Japanese kanji) show greater engagement of visuospatial processing areas, more right-hemisphere involvement for holistic pattern recognition, and stronger motor area activation (stroke sequences). They tend toward holistic processing — recognizing the whole character as a gestalt.

Alphabetic readers (Latin, Cyrillic) show greater left superior temporal activation (phonological processing), stronger reliance on the phonological route (letters → sounds → meaning), and more sequential, analytical processing.

For metaphor, this means: in logographic systems, a metaphor like 明 (“bright” = sun + moon) is processed as a visual gestalt — the semantic components perceived simultaneously, the metaphor literally visible. In alphabetic systems, a metaphor like “comprehend” (com + prehendere = “seize completely”) is opaque — the metaphorical history buried in Latin etymology.

Figure 7: Writing systems vary in how visible they render metaphorical structure. Chinese is maximally transparent (明 = sun + moon is literally visible). Arabic’s root-pattern system is partially transparent. Japanese occupies a mixed position. English is maximally opaque (“comprehend” = “seize” is invisible). Brain processing differs accordingly, though script relativity has only weak empirical support for effects beyond reading.

Important Caveat

The evidence supports only a weak version of the script relativity hypothesis. There is a channeling effect on attention while actually reading, but whether these effects extend to general cognition or metaphorical thinking outside of reading contexts remains unclear (Bassetti & Cook, 2022).

9. The Deep Pattern: Universal Foundations, Cultural Scaffolding

Everything Converges

The evidence from Chinese, Arabic, Indo-European languages, Japanese, Aymara, and Kuuk Thaayorre converges on a single architectonic pattern with two levels and a relationship between them:

Level 1: Universal Embodied Foundations

  • Primary metaphors (MORE IS UP, KNOWING IS SEEING, AFFECTION IS WARMTH) derived from universal bodily experience
  • The strategy of using concrete/physical domains to understand abstract/mental domains
  • The use of spatial concepts to structure temporal experience
  • The tendency to locate emotions in the body
  • The directionality of semantic change: physical to mental, never the reverse
  • The accumulation of dead metaphors as fossil records of past conceptual mapping

Level 2: Culture-Specific Structural Scaffolding

  • Which spatial axis maps to time (horizontal, vertical, cardinal, reversed)
  • Which body parts are salient for emotion (head, heart, belly, liver)
  • How the structural machinery implements and extends metaphorical mappings
  • Which metaphors are elaborated, neglected, or unique to a culture
  • How the writing system renders metaphor visible or invisible

The Relationship: Level 1 provides the raw material. Level 2 provides the construction tools. Every language builds metaphorical structures from the same embodied foundations using different structural machinery.

What This Reveals About Cognition

The cross-linguistic evidence establishes five conclusions:

1. The human mind is inherently metaphorical. We understand abstract concepts through concrete experience, regardless of which language we speak. This is not a feature of language. It is a feature of cognition that language reflects.

2. Language structures channel metaphorical thought. The tools available in a language — roots, radicals, affixes, compounds, classifiers, honorific systems — shape which metaphors are easy to construct, extend, and transmit. Metaphor is universal; the pathways through which metaphor flows are language-specific.

3. Writing systems add a layer. Logographic systems keep metaphorical origins visible; alphabetic systems bury them. This may influence how aware speakers are of the metaphorical nature of their own language.

4. Cultural history shapes metaphorical preference. Which metaphors a culture elaborates depends on its history, philosophy, religion, and everyday practices. These are not random choices; they reflect deep cultural commitments.

5. Metaphor translation is conceptual renegotiation. When metaphors cross linguistic boundaries, the conceptual mapping must be reconstructed using the target language’s structural resources. Arabic maktub (fate-as-written-text) cannot simply be translated as “fate” without losing the writing metaphor that the root system preserves. The untranslatability is not about linguistic incapacity — any idea can be expressed in any language — but about the loss of the metaphorical scaffolding that makes a concept feel natural.

The Grand Convergence

Same cognitive foundation. Different structural machinery. Universal origin. Culture-specific expression. This is the pattern that emerges when structural diversity and metaphor theory converge: the body provides the foundation; language provides the tools; culture chooses the architecture.

A Note on What Is Missing

Research Gaps

This explanation has significant gaps that deserve acknowledgment. Sign language metaphor is entirely absent — a major omission, because sign languages provide unique evidence for embodied metaphor theory by operating in the visual-manual modality. Korean, a major language with distinctive metaphor research, is not covered. African language families (Bantu, Yoruba, and others) receive only passing mention. Bilingual metaphor processing — how speakers navigate metaphorical systems across their languages — is another important area omitted here.

Glossary

TermDefinition
AbjadA writing system in which only consonants are written, with vowels left unmarked or indicated by optional diacritics. Arabic script is the most widely used abjad.
BalaaghaThe Arabic science of rhetoric and eloquence, developed between the 11th and 13th centuries CE. Divided into semantics (ilm al-ma’ani), figurative speech (ilm al-bayan), and rhetorical embellishment (ilm al-badi’).
ClassifierA grammatical particle required in Chinese between a number and a noun, categorizing objects by perceived properties such as shape, function, or material.
Compound ideographA Chinese character (会意, huiyi) whose meaning emerges from the semantic interaction of its component parts (e.g., 明 = sun + moon = bright).
Conceptual Metaphor TheoryThe theory (Lakoff & Johnson, 1980) that metaphor is a fundamental cognitive mechanism by which abstract concepts are understood through systematic mappings from concrete, bodily experience.
Dead metaphorA metaphor whose figurative origin has been forgotten through extensive use, processed as having a single, conventional meaning. “Comprehend” (from “seize completely”) is a dead metaphor.
EvidentialityA grammatical feature requiring speakers to mark the source of their knowledge (direct experience, hearsay, inference). Present in Aymara.
Generic-level metaphorA conceptual metaphor operating at a high level of abstraction that tends to be universal across cultures (e.g., ANGER IS HEAT).
GitaigoJapanese mimetic words representing states, conditions, textures, or appearances through sound symbolism rather than imitation of actual sounds.
HaraThe Japanese concept of the belly (腹) as the seat of thinking, intention, character, and moral judgment.
Isti’araThe Arabic rhetorical term for metaphor, analyzed within the balaagha tradition with distinctions between explicit, implicit, and dead metaphor.
KeigoThe Japanese honorific speech system, comprising sonkeigo (respectful), kenjogo (humble), and teineigo (polite).
KokoroJapanese for “heart/mind/spirit” (心) — a holistic concept unifying emotion and cognition.
Kovecses frameworkThe analytical distinction (Kovecses, 2005) between generic-level metaphors (near-universal, embodied) and specific-level metaphors (culturally variable), with five dimensions of variation.
MIND-AS-BODY metaphorSweetser’s (1990) term for the systematic pattern in which physical/sensory vocabulary extends to cognitive/emotional domains across Indo-European languages.
MimeticsWords representing non-auditory sensory experiences through sound symbolism. Japanese has over 4,500.
Primary metaphorA conceptual metaphor directly grounded in universal bodily experience (Grady, 1997), such as MORE IS UP or KNOWING IS SEEING.
RadicalA recurring semantic component in Chinese characters (部首) indicating the general meaning category.
Script relativity hypothesisThe proposal that writing systems influence cognition. Currently supported only at the weak level.
Specific-level metaphorA culturally particular elaboration of a generic-level metaphor. The level where cross-cultural variation enters.
Trilateral rootA three-consonant skeleton in Arabic morphology carrying core semantic meaning. The wazn system generates word families from roots.
UnidirectionalityThe principle that metaphorical extension proceeds from concrete to abstract, physical to mental — almost never reversed.
WaznThe Arabic morphological pattern/template system. ~15 verb forms, each applying a specific semantic operation to a trilateral root.
