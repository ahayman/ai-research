Semiotics and the Philosophy of Signs

How Thinkers from Saussure to Eco Mapped the Relationship Between Signs and Meaning -- and Progressively Destabilized It

1. Introduction: Why Signs Matter

Every time you read a word, interpret a facial expression, follow a road sign, or understand that smoke means fire, you are engaged in semiosisThe process of making meaning through signs. For Peirce, semiosis is the irreducibly triadic cooperation of sign, object, and interpretant. -- the process of making meaning through signs. SemioticsThe study of signs and sign processes. "Semiology" is associated with the Saussurean (Continental) tradition; "semiotics" with the Peircean (Anglo-American) tradition. is the systematic study of that process: how signs work, what gives them meaning, and why meaning sometimes shifts, breaks down, or resists being pinned down.

This matters for understanding language because language is the most complex sign system humans have ever produced. But the question "how do signs mean?" turns out to be far harder than it looks. Over the past 130 years, a succession of thinkers have proposed increasingly sophisticated answers -- and each new answer has revealed problems with the previous one. The result is a grand intellectual arc that moves from fixed, stable meaning toward fluid, contextual meaning, with each thinker reacting to specific limitations in the work of their predecessors.

The Pattern to Watch For

Here is the core pattern that runs through everything below:

  1. Saussure (1916) says meaning comes from a closed system of differences -- stable and structural.
  2. Peirce (working independently, influence from the 1930s onward) says meaning is a dynamic process that never fully terminates -- signs generate more signs.
  3. Frege (1892) says meaning has two precise dimensions (sense and reference) -- logical and determinate.
  4. Wittgenstein embodies the transition in his own career: the Tractatus (1921) treats language as a logical mirror of the world; the Investigations (1953) shatters that mirror and replaces it with a toolkit of social practices.
  5. Derrida (1967 onward) radicalizes Saussure's own principles to argue that meaning can never come to rest -- it is always deferred.
  6. Barthes (1957-1970) shows how supposedly stable signs serve ideological purposes, then celebrates the reader's power to generate meaning.
  7. Eco (1962-1984) synthesizes Peirce and Saussure, insisting that meaning is open-ended but not unlimited -- texts push back against wild interpretation.
Key Insight

The movement is consistently from closed to open, from determined to negotiated, from structure to process. Each stage preserves genuine insights from what came before while identifying what it missed.

2. Saussure: The Architecture of Difference

The Problem Saussure Addressed

Before Saussure, the common-sense view of language was what philosophers call naive referentialism: words are labels attached to things. "Cat" means cat because the word points to the furry animal. This seems obvious, but it collapses under scrutiny. What does "freedom" point to? What about "the" or "very" or "if"? And why does the same animal get different labels in different languages?

Ferdinand de Saussure (1857-1913), a Swiss linguist whose posthumous Course in General Linguistics (1916) became the founding document of modern semiotics, proposed a radically different architecture.

The Dyadic Model: Signifier and Signified

A linguistic sign, Saussure argued, is not a link between a word and a thing in the world. It is a composite of two psychological elements:

  • SignifierIn Saussure's model, the sound pattern or written mark of a sign (understood as a mental impression, not the physical sound itself). (signifiant): the sound pattern or written mark -- but understood as a mental impression, not the physical sound itself.
  • SignifiedIn Saussure's model, the concept or mental image evoked by the signifier (not the real-world object). (signifie): the concept or mental image the signifier evokes -- not a real-world object but an idea.

The two are bound together "like the recto and verso of a piece of paper" -- inseparable within the sign, yet analytically distinct.

Notice what is deliberately excluded: the real-world referentThe actual object or state of affairs in the world that a sign relates to. Excluded from Saussure's model, included in Peirce's as "the object.". Saussure's model has no slot for the actual cat sitting on the actual mat. For him, semiology concerns the internal system of language, not its correspondence with external reality.

Three Core Principles

Principle 1: Arbitrariness

The relationship between signifier and signified is arbitrarySaussure's principle that the relationship between signifier and signified has no natural, inherent, or necessary basis.. There is no natural, inherent, or self-evident connection between the sound "tree" and the concept of a tree. The proof: different languages use entirely different signifiers for the same signified -- arbre in French, Baum in German, derevo in Russian.

Even apparent exceptions prove the rule. Onomatopoeia -- words like "buzz" or "splash" that seem to imitate sounds -- varies across languages. A rooster says "cock-a-doodle-doo" in English, cocorico in French, kikeriki in German. If the sound-meaning connection were natural, onomatopoeia would be universal. It is not.

Principle 2: Differential Value

This is Saussure's most profound insight. Signs derive their value not from any positive content but from their differences from other signs in the system.

"In language there are only differences without positive terms." -- Ferdinand de Saussure

The word "dark" means what it means not because of any inherent connection to darkness, but because it occupies a specific position in a network of contrasts -- it is not "dim," not "gloomy," not "murky," not "black," not "light." Each sign is defined by what it is not.

Principle 3: System over Instance

Saussure distinguished langueSaussure's term for the abstract, social system of language shared by a community (contrast with parole). (the abstract, social system of language shared by a community) from paroleSaussure's term for individual, concrete acts of speech or writing (contrast with langue). (individual acts of speech or writing). Langue is the underlying code. Parole is actual, concrete use. Saussure argued that linguistics should study langue, because the system makes individual utterances intelligible.

Synchronic vs. Diachronic

Saussure drew a sharp line between synchronicStudy of a language as a self-contained system at a given moment in time (contrast with diachronic). analysis (studying a language as a self-contained system at a given moment) and diachronicStudy of how a language changes over time (contrast with synchronic). analysis (studying how a language changes over time). He compared this to cutting a tree trunk horizontally (synchronic cross-section) versus cutting it vertically (diachronic longitudinal section). He prioritized the synchronic because speakers experience language as a present system, not as a historical sequence.

What Saussure Left Out

The model is static (synchronic emphasis), closed (no external referent), and two-dimensional (no account of interpretation as a process). These are precisely the limitations that subsequent thinkers will target.

3. Peirce: Signs in Motion

The Problem Peirce Addressed

Charles Sanders Peirce (1839-1914), an American logician and philosopher, developed his theory of signs independently of Saussure and in sharp contrast to him. Where Saussure asked "how is the sign system structured?", Peirce asked "how does meaning actually happen?" -- and his answer introduces the element Saussure deliberately excluded: the process of interpretation.

The Triadic Architecture

Every sign, for Peirce, involves three irreducible elements:

  1. The RepresentamenIn Peirce's triadic model, the form the sign takes -- the perceptible entity that functions as a sign. (or sign-vehicle): the form the sign takes -- word, image, gesture, sound.
  2. The Object: that which the sign refers to or stands for -- the thing in the world to which the sign is related.
  3. The InterpretantIn Peirce's triadic model, the meaning-effect a sign produces -- not the interpreter, but the understanding or further sign generated by the sign.: the effect the sign produces -- not the interpreter themselves, but the understanding, response, or further sign that the original sign generates.

The Taxonomy: Icon, Index, Symbol

Peirce classified signs by the nature of the relationship between sign and object:

IconsIn Peirce's taxonomy, a sign that relates to its object through resemblance (photographs, maps, onomatopoeia). relate to their objects through resemblance. A photograph resembles its subject; a map resembles the territory. Subtypes include images, diagrams, and metaphors.

IndexesIn Peirce's taxonomy, a sign that relates to its object through direct causal or existential connection (smoke/fire, pointing). relate through direct causal or existential connection. Smoke is an index of fire. A weathervane indexes wind direction. Subtypes: tracks, symptoms, designations.

SymbolsIn Peirce's taxonomy, a sign that relates to its object through convention, habit, or arbitrary rule (most words, mathematical notation). relate through convention, habit, or arbitrary rule. Most words are symbols: no resemblance or causal connection between "dog" and actual dogs.

Unlimited Semiosis

Peirce's most far-reaching idea is unlimited semiosisPeirce's principle that the interpretant of a sign is itself a sign, generating further interpretants in a potentially endless chain, arrested in practice by habit.: the interpretant of a sign is itself a sign, which generates its own interpretant, which is again a sign, and so on potentially without end.

Peirce vs. Derrida -- A Critical Distinction

For Peirce, the process of semiosis, while theoretically unlimited, converges toward truth through the self-correcting process of inquiry. Meaning is not infinitely deferred but progressively refined. This is a critical distinction from Derrida's later position.

Both offer genuine insights. Saussure shows that meaning cannot be understood by looking at signs in isolation -- the system matters. Peirce shows that meaning cannot be understood by freezing the system -- interpretation is an ongoing activity. The tension between these two perspectives generates much of what follows.

4. Frege: The Logic of Meaning

The Puzzle That Launched a Tradition

Gottlob Frege (1848-1925), a German mathematician and logician, posed a deceptively simple question in his 1892 paper "Uber SinnFrege's term for sense: the mode of presentation through which a reference is given. und Bedeutung" that transformed the philosophy of language. Consider two statements:

  • (a) "The morning star is the morning star."
  • (b) "The morning star is the evening star."

Both are true -- both expressions refer to the planet Venus. Yet (a) is a trivial tautology, while (b) is a genuinely informative empirical discovery. If meaning were simply a matter of reference, then (a) and (b) would have the same meaning. But they manifestly do not.

Two Dimensions of Meaning

Bedeutung (Reference): The object in the world that an expression picks out. The reference of both "the morning star" and "the evening star" is Venus.

Sinn (Sense): The mode of presentation -- the way the object is conceived. "The morning star" and "the evening star" present Venus under different modes: as the celestial body visible in the morning sky versus the one visible in the evening sky.

Frege extended this to entire sentences: the sense of a sentence is the thought (Gedanke) it expresses; the reference of a sentence is its truth value -- True or False.

Compositionality and Its Tension

Frege is widely credited with the principle of compositionalityThe principle that the meaning of a complex expression is determined by the meanings of its parts and the rules combining them.: the meaning of a complex expression is determined by the meanings of its parts and the rules combining them.

Interestingly, he also endorsed a seemingly contrary context principle: "Only in the context of a sentence does a word have meaning." Modern semantics reconciles these: word meanings are contextually determined, and once determined, they compose according to syntactic structure. But the tension is real and foreshadows later debates.

Where Frege Fits

Frege addresses what Saussure's model ignores: the relationship between language and the world. His framework is precise, logical, and foundational -- but it assumes that senses are objective, determinate, and publicly accessible. Later thinkers will challenge every one of those assumptions.

5. Wittgenstein: The Great Pivot

Why Wittgenstein Matters Especially

Ludwig Wittgenstein (1889-1951) is unique in the history of the philosophy of signs because he produced two radically different and mutually contradictory philosophies of language. His career enacts, in a single biography, the transition from fixed to fluid meaning that took the broader field a century to traverse.

Phase One: The Tractatus (1921) -- Language as Mirror

The Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus is a compressed, numbered sequence of propositions. Its core idea is the picture theory of meaning: a proposition is a logical picture of a possible state of affairs.

Key principles:

  • Logical atomism: The world consists of atomic facts. Language must mirror this structure.
  • Isomorphism: A proposition and the fact it depicts share the same "logical form."
  • The limits of language: "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent."
  • The self-consuming ladder: The propositions of the Tractatus themselves are, by the book's own criteria, nonsensical -- a ladder that must be thrown away after climbing.

This is the high-water mark of the fixed-meaning tradition.

Phase Two: The Philosophical Investigations (1953) -- Language as Toolkit

Language GamesWittgenstein's concept of distinct practices of using language, each with its own rules and purposes, embedded in forms of life. (Sprachspiele): Instead of a single logical structure, Wittgenstein argued that language consists of an indefinite plurality of language games -- giving orders, describing, joking, telling stories, guessing riddles, translating, greeting, praying. Each has its own rules within different "forms of life."

Meaning as Use: "The meaning of a word is its use in the language." Instead of searching for abstract entities that words "stand for," look at how expressions are actually employed in concrete situations.

Family ResemblanceWittgenstein's concept that categories are held together by overlapping similarities rather than a single shared essence. (Familienaehnlichkeit): Categories work without essential definitions. "Games" -- board games, card games, ball games, Olympic games -- share no single feature, only overlapping similarities, like resemblances among family members.

The Private Language Argument and the Beetle in the Box: Imagine everyone has a box containing something they call a "beetle," but no one can look into anyone else's box. The word "beetle" could not function as a name for the thing in the box -- whatever is inside "drops out of consideration as irrelevant." Meaning is determined by public language games, not private mental events.

The Pivot Point

Wittgenstein is the pivot. The early Wittgenstein represents the apotheosis of the fixed-meaning tradition. The later Wittgenstein opens the door to fluid, contextual, practice-based accounts of meaning -- without fully walking through it. He still insists on public norms and shared practices as the ground of meaning.

6. Post-Structuralism: The Destabilization of the Sign

The thinkers in this section take the insights of Saussure, Peirce, and Wittgenstein and push them to more radical conclusions. If meaning is differential (Saussure), processual (Peirce), and contextual (late Wittgenstein), what happens when you follow those principles to their logical endpoints?

Derrida: Differance and the Impossibility of Fixed Meaning

Jacques Derrida (1930-2004) took Saussure's own principles and turned them against the structuralist enterprise. His core move is elegant and devastating: if meaning is differential (as Saussure argued), then the process of differentiation can never come to rest.

DifferanceDerrida's neologism combining "to differ" (spatial distinction between signs) and "to defer" (temporal postponement of final meaning).: Derrida coined this term (with an "a" -- a deliberate misspelling) to capture a double movement: the French verb differer means both "to differ" (spatial difference between signs) and "to defer" (temporal postponement of final meaning). The neologism is itself a demonstration: the difference between differance and difference is visible in writing but inaudible in speech -- undermining the traditional privileging of speech over writing.

LogocentrismDerrida's term for the Western philosophical tendency to privilege speech over writing, presence over absence.: Western philosophy has been governed by the privileging of speech over writing, presence over absence. Derrida inverts this: the features traditionally attributed to writing -- absence, recontextualization, detachment -- are actually constitutive of all language. He calls this general condition arche-writingDerrida's term for the general condition of absence, repetition, and recontextualization that underlies all signification..

TraceDerrida's concept that every sign bears within it the absent presence of all the signs it is not; identity is constituted by difference.: Every sign bears within it the trace of all the signs it is not. "Cat" carries within it the absent presence of "bat," "cut," "car," and every other sign from which it differs. These traces are constitutive, not secondary.

No Transcendental SignifiedA hypothetical final, foundational meaning that would anchor the chain of signification. Derrida argues no such thing exists.: One signifier does not lead to a stable signified but to another signifier, in an endless chain. There is no final meaning that anchors the chain.

DeconstructionA practice of reading (associated with Derrida) that attends to the internal tensions, contradictions, and undecidabilities within texts.: A practice of reading that attends to internal tensions within texts -- showing how texts undermine their own premises, how binary oppositions depend on the subordinated term they claim to exclude.

Derrida's Parasitic Relationship to Saussure

Derrida uses Saussure's own principle (meaning is differential) to demolish Saussure's conclusion (meaning is stable within the system). If meaning is truly differential, the system can never close.

Barthes: From Structure to Pleasure

Roland Barthes (1915-1980) occupies a unique position: he was both a leading structuralist and one of the first to move beyond structuralism.

Early Barthes: Mythologies and the Orders of Signification

In Mythologies (1957), Barthes applied Saussure's method to everyday culture. His innovation was to show how Saussure's model operates at multiple levels:

  • DenotationIn Barthes's system, the first order of signification: the straightforward pairing of signifier and signified. (first-order): A photograph of a rose denotes the flower.
  • ConnotationIn Barthes's system, the second order of signification, where a complete first-order sign becomes the signifier of a new sign carrying additional cultural or emotional meaning. (second-order): The photograph of a rose connotes romance, love, passion.
  • MythIn Barthes's system, a second-order semiological system that transforms culturally contingent values into apparently natural, self-evident truths.: When connotation operates at the level of ideology, it produces myth -- transforming culture into nature. Myth takes what is historically contingent and presents it as natural, obvious, eternal.

Later Barthes: The Death of the Author

Barthes's 1967 essay "The Death of the Author" marked his transition to post-structuralism. Once a text is written, the author's intentions cease to be relevant. The text is "a multi-dimensional space in which a variety of writings, none of them original, blend and clash." Meaning resides not in origin (the author) but in destination (the reader).

S/Z and the Writerly Text

In S/Z (1970), Barthes distinguished readerly texts (positioning the reader as passive consumer) from writerly texts (making the reader a co-producer of meaning). He identified five codes through which narrative operates -- hermeneutic (enigma), proairetic (action), semic (connotation), symbolic (deep oppositions), and cultural (shared knowledge) -- demonstrating that even a seemingly "readerly" text contains a "galaxy of signifiers" rather than a "structure of signifieds."

Eco: The Encyclopedia of Signs

Umberto Eco (1932-2016), the Italian semiotician and novelist, charts a middle course. Where Derrida dissolves structure into play and Barthes celebrates the death of the author, Eco insists that meaning is open but not unlimited.

The Encyclopedia Model

Eco's most distinctive contribution is the distinction between dictionary and encyclopediaEco's model of meaning as an open-ended, rhizomatic network of cultural knowledge, in contrast to the dictionary model's finite feature sets. models of meaning. The dictionary model treats meanings as finite sets of necessary and sufficient features. The encyclopedia model treats meaning as an open-ended network of cultural knowledge, contextual associations, inferences, and interpretive pathways.

Sign Production

Eco examined how signs are actually created, identifying three key modes: overcodingEco's term for the addition of rules narrowing interpretation (e.g., genre conventions specifying that a particular chord means "danger"). (additional rules narrow interpretation), undercoding (signs produced without fully established rules), and creative abduction (inventing entirely new codes).

The Limits of Interpretation

Eco drew on Peirce's unlimited semiosis but insisted on constraints. Texts have an intentio operis (the work's own intention) distinct from both the intentio auctoris (what the author meant) and the intentio lectoris (what the reader projects). Not every reading is equally valid. The text pushes back.

Eco's Synthesis

Eco synthesizes Peirce and Saussure, incorporating Derrida's insights about openness while resisting his more radical conclusions. Meaning is encyclopedic and open-ended, but the encyclopedia has structure; interpretation is unlimited in principle, but texts and communities constrain it in practice.

7. The Grand Arc: Seeing the Whole Pattern

The Progression as a System

Stage 1: The Foundations (Frege, Saussure, early Peirce) -- Meaning is structural, determinate, and analyzable. Frege gives us two precise dimensions. Saussure gives us a closed system of differences. The early Tractatus gives us language as a logical mirror of reality.

Stage 2: The Pivot (Late Wittgenstein, Peirce's influence) -- Meaning is plural, contextual, and practice-based. The later Wittgenstein shatters the logical mirror. Peirce's unlimited semiosis introduces the idea that interpretation never fully terminates.

Stage 3: The Dissolution and Reconstruction (Derrida, Barthes, Eco) -- Meaning is differential, deferred, and politically charged. Derrida radicalizes Saussure's own premises. Barthes shows how meaning serves power. But Eco reconstructs: meaning is open but not unlimited.

The Five Living Tensions

  1. Structure vs. Process. Is meaning primarily a system of differences (Saussure) or an ongoing activity of interpretation (Peirce, late Wittgenstein)?
  2. Stability vs. Instability. Can signs have determinate meanings (Frege), or is meaning always deferred and shifting (Derrida)?
  3. System vs. Context. Is meaning a property of the langue-system (Saussure) or of particular language games within specific forms of life (Wittgenstein)?
  4. Author vs. Reader. Does meaning originate with the producer of signs or with the interpreter (Barthes, Eco)?
  5. Limits of Interpretation. Is semiosis truly unlimited (Derrida), or are there textual and communal constraints (Eco, Peirce)?
What the Pattern Tells Us

The overall movement from fixed to fluid meaning is not a simple story of progress. It is a dialectical process in which each position captures something real. Saussure is right that meaning is systemic and differential. Peirce is right that interpretation is dynamic. Frege is right that meaning has multiple dimensions. Wittgenstein is right (both times). Derrida is right that meaning can never be fully pinned down. Barthes is right that signs serve power. Eco is right that interpretation has limits. The challenge is not choosing one position but understanding how they relate.
