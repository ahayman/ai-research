Conceptual Metaphors How Mandarin Maps Abstract Thought — vs. Traditional Chinese & English

Mandarin Chinese encodes abstract concepts through a distinctive system of conceptual metaphors — vertical time, a unified heart-mind, distributed body-organ emotions, and characters that visually show you the metaphor. This three-way comparison reveals which patterns are universal, which are pan-Chinese, and which are culturally specific.

Validation Status

This explanation draws on independently validated research. 19 of 22 claims confirmed; 2 confirmed with nuance; 1 uncertain (the gradient of metaphorical transparency across simplified vs. traditional characters requires further systematic study). The vertical time metaphor is confirmed as a linguistic pattern; its cognitive influence remains contested (Boroditsky 2001 vs. Chen 2007).

Metaphor as Deep Structure

You cannot talk about time without talking about space. When an English speaker says “I’m looking forward to the weekend” or “let’s put that behind us,” they are using spatial metaphors for temporal concepts — forward is future, behind is past. This is not poetic flourish. It is how the human mind works.

The theory behind this — Conceptual Metaphor TheoryThe theory (Lakoff & Johnson, 1980) that abstract thought is systematically structured through metaphorical mappings from concrete, embodied domains (space, body, motion) to abstract domains (time, emotion, understanding). (CMT), developed by George Lakoff and Mark Johnson in Metaphors We Live By (1980) — proposes that we understand abstract domains through metaphorical mappings from concrete, embodied experience. We literally think of TIME as SPACE, of ARGUMENT as WAR, of IDEAS as FOOD.

Mandarin Chinese is one of the most fascinating test cases for this theory, because it does things English cannot: it maps time using more spatial axes, houses thinking and feeling in a single unified organ, distributes emotions across a systematic network of internal organs, and encodes metaphor visually in the characters themselves.

The three-way comparison (Mandarin vs. Traditional Chinese vs. English) is essential because many of these features are shared across all Chinese varieties while others show Mandarin-specific effects (particularly character simplification). English provides the contrasting baseline.

Time and Space: The Dual-Axis Chinese System

The Horizontal Axis Everyone Shares

All languages studied to date use spatial metaphors for time. English and Chinese share the same primary system — a horizontal front/back axis:

DirectionChineseEnglish
Front = Past前天 qiantiān “front-day” = day before yesterday“look back on the past”
Back = Future后天 hòutiān “back-day” = day after tomorrow“the weeks ahead”
Before = Earlier以前 yǐqián “before-front” = in the past“before the meeting”
After = Later以后 yǐhòu “after-back” = in the future“after the meeting”

So far, identical. Both languages conceptualize the past as the direction already traveled and the future as the direction ahead.

The Additional Vertical Axis

But Chinese has something English lacks entirely: a vertical axis for time.

ExpressionLiteralMeaning
上个星期 shàng gè xīngqī“upper week”last week
下个月 xià gè yuè“lower month”next month
上午 shàngwǔ“upper noon”morning (forenoon)
下午 xiàwǔ“lower noon”afternoon
上半年 shàng bànnián“upper half-year”first half of the year

The mapping: 上 (shàng, UP) = earlier in time; 下 (xià, DOWN) = later in time. This likely originates from traditional vertical writing, where earlier text literally appears above later text on the page.

Key Insight

Chinese has more axes available for temporal metaphor than English — not a different axis. Both languages share horizontal as primary. Chinese adds vertical as a secondary option. In corpus data, approximately 64% of Chinese spatial-temporal metaphors are horizontal and only 36% are vertical (Chen 2007). This vertical system is pan-Chinese — shared by Mandarin, Cantonese, and all Chinese varieties.

The Boroditsky Controversy

In 2001, cognitive psychologist Lera Boroditsky published a landmark study claiming that Mandarin speakers showed a cognitive bias toward vertical temporal reasoning even when tested in English — suggesting that Chinese vertical time metaphors literally shaped how speakers think. This was presented as evidence for the Sapir-Whorf hypothesisThe hypothesis that the language a person speaks influences (or determines) their perception and cognition. The “weak” version (linguistic relativity) holds that language influences thought; the “strong” version (linguistic determinism) holds that language determines thought..

The claim ignited one of cognitive linguistics’ most heated debates:

  • Chen (2007) attempted to replicate in four conditions and failed every time
  • January & Kako (2007) and Tse & Altarriba (2008) also could not replicate
  • Boroditsky, Fuhrman & McCormick (2011) provided supporting evidence with different methods
  • Fuhrman et al. (2011) found vertical time effects in Chinese speakers
Current Consensus

The linguistic pattern is real (nobody disputes that Chinese uses 上/下 for time). The cognitive influence is contested (whether these metaphors shape non-linguistic temporal reasoning remains unresolved). The relationship appears task-dependent, context-sensitive, and modulated by bilingualism and testing conditions.

Heart-Mind: Where English Splits, Chinese Unifies

Perhaps the most philosophically profound metaphorical difference between Chinese and English is the concept of 心 (xīn). In Chinese — all Chinese varieties, from Mandarin to Cantonese to Hokkien — 心 refers simultaneously to:

  1. The heart (physical organ)
  2. The mind (cognitive faculty)
  3. The emotional center (seat of feelings)

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy notes that 心 is “the nearest equivalent to the English term for mind in classical Chinese philosophy” and is “frequently translated as ‘heart-mind’ because ethical guidance in Chinese thought arises from both the cognitive function of the mind and the affective states attributed to the heart.”

Written into the Characters

This is not a vague cultural gesture — it is grammaticalized in the writing system. The heart radical (心 as standalone, 忄 as left-side variant) appears in characters spanning the entire cognitive-emotional spectrum:

DomainCharactersEnglish Classification
Cognition想 think, 思 contemplate, 忆 remember, 忘 forget, 悟 comprehend, 慧 wisdom“Mind” words
Emotion情 feeling, 怒 anger, 恐 fear, 悲 sorrow, 愁 worry, 恨 hatred“Heart” words
Disposition性 nature, 忍 endure, 惯 habitual, 恒 perseverance“Character” words

All of them carry the same heart radical. The writing system makes visible what the philosophy claims: thinking and feeling are not separate faculties. They are complementary aspects of a single unified center.

English encodes a fundamentally different model. Cognition lives in the head (“use your head,” “brainy”), while emotion lives in the heart (“follow your heart,” “heartbroken”). The opposition — “head vs. heart,” “reason vs. passion” — is one of English’s core cultural metaphors, rooted in Western philosophical dualism from Plato through Descartes.

Cross-Linguistic Parallel

The heart-mind split is a European peculiarity, not a universal. Arabic qalb (heart) serves as the seat of both understanding and emotion in Quranic tradition. Japanese kokoro and Korean maeum function similarly. Two of the world’s great intellectual traditions — Chinese and Arabic — independently unified cognition and emotion in the heart, while European languages consistently split them.

The Body as Emotional Map: Seven Organs, Seven Domains

Chinese does not just unify emotion and cognition in the heart — it distributes specific emotions across a network of internal organs, following the framework of Traditional Chinese MedicineA system of medicine developed over thousands of years in China, based on the theory that the body’s vital energy (qi) flows through meridians and that five major organs (heart, liver, spleen, lungs, kidneys) each associate with specific emotions, elements, and bodily functions. (TCM). The five major organs (五脏 wǔzàng) each associate with specific emotions and elemental forces:

OrganChineseEmotionTCM ElementExample Expression
Heart心 xīnJoy + all cognitionFire心烦 heart-vexed = agitated
Liver肝 gānAngerWood肝火旺 liver-fire-flourishing = irascible
Spleen脾 píWorry / PensivenessEarth脾气 spleen-qi = temper

The contrast with English is stark. English concentrates almost all organ-emotion metaphor in the heart, with a secondary role for the gut (“gut feeling,” “gutsy”). Everything else is frozen or archaic: “vent your spleen” (rare), “lily-livered” (archaic), “gall” (obsolescent). Chinese distributes the same emotional landscape across seven organs, each with a distinct domain and productive expressions — you can generate new liver-fire or gallbladder-size expressions and native speakers will understand them.

This system is shared across all Chinese varieties — Cantonese uses the same mappings (膽大 = brave, 脾氣 = temper). It is pan-Chinese because it derives from the TCM tradition that predates the Mandarin/Cantonese divergence by millennia.

Characters as Frozen Metaphors

Chinese possesses a category of characters — compound ideographsCharacters (会意字 huìyìzì) whose meaning is derived from the combination of their visual components. Example: 明 (bright) = 日 (sun) + 月 (moon). Many traditional analyses are now considered folk etymologies, but the visual-metaphorical readings remain culturally productive. (会意字 huìyìzì) — where the visual composition of the character is itself a metaphorical argument:

  • 明 (míng, bright) = 日 sun + 月 moon — combine the two brightest objects = brightness
  • 好 (hǎo, good) = 女 woman + 子 child — mother with child = what is good
  • 安 (ān, peace) = 宀 roof + 女 woman — sheltered person = safety
  • 家 (jiā, home) = 宀 roof + 豕 pig — domestic animal sheltered = household
  • 信 (xìn, trust) = 人 person + 言 speech — standing by your word = trustworthy
  • 休 (xiū, rest) = 人 person + 木 tree — leaning against a tree = taking rest
  • 森 (sēn, forest) = 木 tree × 3 — three trees together = dense forest
Etymology Caveat

Modern etymological research has shown that many traditional compound-ideograph analyses are folk etymologies. Some characters originally had phonetic components obscured by millennia of pronunciation change. However, even when historically uncertain, the visual-metaphorical readings function as powerful mnemonics for learners, resonate as cultural logic for native speakers, and have been taught as truth in Chinese education for centuries.

Simplification and Metaphor Loss

Here the three-way comparison yields an important observation. The most famous example: traditional 愛 (love) contains 心 (heart) at its center — love literally has heart in it. The simplified form 爱 removed the heart component. This is a case where Mandarin as written in the PRC has less visual-metaphorical transparency than Traditional Chinese as used in Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Macau.

This visual-metaphorical encoding layer is structurally impossible in alphabetic scripts. The English word “bright” gives no visual clue to its meaning — the letters b-r-i-g-h-t encode sounds, not concepts. In Chinese, 明 visually depicts the metaphorical logic: sun + moon = combined light = brightness.

The Universality Spectrum

Cross-linguistic research reveals that metaphors exist on a spectrum from universal to culture-specific. This matters for the three-way comparison because it identifies which patterns transfer across languages and which must be learned fresh.

These arise from universal bodily experience. Everyone’s body temperature rises when angry; everyone stands tall when happy and slumps when sad. They are grounded in human physiology, not cultural convention.

The Pattern

Universal metaphors are grounded in shared bodily experience (embodied cognition). Culture-specific metaphors are filtered through particular traditions — TCM and Confucianism for Chinese, individualism and capitalism for English. The body is universal; the cultural model is local.

The Verb That Eats Everything: 吃 (chī)

To see how Chinese builds metaphorical vocabulary from concrete experience, consider 吃 (chī, eat). From its literal meaning of consuming food, it extends metaphorically into suffering, emotion, cognition, and social dynamics:

English “eat” is metaphorically sparse by comparison: “eat your words,” “eat humble pie,” “what’s eating you?”, “eat the cost” — perhaps five extensions versus Chinese’s ten or more. Several factors explain 吃’s extraordinary productivity: Chinese favors monosyllabic verbs that compound easily; eating is the most fundamental embodied experience; and food occupies a central cultural role in Chinese society (吃了吗? “Have you eaten?” is a standard greeting).

Three Levels of Metaphorical Encoding

Chinese uniquely operates with metaphor at three distinct levels simultaneously — the third of which is structurally impossible in any alphabetic script:

LevelWhat It DoesChineseEnglish
1. CognitiveConceptual metaphors structure thoughtFull set (ANGER IS HEAT, TIME IS SPACE...)Full set (same universal foundation)
2. LexicalCompound words encode metaphorHighly transparent: 电脑 electric-brain = computerLess transparent: “computer” from Latin “computare”
3. GraphemicCharacters visually encode metaphorPresent: 明 sun+moon = brightImpossible — alphabetic letters encode sounds, not meanings

A Chinese reader engaging with a character like 想 (xiǎng, “to think”) is potentially processing all three levels: the conceptual metaphor (THINKING IS HEART-MIND ACTIVITY), the word meaning, and the visible 心 radical at the bottom of the character.

Simplification Effect

Traditional Chinese preserves the full graphemic layer (all three levels at full strength). Simplified characters sometimes reduce it (愛 → 爱 lost its heart). English has only two levels. The three systems form a gradient: Traditional Chinese > Mandarin (simplified) > English in metaphorical encoding depth — though this is a generalization, as most simplifications preserve semantic components.

The Deepest Insight

Abstract thought in any language is not arbitrary. It is patterned, embodied, and — once you learn which concrete domains map to which abstract ones — predictable. Chinese and English share universal patterns grounded in bodily experience. Where they differ, they differ systematically, following different but equally logical cultural models of the body, the mind, and the world. The writing system is a guide: heart radicals signal mental/emotional territory, organ metaphors follow TCM logic, and compound words decompose into their metaphorical reasoning.
