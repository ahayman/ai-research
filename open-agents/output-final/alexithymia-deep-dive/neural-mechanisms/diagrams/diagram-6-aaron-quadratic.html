The Aaron 2020 Quadratic Relationship

Aaron, Blain, Snodgress & Park (2020). "Quadratic Relationship Between Alexithymia and Interoceptive Accuracy, and Results From a Pilot Mindfulness Intervention." Frontiers in Psychiatry 11:132. U-shaped curve: both LOW and HIGH IA can co-occur with high alexithymia.

Citation correction. This paper is by Aaron, Blain, Snodgress & Park, NOT Trevisan, Mehling & McPartland. The misattribution has been propagated through the literature several times. PMC7076086. Trevisan does have separate contributions to this literature (a 2019 J Abnormal Psychology meta-analysis and a 2021 Autism Research paper), but neither is the quadratic-relationship paper.

Profile A: Detection-deficit

Low IA + High alexithymia. Reduced upstream signal. Body signals are weak or absent. Interoceptive amplification training has theoretical motivation here.

Profile B: Typical baseline

Mid IA + Lower alexithymia. The non-alexithymic majority of the population. Interoception works; emotion-labelling works.

Profile C: Parsing-deficit

High IA + High alexithymia. Body signals detected acutely but interpreted poorly. Open-ended interoceptive amplification can backfire here.

Why this matters mechanistically. If the relationship were strictly negative (alexithymia going up as IA goes down), interpretation would be straightforward: amplifying detection should help. The quadratic finding rules this out. Two distinct alexithymic profiles are present in the same population: a low-IA profile consistent with detection failure, and a high-IA profile consistent with hypervigilance-with-mislabelling — signals detected acutely but interpreted poorly. The pilot mindfulness body-scan intervention in the same study showed effect-modification by baseline IA — the same intervention works differently in the two profiles. This is the clearest single-paper architectural support for treating alexithymia as a parsing failure rather than a uniform detection failure. Single pilot study (N=76); not yet directly replicated but consistent with the Garfinkel three-dimensional architecture.

Source: Aaron RV, Blain SD, Snodgress MA, Park S. (2020) Frontiers in Psychiatry 11:132. PMC7076086.
