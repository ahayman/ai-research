Autism Bidirectional Bird & Cook 2013 hypothesis: alexithymia predicts emotional symptoms within autism. Constitutional shared substrate likely. Cumulative-overwhelm architecture in adulthood. → Cat 8 (full architecture)
PTSD Bidirectional Pre-trauma alexithymia → post-trauma PTSD severity (McCaslin 2006 NYC police prospective, n=54 over and above pre-9/11 symptoms). Trauma → secondary alexithymia (Honkalampi multi-wave). Treatment moderation (Pugach PE outcome). → Cat 7 (full architecture)
Complex PTSD Bidirectional Developmental trauma → alexithymia (Schimmenti / Caretti / Cloitre). Chronic CPTSD → maintenance via alexithymia (longitudinal evidence). STAIR-NT lineage operationalises this. → Cat 7
Eating disorders Prospective vulnerability DIF specifically predicts poor prognosis (Nowakowski 2013). Inpatient prospective work shows persistent trait component predicts post-discharge recurrence. Direction of original onset less clear. —
Depression Bidirectional Genetic correlation r=0.65 (Picardi 2011 Italian twin) suggests shared substrate. State component fluctuates with episodes; trait core stable. Reduced CBT response in high-alex patients. —
Anxiety disorders Cross-sectional Strong cross-sectional comorbidity. Somatic-amplification pathway. Prospective directional work limited; shared-substrate reading parsimonious. —
Chronic pain / SSD / FM Prospective vulnerability Aaron 2019 meta-analysis: 15–52% prevalence. Critical caveat: alexithymia–pain association attenuates substantially when controlling for negative affect. Lumley EAET RCTs show alexithymia-targeted treatment moderates pain reduction. → Cat 7 (somatization mechanism)
ADHD Cross-sectional Strong cross-sectional comorbidity (20–44%). Prospective directional work thin. Shared-substrate hypothesis (executive-function deficits underlying both) parsimonious. —
Substance use Bidirectional Honkalampi 2022 d=0.83 in clinical samples. Pre-existing alexithymia → vulnerability via emotion-regulation-via-substance hypothesis. Chronic use → degraded emotion-recognition capacity. Both limbs supported. —
Schizophrenia spectrum Cross-sectional Cognitive alexithymia gradient across psychosis-risk continuum (van der Velde 2015; Ozdemir 2025). Caveat: narrow research lineage (Polish, Dutch). Direction (vulnerability vs consequence vs shared substrate) underdetermined. —
FND / PNES Cross-sectional ~40% prevalence (Demartini 2014; PMC11608540). Autonomic-symptom misattribution mechanism (PMC4173967). Trauma-subtype FND shows alexithymia partially accounting for severity. Prospective causal evidence limited. → Cat 7 (trauma-somatization)
First responders Contested direction Three readings active: (1) alexithymia as PTSD risk factor (McCaslin NYC police prospective); (2) alexithymia as protective trait allowing trauma-exposed work without acute decompensation; (3) alexithymia as trauma-induced consequence (Heinrichs firefighter prospective showed alexithymia increased with PTSD, not predicting it). Direction may differ by occupation and individual. → Cat 7
Reading this matrix. The strongest directional claims sit in autism, PTSD/CPTSD, and SUD — populations where bidirectional evidence is convergent and prospective vulnerability has been demonstrated at least in some samples. The weakest sit in conditions where only cross-sectional data exist (ADHD, anxiety) or where the literature comes from a narrow research lineage (psychosis spectrum). First responders are explicitly contested.

What this means for the user. Where the literature has bidirectional evidence, "alexithymia caused this" or "this caused my alexithymia" are both partly true; the proportion is hard to estimate from current science. Where only cross-sectional evidence exists, careful agnosticism is warranted. The Cat 7 architecture for trauma populations is the most-developed mechanism story; Cat 8 covers the autism case; for the other populations, shared-substrate readings dominate.
