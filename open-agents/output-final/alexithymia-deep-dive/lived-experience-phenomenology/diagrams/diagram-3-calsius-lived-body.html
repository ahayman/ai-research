The Lived Body — Two Axes (Calsius 2015)

A small but careful IPA study of fibromyalgia + alexithymia found that the lived-body experience is heterogeneous: it goes both ways at once.

Source. Calsius J, De Bie J, Meganck R, Truijen S. (2015) Touching the Lived Body in Patients With Medically Unexplained Symptoms. Sage Open 5(1), DOI 10.1177/2158244015574631. Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis, fibromyalgia + alexithymia. Small qualitative sample, single clinical population, peer-reviewed but lower-impact journal. The cleanest single-paper anchor for the lived-body phenomenology of alexithymia.
Axis 1

Hyper-presence of the lived body

  • The body is overwhelming, intrusive, demanding attention
  • Pain, sensation, fatigue, posture, breath — all foregrounded
  • The body is screaming, but I can't tell you what about
  • What's absent is not awareness — it's the labelling apparatus
Phenomenological texture:
  • Body is everywhere, impossible to ignore
  • Sensations have presence without categorical meaning
  • "Something is wrong" is felt; "what kind of wrong" isn't
  • Often misread by clinicians as somatisation or health anxiety alone
Axis 2

Alienation from the lived body

  • This body is doing something to me
  • I don't recognise this body
  • The body is a stranger, an adversary, a betrayer
  • Not-mine, not-trustworthy, not-integrated into self
Phenomenological texture:
  • Somatic experience is registered but severed from self-narrative
  • Body-as-stranger / body-as-traitor / body-as-malfunctioning-machine
  • Disrupted relationship between embodied experience and identity
  • Often misread by clinicians as dissociation alone

The non-obvious finding

These two axes often co-exist in the same person. The body can be hyper-present and alien at the same time — present as overwhelming sensation, alien as not-mine. Both axes are about a disrupted relationship between embodied experience and self-narrative. In a non-alexithymic person, the body provides a continuous affective ground that informs emotional self-knowledge. In the Calsius participants, the body either dominated that ground or was severed from it — but in either case, the smooth integration that produces "I feel X" was broken.

What this means: the lived-body phenomenology of alexithymia is heterogeneous. Some report feeling everything somatically with no labels; others report feeling nothing and being told they should be feeling something. Both presentations are alexithymia.

Both can be true at once: the body screams + the body is not-mine. This is one of the most-reported lived patterns and one of the least-acknowledged in the standard deficit-model framing.
Epistemic note. IPA methodology produces small samples (typically dozens), single-population focus, and rich thematic material rather than population statistics. Calsius is the cleanest single-study anchor in this part of the literature. Subsequent qualitative work in adjacent populations (chronic pain, eating disorders, autistic adults) has cited and built on the Calsius themes. The paper is not high-impact-journal, but the themes have stable cross-citation in the qualitative literature on embodied alexithymic experience.
