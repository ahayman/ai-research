The "Failed Therapy Patient" Cycle

A documented iatrogenic pattern. Where the alexithymia + therapy mismatch becomes a verdict on the person — and why that verdict is wrong.

The pattern is iatrogenic — therapy-caused. It is not a personal failing. It is documented in clinical writing (Ogrodniczuk lineage; broader writing on alliance ruptures with alexithymic clients), in the autistic-affirming literature on therapy mismatch, and in first-person accounts of repeated therapy disappointments.
Step 1

Patient enters therapy

Comes in with a problem they want help with. They have a real concern, and they are willing to do the work.

Step 2

Framework expects affective access

The therapy framework expects them to access feelings, name them, work with them in real time during sessions.

Step 3

Patient cannot reliably do this

Not because they are defended but because the architecture for it is unreliable. Substitution patterns, late recognition, and confusion arrive instead.

Step 4

Therapist reads it as resistance

Reading the difficulty through a defence-based frame, the therapist attributes it to resistance, intellectualisation, or being closed off.

Step 5

Patient hears they're "doing therapy wrong"

Implicit or explicit messaging that they are not engaging well, not bringing affect, intellectualising too much, defended.

Step 6

Patient internalises personal failing

The framing is absorbed: the problem must be with me. I'm not good at this. I'm a bad therapy patient.

Step 7

Continue feeling inadequate, or leave

Either continue and feel chronically inadequate, or leave and add this to a growing list of failed therapy attempts.

Step 8

Pre-loaded shame for next attempt

The next time therapy is considered, the failed-patient narrative is already in place. Help-seeking itself becomes harder.

→ Cycle repeats with next clinician →

What this compounds with

  • The original alexithymia is still present. The architecture didn't change.
  • A new layer of self-pathologisation is added on top — "I'm bad at therapy" becomes a stable self-belief.
  • Help-seeking avoidance. Future contexts where emotional disclosure is invited become more aversive.
  • Relational consequences. The "I can't do this" frame transfers to friendships and relationships where emotional reciprocity is part of the structure.
  • Worsening of the original problem. The thing that brought the person to therapy in the first place often hasn't been addressed; only their willingness to address it has been damaged.

The corrective frame

The "failed therapy patient" narrative is a known pattern. It is iatrogenic. It is not a personal verdict.

The therapy relationships in which it was produced were operating on assumptions about emotional access that did not match your architecture. A new therapy relationship — particularly one with a clinician who understands alexithymia, autism, or both — operates on different assumptions and may produce different outcomes.

The previous experiences are evidence of the mismatch, not of your failure. Naming this thread is part of what this category is for. The shame you may carry from those experiences is real; its source is iatrogenic; the verdict it implies about you is not the right verdict.
