Type-Specific Phenomenology

Three different inner experiences that all get called "alexithymia." The structural picture (Cat 2) and the lived-experience picture (this diagram) together help with calibration.

Type I (Bermond)

Affectless

Texture
Flatness or absence. Not depression-flatness. A more total absence.
Inside-the-head sound: "I don't feel things the way other people seem to. There's nothing there to identify. It's just empty inside."
Body
Often quiet too. The system seems to register events without the affective response.
Behaviour
Can do the actions that would normally accompany emotion (cry, raise voice) without the felt-sense backing them.
Maps to
The classic psychosomatic-medicine phenotype Sifneos and Nemiah described.
Honest take
"I don't have feelings" is often more literal than defence-based clinical training takes it to be.
Type II (Bermond)

Feelings present but illegible

Texture
Intensity is present. Categorical labelling fails. Something big is happening — can't tell you what kind of big.
Inside-the-head sound: "Something is happening — I just can't tell you what. I'm intense, I just don't know what kind of intense."
Body
High arousal, strong sensations. The system is producing affect; the labelling step is what fails.
Behaviour
May be able to identify others' emotions accurately while being unable to identify own — a particularly bewildering dissociation.
Maps to
Often where autistic alexithymia lives. Pairs with high-arousal autistic experience and meltdown phenomenology (cross-link Cat 8).
Honest take
External rules sometimes develop: "if I'm short-tempered I'm probably overwhelmed." Compensation through pattern-recognition rather than direct access.
Hypervigilant cluster

Body loud + mislabelled

Texture
Body signals dominate awareness. They get interpreted somatically and often catastrophically.
Inside-the-head sound: "My body is loud and I keep interpreting it the wrong way. Chest tight = heart attack. Tight gut = serious illness."
Body
Loud, demanding, foregrounded. Pain, sensation, fatigue everywhere. The labelling step happens — toward bodily-medical categories rather than emotional ones.
Behaviour
Health-checking, somatic vigilance, anxiety about body sensations. Often misdiagnosed as somatic-symptom disorder or health anxiety alone.
Maps to
Common in fibromyalgia + alexithymia (Calsius IPA themes), somatic-symptom disorder + alexithymia, anxiety with somatic features.
Honest take
Caution about further interoceptive-awareness training (Cat 8). Building emotional vocabulary as alternative to somatic-medical vocabulary.

Calibration use — three anchor questions

If your experience sounds like Type I: Build observational/inferential routes. Use behaviour, situation, body-state as proxy data even when no felt-emotion is available. Accept that the affective channel may be genuinely thinner than the standard model assumes.
If your experience sounds like Type II: Build granularity (richer vocabulary to apply when intensity is present but unclassified). Affect-labelling practice with permission to be imprecise. Externalised pattern-recognition tracking situations and behaviours over time.
If your experience sounds hypervigilant: Caution about more body-attention training (Cat 8 details). Build emotional vocabulary as alternative to somatic-medical interpretation. Explicit work on de-catastrophising body signals.

Combinations are common. The point is not to assign yourself to one box — it's to give yourself a vocabulary for self-description and a sense of which approaches are more or less likely to fit your experience.
