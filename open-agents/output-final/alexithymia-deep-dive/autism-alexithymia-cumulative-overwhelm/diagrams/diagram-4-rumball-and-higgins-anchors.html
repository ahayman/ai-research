The Two Empirical Anchors — Rumball 2020 and Higgins/Arnold 2025

When the user asks "is the trauma response without a discrete trauma a real phenomenon, or am I imagining it?" — these are the two papers the literature has to point to.

Rumball, Happé & Grey (2020)

"Experience of Trauma and PTSD Symptoms in Autistic Adults: Risk of PTSD Development Following DSM-5 and Non-DSM-5 Traumatic Life Events" — Autism Research 13(12):2122–2132. DOI 10.1002/aur.2306. PMID 32319731.

The foundational empirical paper establishing that autistic adults can develop full PTSD symptom profiles from events that do not meet formal DSM-5 Criterion A.

Non-DSM-5 trauma sources identified by autistic adults included:

  • Abandonment / loss
  • Transitions / change
  • Social difficulties / confusion
  • Events related to one's own mental health (psychotic experiences, breakdowns) — this is where meltdowns/shutdowns implicitly fit, though not separately tabulated

Higgins, Arnold and colleagues (2025)

"An analysis of how autistic burnout relates to posttraumatic stress in autistic adults" — Research in Autism, S3050579825000105. n = 91 trauma-exposed autistic adults.

Currently the strongest empirical link between autistic burnout and posttraumatic-stress symptomatology in the peer-reviewed literature.

r = 0.70
Pearson correlation between autistic burnout (ABSI) and PTSS (PCL-5), p < .001.
r = 0.61
Burnout ↔ Depression (PHQ-9). Burnout ↔ Anxiety (GAD-7) was r = 0.67.

Exploratory factor analysis suggested shared underlying factors, particularly:

  • Self-image factors (self-criticism, identity erosion, "failed person" narratives)
  • Memory factors (impaired emotional/episodic recall during burnout periods, similar to PTSD memory disturbances)
Critical hedge — what r = 0.70 does and does not establish r = 0.70 is substantial shared variance — but NOT identity. Burnout and PTSD remain distinct constructs with different recovery pathways: burnout responds to environment / rest / unmasking; PTSD requires trauma processing. The Higgins/Arnold authors' interpretation is appropriately cautious: shared factors do not establish that burnout causes PTSS or vice versa; the constructs may simply capture overlapping aspects of chronic dysregulation in autistic adults. Both may be needed concurrently in treatment for many autistic adults.

What these two anchors together establish

Read together, Rumball and Higgins/Arnold establish:

  • The phenomenon is real — autistic adults DO develop full PTSD symptom profiles without discrete Criterion-A stressors.
  • Autistic burnout and PTSS are not the same thing, but they share substantial variance, and in autistic populations they are entangled in ways that complicate clean differentiation.
  • The "trauma without a discrete trauma" pattern is what the literature has actually documented — not what the user is misperceiving about their own experience.

What the two anchors do NOT establish: that the proposed cumulative-overwhelm cascade is the specific causal mechanism producing this picture. That hypothesis is consistent with the data but has not been longitudinally tested as a unified chain.
