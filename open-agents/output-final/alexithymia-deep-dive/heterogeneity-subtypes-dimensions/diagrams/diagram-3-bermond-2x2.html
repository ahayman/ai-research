Bermond's Type I / Type II Typology — 2x2 Cognitive-Affective Matrix

The BVAQ measures two dimensions: cognitive (identifying, verbalizing, analyzing) and affective (fantasizing, emotionalizing). Cross them for four cells. The dimensions are robust; the categorical types have weaker support.

Type I — "Affectless"

Low cognitive AND low affective. Sifneos's original psychosomatic phenotype. Feelings appear absent both as inner experience and as cognitive accessibility.
"I don't really feel things, and I can't describe what little I feel."

Type II — "Feelings Illegible"

High/normal affective, low cognitive. Feelings are present (the body reacts, arousal happens, fantasy life is intact) but illegible — the person cannot identify or describe them.
"Something's happening inside me but I have no idea what it is."
Cognitive High/Normal
can identify, verbalize, analyze

Type III — "Numb but Articulate"

Low affective, high/normal cognitive. The person can name and describe emotions but does not feel them strongly. Sometimes called "emotionally flat but cognitively-articulate."
"I know the words for emotions and can describe them, but I don't feel much."

Type IV — Non-alexithymic

High/normal cognitive AND high/normal affective. Both dimensions intact. The non-alexithymic baseline.
"I feel things, and I can identify and describe what I feel."
What replicates and what doesn't. The two-dimensional cognitive-affective structure of the BVAQ replicates across multiple languages and is the most robust measurement finding in the alexithymia literature. The categorical Type I/II typology derived from it has weaker support — a five-factor BVAQ model fits the data better than the higher-order two-factor model on which Type I/II rests. Use the dimensions as continuous measures; treat the type labels as descriptive shorthand, not as validated discrete entities.
Type II → psychosis vulnerability — careful framing. Higher cognitive alexithymia (high DIF/DDF) is elevated parametrically across the psychosis-risk spectrum (van der Velde et al. 2015 PMC4451258 — controls 48.8 → siblings 55.2 → ultra-high-risk 68.0 → patients 64.6). Ozdemir et al. 2025 (PMC12050105) confirms moderate associations in meta-analysis. However: the lineage is largely from one Dutch group plus Polish replications (Cedro 2001), no prospective UHR transition data exists, and the categorical Type II distinction itself is weakly supported — the cognitive-alexithymia dimension is the more defensible vulnerability marker than the type label.

Sources: Vorst & Bermond (2001); Larøi 2008; Cedro et al. (2001) Psych Reports 89(1):95-98; van der Velde et al. (2015) PMC4451258; Ozdemir et al. (2025) PMC12050105.
