ADIE Trial — Suggestive, Not Clean Counter-Evidence

Quadt L, Garfinkel SN et al. (2021) Interoceptive Training to Reduce Anxiety in Autistic Adults. eClinicalMedicine 39:101061. PMC8350004. The closest published test of interoception-first training in alexithymia-enriched population — but the framing matters.

Common (over-strong) framing

"ADIE shows interoceptive accuracy training improves anxiety in autistic adults — this complicates the parsing-deficit caution against body-attention work."

Why this is too strong: A 0.30 effect on a small autistic sample, missing predefined clinical-meaningfulness criteria, with 50% attrition at endpoint — is a weak counter-example, not a clean refutation.

Correctly hedged framing

ADIE produced a statistically significant but clinically modest reduction in trait anxiety in autistic adults; did NOT meet predefined criteria for minimal clinically meaningful difference; recovery-rate difference was non-significant; 50% attrition; single-centre, no replication.

This is a suggestive single-trial finding, not clean counter-evidence to parsing-deficit caution.

What ADIE does and doesn't tell us: ADIE is the closest existing test of interoception-first training in a population highly enriched for alexithymia — autistic adults. The trial is not stratified by alexithymia subtype, and somatic-symptom amplification or dropout patterns by subtype were not assessed. A modest positive signal in this trial does not refute the strong parsing-deficit caution against body-attention work, particularly given high attrition and the missed clinical-meaningfulness threshold.
Bottom line: Treat ADIE as a SUGGESTIVE single-trial finding warranting further replication and subtype-stratified analysis. Do NOT cite ADIE as evidence that interoceptive accuracy training is broadly safe for parsing-deficit alexithymia, and do NOT cite it as clean refutation of the body-attention caution. Important context for autism + alexithymia therapy choices, with appropriate epistemic humility.
