Alexithymia — literally “no words for feelings,” coined by Peter Sifneos in 1972 with John Nemiah — is a multifaceted personality trait characterised by difficulty identifying feelings (DIF), difficulty describing feelings (DDF), an externally oriented thinking style (EOT), and (in the original four-feature model) a restricted imaginative or fantasy life. It is not a DSM-5 or ICD-11 diagnosis. It is a dimensional trait continuously distributed in the general population, with clinically significant levels affecting roughly 5–13% of adults and far higher rates in clinical populations — about 50% of autistic adults, 53% (95% CI 42–65%) of people with PTSD, and 30–67% across eating disorders, substance use, and somatic-symptom populations.

The heterogeneity thesis is the spine of this deep dive. Alexithymia is not one thing. The TAS-20 facets dissociate from each other (DIF and DDF correlate; EOT often goes its own way). Bermond’s Type I (low arousal + low cognitive insight) and Type II (normal arousal + low cognitive insight) profiles capture a real cognitive–affective distinction even when the categorical types themselves don’t fully replicate. Messina’s primary / secondary / organic split divides constitutional from trauma-induced from lesion-acquired etiologies. And the most practically consequential split is whether a given alexithymic fails to detect interoceptive signals, detects but cannot parse them as emotions, or over-attends to bodily signals and mislabels them somatically. Standard clinical advice — “tune in to your body, notice your feelings” — only fits the first profile cleanly; for the third it can amplify symptoms, panic, and dissociation. Treatment matching, not treatment intensity, is the unresolved central question.

This deep dive holds alexithymia as a trait with trade-offs, not a pure deficit. The dominant clinical literature treats it as a deficit; the user has asked us to take seriously the smaller body of work documenting selectively altered (not always worse) cognition in alexithymics. That literature now spans at least eight domains: resistance to the framing effect (Hsu, Wang & Lin 2021); preserved decision-making under explicit risk alongside selective impairment under ambiguity (Zhang et al. 2017); emotional-attention bypass that protects task focus under affect load (Lee & Lee 2024; Shapiro 2011 — with Shapiro’s caveat that the medical-education literature is critical of the result); preserved cognitive empathy alongside reduced emotional empathy (Pisani 2020); cultural fit in interdependent emotional norms (Soto 2011); a narrow positive correlation with divergent-thinking idea generation; reduced anticipatory affect (Starita & Cellini); and contested occupational fit in first-responder roles. Each domain comes with cost-side hedging. The trade-off is not balanced everywhere, but it is real.

Two new categories sit at the centre of this expanded map. Category 7 traces the bidirectional alexithymia↔trauma dance — pre-trauma alexithymia as a vulnerability factor (McCaslin 2006), alexithymia as a maintaining factor in chronic PTSD (Frewen, Honkalampi), alexithymia as a treatment moderator (Pugach et al.), and trauma as one of the etiological pathways into adult alexithymia (Cloitre, Schimmenti). Category 8 examines the autism × alexithymia × cumulative-overwhelm cascade — PTSD-pattern symptoms in autistic adults without a Criterion-A stressor (Rumball 2020), autistic burnout (Raymaker 2020) entangled with PTSS at r = 0.70 (Higgins/Arnold 2025), and the predictive-coding scaffolding (Quattrocki & Friston 2014) that links alexithymia’s late-state-recognition to the meltdown/burnout cascade. Both categories matter especially for autistic readers.
