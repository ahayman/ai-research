The Self-Directed Tools Landscape

Nine clusters of tools that can be used without (or alongside) a therapist. Each card shows the central evidence weight, the core technique, and representative tools.

1. Granularity-building

Empirical: moderate · theoretical: strong

Building a richer emotional vocabulary so that a wider range of states can be distinguished. The conceptual basis for most other tools.

  • Vocabulary expansion drills
  • Structured emotion ontologies
  • Contextual differentiation practice
  • Naming-when-experiencing
Tools: Plutchik wheel, feelings wheel, Atlas of the Heart (Brown), How Emotions Are Made (Barrett).

2. Affect-labelling

Mechanism: strong · scale-up: moderate

Putting a feeling into words reduces amygdala reactivity (Lieberman 2007). Even approximate labels share some of the regulatory effect.

  • Name-it-to-tame-it practice
  • Structured emotion check-ins
  • Body-state-as-label substitution
Tools: emotion-label drills; structured check-in apps; body-state vocabulary cards.

3. Expressive writing

Mixed in alexithymia samples

Pennebaker base protocol: 15–20 min/day, 3–4 days, about a stressful experience including emotional content. Mixed effects in alexithymic samples — some benefit more, some less.

  • Cognitive/contextual scaffolding
  • Structured prompts (not just "feelings")
  • Templated language; vocabulary at the elbow
  • Permission to be imprecise
Sources: Pennebaker base; Baikie 2020 PMC7513612; Lumley EAET adaptations.

4. Structured journaling

Practitioner-validated

Templates with explicit emotion-vocabulary scaffolding. Alexithymic users typically benefit MORE from structured prompts than free journaling.

  • Sentence-starter templates
  • Body-state proxy entries
  • Behaviour and avoidance logs
  • External-indicator approach
Tools: Bergenfield/Lehtinen IFS templates; Neff structured check-ins; Mahler interoception worksheets.

5. Smartphone apps

One RCT (MT-ALEX); rest early-deployment

Digital interventions designed for alexithymia. MT-ALEX RCT (PMC6510700) is the strongest empirical anchor; Animi and AlexiLearn have coherent design rationale, limited published efficacy data.

  • Alternatives to emotion-label-only inputs
  • Allow "I don't know" as valid
  • Integrate biometric data
  • Avoid forced interoceptive practices
Apps: Animi · AlexiLearn · MT-ALEX (RCT-tested) · structured journaling apps.

6. Psychoeducation

Documented therapeutic value

Reading about alexithymia, learning the construct, distinguishing it from related conditions — itself a form of treatment for many users.

  • Reduces shame; resolves "what's wrong with me"
  • Sets proportionate expectations
  • Provides distinctions vs. apathy / suppression / depression
  • Enables informed self-advocacy
Reading: Bergenfield, Sweezy, Malow lineage; Levant ART materials; Neff (Neurodivergent Insights); Goleman Emotional Intelligence; Barrett How Emotions Are Made.

7. Externalisation

Structurally well-suited; cross-link to IFS

Bypassing the unreliable internal channel by working with reliably-detectable external signals as proxy data for internal state.

  • Heart rate / sleep / behaviour / posture
  • Avoidance and reactivity patterns
  • Drawing parts and feelings
  • Sand tray, miniatures, physical objects
Cross-link: IFS Deep Dive — Self-Led IFS & Journaling page covers parts-externalisation comprehensively.

8. Reading lists & vocabulary tools

Practice-integration required

Visual ontologies and structured vocabularies that let a user navigate from a vague approximation toward a specific term.

  • Plutchik wheel (8 primary, 8 secondary, dyads)
  • Feelings wheel (Willcox 1982; Roberts variant)
  • Atlas of the Heart (Brown 2021)
  • Atlas of Emotions (Ekman / Dalai Lama)
  • Emotion Thesaurus (Puglisi & Ackerman)
Note: reading these passively does not build granularity; using them as vocabulary aids during journaling or naming practice does.

9. Trade-off framing

User-decision framework

Build skill where alexithymia costs you. Accept where it serves you. Learn to manage cases where it does both. The integrative principle holding the toolkit together.

  • Skill where it costs (relationships, self-knowledge, early-warning detection)
  • Accept where it serves (decision rationality, emotional bypass in time-pressured work, reduced contagion in some caregiving)
  • User as the decision-maker about which side to work on
Source: the supplement Topic 3 adaptive-function evidence base.
