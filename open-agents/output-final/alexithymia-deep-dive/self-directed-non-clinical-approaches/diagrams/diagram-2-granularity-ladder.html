The Granularity Ladder — Low to High

"Granularity" (Barrett 2017) is the precision with which a person carves up emotional states. High-granularity emotional vocabularies distinguish dozens of related-but-distinct states; low-granularity ones collapse them all into "good" or "bad."

↑ More precise   ·   more useful in close relationships   ·   better self-knowledge ↑
Level 5 — Very high
"unsettled"
melancholy / wistful / bittersweet / poignant / heartsick / homesick / nostalgic / longing / contemplative / disquieted / restless-soul-tired
Highly differentiated; small but functionally meaningful distinctions; useful for memoir, poetry, intimate relationships, careful self-knowledge.
Level 4 — High
"sad"
grief / despondent / dejected / dispirited / blue / down / glum / forlorn / discouraged / heartbroken
Distinguishes intensity, duration, cause, and quality. Workable for everyday self-narration and most therapeutic work.
Level 3 — Mid
"down"
sad / disappointed / hurt / lonely / tired-of-everything
Common-vocabulary level. Sufficient for ordinary conversation; insufficient for fine-grained self-tracking.
Level 2 — Low
"bad"
not good / off / out of sorts / something is wrong / not right
Valence-only; no quality differentiation. Cannot distinguish sad-from-angry-from-anxious.
Level 1 — Very low
"I don't know"
I don't know / something / nothing / I'm fine / I can't tell
Unable to tag the state with even a valence. Often the alexithymic experience under stress or load.
↓ Less precise   ·   harder to act on   ·   harder to communicate ↓
How the ladder is climbed

Granularity is built by vocabulary expansion + practice, not by trying harder to feel. The path:

  • Study a structured emotion ontology (Plutchik wheel, feelings wheel, Atlas of the Heart).
  • Use the vocabulary at the elbow during journaling — pick the closest word, then pick the next-closest.
  • Differentiate by context: given the same situation, which level-4 word fits today versus yesterday?
  • Drill the close-pairs that matter: irritated vs. resentful vs. exasperated; sad vs. wistful vs. heartsick.
  • Allow imprecise labels to accumulate; over weeks, the categories find their referents.

Honest hedge: granularity ↔ alexithymia is conceptually well-grounded; published correlations vary across samples. Frame the relationship as "low granularity is essentially what alexithymia measures" rather than as a tight quantitative correlation.
