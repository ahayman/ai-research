Framing this category

A casual reading of the body-focused mindfulness literature produces two conflicting impressions. The first is that mindfulness, body-scan practice, somatic experiencing, and "tune in to your body" approaches are the default first-line response to alexithymia and to trauma. The second is that the same body of practices carries non-trivial documented harm: between roughly a third and half of meditators in measured samples report meaningful adverse effects, the rates spike in trauma-exposed populations, and clinicians have published explicit cautions for somatic-symptom disorder, dissociative-subtype PTSD, and parsing-deficit alexithymia profiles.

Both impressions are partly true. This category's job is to render them coherent without overclaiming on either side, and to give you an evidence-grounded basis for evaluating, modifying, or declining recommended interventions when the recommendation does not match your architecture.

One foundational distinction this category honours throughout

The harm literature for body-focused / interoception-based interventions is real and well-documented for general populations. The harm literature specifically for alexithymia subtypes is essentially absent. What exists for the alexithymia case is theoretical extrapolation from the meditation-related-adverse-effects literature, the somatosensory-amplification literature, and Treleaven's trauma-sensitive mindfulness framework — combined with the parsing-deficit-versus-detection-deficit framework from the interoception literature. Norman et al.'s 2019 systematic review of mindfulness for alexithymia explicitly notes the absence of adverse-event data from the four trials it reviewed.

This is the kind of evidence gap that gets papered over in many syntheses. This category does not paper over it.

The three-tier evidence framework

Every major claim in this category gets labelled by evidence tier. Tier 1 claims are well-established — direct empirical evidence from measured samples, replicated, uncontested at the order-of-magnitude level. Tier 2 claims are mechanistically supported — the mechanism is well-attested in adjacent literatures and is plausibly at work in the alexithymia case, but the alexithymia-stratified data does not exist to test it directly. Tier 3 claims are theoretical only — the synthesis of Tier 1 + Tier 2 leads to a specific subtype-stratified prediction, but no published study has tested it directly.

Treating Tier 3 claims with Tier 1 confidence is overclaiming. Treating Tier 1 findings with Tier 3 skepticism is underclaiming. The framework lets you weight conclusions by what the data actually supports.

What this category gives you, in service of one larger thing: a way to evaluate whether a body-focused recommendation matches your profile; a way to monitor for iatrogenic deterioration if you proceed; a way to redirect to alternatives if the profile mismatch is serious. The larger thing: an evidence-grounded basis for distinguishing "I am bad at this therapy" — often wrongful self-blame — from "this therapy is wrongly matched to my subtype."

The MRAE rate question — corrected

The first thing to flag is numerical attribution. Earlier summaries in this literature cited "20-25 per cent of meditators reporting particularly bad or frightening experiences" and attributed this to Britton 2021. Both halves of that claim need correction.

The 20-25 per cent figure was the field's a priori expectation threshold — not Britton's measured rate. It is an artefact of how mindfulness research approached harm before Britton's measurement framework was developed. Both Britton's measured rates and Goldberg's measured rates exceed that threshold.

The second issue is that early summaries blended Britton 2021 and Goldberg 2022 figures as if they came from one study. They did not.

Britton 2021 vs Goldberg 2022 — cite separately

Britton, Lindahl, Cooper, Canby, and Palitsky (2021) — Clinical Psychological Science, indexed at PMC8845498 — defined the MRAES-MBP measurement framework. Sample drawn from MBCT trials, N≈96. Britton's measured rates: 83 per cent any meditation-related side effect; 58 per cent negatively-valenced; 37 per cent negative impact on functioning; 14.1 per cent effects lasting longer than one day; 6.4 per cent effects lasting longer than one month.

Goldberg, Lam, Britton, and Davidson (2022) — Psychotherapy Research, indexed at PMC8636531 — is a different study with different methodology. Population-based US sample with lifetime meditation exposure, N=434. Goldberg's measured rates: 32.3 per cent general-item MRAE rate; 50.0 per cent one-or-more-specific-item rate; 10.4 per cent MRAE duration ≥ 1 month; 1.2 per cent functional impairment lasting ≥ 1 month.

Goldberg also reported the most common adverse-effect types: anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity. And the prospectively-identified risk factor: childhood adversity.

A defensible composite for narrative use: between roughly a third and half of meditators in measured samples report some meaningful adverse effect; roughly 6-15 per cent report effects lasting longer than a day to a month, depending on study and threshold; roughly 1 per cent report functional impairment lasting longer than a month. Anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity are among the most common types. Childhood adversity is a documented risk factor.

A separate landmark paper from this lineage is the Lindahl, Fisher, Cooper, Rosen, and Britton 2017 Varieties of Contemplative Experience study at PMC5443484. The team developed a 59-category taxonomy of meditation-related experiences across seven domains: cognitive, perceptual, affective, somatic, conative, sense of self, and social. Among 32 expert Western-Buddhist teachers in the sample, 56 of 59 categories — 95 per cent of the taxonomy — were reported, with an average of 8.5 categories per expert.

The Lindahl 2017 taxonomy does not stratify by alexithymia subgroup. It cannot directly speak to alexithymia-specific harm. It speaks to the breadth and seriousness of meditation-related experience in a population of experts — presumably the population least at risk and most able to navigate the practices. The breadth of categories reported even by experts is itself an argument that the practices are not adverse-effect-free for any population, including the most-prepared.

All three studies sit firmly in Tier 1. The findings are well-established, replicated, and uncontested at the order-of-magnitude level.

Mechanisms of harm

Underneath the prevalence figures is a mechanism question: when body-focused practice produces iatrogenic deterioration rather than benefit, what is the mechanism? The answer is not single. There are at least four well-attested mechanism families, each documented in adjacent literatures.

1. Somatosensory amplification (SSA)

Originally framed in Barsky's work on somatic-symptom-disorder populations. The mechanism: attention to a low-intensity body sensation increases its perceived salience, increases interpretation-as-threat, increases physiological reactivity, increases sensation, and loops. In somatic-symptom-disorder populations this is the central mechanism of chronic illness anxiety. Reduce body-attention focus and the loop attenuates. Add body-attention focus — including via body-scan mindfulness — and the loop intensifies.

The empirical support for this mechanism in somatic-symptom-disorder populations is strong. The mechanism's application to parsing-deficit alexithymia is not directly tested but is structurally coherent: if the alexithymic individual already has heightened interoceptive sensibility (signals are louder than baseline) but lacks the labelling architecture to translate signals into nameable affect, then sustained body-focus practice may amplify already-loud signals into unstructured distress. Tier 2.

2. Panic induction

Body sensations — palpitations, dyspnea, dizziness, tingling — can trigger panic in panic-prone individuals via the catastrophic-misinterpretation model formalised by Clark in 1986 and elaborated by Barlow and Craske. The same sensations that interoceptive-exposure protocols target deliberately in panic-disorder treatment can trigger uncued panic during open-ended body-scan practice. The asymmetry between these two contexts — therapeutic in one, harm-inducing in another — is itself important and gets a full treatment later in this category.

3. Depersonalisation deepening

Depersonalisation and derealisation are relatively common in dissociative-subtype PTSD, in dissociative disorders proper, and in some anxiety presentations. There is published clinical literature documenting that mindfulness can deepen rather than relieve depersonalisation in already-dissociating individuals. Lindahl, Britton, and colleagues published a specific paper on the nuanced mindfulness-depersonalisation relationship at PMC9043097, arguing that the relationship is bidirectional. In some populations, mindfulness reduces depersonalisation by re-grounding attention in the body. In others, mindfulness deepens depersonalisation by intensifying observer-stance and detachment from embodied experience. The clinical task is identifying which subgroup the individual sits in before prescribing the practice.

4. Trauma re-experiencing

Body-focus can trigger trauma memory and sensation re-experiencing in trauma-exposed individuals. This is the central concern that Treleaven's Trauma-Sensitive Mindfulness framework was developed to address. Lanius and colleagues' 2024 work on choice points for dissociative-subtype PTSD (PMC11257171) provides further structured clinical guidance for navigating this in trauma populations specifically.

These mechanisms do not operate in isolation. A parsing-deficit alexithymic with trauma history and somatic-symptom comorbidity sits at the intersection of all four. Body-focus may simultaneously amplify already-loud signals, trigger panic if those signals are catastrophically interpreted, deepen depersonalisation if dissociation is already present, and surface trauma re-experiencing. The mechanisms are partly overlapping pathways through which the same intervention can produce iatrogenic deterioration in the same individual.

Why parsing-deficit alexithymia is not detection-deficit alexithymia

The single most important conceptual distinction in this category. Without it, the "body-focused work for alexithymia" debate collapses into incoherence, because the same intervention has opposite-direction predictions for the two profiles.

Detection-deficit profile

Body signals are absent, low-amplitude, or below subjective threshold. The person reports "I don't feel anything in my body when I'm asked to check." Self-reported interoceptive sensibility on instruments like the MAIA or the SAQ is low. Heartbeat-detection performance is at or near chance. The deficit is in detecting the signal in the first place.

For this profile, the standard recommendation applies. Interoceptive-awareness training is the appropriate first-line intervention because the training builds detection capacity that is genuinely missing. MABT (Price and Hooven 2018, PMC5985305), MBSR adapted for low-interoception, and structured heartbeat-detection training are the protocols of record. Quadt and colleagues' 2021 ADIE trial (Lancet eClinicalMedicine) — interoceptive training for anxiety in autistic adults — showed positive results in this kind of profile. This is important counter-evidence to any strong "body-focused work always harms parsing-deficit alexithymics" claim. Body-focused work has population-specific value where the underlying architecture matches.

Parsing-deficit profile

Body signals are present, often loud or hypervigilantly attended, but uninterpretable as nameable affect. The person reports "I can feel my heart racing and my chest tight, but I don't know what I'm feeling — angry? scared? excited? It's just intensity." Self-reported interoceptive sensibility may be elevated. Heartbeat-detection performance can be at or above chance. The deficit is downstream of detection — in interpreting and labelling what is detected.

For this profile, the standard recommendation breaks down. More body-focus on already-loud signals risks amplifying the unstructured distress that the parsing failure produces. The Tier 2 mechanism evidence — Barsky's SSA, Lindahl on depersonalisation, Treleaven on trauma re-experiencing — all converges on this prediction.

Aaron, Blain, Snodgress, and Park's 2020 pilot at PMC7076086 reported a quadratic relationship between alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy: both very-low and very-high IA can co-occur with high alexithymia. The very-low-IA pole approximates the detection-deficit profile. The very-high-IA pole approximates the parsing-deficit / hypervigilant profile. Both poles have elevated alexithymia. The same study reported that body-scan-style mindfulness produced different responses across the IA distribution — suggestive of a moderation effect but the study is pilot only. Tier 2 for the dissociation between detection and parsing; Tier 3 for the subtype-by-protocol moderation prediction.

Hypervigilance with mislabelling

A specific subtype of parsing-deficit deserves separate attention. The person already attends to body signals at elevated rates — common in anxiety with somatic focus, in chronic-pain populations, in panic-prone individuals, in somatic-symptom disorder. They mislabel the signals, attributing them to threat, illness, or imminent loss-of-control. Adding body-focus practice deposits more attention onto an already over-attended channel. The mechanistic prediction is symptom amplification.

The hypervigilance-with-mislabelling profile is the highest-caution subgroup for body-focused interoceptive work in the framework that follows.

The "alexithymic patient bored / closed off in therapy" narrative

A theme that runs through alliance literature and clinical case-discussion: the alexithymic patient is described as bored, closed off, stiff, somatic-only, low-affect, hard-to-engage. The narrative frames it as a patient-side problem. Ogrodniczuk, Piper, and Joyce's 2020 paper at PMC7451330 documents the alliance-quality gap in high-alexithymia populations.

But the same data permit a therapy-mismatch reading. The clinician's protocol assumes emotional engagement that the patient cannot currently provide. The patient is not refusing; their architecture cannot generate the inputs the protocol requires. The "bored / closed off" presentation is a signal of mismatch, not a sign of pathology.

Distinguishing patient pathology from wrongly-matched therapy is itself a clinical skill. It is not the default reading in standard supervision frames.

The default reading attributes treatment-resistance to patient factors. The therapy-mismatch reading attributes the same data to protocol factors. Both readings need consideration; the second is systematically under-emphasised.

For you, this matters because it grants permission to take treatment-aversion seriously as data about protocol fit rather than as data about personal failing. If you are doing the practice as instructed, showing up consistently, not avoiding, and finding it iteratively worse rather than better — the wrongful-self-blame reading ("I must be doing it wrong") is one possibility, but it is not the only one. Profile-mismatch is another. The decision framework that follows gives a structure for evaluating which reading is more likely.

The decision framework — who should not start with body-focused work

This is the most clinically actionable section of the category. A framework for matching profile to caution-level when evaluating any body-focused or interoceptive recommendation. The framework is a clinical-decision aid for evaluation, not a definitive contraindication list. You (or a clinician) review the profile and assess caution-level, then make the protocol decision in light of the assessment.

The framework has six profiles, arranged from highest caution to lowest. Hypervigilance-with-mislabelling, dissociative-subtype PTSD, and high-trauma-load-with-low-integration-capacity sit at high caution. Parsing-deficit alexithymia sits at moderate caution. Detection-deficit alexithymia sits at low caution — standard recommendation applies. A profile with no trauma load and no somatic-symptom complications falls at standard mindfulness caution.

How to use this with a clinician. If a therapist recommends body-scan, interoceptive-awareness, or open-ended mindfulness without first asking which profile you sit in, that is a signal of a one-size-fits-all protocol rather than a profile-matched one. A reasonable conversation: "I have [profile]. The mechanism research suggests this profile may not be the best match for body-focused practice. Can we talk about external-vocabulary alternatives like granularity-building or affect-labelling first?" The framework gives you the language for the conversation.

What this framework is and is not

The framework codifies clinical reasoning that is well-supported in adjacent literatures but extrapolated to the alexithymia case. It does not say "you must not do X." It says "for this profile, the mechanism case for caution is plausible enough that a profile-matched alternative deserves consideration first." Decision authority remains with you. Protocol authority remains with the clinician. The framework's job is to make the profile-matching question explicit rather than letting it default to a protocol that may not fit.

Markers of iatrogenic deterioration to watch for

If you proceed with body-focused work and need to monitor, these are the signals that the protocol is producing iatrogenic deterioration rather than benefit. None are alexithymia-specific. They apply to any of the at-risk profiles in the decision framework.

The markers, in brief: symptom amplification over weeks of practice (sleep worse, anxiety worse, pain worse, somatic symptoms worse over a sustained period). Increased depersonalisation or derealisation. New-onset somatic symptoms emerging during a practice period. Increased shutdowns or dissociation. Increased intrusive trauma re-experiencing. Treatment-aversion despite genuine engagement effort. Increased emotional sensitivity or overwhelm. Loss of ground-state stability.

The critical reframe

When these markers appear, the protocol is the variable, not you. The default reading from many supervision frames is "the patient must keep going / try harder / surrender into the practice." The iatrogenic-harm reading is "the protocol is wrongly matched and continuing it deepens the harm." Both readings need consideration; the second is systematically under-emphasised.

What to do when markers appear. Reduce duration first. Change attention object — move to external anchors (sound, sight, contact-with-floor). Pause the practice if needed (a break of weeks to months is profile-responsive titration, not failure). Switch modalities to external-vocabulary alternatives. Reassess the profile (did you assume detection-deficit when actually parsing-deficit? did you assume no trauma load when there is one?). Consult with someone trained in trauma-sensitive mindfulness who has the framework to titrate rather than abandon.

Treleaven's framework — and the alexithymia extrapolation

Treleaven and Britton's Trauma-Sensitive Mindfulness: Practices for Safe and Transformative Healing (W.W. Norton, 2018) is the foundational practical text for this category. It provides 36 specific modifications for trauma-survivor practitioners across five principles: stay within the window of tolerance; shift attention to support stability; keep the body in mind through gradual titration; support relationship; understand social context.

Critical for this category's honesty

Treleaven's framework is trauma-focused, not alexithymia-focused. Alexithymia is not directly named as a contraindication or as a modifiable factor. The downstream parsing-deficit-alexithymia caution is an extrapolation from Treleaven's trauma-focused framework, motivated by the architectural overlap between high-alexithymia trauma profiles and the trauma profiles Treleaven addresses directly.

The extrapolation is plausible. Three points of architectural overlap support the mapping. First, high-alexithymia populations often carry trauma history — the 2026 meta-analysis on alexithymia in PTSD pooled the prevalence at 53 per cent (95% CI 42-65%). Second, the parsing-deficit profile shares the "loud signal, no label" architecture that Treleaven's framework is designed to titrate around. Third, the Tier 2 mechanism support — somatosensory amplification, depersonalisation deepening, panic induction, trauma re-experiencing — is the same mechanism family Treleaven's modifications address.

But plausibility is not direct evidence. The honest reading: Treleaven gives well-structured guidance for any profile where body-focus may overwhelm capacity. Whether the modifications transfer cleanly to specific alexithymia subgroups has not been formally tested.

In practice, this means: a clinician trained in trauma-sensitive mindfulness — Treleaven offers a certification programme — has the framework to titrate body-focused practice around the at-risk profiles in the decision framework. They may not have explicit alexithymia-specific protocols (that literature does not yet exist), but the trauma-modifications transfer plausibly. A clinician without trauma-sensitive-mindfulness training applying standard MBSR or MBCT to a high-caution profile is the iatrogenic-harm pathway.

Iatrogenic harm in trauma therapy specifically

A specific application of the general framework. Body-focused trauma therapies — Somatic Experiencing developed by Levine, Sensorimotor Psychotherapy developed by Ogden, body-based variants of EMDR — are simultaneously promoted as alexithymia-friendly (because they bypass language) AND identified as carrying amplified risk in alexithymic patients (because the parsing-deficit version means body signals are detected but mislabelled, often as catastrophic).

The risk profile for alexithymic trauma patients spans three intervention types. Standard exposure protocols (Prolonged Exposure, Cognitive Processing Therapy, trauma-focused CBT) produce desensitization without integration in this population, with possible chronic stuck-ness. Pugach and colleagues at PubMed 37917810 found that high-alexithymia patients were more likely to retain a full PTSD diagnosis at post-treatment and at six-month follow-up; alexithymia was associated with delayed treatment effects on avoidance; and high-alexithymia groups showed marginally lesser magnitude on negative changes in thinking and mood. Body-focused work without interoceptive scaffolding produces amplified somatic signal interpreted catastrophically, with possible re-experiencing or dissociative episodes. Mindfulness without trauma-sensitivity modifications produces re-experiencing and dissociation; Treleaven's framework was developed precisely for this last problem.

The phase-based protocol consensus — emerging across STAIR-NT (Cloitre lineage), Phase-Based CPTSD treatment, Treleaven's trauma-sensitive mindfulness, and Lanius's sensory-pathways model — is: stabilise → build interoceptive parsing skills → process trauma narratively → integrate. Direct exposure or unmodified body-focus before stabilisation and parsing-skill-building is the iatrogenic-harm pathway.

The interoceptive-exposure counter-example

Interoceptive exposure (IE) in panic disorder is therapeutic. It deliberately induces panic-related body sensations and works. This is sometimes cited as refuting the "body-focus is harmful" framing. The counter-example is real and the question deserves direct engagement.

IE in panic disorder is structured (specific induction protocols: chair-spinning for dizziness, straw-breathing for dyspnea, stair-climbing for palpitations, hyperventilation for tingling). Each exercise has a target sensation, a duration, and a response-prevention element. The course is time-limited — typically 8 to 12 sessions. The mechanism is prediction-error correction: the patient learns "this sensation does not predict catastrophe" through repeated exposure. The catastrophic-misinterpretation cycle is broken by direct counter-evidence. The population is panic disorder, specifically — individuals whose central problem is catastrophic misinterpretation of body sensations. Multiple RCTs support effectiveness.

Open-ended body-scan mindfulness in a high-caution profile is none of those things. It is open-ended attention to body sensations broadly, with no specific induction target. It is sustained inward focus, often 30 to 45 minutes per session, with limited per-session adaptation to participant state. The time horizon is often framed as a lifelong practice with no clear endpoint. The mechanism is cultivation of present-moment awareness, non-judgmental observation, and equanimity — not prediction-error correction in a specific catastrophic loop. The population is heterogeneous.

The two share "directing attention to body sensations" as a surface-level description but differ on every architectural variable that determines whether the intervention produces therapeutic prediction-error or iatrogenic amplification.

Not all body-focused interventions are equivalent. The same surface-description covers interventions with very different mechanisms and very different population-fit profiles.

The IE counter-example does not refute the parsing-deficit caution. It demonstrates that the structure of the intervention matters — and that evaluating any specific recommendation requires asking about structure, time horizon, mechanism, and population-fit, not just whether the intervention involves body-focus.

The right questions when evaluating any specific recommendation are not "is body-focus good or bad" — that is the wrong frame — but: What is the structure of the intervention? What is the time horizon? What is the mechanism it works through? What population is it designed for? Does my profile match that population? An IE protocol for panic disorder may be exactly right for a panic-prone alexithymic with detection-deficit profile. Open-ended MBSR body-scan may be exactly wrong for a parsing-deficit alexithymic with somatic-symptom-disorder comorbidity. Both can coexist as accurate observations.

Alternatives when interoception-first is contraindicated

When the decision framework flags moderate or high caution, alternatives that operate from the outside in rather than the inside out tend to fit better. These alternatives share a design principle: each builds external-vocabulary architecture (concepts, labels, behavioural anchors, narrative structures, intersubjective context) that gives parsing-deficit alexithymia something to parse with. They do not require accurate internal-introspection as a prerequisite. They build the parsing architecture first, then map it back to internal states gradually. This is the inverse of the standard "tune in to your body" sequence.

ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) works at the level of cognition and behaviour, not internal-affective-identification. Acceptance and values-based action without forcing identification of internal affective states. Cognitive defusion. Observer-self as a stance accessible without interoceptive accuracy. Useful even when "what am I feeling" remains uncertain.

Granularity-building from Barrett's framework is external-vocabulary expansion. Reading dense emotion words. Categorising others' emotional states in fiction or video. Discriminating high-arousal-positive from high-arousal-negative. Practicing nuanced labels in writing. Builds the conceptual architecture that parsing requires; moves from external observation to internal mapping after the vocabulary is in place. Exactly the inverse of "tune in first."

Affect-labelling from Lieberman's 2007 work. Putting feelings into words, even tentatively, reduces amygdala reactivity via a right ventrolateral PFC → mPFC → amygdala pathway. Labels can be scaffolded externally with context cues, observer description, or behavioural inference rather than requiring internal introspection. The mechanism is the labelling act itself, not the accuracy of the label.

Structured journaling scaffolded with explicit emotion-vocabulary lists, situation-feeling-thought-behaviour columns, and external-event triangulation. Pennebaker-style expressive writing modified for alexithymic samples per Baikie and colleagues 2020 at PMC7513612. The structure carries the parsing work that internal-introspection cannot reliably do alone. The page becomes the parsing architecture.

Smartphone emotion-recognition tools — MT-ALEX, Animi, AlexiLearn, structured emotion-recognition skills training (PMC6510700). Most use external-vocabulary scaffolding, situation-cue-based prompts, and structured rather than open-ended formats. The tool carries the framework so the user does not have to generate it from inside.

Mentalization-Based Therapy (MBT) from Bateman and Fonagy. Perspective-taking and reflective-function building before interoception. Operates from the intersubjective layer — "what might the other person be feeling, and how does that map to what I might be feeling" comes before "what am I feeling internally." Fischer-Kern and colleagues' 2018 MBT-for-BPD work at PMC5996479 shows alexithymia reductions.

EAET (Emotional Awareness and Expression Therapy) developed by Lumley. Structured emotional engagement focused on conflict and expression. Multiple RCTs in fibromyalgia, IBS, MUS, and older veterans with chronic pain. Higher baseline emotional dysregulation moderates greater pain reduction after EAET than after CBT — meaning the more dysregulated subgroup gains disproportionately. Structured, time-limited, conflict-focused rather than open-ended body-focus.

STAIR-NT phase-based trauma treatment from the Cloitre lineage. Phase 1 — affect-skills, vocabulary-building, distress-tolerance, interpersonal scaffolding. Phase 2 — narrative trauma processing once Phase 1 skills are in place. Designed precisely for complex-trauma populations with severe affect-regulation deficits.

The honest evidence audit

For each major claim in this category, what tier of evidence supports it.

Tier 1 Well-established

  • Meditation-related adverse effects are common in measured samples (Britton 2021; Goldberg 2022).
  • Childhood adversity is a documented risk factor (Goldberg 2022).
  • Anxiety, traumatic re-experiencing, and emotional sensitivity are among the most common MRAE types (Goldberg 2022).
  • The 32 expert teachers in the Lindahl 2017 Varieties study reported 56 of 59 categories of meditation-related challenges (95%).
  • Treleaven's 36 specific modifications are established practical guidance for trauma populations.
  • Dissociative-subtype PTSD requires specific protocol modifications when integrating mindfulness (Lanius 2024).
  • High-alexithymia patients show attenuated and delayed response to standard prolonged exposure for PTSD (Pugach et al.).

Tier 2 Mechanistically supported — theoretical extrapolation with adjacent evidence

  • Body-attention focus amplifies symptom intensity in somatic-symptom-disorder populations (Barsky SSA framework).
  • Mindfulness can deepen depersonalisation in already-dissociating individuals (Lindahl PMC9043097).
  • The parsing-deficit alexithymia profile is plausibly at higher risk for body-focused interventions on mechanistic grounds (Aaron 2020 quadratic; Garfinkel 2015 three-dimensional model; SSA framework).
  • Hypervigilance-with-mislabelling profiles are plausibly at higher risk on the same mechanistic grounds.
  • The somatization pathway from trauma to body-symptom expression in alexithymic populations is theoretically well-supported but causally underdetermined.

Tier 3 Theoretical only — direct evidence absent

  • The specific claim that interoceptive-awareness training is risky for parsing-deficit alexithymics specifically — not directly tested in subtype-stratified RCTs.
  • The Norman 2019 systematic review of mindfulness for alexithymia (PMC10270453) explicitly notes the absence of adverse-event data from the four trials it reviewed.
  • Subtype-stratified prediction of which alexithymia profile will be harmed by which protocol — extrapolated from Tier 1 + Tier 2, not directly tested.
  • Whether alexithymics specifically have higher dropout from MBSR or interoceptive training — no identified RCT explicitly reports this.

What this category gives you

Three actionable things, in service of one larger one.

The decision framework is an evaluation tool. When a clinician proposes body-focused or interoceptive work, the framework lets you assess whether your profile matches the population for which that protocol has the strongest evidence. The framework does not say "do not do this." It says "consider whether the profile fits, and consider alternatives if it does not."

The markers of deterioration are a monitoring tool. If you proceed with body-focused work, the markers tell you what to watch for and what to do if they appear. The wrongful-self-blame reading is one possibility, but it is not the only one. Profile-mismatch is another, and worth taking seriously.

The alternatives are a redirection tool. When the decision framework flags caution, external-vocabulary approaches give parsing-deficit alexithymia something to parse with, scaffolded externally rather than introspectively.

The larger thing these tools serve

An evidence-grounded basis for distinguishing "I am bad at this therapy" — often wrongful self-blame — from "this therapy is wrongly matched to my subtype." Distinguishing these two readings is itself a clinical skill. It is not the default reading in standard supervision frames. The default reading attributes treatment-resistance to patient factors. The therapy-mismatch reading attributes the same data to protocol factors. Both readings need consideration; the second is systematically under-emphasised. This category gives you the framework to consider both.

The honest bottom line: the harm literature for body-focused interventions in general populations is real and well-documented. The harm literature for alexithymia subtypes specifically is essentially absent — what exists is theoretical extrapolation from adjacent literatures. The clinically actionable response is profile-stratified evaluation, not a blanket recommendation either way. The framework, the markers, and the alternatives give you the structure to evaluate, monitor, and redirect — without overclaiming on the strength of the underlying alexithymia-specific evidence and without dismissing the well-attested mechanism case.

Glossary

TermDefinition
MRAEMeditation-Related Adverse Effects. The umbrella term for adverse experiences arising during or after meditation practice. Britton 2021's MRAES-MBP and Goldberg 2022's population-based study are the principal measured-prevalence sources.
MRAES-MBPMeditation-Related Adverse Effects Scale — Mindfulness-Based Programs. The measurement framework Britton 2021 developed; sensitive instrument capturing mild and transient experiences as well as severe ones.
SSASomatosensory amplification. Barsky framework. Body-attention focus amplifies the perceived intensity of body signals; iteratively reinforcing in somatic-symptom-disorder populations.
Detection-deficit alexithymiaBody signals are absent, low-amplitude, or below subjective threshold. Deficit is in detecting the signal in the first place. Standard interoceptive-awareness training is the appropriate first-line recommendation for this profile.
Parsing-deficit alexithymiaBody signals are present, often loud or hypervigilantly attended, but uninterpretable as nameable affect. Deficit is downstream of detection — in interpreting and labelling. Standard recommendation breaks down for this profile; external-vocabulary alternatives tend to fit better.
Hypervigilance with mislabellingA specific subtype of parsing-deficit. Person already attends to body signals at elevated rates and mislabels them as threat / illness / loss-of-control. Highest-caution subgroup for body-focused interoceptive work.
Three-tier evidence frameworkTier 1 well-established; Tier 2 mechanistically supported; Tier 3 theoretical only. The category labels each major claim by tier so users can weight conclusions by what the data actually supports.
Treleaven's Trauma-Sensitive MindfulnessFoundational practical text (Treleaven & Britton 2018, W.W. Norton). 36 specific modifications across 5 principles for trauma-survivor practitioners. Trauma-focused, not alexithymia-focused; the alexithymia application is an extrapolation.
Interoceptive exposure (IE)Structured exposure protocol in panic-disorder treatment. Deliberately induces panic-related body sensations to break catastrophic-misinterpretation cycles via prediction-error correction. Therapeutic in panic disorder; structurally different from open-ended body-scan mindfulness.
STAIR-NTSkills Training in Affective and Interpersonal Regulation Narrative Therapy. Cloitre lineage. Phase-based: affect-skills first, trauma processing second. Designed for complex-trauma populations with severe affect-regulation deficits.
Phase-based protocol consensusStabilise → build interoceptive parsing skills → process trauma narratively → integrate. Emerges across STAIR-NT, Phase-Based CPTSD treatment, Treleaven trauma-sensitive mindfulness, and Lanius's sensory-pathways model.
Iatrogenic harmHarm caused by medical or therapeutic treatment itself rather than by the underlying condition. In this category: the documented adverse effects of body-focused / interoceptive interventions in at-risk profiles.
Lanius choice points2024 framework (PMC11257171) for integrating mindfulness with dissociative-subtype PTSD. Real published clinical literature; not alexithymia-specific but applies to many alexithymics with trauma history.
