The Interoceptive-Exposure Counter-Example

Interoceptive exposure (IE) in panic disorder is therapeutic. It deliberately induces panic-related body sensations — and works. This is sometimes cited as refuting the "body-focus is harmful" framing. The counter-example is real but does not refute the parsing-deficit caution. Not all body-focused interventions are equivalent.

Interoceptive Exposure (IE) in Panic Disorder

Standard CBT-for-panic protocols (Barlow / Craske); PMC1559685; PMC3727659; Psychology Tools framework
  • StructureSpecific induction protocols: chair-spinning for dizziness; straw-breathing for dyspnea; stair-climbing for palpitations; hyperventilation for tingling. Each exercise has a target sensation, a duration, a response-prevention element.
  • Time-limitedTypical course: 8-12 sessions. Clear endpoint. Not a lifelong practice.
  • MechanismPrediction-error correction. Patient learns "this sensation does not predict catastrophe" through repeated exposure. The catastrophic-misinterpretation cycle is broken by direct counter-evidence.
  • PopulationPanic disorder. Specifically, individuals whose central problem is catastrophic misinterpretation of body sensations. Diagnostic core: agoraphobia / panic.
  • GoalReduce catastrophic interpretation. Build tolerance for harmless body sensations. Restore behavioural and contextual access (return to driving, public transit, exercise).
  • OutcomeTherapeutic. Multiple RCTs support effectiveness in panic disorder.

Open-Ended Body-Scan Mindfulness in High-Caution Profile

Standard MBSR/MBCT body-scan; the protocol the decision framework (Diagram 4) flags caution against
  • StructureOpen-ended attention to body sensations, broadly. No specific induction target. Sustained inward focus, often 30-45 minutes per session. Practitioner-led with limited per-session adaptation to participant state.
  • Time horizonOften framed as a lifelong practice. No clear endpoint.
  • MechanismCultivation of present-moment awareness; non-judgmental observation; equanimity. The mechanism is not prediction-error correction in a specific catastrophic-loop.
  • PopulationHeterogeneous. Often delivered to mixed groups including individuals with somatic-symptom disorder, dissociative-subtype PTSD, parsing-deficit alexithymia — populations for which the intervention's structure does not address their specific architecture.
  • GoalBroad: stress reduction, emotion regulation, "tune in to your body." Less population-specific framing.
  • Outcome in high-caution profilesRisk of somatosensory amplification, panic induction, depersonalisation deepening, trauma re-experiencing. The mechanisms in Diagram 3.
The asymmetry that explains the counter-example without resolving it. IE in panic disorder is structured, time-limited, prediction-correcting, and population-specific. Open-ended body-scan in a heterogeneous population is none of those. The two share "directing attention to body sensations" as a surface-level description but differ on every architectural variable that determines whether the intervention produces therapeutic prediction-error or iatrogenic amplification. This is not a refutation of the parsing-deficit caution — it is evidence that the structure of the intervention matters, and that the same surface-description ("body-focused work") covers interventions with very different mechanisms and very different population-fit profiles.
What this means for evaluating any specific recommendation. When a clinician proposes "body-focused work," the relevant questions are not "is body-focus good or bad" (the wrong question) but: What is the structure of the intervention? What is the time horizon? What is the mechanism it works through? What population is it designed for? Does my profile match that population? An IE protocol for panic disorder may be exactly right for a panic-prone alexithymic with detection-deficit profile. Open-ended MBSR body-scan may be exactly wrong for a parsing-deficit alexithymic with somatic-symptom-disorder comorbidity. Both can coexist as accurate observations.
