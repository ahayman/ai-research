Markers of Iatrogenic Deterioration to Watch For

If body-focused / interoceptive practice is iteratively making things worse rather than better, these are the signals to watch for. None are specific to alexithymia — they apply to any at-risk profile from Diagram 4.

The critical reframe. When these markers appear, the protocol is the variable, not you. The default reading from many supervision frames is "the patient must keep going / try harder / surrender into the practice." The iatrogenic-harm reading is "the protocol is wrongly matched and continuing it deepens the harm." Both readings need consideration; the second is systematically under-emphasised in standard mindfulness teaching.
1

Symptom amplification over weeks

Sleep worse, anxiety worse, pain worse, somatic symptoms worse over a sustained period of practice. Not transient discomfort — iterative deepening across weeks. The somatosensory-amplification mechanism in action.

2

Increased depersonalisation / derealisation

More "I'm not in my body," more "this isn't real," more "I'm watching myself from outside." More observer-distance, less embodied presence. Especially concerning if depersonalisation was already present before practice started.

3

New-onset somatic symptoms

Body symptoms emerging during a practice period that were not present before. New chronic pain, new gastrointestinal complaints, new cardiac sensations, new fatigue patterns. The body-attention-driven amplification can produce symptoms that were sub-threshold before sustained focus brought them into salience.

4

Increased shutdowns or dissociation

More freezing, more zoning-out, more time-loss, more "I don't know what just happened." Especially concerning in trauma-history populations where dissociation is already a coping pattern.

5

Increased intrusive trauma re-experiencing

More flashbacks, more nightmares, more body-memory floods. Especially concerning if practice is unstructured / open-ended rather than trauma-sensitive-modified per Treleaven.

6

Treatment-aversion despite genuine engagement effort

You are doing the practice as instructed. You are not avoiding. You are showing up. And it is iteratively worse, not better. Self-blame ("I must be doing it wrong") is the wrong reading. Profile-mismatch is a real possibility worth considering.

7

Increased emotional sensitivity / overwhelm

Affective signal becoming louder without becoming more legible. More flooding, less capacity to navigate. Goldberg 2022 documents emotional sensitivity as among the most common MRAE types — and it can present as either useful (more granular access) or harmful (overwhelm without skill scaffolding).

8

Loss of ground-state stability

Day-to-day functional baseline shifting downward. More days where you cannot do tasks you used to manage. More relational rupture. More avoidance. Practice that is doing its job should make ground-state more stable, not less.

What to do when markers appear.
  • Reduce duration first. Shorter sessions, more breaks, less daily volume.
  • Change attention object. Move away from internal body-focus to an external anchor (sound, sight, contact-with-floor).
  • Pause the practice. A break of weeks to months is not a failure — it is profile-responsive titration.
  • Switch modalities. Move to external-vocabulary alternatives (Diagram 6): granularity-building, affect-labelling, structured journaling, ACT.
  • Reassess the profile. Did you assume detection-deficit when actually parsing-deficit? Did you assume no trauma load when there is one?
  • Consult with someone trauma-sensitive-mindfulness-trained. Treleaven's framework names 36 specific protocol modifications — a clinician with this training can help you titrate rather than abandon.
The most important reframe. Treatment-aversion despite good engagement effort is not a sign you are bad at therapy. It can be a sign that the therapy is wrongly matched to your subtype. Distinguishing patient pathology from wrongly-matched therapy is itself a clinical skill — and it is the skill this category exists to support.
