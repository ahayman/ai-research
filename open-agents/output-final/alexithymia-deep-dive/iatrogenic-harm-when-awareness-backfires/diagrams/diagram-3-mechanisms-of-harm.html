Mechanisms of Harm in Body-Focused / Interoceptive Interventions

Four well-attested mechanisms through which body-focused practice can produce iatrogenic deterioration. Tier 2 evidence: each mechanism is documented in adjacent literatures; the alexithymia-stratified application is plausible but not directly tested.

1. Somatosensory Amplification (SSA)

Highest risk: somatic-symptom disorder, hypochondriasis, chronic pain

Body-attention focus amplifies the perceived intensity of body signals. Originally framed in somatic-symptom-disorder populations where chronic illness anxiety and body-focus iteratively reinforce each other.

attention to low-intensity sensation → increased salience → threat interpretation → increased physiological reactivity → increased sensation → loops

For parsing-deficit alexithymia: if signals are already louder than baseline but lack labelling architecture, sustained body-focus may amplify already-loud signals into unstructured distress.

Barsky framework; A Neural Circuit Framework for SSA, Psychiatryonline 10.1176/appi.neuropsych.13070170

2. Panic Induction

Highest risk: panic-prone, anxiety with somatic focus

Body sensations — palpitations, dyspnea, dizziness, tingling — can trigger panic via the catastrophic-misinterpretation model. The same sensations that interoceptive-exposure protocols target deliberately in panic-disorder treatment can trigger uncued panic during open-ended body-scan practice.

body sensation → catastrophic interpretation ("I'm dying") → sympathetic surge → intensified sensation → full panic

Asymmetry: structured interoceptive exposure in panic disorder is therapeutic. Open-ended body-scan in panic-prone alexithymics may not be. The structure of the intervention matters — see Diagram 8.

Clark 1986; Barlow / Craske CBT-for-panic

3. Depersonalisation Deepening

Highest risk: dissociative-subtype PTSD, dissociative disorders

Mindfulness can deepen rather than relieve depersonalisation in already-dissociating individuals. The relationship is bidirectional: in some populations, mindfulness reduces depersonalisation by re-grounding attention in the body; in others, it deepens depersonalisation by intensifying observer-stance and detachment from embodied experience.

observer stance → intensified detachment from embodied self → "I'm not in my body" deepens → further dissociation

The clinical task: identify which subgroup the individual sits in before prescribing the practice.

Lindahl PMC9043097; Lanius 2024 PMC11257171 on dissociative-subtype-PTSD choice points

4. Trauma Re-Experiencing

Highest risk: trauma-exposed, PTSD, complex trauma

Body-focus can trigger trauma memory and sensation re-experiencing. This is the central concern that Treleaven's Trauma-Sensitive Mindfulness framework was developed to address.

sustained inward attention → trauma-related body memory surface → flashback / sensation flood → re-experiencing without containment

Practice modifications from Treleaven include: choice in attention object, eyes-open practice options, shorter durations, anchoring to neutral or safe-feeling body regions, explicit titration around dissociation markers. The framework is trauma-focused, not alexithymia-focused; the alexithymia-specific application is an extrapolation.

Treleaven & Britton (2018) Trauma-Sensitive Mindfulness, W.W. Norton
How these mechanisms combine. A parsing-deficit alexithymic with trauma history and somatic-symptom comorbidity sits at the intersection of all four mechanisms. Body-focus may simultaneously: amplify already-loud signals (SSA), trigger panic if the signals are catastrophically interpreted, deepen depersonalisation if dissociation is already present, and surface trauma re-experiencing. The mechanisms are not alternatives to be chosen between — they are partly overlapping pathways through which the same intervention can produce iatrogenic deterioration.
