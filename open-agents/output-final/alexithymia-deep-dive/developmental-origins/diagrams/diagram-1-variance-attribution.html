Variance Attribution — What Twin Studies Tell Us

Alexithymia is partly heritable, mostly environmentally shaped. Two large twin studies; the heritable fraction is real but the larger fraction is from individual life experience.

Heritable (genetic)
Non-shared environment (individual experience)
Shared environment (family climate)

Danish twin study

Jorgensen, Zachariae, Skytthe, Kyvik (2007) · N = 8,785 twin pairs · PubMed 17917473
Heritable~30%
Non-shared environment~52%
Shared environment~18%
The largest alexithymia twin study to date. Heritability range 30-33% across TAS-20 total and facets. Non-shared environment is the dominant variance component.

Italian twin study

Picardi, Fagnani, Gigantesco et al. (2011) · N = 729 twin pairs · PubMed 21911104
Heritable~42%
Non-shared environment~46%
Shared environment~12%
Younger sample. Heritability 42% (drops to 33% with depression as covariate; genetic correlation with depression r = 0.65). Higher heritability than Danish, plausibly because younger samples have had less time to accumulate environmental variance.
The honest reading. Across the two largest twin studies, alexithymia heritability falls in the 30–42% range depending on sample age and how depression is partialled out. The remaining 58–70% is environmental, dominated by non-shared environment — i.e., the individual's own life history, not the family climate they grew up in. This is a behavioural-genetic profile typical of partly-heritable personality traits.

What this means for "constitutional vs modifiable": a non-trivial fraction is constitutional and likely lifelong; the larger fraction is shaped by individual life experience and is — in principle — partly modifiable. No single-gene story; the genetics is polygenic and trait-level.

Sources: Jorgensen et al. (2007) Psychosomatics 48(2), PubMed 17917473; Picardi et al. (2011) J Psychosomatic Research 71(4), PubMed 21911104.
