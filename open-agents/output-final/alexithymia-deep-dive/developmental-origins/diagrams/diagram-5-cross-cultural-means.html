Cross-Cultural Patterns — TAS-20 Means and the Construct-Equivalence Question

East Asian samples consistently show elevated TAS-20 means relative to Western samples. The harder question is whether this reflects genuinely higher rates or whether the TAS-20 measures something different in interdependent cultural contexts.

Indicative TAS-20 means by region

Schematic — exact means vary by sample. The pattern across studies is consistent: Western samples lower, East Asian samples elevated, some Middle Eastern samples elevated, Mexican PTSD outpatients an extreme outlier.

The Hernandez-Ramirez 2025 figure should be cited as an outlier from a single Mexican clinical-treatment-seeking cohort, not as a representative cross-cultural prevalence.

Two readings of the cross-cultural pattern

Reading 1: Genuine higher rates

Some of the elevation may reflect real differences in cognitive alexithymia, plausibly via developmental-cultural shaping — interdependent cultures emphasise emotional restraint as a relational virtue, which over development reduces emotional vocabulary practice and self-reflection on emotion.

Reading 2: Construct non-equivalence

The TAS-20 was developed in Western-independent contexts. Items like "I prefer to think about practical problems rather than discuss my feelings" may indicate culturally-fitted self-presentation in interdependent contexts rather than a deficit in emotional self-knowledge. Mean differences may be partly artefactual.

The literature has not produced a clean answer. Both readings are plausible. Most cross-cultural alexithymia researchers (e.g., Ryder et al. 2018) argue that both are partly true — there are real cultural differences in emotion-language and there is real construct non-equivalence in the TAS-20.

The Schouten 2020 finding — when "suppression" has different functional outcomes. Belgian and Japanese couples were assessed on expressive suppression and relationship outcomes. In Belgian (Western-independent) couples, suppression correlated with lower relationship quality — the standard "suppression is costly" finding. In Japanese (interdependent) couples, the negative correlation was absent or weakly positive. This is corroborated by Soto et al. 2011 (PMC4341898) showing the same pattern in Hong Kong Chinese vs European Americans.

The careful caveat. This finding applies to expressive suppression specifically — outward control of emotional display. It does not directly speak to the deeper emotion-identification deficit that defines alexithymia. A person who has fluent emotional self-knowledge but suppresses outward expression is not the same as a person who lacks the self-knowledge in the first place. Both can score high on TAS-20-style measures, but they are different constructs. Don't flatten this into "alexithymia is functional in East Asian cultures." The honest reading: the cost-benefit calculus of emotional restraint is culture-dependent.
