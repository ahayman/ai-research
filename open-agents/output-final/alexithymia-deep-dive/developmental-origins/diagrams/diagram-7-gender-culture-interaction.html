Gender × Culture Interaction — Why "Men Are More Alexithymic" Oversimplifies

The gender effect in alexithymia is small in absolute terms (d ≈ 0.22) and heavily moderated by culture. Levant's "normative male alexithymia" is documented as a cost in independent-cultural contexts, but Schouten 2020's cross-cultural finding shows the cost calculus differs by context.

The headline meta-analytic finding

A 2024 meta-analysis of gender differences in alexithymia produced an effect size of d ≈ 0.22 — men slightly higher than women, but small in absolute terms. Within-gender variance dwarfs the between-gender mean difference.

Most alexithymic men are not more alexithymic than most non-alexithymic women. The mean shift is real but small. Treating gender as predictive of alexithymia at the individual level is overinterpretation.

The Levant normative male alexithymia frame

Restrictive masculine gender socialisation — "boys don't cry," "be tough," "don't show weakness" — over development reduces emotional vocabulary practice. This is the secondary/developmental etiology pathway specifically for men.

  • NMAS (Normative Male Alexithymia Scale) — Levant et al. measurement instrument designed to capture this specific pathway.
  • ART (Alexithymia Reduction Treatment) — Levant's brief psychoeducational group intervention. Small-to-moderate effects. Not a large RCT base.
  • Cost side — relationship dissatisfaction, failure to seek mental-health support, somatic-symptom expression of distress, downstream emotion-suppression costs.

The cultural moderation — Schouten 2020 and Soto 2011

The cost calculus of emotional restraint is not uniform across cultures. The Belgian-Japanese (Schouten et al. 2020) and US-Hong-Kong (Soto et al. 2011) studies show that expressive suppression has different functional outcomes in independent vs interdependent cultural contexts.

The careful caveat (per validator). Schouten 2020's finding applies to expressive suppression specifically — outward control of emotional display. It does not directly speak to the deeper emotion-identification deficit that defines alexithymia. A person who has fluent emotional self-knowledge but suppresses outward expression is not the same as a person who lacks the self-knowledge in the first place. Both can score high on TAS-20-style measures, but they're different constructs.

Do not flatten this into "male alexithymia is good in some cultures." The honest reading: in independent-cultural contexts, normative male alexithymia is a documented cost (Levant). In interdependent-cultural contexts, the same emotional-restraint pattern may serve different social functions and the cost calculus is different. Both the cost side and the context-dependent benefit side need to be held simultaneously.
For the user. If you are male and culturally exposed to restrictive masculine emotional socialisation, that probably contributes to your alexithymia at the population level. The size of the gender effect is small (d ≈ 0.22), so it doesn't predict much for any one individual. Most of your alexithymia profile is not from gender per se — it's from the larger constitutional + developmental pattern. The Levant ART intervention exists if gender-socialisation is a main contributor; relational therapy (MBT, attachment-focused, EFT, IFS) is the broader category for developmental etiology generally.
