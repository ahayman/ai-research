Measurement: Which Instrument, Which Subtype, Which Limit

A navigation guide to the major alexithymia instruments — TAS-20, BVAQ, PAQ, TSIA, LEAS, OAQ-G2 — and how to triangulate them for self-classification.

Why measurement is the load-bearing question

Almost everything in this deep dive depends on a single operational decision: how was a person classified as “alexithymic”? The therapy claim, the prevalence estimate, the comorbidity finding, the genetic correlation — each rests on whatever instrument that study used. And the instruments are not interchangeable. The same person can score in different subtype lanes depending on which questionnaire is in front of them.

For self-classification, the consequence is concrete. The question “am I alexithymic, and if so, which kind?” cannot be fully answered by any single tool. The honest path is triangulation — pairing instruments so that disagreements between them carry diagnostic information. That is what this page is for: a navigation guide through the instruments, what each one captures, what each one misses, and how to combine them.

The construct itself is contested across competing theoretical frames (Sifneos psychoanalytic, Bermond cognitive–affective, Preece attention–appraisal, Bird interoceptive, Barrett constructed-emotion). Different frames prefer different instruments. Even the gold-standard combination cannot fully resolve the construct-level indeterminacy. That is a real limitation, but it does not block useful self-classification. It just rules out single-instrument certainty.

Key Takeaways

  • No single instrument captures the full construct. TAS-20, BVAQ, PAQ, TSIA, LEAS, and OAQ-G2 each measure overlapping but partially distinct facets.
  • Self-report has a structural limit. The TAS-20 asks alexithymic people to introspect on their introspection — the “Lundh paradox.” A single self-report score is a starting point, not a verdict.
  • Triangulation is the practical answer. Pairing TAS-20 with MAIA, BVAQ or PAQ, and (where possible) LEAS or TSIA generates discrepancies that are themselves diagnostic.
  • Free self-administration covers most of the territory. TAS-20 + MAIA + PAQ takes about 30 minutes and is freely available.
  • Garfinkel’s three-dimensional interoception model — accuracy, sensibility, awareness — is the architecture that distinguishes parsing-deficit from detection-deficit profiles, and identifies the somatic-symptom-disorder profile (high attention + poor inference) for whom open-ended interoceptive training is contraindicated.

Prerequisites

This page assumes you have read Cat 1 (Definition, History, Status) for the construct’s origins and Cat 2 (Heterogeneity / Subtypes / Dimensions) for the subtype taxonomy this category operationalises. The instruments below are the tools by which the Cat 2 subtypes are detected.

The dominant tool: TAS-20

The Toronto Alexithymia Scale, 20-item version, is the instrument the literature was built on. Bagby, Parker, and Taylor published its development as a pair of papers in the Journal of Psychosomatic Research in 1994. Twenty Likert items. Three-factor structure. Total scores from 20 to 100, higher meaning more alexithymic. Conventional cutoffs of 51 and 61 separate non-alexithymic, borderline, and alexithymic categories.

The three factors are stable parts of the alexithymia vocabulary. DIF, Difficulty Identifying Feelings, asks the respondent how often they cannot tell what emotion they’re experiencing. “I am often confused about what emotion I am feeling.” “When I am upset, I don’t know if I am sad, frightened, or angry.” Seven items. DDF, Difficulty Describing Feelings, addresses the verbal step — can you put what you feel into words? “It is difficult for me to find the right words for my feelings.” Five items. EOT, Externally-Oriented Thinking, captures the disposition to focus on practical externals rather than inner states. “I prefer to talk to people about their daily activities rather than their feelings.” “I find examination of my feelings useful in solving personal problems.” Eight items, several reverse-scored.

The single most rigorous psychometric work on TAS-20 is Schroeders, Kubera, and Gnambs (2022), a meta-analytic confirmatory factor analysis spanning 28 studies, more than 17,000 participants, and 19 languages. The three-factor structure replicates. Bifactor models that add a general alexithymia factor to the three specific factors fit slightly better, suggesting a hierarchical structure: a global alexithymia trait alongside the three subdomain facets.

Strengths

TAS-20 is the most validated alexithymia measure in any language; it has decades of cross-cultural data and normative ranges in clinical populations from autism to chronic pain to psychosis. It’s brief — three minutes for most respondents. It’s free for non-commercial individual use, and circulating online versions make it accessible to almost anyone with a browser. If the goal is to compare oneself to published research populations, TAS-20 is the instrument those populations were measured with.

Weaknesses

The DIF and DDF factors are robust, with internal consistency reliably in the 0.78–0.85 range across populations. The EOT factor is the troubled one. Internal consistency typically lands lower — alphas around 0.55–0.70 — and the factor sometimes splits or fails to emerge cleanly in cross-cultural samples, especially in some East Asian datasets where cultural-loading effects on the reverse-scored items are suspected.

The TAS-20 also covers only negative or neutral emotion. The items are about identifying feelings under distress, not identifying joy, pride, contentment, or curiosity. A person who is fluent with positive affect but illiterate with negative affect gets the same score as someone impaired across all valences. The construct narrows accordingly.

The instrument also dropped the imaginative-fantasy facet that Sifneos’s original 1970s clinical description included. Items related to fantasy did not cluster reliably in factor analyses during scale development, so they were removed. The TSIA later restored that fourth facet. The TAS-20 does not measure it.

The self-report-on-introspection paradox

The deepest critique is structural. Lundh (2001) articulated what’s now called the self-report-on-introspection paradox. The TAS-20 asks an alexithymic person to introspect on their introspection. To answer accurately whether you are “often confused about what emotion you are feeling,” you need the very introspective access whose impairment the scale is trying to detect. People with severe DIF cannot reliably know how confused they are; they only know how confused they notice themselves to be. They can underrate (because they don’t notice the confusion) or overrate (because they confuse other states with confusion).

The TAS-20 manual acknowledges this and treats it pragmatically — a self-report measure with known limits is better than no measure — but the structural issue does not go away. The empirical signature is the weak correlation between TAS-20 and the performance-based LEAS (typically r = .15 to .30). The two methods correlate modestly because they tap related but partially distinct constructs.

For self-classification, the take-home is: TAS-20 is a useful starting point, not a verdict. It is the right instrument to begin with, but a single TAS-20 score does not finish the job.

Adding the affective dimension: BVAQ

Vorst and Bermond’s 2001 BVAQ — Bermond–Vorst Alexithymia Questionnaire — was designed to extend the construct in exactly the direction the TAS-20 narrowed it. Forty items in the full version (a 20-item BVAQ-B short form exists), five factors, organised into two dimensions.

The cognitive dimension contains three factors: identifying, verbalising, and analysing. These cover the same conceptual ground as the TAS-20’s DIF and DDF. The affective dimension contains two factors that the TAS-20 does not measure: fantasising (engagement in imaginative life, daydreaming, vivid imagery) and emotionalising (strength of emotional arousal and responsiveness).

This two-axis structure is what allows the BVAQ to distinguish Bermond’s Type I from Type II profile. A person low on both dimensions — low cognitive insight AND low emotional arousability — fits Type I, the classic “affectless” presentation that maps closely to the original psychosomatic phenotype Sifneos was writing about. A person low on the cognitive dimension but normal or high on the affective dimension fits Type II — feelings are present but illegible to the self. The difference is real and clinically meaningful: Type II has been linked specifically to psychosis vulnerability in van der Velde and colleagues’ 2015 prospective-cohort work.

Strengths and weaknesses

The BVAQ’s strengths are precisely where TAS-20 falls short. It captures the affective dimension. It supports the cognitive-vs-affective comparison. It tracks the imaginative facet that Sifneos considered foundational.

The weaknesses are also specific. Less cross-cultural data — the norm base is primarily Dutch and German with smaller validation samples in English and elsewhere. The categorical Type I / Type II split that BVAQ enables is itself empirically weakly supported; latent profile and factor mixture analyses (Schaeuffele et al. 2021 is a clean example) tend to find that the underlying cognitive and affective dimensions are continuous rather than discrete, and the discrete typology does not robustly emerge. The pragmatic implication is that BVAQ is best used dimensionally — “where am I on cognitive? where am I on affective?” — rather than categorically. The same self-report paradox applies.

For self-classification, the BVAQ is the natural second instrument when the question is whether your alexithymia is cognitive-only or cognitive-plus-affective. It pairs with TAS-20 cleanly: TAS-20 anchors against the broader literature, BVAQ adds the dimension TAS-20 omits.

The newer entrant with positive emotion: PAQ

Preece, Becerra, Robinson, Dandy, and Allan published the Perth Alexithymia Questionnaire in 2018. It’s the newest of the major instruments and has been gaining ground in research samples. Twenty-four items in the full version, six items in the screening version PAQ-S.

The PAQ’s structural innovation is splitting valence. Where TAS-20 measures only difficulty with negative emotions, the PAQ measures DIF and DDF separately for positive and negative emotions. A person who can identify and describe sadness but not joy gets a different profile than a person impaired across both valences.

The full structure is five facets: Negative-DIF, Positive-DIF, Negative-DDF, Positive-DDF, and a General-EOT.

The PAQ is also theoretically grounded in the Preece group’s attention–appraisal model of alexithymia, which frames the construct as a failure at the attention or appraisal stage of emotion regulation rather than (or in addition to) the verbal-labelling step. Items map onto that model directly, so PAQ scores have theoretical interpretation, not just empirical description.

Strengths and weaknesses

Strengths: theoretical grounding, both valences, good replicating five-factor structure, internal consistency above 0.80 across factors in most validation samples, growing language coverage.

Weaknesses: less cross-cultural data than TAS-20 (the instrument is younger; the literature has not had decades to accumulate norms); cutoffs are less standardised, so it’s harder to draw a clean “this score = alexithymic” conclusion the way TAS-20’s ≥61 allows; same self-report paradox; the negative-valence facets overlap heavily with TAS-20, so the unique value of the PAQ comes from the positive-valence facets and the EOT measure.

For self-classification, PAQ is the right tool when valence-specific patterns matter. If you suspect different impairment for positive versus negative emotion, or if you want the attention–appraisal model as a theoretical scaffold, PAQ is the choice. PAQ + TAS-20 is a defensible pairing — established benchmark plus broader emotional coverage.

Beyond self-report: TSIA

The Toronto Structured Interview for Alexithymia, developed by Bagby, Taylor, Parker, and Dickens in 2006, is observer-rated. The interviewer asks open-ended questions and rates the respondent’s answers on a 24-item, four-factor structure: DIF, DDF, EOT, and imaginal processes — restoring the imaginative facet that the TAS-20 lost. Items are rated by the trained interviewer on a 0–2 scale based on what the respondent actually says and how they engage during the interview.

The strength of the TSIA is that it bypasses the self-report-on-introspection paradox. A respondent who underrates their own confusion, or who lacks the introspective access needed for accurate self-report, is rated by an external observer based on their actual performance. The interview also surfaces qualitative material that scales miss — the way a person describes (or fails to describe) an emotional event tells the rater things a Likert response cannot. Convergent validity with TAS-20 is moderate (typically r = .50–.70), and TSIA shows incremental validity in predicting external criteria.

The weaknesses are practical. Trained interviewer required. Thirty to forty-five minutes plus rating time. Inter-rater reliability requires calibration; without proper training, two raters can produce divergent scores. The administration manual and training materials are typically purchased from MHS. And the interview cannot be self-administered.

For most self-classifying users, TSIA is impractical. For users working with a clinician who can administer it (or an unstructured clinical interview informed by the same domains), it is the gold standard for cutting through self-report limits. Its value rises specifically when self-report results seem misaligned with clinical observation.

Performance-based: LEAS

Lane, Quinlan, Schwartz, Walker, and Zeitlin’s Levels of Emotional Awareness Scale, published in 1990, takes a different approach. Rather than asking the respondent to rate their own awareness, it asks them to demonstrate it. Twenty short interpersonal vignettes — a paragraph each, typical structure: “You and your best friend are in the same line of work. There is a prize given annually to the best performer. The two of you work hard to win the prize…” The respondent writes free-response answers describing what they would feel and what the other person would feel. Trained coders rate each response on a five-level developmental scale modelled on Piaget and Werner.

  • Level 0: No emotion words, only cognitive content.
  • Level 1: Bodily-sensation words (“I would feel hot”).
  • Level 2: Action-oriented words (“I would want to leave”).
  • Level 3: Single, undifferentiated emotion words (“I would feel bad”).
  • Level 4: Complex or blended emotions (“I would feel sad and proud at the same time”).
  • Level 5: Understanding of complex blends in both self and the other person.

The LEAS is the cleanest performance-based bypass of the self-report paradox in widespread use. The respondent doesn’t rate their own emotional awareness; they demonstrate it. The coder rates the actual emotional language they produce. If the construct is something like “the developmental complexity of the emotion concepts available to you,” LEAS measures it directly.

This is also why LEAS correlates only weakly with TAS-20, typically in the r = .15 to .30 range. The two instruments are tapping related but partially distinct constructs. Someone can have high TAS-20 (self-perceived difficulty) but reasonable LEAS (their actual emotion language is fine when produced) — that pattern points to an attention or appraisal issue, not a concept-impoverishment issue. Someone can have moderate TAS-20 but low LEAS — that pattern points to genuinely impoverished emotion concepts even if self-perceived difficulty is mild.

The weaknesses are practical: trained coders required, time-intensive coding (often thirty minutes per protocol), free-response format unfamiliar to many respondents, and limited norms for some clinical populations.

For self-classifying users, formal LEAS is hard to self-administer because it needs a trained coder. But the LEAS vignettes are described publicly in the literature, and using them as a reflective exercise — writing free responses and reflecting on the developmental level of the emotion-language they produced, even without formal coding — can be diagnostically illuminating. The exercise itself, separate from any score, can surface whether your emotion vocabulary is rich or compressed, blended or single-toned, applied to others as well as to yourself.

Observer-rated phenotype: OAQ-G2

Haviland, Warren, and Riggs’s Observer Alexithymia Questionnaire (2000), recently revised to a 12-item short form (OAQ-G2), takes the rating from someone other than the respondent — an intimate other, spouse, parent, close friend, or clinician. The rater scores the respondent on five facets: distant, uninsightful, somatising, humourless, and rigid.

The strengths are exactly the bypass of the self-report paradox. The rater is not the alexithymic person. This makes OAQ-G2 useful when self-report is suspect — severe alexithymia, dissociation, defensive responding, or simply poor self-insight. It also captures externally-observable features that self-report scales don’t directly tap; rigidity and humourlessness are examples.

The weaknesses are equally specific. Less commonly used; the published literature is much smaller than for TAS-20, with fewer cross-cultural validations. Rater bias is real — a spouse rating a partner is rating through their own perception and relationship dynamics, and ratings can be confounded by relationship satisfaction. The OAQ measures the externally-observable phenotype, which overlaps with but is not identical to the internal self-perceived construct. And it requires a willing rater with substantial knowledge of the respondent.

For self-classification, OAQ-G2 is most valuable when your self-report seems off — when scores don’t match your felt experience, or when you have reason to suspect defensive responding or limited self-insight. Asking an intimate other to complete it can surface discrepancies that diagnostic interviews can then explore.

The self-report paradox, taken seriously

Lundh’s 2001 critique deserves more than a footnote. The simplest formulation: the TAS-20 asks the alexithymic person to describe their emotional experience using introspection, but the construct being measured is precisely the impairment of that introspection. Self-reports of introspective ability cannot be more accurate than the underlying introspective ability. People with severe DIF cannot know how confused they are; they can only know how confused they notice themselves to be.

This applies equally to TAS-20, BVAQ, PAQ, and MAIA. It is part of why these instruments correlate only modestly with each other and only modestly with performance-based measures.

The field has three pragmatic responses. The first is to triangulate — pair self-report with performance-based measurement (LEAS) and observer rating (TSIA, OAQ-G2). Discrepancies between them are diagnostically informative rather than mere noise. The second is to bypass the paradox via observer-rated and performance-based measures, accepting the practical burden in exchange for cleaner construct access. The third is to hedge — treat self-report scores as one piece of evidence among others, hedge interpretations of borderline scores, and refuse to treat the self-report number as ground truth.

For self-classifying users, the honest take is that a single TAS-20 score is a starting point, not a verdict. Pairing instruments is the practical path forward.

Interoception measurement

Because the parsing-deficit framework distinguishes between failing to detect interoceptive signals and failing to interpret signals one already detects, interoception measurement is the natural complement to alexithymia measurement. The two together give a richer picture of where the deficit sits.

The heartbeat tasks — methodologically complicated

Interoception measurement has its own gold-standard task — the heartbeat-counting task (HCT), which Schandry introduced in 1981. The participant silently counts their heartbeats over short time windows (typically 25, 35, and 45 seconds) without taking a pulse. Their estimate is compared to the actual count from ECG. Interoceptive accuracy equals one minus the mean error.

The HCT has dominated interoception research for four decades. It is also methodologically problematic. Zamariola and others have shown that HCT performance is heavily confounded by knowledge of average heart rate, BMI, gender, time-estimation ability, age, anxiety, and depression. Without controlling for these, “interoceptive accuracy” partly reflects general knowledge and cognitive style rather than actual signal-detection.

Murphy 2018b. Murphy, Brewer, Hobson, Catmur, and Bird’s 2018 paper made this concrete. With N=287, initial bivariate analyses showed no relationship between alexithymia and HCT. After controlling for the confound battery, the predicted negative relationship between alexithymia and HCT performance emerged. Two findings sit side by side: the alexithymia–interoception association is real but small, and HCT alone, without confound control, is uninformative.
Nicholson 2018 — an independent challenge. The same year, an independent group — Nicholson, Williams, Grainger, Christensen, Calvo-Merino, and Gaigg, publishing in the Journal of Abnormal Psychology — used HCT and Bayesian analysis and found no association between either alexithymia or autism and HCT performance. The Bayesian analyses supported the null. This is an independent third-party challenge to the position that alexithymia involves a multidomain interoception failure, not an internal walk-back from the Bird group. The field debate is more contested than it might appear.

The reading the field has converged on is that HCT alone is too noisy to reliably detect alexithymia–interoception associations. Multidomain interoception measurement — cardiac plus respiratory plus gastric plus muscular — is more informative. The cardiac HCT in isolation is best treated as one channel among several.

MAIA — the standard self-report

The MAIA, the Multidimensional Assessment of Interoceptive Awareness developed by Mehling and colleagues (current version MAIA-2), is the standard self-report. Thirty-seven items across eight scales: noticing, not-distracting, not-worrying, attention regulation, emotional awareness, self-regulation, body listening, trusting. It captures interoceptive sensibility — the self-reported tendency to attend to body signals — in Garfinkel’s three-dimensional architecture. It’s free for non-commercial use and widely available online. It also suffers a milder version of the self-report paradox; high-alexithymia respondents can still misread their own attentional patterns, but the question (about attention to body) is one degree less recursive than asking about introspective ability per se.

Garfinkel’s three-dimensional model

The architectural backbone for thinking about all of this is Garfinkel, Seth, Barrett, Suzuki, and Critchley’s 2015 paper “Knowing your own heart”. They distinguished three dimensions of interoception that turn out to be dissociable.

  • Interoceptive Accuracy (IAcc): objective performance on a behavioural task — heartbeat detection done well or poorly.
  • Interoceptive Sensibility (ISen): self-reported tendency to attend to body signals — what MAIA measures.
  • Interoceptive Awareness (IAw): metacognitive correspondence between accuracy and sensibility — whether your self-perception of body-signal-reading matches your actual performance.

These three are dissociable. A person can have high ISen (lots of body attention) and low IAcc (poor performance), meaning they pay close attention to their body but their inferences about what the signals mean are unreliable. This is the somatic-symptom-disorder profile in compact form: high body attention, catastrophic misinterpretation. It overlaps with what the parsing-deficit framework calls “hypervigilance with mislabelling.” It is also the profile for whom open-ended interoceptive awareness training is most likely to backfire — Category 8 of this deep dive returns to that point in detail.

The quadratic finding

The most striking finding linking alexithymia to interoception is the quadratic relationship reported by Aaron, Blain, Snodgress, and Park (2020). Sample of 76, small but suggestive. Both low-IAcc and high-IAcc participants tended to show elevated alexithymia, with mid-range IAcc as the protective profile. The finding maps cleanly onto the parsing-deficit framework. Low IAcc = no-detection-deficit alexithymia (signals not registered). High IAcc + poor interpretation = hypervigilance-with-mislabelling alexithymia. Mid-range IAcc + good metacognition = non-alexithymic. The deficit is in appropriate body attention and the labelling that follows, not in raw detection.

A 2026 PLoS One systematic review (PMC11542822) of subjective interoception × alexithymia provides current triangulation guidance and is the most recent integrative source for measurement decisions in this space.

Triangulation: pairing instruments

The user’s stated goal is identifying which alexithymia subtype applies to them. The single best instrument doesn’t exist. Triangulation is the answer.

Pairing levels

The minimal pairing for self-classification is TAS-20 + MAIA. This covers two axes: cognitive alexithymia (TAS-20) and interoceptive sensibility (MAIA). It distinguishes parsing-deficit profiles (high TAS-20 + low MAIA = no-detection-deficit; high TAS-20 + high MAIA + somatic complaints = hypervigilance with mislabelling) from cleaner cognitive deficits (high TAS-20 + average MAIA). Both instruments are free, self-administrable, and take under twenty minutes total.

A stronger pairing for the cognitive-vs-affective distinction is TAS-20 + BVAQ + MAIA. The BVAQ adds the affective dimension TAS-20 omits — fantasising and emotionalising. With this trio, the user can distinguish Type I (low cognitive AND low affective) from Type II (low cognitive, intact affective).

A stronger pairing for valence sensitivity is TAS-20 + PAQ + MAIA, which captures whether your pattern differs across positive and negative emotions.

Comprehensive triangulation adds the LEAS — TAS-20 + (BVAQ or PAQ) + MAIA + LEAS-self. LEAS is harder to formally self-administer, but the vignettes can be used reflectively even without coder scoring; the exercise itself is illuminating.

With clinician access, adding TSIA and clinician-administered LEAS bypasses the self-report paradox most fully via observer rating and performance-based measurement.

Discrepancies are diagnostic

The diagnostic yield is largest when instruments disagree. Disagreement is not noise; it is the most informative output of triangulation.

Pattern Likely profile Practical implication
High TAS-20 + low LEAS Impoverished emotion concepts (Barrett-style developmental deficit) Granularity-building, expressive writing, emotion-vocabulary scaffolding
High TAS-20 + average LEAS Concepts present; attention/appraisal upstream issue Attention–appraisal therapy (Preece) or ACT-style approaches that don’t insist on labelling
High TAS-20 + high MAIA + chronic body symptoms Hypervigilance-with-mislabelling Cognitive reappraisal of body signals; contraindicates open-ended interoceptive awareness training
High TAS-20 + low MAIA No-detection-deficit alexithymia Scaffolded interoceptive training (with trauma caution); MABT-style touch-mediated work
BVAQ low cognitive only (intact affective) Type II profile — feelings present but illegible Track the psychosis-risk literature; consider attention/appraisal-targeted approaches
BVAQ low cognitive AND affective Type I profile — classic “affectless” presentation Consider trauma history; somatic-focused work may apply (with trauma-sensitive scaffolding)
High TAS-20 self-rating + low TSIA observer rating Defensive responding or inflated self-perceived difficulty Clinically interesting; warrants exploration with a therapist
Low TAS-20 self-rating + high TSIA observer rating Poor self-insight (the self-report paradox at full strength) Self-report unreliable; rely on observer-based measurement and performance tasks

These patterns are not exhaustive, but they cover the most common diagnostic readings. Each translates into a different therapy lane in Category 7 and a different set of contraindications in Category 8.

Practical access — where to find these instruments

  • TAS-20: widely circulated for individual self-test and non-commercial research use; many online versions exist (Embrace Autism’s adapted version is frequently cited; numerous research sites publish administrable forms). The official scale is owned by Multi-Health Systems Inc.; commercial use requires licensing.
  • MAIA / MAIA-2: freely available from the Osher Center at UCSF (osher.ucsf.edu/research/maia/). Self-administered.
  • PAQ / PAQ-S: available from the Preece group at the University of Western Australia for non-commercial research use.
  • BVAQ: available for non-commercial research access generally; copyright held by Vorst and Bermond.
  • TSIA: administration manual purchased from MHS; requires a trained interviewer.
  • LEAS: research instrument with a coding manual; coder training required. A self-administered web version (eLEAS) exists with less validation.
  • OAQ-G2: variable access; some versions in published literature, others through licensing.

What this means in practice: a user can independently complete TAS-20 + MAIA + PAQ at no cost in under thirty minutes total. This combination covers the cognitive-alexithymia dimension, the affective dimension via PAQ valence split, and interoceptive sensibility — three of the four key triangulation axes. Adding the BVAQ where available extends affective coverage further. LEAS-style reflection using published vignettes can supplement even without formal coding. With clinician access, adding TSIA and clinician-administered LEAS bypasses the self-report paradox.

Caveats worth carrying forward

A few points apply across instruments and across self-classification more broadly.

Scores fluctuate with state. Depression, acute distress, sleep deprivation, recent emotional events — all shift TAS-20 scores. A single administration is a snapshot, not a fingerprint. For trait stability, multiple administrations across weeks or months are needed.

Cutoffs are conventions, not natural kinds. A score of 60 is not categorically different from a score of 62. Treating cutoffs as binary diagnostic thresholds overstates their precision. Use the scores dimensionally where possible, and reserve categorical labels for cases where the score is well above or well below the convention.

Cultural validity is not guaranteed. The TAS-20 has known cross-cultural concerns; the BVAQ, PAQ, and others have less cross-cultural data. Users from non-Western cultural backgrounds should weight scores accordingly and treat between-culture comparisons with skepticism.

Pre-post change is interpretable but with caution. If a user takes TAS-20 before and after an intervention and the score drops, it could mean the intervention worked, or it could mean the user has learned to answer the items differently. Multi-method pre-post (TAS-20 plus LEAS plus an observer rating) is more defensible than single-instrument pre-post.

No instrument bypasses construct ambiguity. All these tools are measuring a contested construct. Different theoretical frames emphasise different aspects and prefer different instruments. Pairing instruments helps but does not resolve the deeper indeterminacy. The honest framing is: alexithymia is a useful, contested, clinically meaningful construct, and these instruments are the field’s current best tools for operationalising it. They are not perfect, and they are not interchangeable.

What this category opens up

For the rest of the deep dive, the take-home is that any therapy claim, prevalence finding, or comorbidity statistic should be read with the question “which instrument did they use?” front of mind. A study using TAS-20 only is silent on affective alexithymia. A study using BVAQ is silent on positive emotion. A study using HCT alone without confound control is methodologically suspect. A study using TSIA is more robust to self-report bias but constrained by the trained-rater requirement.

For self-classification specifically, the path is concrete: start with TAS-20 to anchor against the published literature, add MAIA to capture interoceptive sensibility, add PAQ or BVAQ depending on whether valence or cognitive-vs-affective distinction matters more to you, and treat discrepancies between the instruments as the most informative output. If clinician access is available, TSIA and LEAS bypass the self-report paradox.

Once the profile is in hand, the next category — Neural Mechanisms — explains why the subtypes differ at the level of brain architecture. The architecture in turn explains which therapy approaches are theoretically appropriate for each subtype, which is what Category 7 will eventually settle. The measurement work in this category is the bridge: it’s how you find your subtype, so the rest of the deep dive can speak to your situation rather than to the average alexithymic person across all studies.
