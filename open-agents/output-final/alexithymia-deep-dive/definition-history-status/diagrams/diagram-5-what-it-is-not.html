What Alexithymia Is Not

Boundary distinctions that protect against common misconceptions — including the most damaging one (that alexithymics don't have feelings).

Alexithymia

A trait-level pattern of difficulty identifying, describing, and reflecting on one's own emotions — while the underlying emotions (and the body that hosts them) may be entirely present.

It is NOT

Apathy

Apathy is reduced motivation, interest, or engagement — a deficit in approach behaviour.

Distinguishing principle: An alexithymic person can have strong motivations and full engagement; they just cannot tell you (or themselves) what they're feeling about it.

It is NOT

Emotional suppression

Suppression is the deliberate inhibition of emotional expression. The person knows what they feel and chooses not to show it.

Distinguishing principle: Alexithymia is the absence of access to feeling labels in the first place. Suppression presupposes access; alexithymia does not.

It is NOT

Avoidant attachment

Avoidant/dismissive attachment is a relational style — discomfort with closeness, downregulation of attachment-related affect.

Distinguishing principle: Empirically correlated with alexithymia, but distinct. A securely attached person can be alexithymic; a dismissively attached person can have full emotion access.

It is NOT

Depression

Depression is a mood and vegetative syndrome with persistent low mood, anhedonia, sleep/appetite changes, and cognitive distortions.

Distinguishing principle: Alexithymia is a trait-level processing pattern. State alexithymia rises with depression severity, but the trait component is largely independent. Many high scorers are not depressed.

It is NOT

Lack of emotion

"Alexithymics don't have emotions" is the most damaging misconception. Type II alexithymics report normal-to-elevated emotional arousal — feelings are present and physiologically active — but cannot read or articulate them.

Distinguishing principle: Emotions happen. The person's relationship to them is impoverished, fractured, or illegible to self — not absent.

Why this matters. Each of these confusions has therapy and identity consequences. If a clinician treats alexithymia as suppression, they will recommend "be more in touch with your feelings" — missing that the person has no labels to access. If they treat it as apathy, they will reach for activation. If they treat it as lack of emotion, they will assume nothing is there to work with.
