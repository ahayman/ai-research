The Five Theoretical Frames — Where the Field Splits

Same surface trait, five upstream stories. Each frame quietly determines which therapy gets recommended.

Sifneos & Nemiah, Boston, 1960s–1970s Clinical observations of psychosomatic patients — the original construct. Four features: DIF, DDF, EOT, restricted fantasy.
↓
Frame A

Original lineage (Taylor, Bagby; defended 2024)

Multifaceted deficit in emotion processing, with both cognitive and affective components. Imaginal capacity essential.

Therapy implication: long-term psychotherapy, attachment-informed work, recovery of imagination. Instrument: TAS-20 (cognitive) + BVAQ (affective). Status: hundreds of empirical studies; defended explicitly in Taylor & Bagby 2024.

Frame B

Attention-appraisal (Preece, Becerra et al. 2017)

A CBT reformulation: alexithymia = deficit at the attention (EOT) and appraisal (DIF/DDF) stages of the Gross emotion-regulation model. Drops imagination on theoretical grounds.

Therapy implication: CBT-style emotion-schema training. Instrument: PAQ (Perth, 2018) — covers positive and negative emotion. Status: theoretically derived; correlation-based; mostly non-clinical samples.

Frame C

Constructed emotion (Barrett 2017)

Emotions are conceptual categories built from interoception + context + learned concepts. Alexithymia = impoverished emotion-concept granularity.

Therapy implication: vocabulary-building, affect-labelling, expressive writing. Strength: natural fit for cross-cultural variability and granularity training. Status: theoretical lens, multiple lines of indirect support.

Frame D

Interoception deficit (Bird group, 2016+)

Multidomain failure of interoception — cardiac, respiratory, gastric, muscular, taste, thermal. Deficits at detection, recognition, or labelling. Brewer/Murphy/Bird 2021 affirmation.

Therapy implication: body-scan, MABT, interoception training (with caveats — see Cat 8). Independent challenge: Nicholson et al. 2018 Bayesian-null cardiac findings. Status: active dispute.

Frame E — Synthesis

Multiroute model (Hobson, Brewer, Catmur, Bird 2019)

Alexithymia is the common downstream phenotype of multiple upstream impairments. Interoception is one route. Language/labelling is another. Conceptual scaffolding may be a third. Different individuals, different upstream causes, similar surface.

Therapy implication: route diagnosis is the first task. Subtype-matched intervention. Status: most recent published synthesis — encompasses all four prior frames as routes.

The synthesis. Frames A through D are not strictly competing; the multiroute model (E) suggests each may be correct about some alexithymics. The clinically useful question is not "which frame is right?" but "which route is dominant in this person?" That is the empirical question the rest of the deep dive operationalises — subtypes (Cat 2), measurement (Cat 3), neural mechanisms (Cat 4), differential therapy (Cat 7), and when one-size-fits-all interventions backfire (Cat 8).
