Diagnostic Status — Why Alexithymia Sits Outside the DSM

A side-by-side comparison of the structural properties that admit a construct to DSM-5 / ICD-11 vs. those of alexithymia.

Typical DSM-5 Categorical Disorder e.g., Major Depressive Disorder
Status Formal mental disorder. Diagnostic code (F32, F33).
Structure Categorical syndrome. Multiple symptoms cluster.
Threshold Explicit (≥5 of 9 symptoms, ≥2 weeks).
Distress / disability Required for diagnosis.
Boundaries Inclusion/exclusion criteria. Differential diagnosis.
Trans-diagnostic? No — it is the diagnosis.
Insurance Billable. Treatment reimbursed.
Recognition Public, clinical, and legal recognition.
Alexithymia not in DSM-5, not in ICD-11
Status Not a mental disorder. No diagnostic code.
Structure Dimensional trait. Continuously distributed.
Threshold Pragmatic (TAS-20 ≥61). Not a natural break.
Distress / disability Not required. Many high scorers report no impairment.
Boundaries Boundaries with apathy, suppression, depression. No DSM rules.
Trans-diagnostic? Yes — appears across autism, PTSD, EDs, SUD, FND, MDD, schizophrenia.
Insurance Not directly billable. Therapists code an associated diagnosis.
Recognition Real research construct. Limited public/clinical recognition.

Why Alexithymia Doesn't Meet the DSM-5 Definition of "Mental Disorder"

  • Dimensional, not categorical. A continuous trait with no natural cutoff — doesn't fit the syndrome-with-threshold structure DSM uses.
  • Trait, not syndrome. A single processing pattern, not a cluster of symptoms that co-occur.
  • Trans-diagnostic. Runs across many disorders. Better understood as a vulnerability/maintenance factor than a primary disorder.
  • Not always associated with distress or disability. Some high scorers have no significant impairment. The definition of "mental disorder" requires distress/dysfunction.
