McCaslin 2006 — The Field's Anchor Finding

The single most-cited prospective demonstration that pre-trauma alexithymia predicts post-trauma PTSD over and above prior symptoms. Small sample, right design, replicated in spirit but never directly replicated at scale.

1998–1999
Baseline cross-sectional survey of 166 NYC police officers (TAS-20 alexithymia + pre-existing PTSD symptoms)
Sept 11, 2001
9/11 attacks — discrete identifiable trauma exposure for many of the cohort
Post-9/11
54 officers followed prospectively; PTSD severity assessed
Analysis
Regression: does baseline alexithymia predict post-9/11 PTSD severity controlling for pre-9/11 PTSD?

Cross-sectional finding (1998–1999)

Alexithymia scores accounted for 11.2% of variance in PTSD symptoms. Did NOT retain significance in the final model after controlling for other predictors.
Variance explained: ~11%, but absorbed by covariates in adjusted model

Prospective finding (the headline)

Alexithymia at baseline significantly predicted post-9/11 PTSD severity OVER AND ABOVE pre-9/11 PTSD symptoms. The right design for the directional claim.
Statistical significance retained after controlling for prior PTSD
Why this is the field's anchor. Most alexithymia-PTSD studies are cross-sectional. McCaslin is the cleanest prospective demonstration that pre-existing alexithymia raises post-trauma PTSD risk — controlling for the obvious confound that already-distressed people might be both more alexithymic and more vulnerable to PTSD.
The honest caveats:
  • Small prospective sample (n=54). A study this size is statistically thin for moderate effects.
  • Police, not general public. Pre-selected for trauma-tolerant occupational fit — generalisation is uncertain.
  • The post-event analysis cannot fully separate "trait alexithymia raising PTSD risk" from "trait alexithymia making people who already had subclinical post-traumatic vulnerability worse."
  • Never directly replicated at scale. The field has been waiting two decades for a large-N study with the same design.
The honest evidence label: Moderate. Right design, small sample, supporting (mixed) replication via Heinrichs 2005 firefighter study (where alexithymia rose alongside PTSD over 2 years rather than predicting it cleanly), Honkalampi 2023 multi-wave (where baseline alexithymia predicted hyperarousal at all subsequent time points), and refugee + military cohorts. The pre-trauma vulnerability claim is supported but the literature still has a structural gap where a large-N prospective with pre-trauma TAS-20 + post-trauma PTSD outcome should be.

Source: McCaslin SE et al. (2006). Alexithymia and PTSD symptoms in urban police officers: cross-sectional and prospective findings. Journal of Traumatic Stress. PubMed 16788996.
