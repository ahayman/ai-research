Frewen's Cognitive-Affective Phenotype

Frewen's proposal: alexithymia in PTSD is not just emotion numbing. It is a distinct sub-phenotype with its own neural signature — default-mode and medial-prefrontal alterations — separable from PTSD-specific salience-network disturbances. Two neural addresses, one diagnostic umbrella.

PTSD core (without alexithymia)

Salience-network dysregulation
Implicated network:
  • Anterior insula (salience tagging of threat)
  • Dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (conflict monitoring)
  • Amygdala (fear conditioning/expression)
  • Hippocampus (contextual gating)
Symptom signature:
Hyperarousal Re-experiencing Threat-anchored Hypervigilance Startle
Standard PTSD frame: dysregulated threat detection and conditioning. Targets fear-extinction and habituation in classical exposure protocols.

PTSD + alexithymia (cognitive-affective phenotype)

Default-mode + medial prefrontal alterations
Additional implicated network:
  • Medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) — self-referential processing
  • Posterior cingulate cortex (PCC)
  • Default-mode network nodes broadly
  • Reduced anterior insula integration (vs. just dysregulation)
Additional symptom signature:
Numbing Dissociative features Somatic morbidity Emotional neglect history Body-without-language
Frewen's claim: this is a distinct neural signature, not just severe-PTSD. Conceptual/labelling failures (not just threat-detection failures) are part of the picture. Pulls the therapy implication toward conceptual/granularity work alongside exposure.

Frewen's core claim

Alexithymic PTSD has a separable neural signature from non-alexithymic PTSD. The default-mode/mPFC pattern is consistent with conceptual-system / emotion-concept-construction deficits (Barrett-style framing) operating alongside salience-network dysregulation. Two distinct neural systems are dysregulated, not just one.

Therapeutically: targeting the salience network alone (classical exposure) leaves the default-mode/conceptual layer untouched — which is the mechanistic sketch for why high-alexithymia PTSD patients show attenuated/delayed exposure response (Pugach finding).

Evidence base for the phenotype

Frewen's 2008 meta-analysis (12 studies, 1,095 PTSD subjects) found a large effect size for the PTSD–alexithymia association, with male combat samples showing higher effect sizes. Frewen 2008 clinical/neural correlates (n=105 PTSD subjects): alexithymia correlated with re-experiencing, hyperarousal, numbing, dissociative symptoms, and retrospectively reported childhood emotional neglect.

Why the phenotype framing matters for treatment. If alexithymic PTSD is just severe-PTSD, the answer is more of the same protocol with longer dose. If it is a separable phenotype with default-mode/mPFC dysregulation, the answer is different protocols — emotion-concept work, granularity training, affect-labelling — alongside or before exposure. Frewen's frame is the mechanistic justification for phase-based / hybrid approaches over straight exposure for the high-alexithymia subgroup.
