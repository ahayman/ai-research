ALS / Motor Neuron Disease & AAC

Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) is a progressive neurodegenerative disease that gradually strips away the ability to move, speak, and eventually breathe. For the vast majority of people living with ALS, augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) becomes not just helpful but essential for maintaining connection, autonomy, and quality of life.

Updated March 2026 ~20 min read Evidence-based

What Is ALS?

Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), also commonly known as motor neuron disease (MND) or Lou Gehrig's disease, is a rare and fatal neurodegenerative disorder characterized by the progressive degeneration and death of both upper motor neurons (in the brain) and lower motor neurons (in the brainstem and spinal cord). These motor neurons are the nerve cells responsible for controlling all voluntary muscle movement, including speaking, walking, breathing, and swallowing.

As motor neurons die, the brain loses its ability to initiate and control muscle movement. Muscles progressively weaken, begin to twitch (fasciculations), and eventually atrophy. The disease is relentlessly progressive, and while the rate of decline varies between individuals, ALS ultimately affects the muscles needed for breathing, with respiratory failure being the most common cause of death.

Crucially, ALS typically does not affect cognitive function, sensory perception, or consciousness. Most people with ALS remain fully aware and intellectually engaged even as their body progressively loses the ability to move. This stark disconnect between a sharp mind and a failing body makes communication support one of the most important aspects of ALS care, and makes AAC technology a lifeline rather than a convenience.

Key Distinction

The terms ALS and MND (Motor Neuron Disease) are often used interchangeably. In the United States, the condition is most commonly referred to as ALS, while in the United Kingdom, Australia, and many other countries, "motor neuron disease" or "MND" is the preferred term. ALS is technically the most common form within a broader group of motor neuron diseases.

Types & Progression

Classification by Cause

  • Sporadic ALS (90-95% of cases): No clear inherited component. This is by far the most common form, occurring in people with no apparent family history of the disease. The causes are believed to involve a complex interplay of genetic susceptibility and environmental factors.
  • Familial ALS (5-10% of cases): Directly inherited through genetic mutations. More than 25 genes have been associated with familial ALS, with the most common mutations found in the C9orf72, SOD1, TARDBP, and FUS genes.

Classification by Onset Location

The location where symptoms first appear has significant implications for the speed of communication loss and the urgency of AAC planning:

The Seven Stages of ALS Progression

Stage 1: Early Symptoms
Initial muscle weakness or twitching in one region of the body (a hand, a foot, the tongue). Often dismissed or misdiagnosed initially. The person remains largely independent.
Stage 2: Diagnosis
A clear pattern of progressive weakness becomes evident. A neurologist typically makes the ALS diagnosis after ruling out other conditions. Muscles in the initial region show increasing weakness and possible muscle wasting.
Stage 3: Regional Spread
Weakness spreads to other regions. A person with limb onset may begin to notice speech changes; a person with bulbar onset may develop limb weakness. Assistive devices may become necessary for some tasks.
Stage 4: Increasing Dependence
Multiple body regions are affected. Speech may become difficult to understand. AAC devices are typically introduced or become the primary communication method. Mobility aids are commonly needed.
Stage 5: Severe Disability
Most voluntary movement is severely impaired. Speech may be unintelligible or lost entirely. Eye-gaze AAC systems become essential. Nutritional support via feeding tube is often needed.
Stage 6: Near-Total Paralysis
Almost complete loss of voluntary movement. The person may be in a locked-in or near-locked-in state. Eye tracking or brain-computer interfaces may be the only viable communication methods.
Stage 7: End of Life
Respiratory failure without invasive ventilation. Some individuals on full ventilatory support may continue for years in a locked-in state, making BCI and eye-tracking AAC critically important.
Important for AAC Planning

Progression speed varies enormously between individuals. Some people live with ALS for over a decade (Stephen Hawking survived 55 years), while others decline rapidly within months. This unpredictability makes early AAC assessment and planning essential, regardless of the current rate of decline.

Key Demographic Patterns

  • Age: The average age of diagnosis is between 55 and 65 years old, with peak incidence in the 60-79 age group. Incidence is approximately 3-4 per 100,000 annually for people in their 60s, though ALS can rarely occur in the 20s and 30s.
  • Gender: ALS is approximately 1.56 times more common in men than women, though this gap narrows significantly after age 70, when incidence becomes nearly equal between sexes.
  • Ethnicity: Studies in the U.S. indicate that white individuals are approximately twice as likely to develop ALS as Black individuals. The disease is more common among non-Hispanic populations.
  • Geography: Incidence is relatively uniform across Western countries, though some "hotspots" of higher frequency exist in the Western Pacific (historically in Guam, the Kii Peninsula of Japan, and western New Guinea).
  • Rising Prevalence: A landmark 2026 study projects that ALS prevalence will rise substantially by 2040 across all countries studied, driven partly by aging populations and improved diagnostic capabilities. In the U.S., prevalence is projected to increase from 9.68 per 100,000 in 2024 to 11.21 per 100,000 in 2040, meaning approximately 8,778 additional people living with the disease.
Growing Need for AAC

With ALS prevalence projected to increase substantially by 2040 due to aging populations worldwide, the demand for high-quality AAC solutions will grow correspondingly. Given that 80-95% of people with ALS ultimately lose functional speech, this means thousands more individuals each year who will need AAC technology to communicate.

How ALS Affects Communication

Communication impairment in ALS is one of the most devastating aspects of the disease. While cognitive function and the desire to communicate typically remain intact, the physical mechanisms of speech production progressively fail. Understanding how this happens is essential for planning appropriate AAC intervention.

The Anatomy of Speech Production in ALS

Normal speech requires the precise, coordinated action of more than 100 muscles across multiple systems:

  • Respiratory system: The lungs and diaphragm provide the airflow that powers vocalization. As ALS weakens respiratory muscles, voice volume decreases and breath support for sustained speech diminishes.
  • Laryngeal system: The vocal folds in the larynx vibrate to produce voice. ALS can cause hoarseness, breathiness, or a strained vocal quality as laryngeal muscles weaken.
  • Velopharyngeal system: The soft palate controls the passage between the mouth and nose. Weakness leads to hypernasality (speech sounds excessively nasal) and nasal air emission.
  • Articulatory system: The tongue, lips, jaw, and facial muscles shape sounds into recognizable speech. This is often the most visibly affected system, resulting in slurred, imprecise speech (dysarthria).

Types of Motor Neuron Damage and Speech

ALS affects both upper motor neurons (UMN) and lower motor neurons (LMN), each producing distinct speech characteristics:

UMN

Upper Motor Neuron (Spastic) Dysarthria

Characterized by slow, effortful, strained-strangled speech quality. The tongue and lips may be stiff and difficult to move. Speech sounds tight and labored. Often associated with emotional lability (pseudobulbar affect), causing involuntary laughing or crying.

LMN

Lower Motor Neuron (Flaccid) Dysarthria

Characterized by a breathy, nasal voice quality with imprecise consonant production. The tongue may appear wasted and show fasciculations. Speech may sound "mushy" or indistinct. Volume is often reduced due to respiratory muscle weakness.

MIX

Mixed Dysarthria (Most Common)

Most people with ALS develop a mixed spastic-flaccid dysarthria that combines features of both UMN and LMN involvement. This is the hallmark pattern of ALS speech deterioration and results in progressively worsening intelligibility.

Beyond Dysarthria: Other Communication Challenges

Speech motor impairment is the primary communication barrier, but ALS creates additional challenges:

  • Reduced writing ability: As hand and arm muscles weaken, handwriting and typing become increasingly difficult or impossible, removing a key backup communication method.
  • Gesture limitations: Progressive limb weakness limits the ability to gesture, point, nod, or use sign language.
  • Facial expression changes: Weakness of facial muscles can reduce the range and clarity of facial expressions, affecting nonverbal communication and emotional expression.
  • Fatigue: The effort required to communicate increases as muscles weaken, leading to communication fatigue, particularly later in the day.
  • Frontotemporal dysfunction (10-15% of cases): A subset of ALS patients develop frontotemporal dementia (FTD) or milder frontotemporal cognitive changes that can affect language processing, word finding, and pragmatic communication skills, adding a cognitive dimension to the communication challenge.
More Than 80%

More than 80% of people with ALS will experience dysarthria at some point during their disease, and the majority will eventually lose intelligible speech entirely. This makes ALS one of the conditions with the highest rates of AAC need.

Progressive Speech Loss

One of the most challenging aspects of ALS is the progressive nature of speech loss. Unlike conditions where communication ability is stable (such as cerebral palsy) or where recovery is possible (such as stroke), ALS presents a trajectory of continuous, irreversible decline. Understanding this trajectory is essential for timely AAC intervention.

Speech Decline by ALS Type

Bulbar-Onset ALS: Rapid Speech Decline

For the approximately 25-30% of people diagnosed with bulbar-onset ALS, speech difficulty is often among the very first symptoms. The timeline is characteristically compressed:

  • Speech changes are typically apparent at or before diagnosis
  • Significant reduction in speaking rate occurs at approximately 23 months after symptom onset (speaking rate drops below 120 words per minute)
  • Speech becomes notably unintelligible at approximately 32 months (intelligibility drops below 85%)
  • Complete anarthria (inability to produce speech) may follow within months

Limb-Onset ALS: Gradual Speech Decline

For the approximately 65-70% with limb-onset ALS, the speech timeline is generally more extended:

  • Speech may remain functional for 60 months or longer after symptom onset
  • Dysarthria develops gradually as the disease spreads to bulbar regions
  • However, once bulbar symptoms begin, decline can accelerate rapidly
  • Up to 85% of limb-onset patients eventually develop significant bulbar symptoms

The Critical 120 WPM Threshold

Research has identified a critical threshold in ALS speech decline. Normal conversational speaking rate ranges from 160-200 words per minute. When a person's speaking rate drops to approximately 120 words per minute, a rapid deterioration in speech intelligibility typically follows. This threshold serves as a critical trigger point for AAC referral and intervention.

Speech Rate Decline Progression

Normal speech: 160-200 words per minute

Early decline: 125-160 WPM, speech still largely intelligible to familiar listeners

Critical threshold: ~120 WPM, rapid intelligibility loss often follows

Severely affected: Below 85 WPM, speech may be unintelligible to unfamiliar listeners

Anarthria: 0 WPM, complete loss of speech production ability

Stages of Communication in ALS

Speech-language pathologists have identified five functional stages of communication in ALS, each with corresponding AAC strategies:

Stage Speech Status Communication Strategy
Stage 1: No Detectable Speech Disorder Speech sounds normal. No clinical signs of dysarthria. Voice banking, message banking. Establish baseline measures. Education about future AAC options.
Stage 2: Obvious Speech Disorder with Intelligible Speech Speech sounds different but is still understandable. Speaking rate may be slowing. Continue voice/message banking. Begin exploring AAC devices. Practice with low-tech backup systems.
Stage 3: Reduction in Speech Intelligibility Speech is becoming difficult to understand, especially for unfamiliar listeners. Repetition frequently needed. Introduce high-tech AAC as primary communication supplement. Refine access methods. Begin transitioning to AAC for some conversations.
Stage 4: Natural Speech Supplemented with AAC Speech is largely unintelligible. Natural speech may only be useful for some words or phrases with familiar partners. AAC becomes primary communication method. Speech used only to supplement. Ensure robust AAC system is fully functional.
Stage 5: No Functional Speech Complete anarthria. No useful speech production. Total reliance on AAC. Eye-gaze, switch, or BCI access. Ensure 24/7 access to communication device.
The Window Closes Fast

Research consistently shows that once speech intelligibility begins to decline, the deterioration is often rapid and irreversible. Multiple studies report that patients who waited until speech was already significantly impaired to begin AAC assessment retrospectively wished they had started earlier. The window for voice banking, device training, and access method optimization is finite and unpredictable.

Voice & Message Banking

Voice banking and message banking represent two of the most emotionally significant AAC interventions available to people with ALS. They allow individuals to preserve their vocal identity, personal expressions, and meaningful phrases before speech is lost, providing a profound sense of continuity and self even after natural speech is no longer possible.

Voice Banking

Voice banking involves recording a large inventory of speech samples that are then processed by specialized software to create a personalized synthetic voice. This synthetic voice can be used with AAC devices, so that when the person types or selects words, the device speaks in a voice that approximates their own rather than a generic computer voice.

How Voice Banking Works

  1. Recording phase: The individual reads aloud a set of carefully designed phrases and sentences (typically 800-3,000+ utterances) that capture the full range of sounds in the language. This usually takes 2-8 hours spread over multiple sessions.
  2. Processing: The recorded speech is analyzed by synthesis software that extracts the acoustic characteristics of the speaker's voice, including pitch, timbre, rhythm, and unique vocal qualities.
  3. Synthesis: A text-to-speech engine is built that can produce any word or sentence using the person's vocal characteristics. Modern voice banking services use neural network-based synthesis for increasingly natural-sounding output.
  4. Integration: The synthesized voice is loaded onto AAC devices and software, allowing the person to "speak" in their own voice even after natural speech is lost.

Major Voice Banking Services

  • ModelTalker (modeltalker.org) -- A free voice banking service developed at the Nemours Alfred I. duPont Hospital for Children and the University of Delaware.
  • Acapela My-Own-Voice -- A commercial service that can create a personalized synthetic voice from as few as 50 sentences, with higher quality from more recordings.
  • VocaliD (now Veritone Voice) -- Uses AI to blend a speaker's residual vocal qualities with a donor voice to create a personalized voice, even from limited recordings.
  • SpeakUnique -- A UK-based service developed in collaboration with the University of Edinburgh, specifically designed for people with degenerative speech conditions.

Message Banking

Message banking is complementary to but distinct from voice banking. Rather than creating a synthetic voice, message banking captures and stores specific phrases, sentences, and sounds in the person's actual recorded voice, preserving natural inflection, emotion, and personality.

What to Bank

  • Frequently used phrases: "I love you," "Thank you," "Good morning," personal greetings
  • Emotional expressions: Laughter, sighs, exclamations, pet names for family members
  • Personal catchphrases: Unique expressions, jokes, or sayings the person is known for
  • Professional phrases: Workplace greetings, common work-related statements
  • Emergency phrases: "I need help," "Call 911," "I'm in pain"
  • Social phrases: Introductions, small talk, conversation starters
  • Family-specific language: Pet names, family jokes, bedtime stories for children
Best Practice: Start Early

Voice banking is most effective when begun before any noticeable speech changes. The ALS Association and major speech-language pathology organizations recommend that voice and message banking should begin as close to diagnosis as possible, ideally at Stage 1 when speech still sounds completely normal. Even if speech decline seems far away, the unpredictable nature of ALS progression means the window for high-quality voice banking may close without warning.

The earlier message banking can begin, the more phrases can be stored. By completing the voice banking process before speech declines, individuals can preserve identity, maintain emotional connections, and ensure continued communication.
-- ALS Association Clinical Guidelines

How AAC Helps People with ALS

Augmentative and alternative communication technology serves as a vital bridge between the intact mind and the failing body in ALS. Approximately 75% of people with ALS are unable to speak by the time of death, making AAC not an optional enhancement but an essential medical intervention that preserves autonomy, dignity, and quality of life.

The AAC Continuum in ALS

AAC for ALS is not a single device or approach but a continuum that evolves as the disease progresses. Effective AAC planning anticipates future needs and prepares transitions before they become urgent.

A

Augmentative Communication

In earlier stages, AAC supplements remaining natural speech. A person might use an alphabet board to clarify misunderstood words, or a speech amplifier to boost weakened voice volume. The goal is to extend the usefulness of residual speech.

A

Alternative Communication

In later stages, AAC replaces natural speech entirely. The person relies fully on devices, eye-gaze systems, or other technology to express all communication needs, from basic requests to complex emotional expression.

Low-Tech AAC Options

Low-tech solutions are important as backups and for situations where high-tech devices may not be practical:

  • Alphabet boards: Letter boards where the person points to (or looks at) letters to spell words. Partner-assisted scanning versions work when pointing is not possible.
  • Eye-linking frames (E-Tran): Clear acrylic frames with letters and phrases arranged around the edges. The communication partner interprets the person's eye gaze to determine their message.
  • Picture/phrase boards: Pre-printed boards with common words, phrases, or pictographic symbols for quick communication of basic needs.
  • Writing aids: Pen grips, weighted pens, or handwriting amplification for people who retain some hand function.

High-Tech AAC Options

High-tech AAC devices are typically the primary communication tool for people with ALS in the middle and later stages of the disease:

  • Dedicated speech-generating devices (SGDs): Purpose-built communication devices like the Tobii Dynavox TD I-Series, designed for durability, eye-tracking integration, and 24/7 reliability.
  • Tablet-based AAC apps: Applications like TD Snap, Proloquo2Go, TouchChat, or Grid on consumer tablets, offering flexibility and familiarity.
  • Computer-based systems: Software like Grid 3, Communicator 5, or CoughDrop running on laptop or desktop computers, often with alternative access methods.
  • Integrated environmental control: Many AAC devices also allow control of TV, phone, lights, doors, and other home automation systems, extending independence beyond just communication.

Why AAC Matters So Profoundly in ALS

The importance of AAC in ALS cannot be overstated. Research consistently demonstrates that access to effective AAC:

  • Preserves autonomy: Allows people to make their own medical decisions, express preferences, and direct their own care
  • Maintains relationships: Enables continued meaningful communication with family, friends, and caregivers
  • Reduces depression and isolation: Communication loss is strongly correlated with depression in ALS; AAC mitigates this
  • Supports end-of-life planning: Enables expression of wishes about care, advance directives, and final messages
  • Allows continued professional and social participation: Some people with ALS continue working, writing, and engaging in social activities through AAC
  • Preserves identity: Through voice banking and personalized communication systems, AAC helps maintain the person's unique identity and communication style
Key Statistic

A systematic review found that the majority of ALS patients who used AAC devices retrospectively wished the device had been introduced earlier. Early introduction at diagnosis was associated with better existential and psychological outcomes compared to delayed intervention.

Access Methods for AAC in ALS

Because ALS progressively affects different muscle groups at different rates, a person may need to change their AAC access method multiple times throughout the disease. Selecting and adapting the access method is one of the most critical aspects of AAC provision for ALS, and a skilled AAC specialist can help ensure continuity of communication as abilities change.

Direct Selection Methods

Touch / Finger Access

In earlier stages, many people with ALS can still use touchscreens or keyboards directly. Modifications like keyguards (overlays with holes for each key to prevent accidental presses), enlarged keyboards, or on-screen keyboards with word prediction can extend the usability of direct touch access.

Eye-Gaze / Eye Tracking

Eye-gaze technology is the most commonly used high-tech access method for people with advanced ALS. It works by using infrared cameras to track the precise position of the user's eyes on a screen. The user "selects" items by looking at them for a set period (dwell time) or by blinking.

Why Eye Gaze Is Central to ALS AAC

Eye movement is typically the last voluntary function to be preserved in ALS, making eye-tracking the most widely adopted and reliable access method for AAC in this population. Modern eye-tracking systems like the Tobii Dynavox TD I-Series work in various lighting conditions, including outdoors, and can track accurately even with glasses or contact lenses.

Head Tracking

Head tracking systems use a camera to follow the movement of the user's head (often via a reflective dot placed on the forehead or glasses) and translate head movements into cursor movements on screen. This can be effective for people who retain good neck control but have limited hand function.

Indirect Selection Methods

Switch Scanning

Switch scanning is used when direct selection methods are not possible. A cursor automatically scans through options on the screen, and the user activates a switch when the desired option is highlighted. Switches can be activated by almost any reliably controllable movement:

  • Button switches: Pressed with a finger, thumb, toe, elbow, or any body part with voluntary movement
  • Proximity/touch switches: Activated by light touch or proximity of a body part
  • Sip-and-puff switches: Activated by controlled inhalation or exhalation through a straw-like tube
  • EMG (electromyography) switches: Detect residual muscle electrical activity, even from muscles that can no longer produce visible movement
  • Piezoelectric/pressure switches: Respond to extremely light pressure, designed for very weak muscle activity

Specialized switches like the Lever Magnetic-spring Mechanical Switch (LeMMS) have been specifically engineered for ALS, requiring minimal activation force for repeated use by severely weakened muscles.

Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI)

For individuals who have lost nearly all voluntary muscle control, including reliable eye movement, brain-computer interfaces represent the frontier of communication access. BCIs detect brain activity directly and translate it into device commands. (See the Emerging Technology section for detailed coverage of BCI advances.)

Multi-Modal Access

Many ALS specialists recommend a multi-modal approach to AAC access, where the person has access to multiple input methods and can switch between them depending on fatigue, positioning, or disease progression. For example, a person might use eye gaze as their primary method but have a switch as a backup for times when eye fatigue or positioning makes gaze tracking less reliable.

Types of AAC for ALS

The specific AAC tools and systems used by people with ALS range from simple, inexpensive aids to sophisticated, technology-driven systems. An effective AAC plan typically incorporates tools from multiple categories, ensuring that the person always has a way to communicate regardless of the situation.

Dedicated Speech-Generating Devices

Purpose-built AAC devices designed specifically for people with communication disabilities are often the backbone of ALS communication systems. Leading devices include:

  • Tobii Dynavox TD I-Series: The most widely used dedicated AAC device for ALS. Features built-in eye tracking, Windows operating system, and can function as both a communication device and a full computer. Designed for durability and all-day use. Typical cost: $10,000-$20,000 (often covered by insurance).
  • Tobii Dynavox TD Pilot: A newer, more portable option with integrated eye tracking and Android-based software, designed for on-the-go communication.
  • PRC-Saltillo Accent Series: Dedicated devices offering switch scanning and touch access with the robust LAMP/Unity language systems.
  • Smartbox Grid Pad: Windows-based dedicated devices running Grid 3 software, popular in the UK and Europe.

Software-Based AAC Solutions

AAC software that runs on standard computers, tablets, or phones:

  • Grid 3 (Smartbox): Comprehensive AAC software for Windows that supports eye gaze, switch, and touch access. Includes text-to-speech, symbol communication, and environmental control.
  • TD Snap (Tobii Dynavox): AAC app available on iOS, Windows, and dedicated devices. Offers both symbol-based and text-based communication.
  • Proloquo2Go (AssistiveWare): iOS-based AAC app with robust language systems, though primarily used with touch access.
  • CoughDrop: Cloud-based AAC solution accessible from any web browser, offering symbol and text communication with support for multiple access methods.
  • Predictable (Therapy Box): Specifically designed for people who can spell, with excellent word prediction and switch/eye-gaze access.

Communication Apps for Early Stages

When speech is declining but some vocal output remains:

  • Speech amplifiers: Portable microphone and speaker systems that amplify weakening voice volume
  • Speak4Yourself / similar apps: Simple, quick-access apps with pre-stored phrases and text-to-speech
  • Standard text-to-speech apps: Apps that convert typed text to spoken output for situations where speech is not clear enough

Environmental Control Integration

Modern AAC systems for ALS often extend beyond communication to provide control over the person's environment. Through the same eye-gaze or switch interface used for communication, a person can:

  • Control television, music, and entertainment systems
  • Operate smart home devices (lights, thermostat, door locks)
  • Make phone calls and send text messages
  • Browse the internet and use social media
  • Access email and other computer applications
  • Control nurse call systems in care settings
Funding and Insurance

In the United States, dedicated speech-generating devices are classified as durable medical equipment and are typically covered by Medicare, Medicaid, and most private insurance plans when prescribed by a physician and recommended by a speech-language pathologist. The ALS Association, Team Gleason, and other organizations also provide loaner programs and financial assistance for AAC equipment.

Stephen Hawking: The Iconic AAC User

No discussion of ALS and AAC would be complete without recognizing Stephen Hawking (1942-2018), the theoretical physicist who became the world's most famous AAC user and whose life demonstrated the profound potential of assistive communication technology. Diagnosed with ALS at age 21, Hawking defied medical expectations by surviving 55 years with the disease, fundamentally reshaping our understanding of the universe while communicating entirely through technology for more than three decades.

The Evolution of Hawking's Communication Technology

1963: Diagnosis
Diagnosed with ALS at age 21 while a doctoral student at Cambridge. Given approximately 2 years to live. His speech gradually deteriorated over the following two decades.
Early 1980s: Declining Speech
Hawking's speech had become so slurred that only those who knew him well could understand him. Family and close colleagues served as interpreters during lectures and conversations.
1985: Emergency Tracheotomy
After a bout of pneumonia, Hawking required a tracheotomy that completely and permanently removed his ability to speak. He initially communicated by raising his eyebrows to indicate letters on a spelling card, an agonizingly slow process.
1986: The Equalizer
Computer engineer Walter Woltosz provided Hawking with a software program called the "Equalizer," originally developed for his own mother-in-law who had ALS. Using a handheld clicker to select words from a database of ~3,000 words, connected to a Speech Plus CallText 5010 synthesizer, Hawking could produce approximately 15 words per minute. This gave him his famous robotic voice.
1997: Intel Partnership Begins
Intel co-founder Gordon Moore offered Hawking technology support, beginning a nearly two-decade partnership. Intel engineers regularly upgraded Hawking's entire computer system every two years to accommodate his gradual loss of muscle control.
2001-2008: Cheek Switch Era
As Hawking lost the ability to use his hand, Intel transitioned his input to an infrared sensor mounted on his glasses that detected movements of his cheek muscle. A cursor would scan the screen, and a small cheek twitch would select the highlighted item.
2014: ACAT System
Intel developed the Assistive Context-Aware Toolkit (ACAT), incorporating SwiftKey's predictive text technology. This reduced Hawking's need to type by nearly 80%, as the system learned his writing patterns and predicted his next words. His communication speed improved significantly.
2015: Open Source Release
Intel released the ACAT software as open source, making it freely available for anyone to use, customize, or improve. This enabled the broader disability community to benefit from technology developed for Hawking.
Medicine was not able to cure me, so I rely on technology to help me communicate and live. I feel I represent a vast and ever-growing constituency of people who live and thrive through technology.
-- Stephen Hawking

Hawking's Legacy for AAC

Hawking's impact on the AAC field extends far beyond his personal use of the technology:

  • Public awareness: Hawking's international fame made the general public aware that people who cannot speak can still think, create, and contribute at the highest levels
  • Technology investment: His partnership with Intel drove significant corporate investment in AAC research and development
  • Open source contribution: The release of ACAT as open-source software democratized advanced AAC technology
  • Identity and voice: Hawking famously refused to update his synthesized voice (a DECTalk voice from the 1980s), insisting it had become his identity. This highlighted the deep personal connection between a person and their AAC voice
  • Inspiration: His example demonstrated that AAC is not merely a medical necessity but a tool that can enable extraordinary achievement and full participation in life
The Voice That Became an Identity

When Intel offered Hawking a more natural-sounding voice, he declined, saying he identified with his original robotic DECTalk voice and that it had become recognized worldwide as "his" voice. This illustrates a profound truth about AAC: the synthesized voice becomes an extension of the person's identity, not just a tool. This is precisely why voice banking, which preserves a person's actual vocal characteristics, is so emotionally significant.

Emerging Technology

The field of AAC for ALS is experiencing a period of unprecedented innovation. Advances in brain-computer interfaces, artificial intelligence, and neural speech decoding are opening possibilities that were science fiction just a decade ago. These technologies hold the promise of dramatically faster, more natural communication for people with even the most severe physical limitations.

Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI)

Brain-computer interfaces detect neural activity and translate it directly into communication commands, bypassing the motor system entirely. This is the most promising technology for people in the locked-in state of advanced ALS, where even eye movement may be unreliable.

Non-Invasive BCIs

These systems use sensors placed on the scalp (EEG, fNIRS) to detect brain activity:

  • P300 speller systems: The user focuses on a letter on a screen while rows and columns flash. When the desired letter flashes, the brain produces a distinctive P300 signal that the system detects. Typical speed: 1-8 characters per minute.
  • Steady-state visual evoked potential (SSVEP): Letters or buttons flicker at different frequencies. The brain "locks on" to the frequency of the attended target, which the system detects. Generally faster than P300 systems.
  • Motor imagery: The user imagines specific movements (e.g., left hand, right hand, feet) to control a cursor or selection system.

Invasive BCIs: The Frontier

Invasive BCIs, which involve surgically implanted electrode arrays, have seen dramatic breakthroughs in 2024-2026:

Breakthrough: 99% Accuracy at 56 WPM

In a landmark study, a person with ALS used an implanted BCI independently at home for more than two years without needing daily recalibration. The multimodal BCI achieved 99% accuracy at decoding his attempted speech into text, communicating over 237,000 sentences at approximately 56 words per minute. Over 4,800 hours of use were logged.

  • Neuralink: Has successfully implanted its N1 device in multiple patients with ALS and spinal cord injury. Received FDA Breakthrough Device Designation for its speech restoration system in May 2025, aiming to decode attempted speech movements from motor and language areas of the brain.
  • Synchron (Stentrode): Uses an endovascular approach -- a stent-like electrode array inserted through a blood vessel into the brain, avoiding open brain surgery. Demonstrated the ability to control an iPad in August 2025. Particularly relevant for ALS due to the less invasive surgical approach.
  • BrainGate: An academic research consortium that has demonstrated some of the most accurate speech decoding results, achieving up to 97% accuracy in translating attempted speech from brain signals to text.

AI-Powered Communication

Artificial intelligence is enhancing AAC for ALS in multiple ways:

  • Predictive text and language modeling: Modern AI language models can predict not just the next word but entire sentences based on context, dramatically reducing the number of selections needed to compose a message.
  • Personalized prediction: AI systems learn individual users' communication patterns, vocabulary, and common phrases, becoming more efficient over time.
  • Voice synthesis: Neural text-to-speech can now produce remarkably natural-sounding synthetic voices, including personalized voices created from limited voice banking recordings.
  • Speech recognition for degraded speech: AI trained on dysarthric speech can understand speech that is unintelligible to human listeners, extending the useful period of natural speech.

Platform Integration

Major technology companies are increasingly building accessibility into their platforms:

  • Apple BCI protocol: In May 2025, Apple announced a BCI Human Interface Device input protocol, allowing brain-computer interfaces to interact natively with Apple products, signaling mainstream technology adoption of BCI input.
  • Smart home integration: AAC devices increasingly integrate with Amazon Alexa, Google Home, and Apple HomeKit, extending communication beyond person-to-person into environmental control.
  • Telehealth AAC: Video call platforms are being optimized for AAC users, with features like text-to-speech output directly into calls.
Clinical Trials Expanding

As of early 2026, approximately 25 clinical trials of BCI implants are currently underway worldwide, with participation growing from single digits to dozens of patients. Leading companies Neuralink and Synchron have expanded trials internationally. While still experimental, these trials represent a rapidly accelerating path toward clinically available BCI-based communication for ALS.

Evidence & Research

The evidence base supporting AAC intervention in ALS is robust and growing. Research consistently demonstrates that timely, well-implemented AAC improves quality of life, reduces caregiver burden, and maintains the person's ability to participate in their own care decisions.

Key Research Findings

Timing of Intervention

  • A systematic review of over 40 studies (1988-2011) concluded that the majority of ALS patients who used AAC devices retrospectively wished the device had been introduced earlier.
  • Early introduction of AAC (at diagnosis) was associated with better existential and psychological outcomes compared to delayed intervention, both in the short term and in reducing long-term outcome decline.
  • Clinical guidelines recommend AAC referral when speaking rate reaches 125 words per minute on the Speech Intelligibility Test, though many experts advocate for even earlier referral at diagnosis.

Speech Decline Patterns

  • A 2022 study in Scientific Reports quantified speech decline rates: bulbar-onset patients showed significant speaking rate reduction at 23 months and intelligibility decline at 32 months, while spinal-onset patients retained functional speech for 60+ months.
  • The 120 WPM threshold has been validated as a clinical marker for impending rapid intelligibility loss, making it a critical trigger for AAC intervention.
  • Up to 85% of all ALS patients (including those with limb onset) eventually develop bulbar symptoms affecting speech.

AAC Effectiveness

  • Eye-gaze systems have been shown to be effective and reliable for people with ALS, with most users able to learn the system within a few sessions when introduced at the appropriate time.
  • Multi-modal access approaches (combining eye gaze with switch scanning as backup) provide the most robust communication reliability.
  • Voice banking quality is significantly higher when completed before any speech decline begins, reinforcing the importance of early intervention.

BCI Research

  • Invasive BCIs have achieved up to 99% accuracy in decoding attempted speech in people with ALS, with communication rates approaching 56 words per minute.
  • Non-invasive BCIs, while slower, have demonstrated the ability to provide communication to individuals in a completely locked-in state.
  • Long-term stability of implanted BCIs has been demonstrated over 2+ years of daily home use, a critical milestone for clinical adoption.

Ongoing Research Directions

  • Silent speech interfaces: Systems that detect articulatory movements or muscle signals from the face and neck, potentially allowing "speech" production before complete paralysis
  • Neural speech prosthetics: Implanted systems that could eventually decode intended speech in real-time at near-conversational speeds
  • AI-enhanced communication: Large language models being adapted to predict and generate full messages from minimal input, dramatically increasing communication speed
  • Remote monitoring: Digital biomarkers from speech and typing patterns being developed to track disease progression and optimize AAC timing without frequent clinic visits

Key Considerations

Successfully implementing AAC for a person with ALS requires attention to multiple practical, emotional, and systemic factors. Here are the most important considerations for clinicians, caregivers, and people living with ALS.

For People Newly Diagnosed with ALS

1

Start Voice Banking Now

Do not wait for speech changes. Begin voice and message banking as soon as possible after diagnosis, while speech quality is at its best. This is a time-limited opportunity that cannot be recovered.

2

Request an AAC Evaluation

Ask your neurologist for a referral to a speech-language pathologist specializing in AAC. Early evaluation allows time for education, device trials, and informed decision-making before communication needs become urgent.

3

Learn About Your Options

Familiarize yourself with the range of AAC options available. Understanding the continuum of low-tech and high-tech solutions helps reduce anxiety about future communication changes.

4

Plan for Access Transitions

Work with your AAC team to develop a plan for how you will access your communication device at each stage, from touch to head tracking to eye gaze to switch scanning. Having the next method ready before you need it prevents communication gaps.

For Clinicians and SLPs

  • Don't wait for speech to deteriorate. Current best practice recommends AAC introduction at or near diagnosis, not after speech becomes unintelligible. The goal is proactive preparation, not reactive crisis management.
  • Monitor speaking rate regularly. Track speaking rate and intelligibility at every visit. The 120 WPM threshold is a validated clinical trigger for intensifying AAC intervention.
  • Address the emotional dimension. Many patients resist AAC initially because it feels like "giving up" on speech. Frame AAC as a supplement to (not replacement for) natural speech, and as a tool for preserving communication, not an admission of defeat.
  • Plan for the full trajectory. Select AAC systems that can accommodate multiple access methods (touch, eye gaze, switch) to provide continuity as the disease progresses, rather than requiring a complete system change at each transition.
  • Include communication partners. Train family members and caregivers in AAC use and partner-assisted strategies. Communication is a two-way process, and partners need to learn how to interact effectively with AAC.

For Caregivers and Family Members

  • Be patient with communication speed. AAC communication is slower than natural speech. Allow extra time, resist the urge to guess or finish sentences, and maintain eye contact and engagement.
  • Learn the technology. Understanding how the AAC device works means you can help troubleshoot, customize vocabulary, and provide support during frustrating moments.
  • Maintain the device. Ensure the AAC device is always charged, accessible, and functioning. A person who depends on AAC is effectively silenced if their device is not available.
  • Support emotional expression. AAC can sometimes make communication feel transactional. Find ways to preserve emotional connection, humor, and intimacy in your communication.
  • Advocate for coverage. Navigate insurance processes early. Work with social workers and ALS organizations to secure funding for equipment before it is urgently needed.

Common Barriers to AAC Adoption in ALS

Barrier Description Solution
Psychological resistance Accepting AAC can feel like accepting decline. Some patients view it as giving up. Frame as empowerment. Introduce early as preparation, not surrender. Highlight continued capabilities.
Late referral Many patients are not referred for AAC until speech is already severely impaired. Educate neurologists about early AAC referral. Monitor speaking rate at every visit.
Cost and insurance Dedicated devices can cost $10,000-$20,000. Insurance processes are slow and complex. Begin insurance process early. Contact ALS Association, Team Gleason, and device loaner programs.
Technology learning curve Learning a new communication system while dealing with disease progression is challenging. Introduce technology gradually. Start with simple features. Provide hands-on training and ongoing support.
Access method transitions Changing access methods (e.g., from touch to eye gaze) can be disorienting and frustrating. Plan transitions proactively. Allow overlap periods where both methods are available. Practice new methods before the old one is lost.
Fatigue AAC use can be physically and cognitively exhausting. Schedule communication for high-energy times. Use pre-stored phrases for routine messages. Allow rest breaks.
Critical Reminder

A person with ALS who loses access to their AAC device is a person who has been silenced. AAC devices should be treated with the same urgency as any life-sustaining medical equipment. Backup communication methods (even simple alphabet boards) should always be available. Device charging, maintenance, and positioning should be part of the daily care routine.
