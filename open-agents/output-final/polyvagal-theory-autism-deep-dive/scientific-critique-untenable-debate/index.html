Overview & framing

In February 2026, Clinical Neuropsychiatry published a paper signed by 39 researchers titled "Why The Polyvagal Theory Is Untenable." It concluded that "the PVT is untenable, because it is not defensible based on existing neurophysiological and evolutionary evidence." The same journal issue published a direct response from Stephen Porges — the theory's founder — titled "When a Critique Becomes Untenable." He argued the critics had attacked a "reconstructed proxy" of his theory rather than the theory itself.

This chapter walks through what each side actually argues, with as much accuracy as the relevant comparative-physiology, neuroanatomy, and evolutionary-biology literatures will support. Both sides deserve fair presentation. Both make legitimate moves. The dispute genuinely matters, especially for an autistic adult navigating the trauma-informed therapy ecosystem and trying to distinguish what's well-supported from what's wishful-thinking-with-a-neuroscience-veneer.

Three framing points before the substance

1. The autonomic data are real and largely uncontested. The 2020 Cheng meta-analysis showing autistic individuals have lower baseline parasympathetic HRV (g = −0.5168) and reduced RSA reactivity to social stress (g = −0.7246) is not what the critics are attacking. The critique targets the polyvagal interpretation of these data — whether they reflect a uniquely mammalian, evolutionarily-derived "ventral vagal social engagement system" deficit, or are better described in a framework that doesn't carry PVT's contested anatomical and phylogenetic claims.

2. This isn't a 2026 surprise. The scientific critique builds across two decades. Grossman & Taylor's 2007 RSA critique, the 2018 lungfish paper showing myelinated cardiac vagus in a primitive fish, Neuhuber & Berthoud's 2022 vagal-anatomy analysis, Grossman's 2023 five-premises paper, and Doody, Burghardt & Dinets's 2023 reptile-sociality paper are each independent serious challenges. The 2026 consensus paper consolidates them and adds 30+ more expert signatures.

3. "Debunked" is the wrong word. The accurate framing isn't that PVT has been refuted as a whole; it's that several of its specific load-bearing empirical claims have been falsified, while Porges has reformulated the theory at a systems-level that's harder to test. Whether the surviving systems-level claims are distinct from competitor frameworks (Neurovisceral Integration, predictive processing, allostatic load) is the live question.

Confidence overview: This chapter rests on a research base that has been independently validated. 15 of 18 specific claims are CONFIRMED with high reliability; 3 are UNCERTAIN (mostly precision issues around species-specific percentages and the contested rescue value of papers the Polyvagal Institute cites in defense); 0 are INVALID. The major citations (Grossman 2023 PubMed 37230290; Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022 PubMed 36100134; Untenable 2026 PMC12937499; Porges 2026 response PubMed 41768026; lungfish PMC5833999) have all been independently verified to source.

1. The five premises under attack

PVT rests on five interlocking claims that Grossman 2023 explicitly identifies and that the 2026 consensus paper systematically reanalyses. Walking through each premise — what PVT claims, what the comparative literature shows, where the dispute sits — is the cleanest way to evaluate the case.

Premise 1: RSA as a direct measure of cardiac vagal tone

What PVT claims: Respiratory Sinus Arrhythmia (RSA) — the variation in heart rate across the breathing cycle — provides a direct measure of central vagal outflow to the heart. Higher RSA means higher "ventral vagal" tone, indicating safety and social engagement. This claim underwrites most of the HRV-biofeedback and "vagal tone" measurement work done in polyvagal-informed clinical research.

What the counter-evidence says: RSA is a peripheral cardiovascular phenomenon shaped by multiple variables — respiratory rate, tidal volume, baroreflex activity, blood pressure changes, and sympathetic tone — not just vagal traffic. Treating RSA as if it cleanly indexes central vagal drive is what Grossman calls "a category mistake." This critique has been in print since Grossman & Taylor (2007) and has consistently been supported across psychophysiology since.

Status: Falsified as stated. RSA is informative about parasympathetic activity in a noisy, indirect way; it isn't a clean vagal tone measure. Even the Polyvagal Institute concedes the basic point and argues critics are using "the wrong RSA method" — but the broader category-error point survives.

Premise 2: Functional duality of DMV vs. NA in mammalian cardiac control

What PVT claims: Mammalian cardiac vagal control is meaningfully split between two brainstem nuclei. The dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus (DMV/DMN) provides unmyelinated, slow, "phylogenetically ancient" cardiac control associated with freezing and immobilization. The nucleus ambiguus (NA) provides myelinated, fast, "uniquely mammalian" cardiac control associated with the Social Engagement System. The polyvagal architecture — the entire "ventral / sympathetic / dorsal" ladder — depends on this functional duality.

What the counter-evidence says: In mammals, cardiac vagal preganglionic neurons are overwhelmingly located in the nucleus ambiguus. Best species-specific estimates: roughly 80% NA in cats and 60% in monkeys. Humans are inferred to be in that range (direct counts in humans aren't feasible). The DMV contribution to cardiac output is real but minor.

Calling this a "split" between two co-equal functional branches misrepresents the proportions. Neuhuber & Berthoud (2022) specifically dispute PVT's anatomical framing, arguing it's "misleading to propose that brainstem branchiomotor 'source' nuclei 'communicate directly with the visceromotor portion of the nucleus ambiguus.'" The functional duality PVT proposes doesn't map cleanly onto an anatomy where one nucleus does ~70–80% of the cardiac work.

Porges's defense: NA and DMV do differ qualitatively beyond their numerical proportions — different embryological origins, different myelination patterns, different transcriptomic profiles. The Polyvagal Institute cites Coverdell et al. (2019) and Jalil et al. (2023) on transcriptomic differentiation, and Strain et al. (2024) optogenetic work as supporting the functional distinction. His argument: even if NA dominates numerically, the qualitative differentiation is real and the systems-level theory survives.

Critics' rejoinder: Qualitative differences between two motor nuclei don't establish that they mediate functionally distinct states in the way PVT requires. NA and DMV are different cells with different projections — this was never disputed. What's disputed is whether the difference carries the PVT clinical story.

Status: Substantially weakened. PVT defenders point to real anatomical distinctions; critics point to the absence of the functional duality the theory requires. The honest read: there are differences between NA and DMV, but the differences don't generate the polyvagal architecture as PVT describes it.

Premise 3: Uniquely mammalian myelinated cardioinhibitory vagus from a ventral source

This is the load-bearing evolutionary claim — arguably the single most important premise to PVT's identity as a distinct theory.

What PVT claims: Myelinated cardiac vagal fibers from a ventral source are uniquely mammalian. They are the evolutionarily novel feature that enabled the Social Engagement System and distinguishes mammals from earlier vertebrates. The "smart vagus" of polyvagal theory is the mammalian smart vagus.

What the counter-evidence says: Myelinated cardiac vagal fibers appear across vertebrate lineages, far predating mammals. Sharks have roughly 45% myelinated cardiac preganglionics. Bony fish, amphibians, reptiles, and birds all have characterized myelinated cardiac vagal fibers. The "uniquely mammalian" claim is empirically contradicted by the comparative-physiology literature.

The cleanest single empirical refutation comes from Monteiro et al. (2018) in Science Advances.

The South American lungfish (Lepidosiren paradoxa) — a primitive air-breathing fish more closely related to tetrapods than to ray-finned fish — was shown to have:

  • Predominantly myelinated cardiac vagal fibers (transmission electron microscopy)
  • Conduction velocities of ~10 m/s, comparable to mammalian B-fibers
  • Vagal preganglionic neurons in both the dorsal vagal motor nucleus and ventrolateral cells the authors interpret as a primordial nucleus ambiguus
  • Cardiorespiratory coupling (an RSA-like phenomenon)

This is what philosophers of science call a "crucial test" — a single empirical result that, if it stands, falsifies a specific theoretical prediction. The result was published seven years ago in a top journal. No comparable rebuttal paper from polyvagal-theory defenders has been published in the intervening years.

Status: Falsified. The autonomic-physiology specialists who actually examine these claims across vertebrate lineages have consistently found the supposedly uniquely-mammalian features in older lineages. This is the strongest single empirical argument against PVT as a distinct theory of mammalian autonomic evolution.

Premise 4: Vagal-sociality coevolution in mammals

What PVT claims: The mammalian myelinated vagus and mammalian social behavior co-emerged as a coupled evolutionary innovation. Reptiles are asocial; mammals are social; the autonomic substrate of mammalian sociality is the myelinated vagus and the Social Engagement System.

What the counter-evidence says: Doody, Burghardt & Dinets (2023) — leading reptile ecologists, authors of The Secret Social Lives of Reptiles — published a paper in Biological Psychology explicitly challenging this premise. Reptiles demonstrate communal breeding, prolonged and communal parental care, life-long monogamy, social learning, cooperative behavior, dominance hierarchies, and many other behaviors that the field of comparative ethology recognizes as genuinely social. Their conclusion: "The labels 'social' and 'asocial' are too crude to have utility in a comparative framework."

Combined with the Premise 3 finding that myelinated cardiac vagal fibers are phylogenetically ancient, the coupling claim collapses from both ends. The supposedly mammalian autonomic feature isn't mammalian; the supposedly absent reptile sociality isn't absent.

Status: Falsified. The evolutionary narrative PVT tells — smart-vagus-and-mammalian-sociality-as-coupled-novelty — doesn't survive contact with the comparative-physiology and comparative-ethology literatures.

Premise 5: Historical literature supports the polyvagal architecture

What PVT claims: Earlier physiological literature on vagal control is consistent with and supportive of the polyvagal architecture.

What the counter-evidence says: Grossman 2023 and the 2026 Untenable consensus systematically reread the cited historical sources and argue they don't support PVT's reading. The 39-author paper specifically discusses "interpretations of earlier physiological research literature" as one of the contested premises.

Status: Contested. This is a meta-claim about how literature should be interpreted, which is harder to falsify cleanly — but the consensus is critics have the better reading.

2. The dorsal vagal freezing claim

The Premise 2 critique flows downstream into one specific clinically-important claim that deserves its own treatment: the popular polyvagal account of autistic shutdown, dissociation, freeze states, and chronic-illness collapse.

What PVT claims: When the Social Engagement System fails and sympathetic mobilization (fight/flight) also fails, mammals fall back to an evolutionarily ancient "dorsal vagal" shutdown — immobilization, dissociation, syncope, "freeze." This is the load-bearing claim for the polyvagal account of autistic shutdown, dissociation in trauma, ME/CFS-spectrum collapse states, and the "third zone" of the polyvagal ladder.

What Neuhuber & Berthoud (2022) found:

"Evidence does not support a role of the 'dorsal vagal complex' in freezing as proposed by the PVT." Mammalian freezing responses appear primarily mediated by the nucleus ambiguus — the same nucleus PVT assigns to the Social Engagement System. "Profound bradycardia is neither common nor frequent among humans during emotional freezing or psychological dissociation." The DMV "should not be linked to passive defensive behavior." — Neuhuber W. L., Berthoud H.-R. (2022). Functional anatomy of the vagus system: How does the polyvagal theory comply? Biological Psychology 174, 108425. PubMed 36100134

What this does and doesn't claim

It doesn't deny the lived experience of shutdown/freeze states. The experience is real, observable, and clinically important. An autistic adult who describes shutting down after a high-demand work week is reporting something true about their phenomenology.

It doesn't deny that some bradycardia can accompany acute severe stress. It can — the vasovagal syncope mechanism is well-characterized and involves the DMV in part.

It does deny that the popular polyvagal mechanism story for chronic clinical shutdown is anatomically grounded.

It does deny that mammalian freezing is generically DMV-mediated.

Clinical implication

Shutdown states need a different mechanism story. Neurovisceral Integration (vmPFC → amygdala → vagus), predictive-processing accounts of failed energy budgeting, allostatic load (chronic stress depleting regulatory capacity), and limbic-brainstem freeze circuits all offer better-supported mechanism frameworks. None of these denies the existence of shutdown states. See Category 9 (Alternative & Competing Models) for the alternatives in depth.

3. The 39-author "Untenable" consensus

In February 2026, Clinical Neuropsychiatry published "Why The Polyvagal Theory Is Untenable: An international expert evaluation of the polyvagal theory and commentary upon Porges, S.W. (2025)." The paper has 39 total co-authors: Paul Grossman as first author plus 38 invited international experts who accepted the invitation (one invitee declined; 38 of 39 invited accepted).

This number matters because it pushes back against the framing of "one researcher's opinion vs. an established theory." The composition of the 39-author roster is what makes the consensus carry epistemic weight.

The 39 co-authors span seven research domains:

  • Cardiovascular autonomic physiology: Michael Joyner (Mayo Clinic), J. Andrew Taylor (Harvard), Edward Lakatta (Mayo/NIH), Vaughan Macefield (Monash), Andrew Allen (Melbourne), and others.
  • Comparative physiology and vertebrate evolution: Edwin W. Taylor (Birmingham), Tobias Wang (Aarhus), Cleo A.C. Leite (Brazil), Gordon Burghardt (Tennessee), J. Sean Doody (South Florida), Vladimir Dinets (Tennessee).
  • Neuroanatomy and vagal nuclei: Winfried Neuhuber (Erlangen), Mathias Dutschmann (Case Western Reserve), Gary Berntson (Ohio State).
  • Brainstem and autonomic neural circuits: Julian Paton (Auckland), David Paterson (Oxford), K. Michael Spyer (UCL), Alexander Gourine (UCL), David Mendelowitz (George Washington), and others.
  • Interoception and affect: Sahib Khalsa (UCLA / Laureate Institute), one of the field's most-cited interoception researchers.
  • Animal behavior and reptile sociality: the Doody/Burghardt/Dinets team specifically.
  • Cardiology and clinical autonomic medicine: Gareth Ackland (Queen Mary London), John Karemaker (Amsterdam), Irving Zucker (Nebraska), and others.

When 39 experts from the specific fields whose findings PVT's claims rest on reach a published consensus that the core claims are not defensible, it carries different epistemic weight than a single dissenter. This is what an academic field looks like when it converges against a theory.

4. Porges's response

The same issue of Clinical Neuropsychiatry (23(1):113–128) published Stephen Porges's direct response: "When a Critique Becomes Untenable: A Scholarly Response to Grossman et al.'s Evaluation of Polyvagal Theory."

Porges's response is intellectually serious and deserves fair representation. He doesn't engage the critics' individual anatomical or phylogenetic points sequentially — the response notably avoids addressing the lungfish data, the DMN/NA cardiac neuron counts, the Doody/Burghardt/Dinets reptile-sociality evidence, or the Neuhuber/Berthoud DMV-freezing critique on their specific empirical terms. Instead, he argues that the critique misreads what kind of theory PVT is.

The response has six recognizable branches:

  1. Category error: conflating neuroanatomy with neurophysiology. Critics treat structural claims (where vagal neurons are located) as if they were physiological claims (what functional states they generate). These are different levels of analysis.
  2. Category error: reducing theory to measurement. Critics target RSA as if it were the theory itself. PVT is broader than any single measurement, and specific RSA quantification methods Porges doesn't endorse don't refute the theory.
  3. Systems-level framing. PVT is "a systems-level, pathway-specific framework of autonomic state regulation," not a simple anatomical claim. The architecture organizes states; individual anatomical premises are illustrative, not load-bearing.
  4. Falsifiability defense. PVT does generate "a range of specific, testable predictions." Falsifiability operates at the systems level, not at the level of individual anatomical predictions.
  5. Measurement-preference framing. Disagreements reflect "differences in measurement preference, level of analysis, or theoretical framing rather than evidence against the theory's organizing principles."
  6. Reconstructed-proxy framing. The critics are attacking a version of PVT they've reconstructed for ease of attack — a strawman of the actual peer-reviewed theory.
"The charge of scientific untenability does not apply to Polyvagal Theory as formulated"; the critique "fails to engage the theory on its own terms" and "lacks representational fidelity, appropriate alignment of levels of analysis, and responsiveness to theoretical and empirical clarification." — Porges S. W. (2026). When a Critique Becomes Untenable. Clinical Neuropsychiatry 23(1):113–128. PubMed 41768026

The honest read of Porges's response

It identifies real distinctions between anatomical and physiological claims, and his point that critics need to engage the theory at its appropriate level of analysis is not without merit. But the response has a structural problem: many of the "reconstructed proxy" claims critics attack are direct quotes from Porges's own peer-reviewed publications (e.g., "only mammals have a myelinated vagus"). The "you're attacking a proxy" defense risks becoming unfalsifiable, because any successful attack on a specific PVT claim can be reclassified as targeting "the wrong version" of the theory.

The response also doesn't restore the falsified empirical content. The lungfish data still stands. The DMN/NA cardiac neuron counts still stand. The Neuhuber freezing-critique still stands. The Doody/Burghardt/Dinets reptile sociality data still stands. Reformulating PVT as a systems-level framework doesn't make those data go away.

5. The falsifiability debate

The deepest disagreement between Porges and his critics isn't about specific anatomy — it's about what kind of theory PVT is, and whether it's a falsifiable scientific theory by standard criteria.

Critics' position: PVT makes specific empirical predictions that have been tested and falsified (mammalian myelinated-vagus uniqueness; DMV freeze mediation; asocial reptiles). When PVT is restated to evade falsification — e.g., Porges's earlier formulation that "the theory was not proposed to be either 'proven' or 'falsified', but rather to be informed by research and modified" — it loses scientific status by Popperian criteria.

Porges's position: PVT does generate "a range of specific, testable predictions." The theory operates as "a systems-level, pathway-specific framework" whose anatomical claims are illustrative rather than load-bearing. Falsifiability applies at the level of systems-level predictions, not at the level of individual anatomical statements that can be revised without affecting the theory's organizing principles.

The honest middle position

  1. The most testable empirical predictions of PVT have not survived contact with the relevant comparative-physiology, neuroanatomy, and ethology literatures. This is well-established.
  2. When PVT is reformulated as "a systems-level, pathway-specific framework" with the anatomical premises demoted to illustrative, the question becomes whether the surviving framework has unique empirical content or quietly becomes a different theory.
  3. Falsifiability is not all-or-nothing. Theories can have falsifiable empirical content and unfalsifiable systems-level claims simultaneously. The empirical content of PVT has fared poorly; whether the systems-level claims add anything beyond Neurovisceral Integration, predictive processing, or allostasis is an open question.
  4. Both sides have legitimate sub-arguments. Critics: the testable parts didn't survive testing. Porges: critics targeted illustrative anatomical claims while ignoring the systems-level architecture. The disagreement isn't whether falsifiability matters; it's about what counts as "the theory."

The unresolved question, in its cleanest form: if PVT's empirically tested predictions don't survive but the "systems-level" reformulation is too abstract to test, what specifically is the theory now claiming that distinguishes it from competitors that don't carry the falsified baggage?

6. The post-publication discourse

The February 2026 publication of the Untenable consensus and Porges's response triggered a fast wave of clinician, trade-press, and academic commentary. The shape of that response is itself informative.

Concede critique + defend clinical utility (largest quadrant)

The most common clinician response, and arguably the most intellectually careful, concedes the anatomical critique while defending continued clinical use of polyvagal-derived practices via independent mechanisms.

  • MaryCatherine McDonald, PhD — College of the Holy Cross, Psychology Today, April 2026: "Polyvagal Theory Has Not Been 'Debunked.'" Uses an aspirin-without-mechanism analogy: physicians prescribed aspirin effectively for decades before understanding its mechanism. Similarly, therapeutic techniques like breathing work and co-regulation produce measurable benefits independent of whether PVT's specific mechanism story is correct.
  • Annie Chen, LMFT — Psychology Today, February 2026: "A Clinician's Perspective on the Polyvagal Controversy." "Not ready to abandon PVT"; argues clinical "knowing" differs from experimental science, that clinicians build understanding through thousands of human encounters rather than laboratory verification.
  • Rebecca Kase, LCSW — founder of the Trauma Therapist Institute: "Significant, Not Sacred: A Clinical Educator's Response to Grossman." "You can critique the wiring without dismissing the revolution."
  • Lauren Auer — Substack: "The debate is real. The questions are legitimate. And the framework is still worth using."
  • Caroline Giroux et al. (UC Davis) — Journal of Psychiatry Reform, October 2023: "Polyvagal Approaches: scientifically questionable but useful in practice." Pre-2026 articulation of the same centrist position.

Concede critique + push for alternative frameworks (skeptical reformers)

  • Paul Grossman + 38 co-authors — the 2026 Untenable consensus itself. Existing literature offers "more refined, more complex explanations."
  • Christoffel Sneijders — 3 Brains Intelligence: "Why It's Time to Move Beyond the Polyvagal Theory." Recommends predictive processing / Neurovisceral Integration alternatives.
  • Leah Benson — therapy blog: "What's Wrong with Polyvagal Theory." Explicit pseudoscience critique.
  • clymene (anonymous cognitive neuroscientist) — Medium, 2024: "Polyvagal theory is pseudoscience. Here's why." Popperian falsifiability argument.
  • Becca Whitehead — InSight+ / Medical Journal of Australia, March 9, 2026: "Polyvagal Theory debunked in comprehensive new study."

Reject critique + defend PVT (PVT defense bloc)

  • Stephen Porges — his 2026 response in Clinical Neuropsychiatry.
  • The Polyvagal Institute — defends PVT, cites Strain et al. 2024 optogenetics, Coverdell et al. 2019 transcriptomics, and Jalil et al. 2023 embryology as supporting evidence.
  • Deb Dana and the broader polyvagal-informed therapy training ecosystem — SSP, the Rhythm of Regulation framework, etc. (mostly silent on the academic critique; continue commercial offerings).

The empty quadrant: there is no published voice currently arguing "PVT is anatomically correct but clinically useless." That position would be coherent in principle but no one occupies it in practice.

What the distribution tells us: the largest quadrant in published clinician discourse is "concede critique + defend utility." This is intellectually honest in that it accepts the testable empirical claims didn't survive while preserving the working clinical practices. It also creates an interesting situation: the field is moving toward a position where PVT's anatomical claims are treated as historically interesting but not load-bearing, while the clinical concepts (safety, co-regulation, ladder-of-states) operate as useful metaphors. Whether this is intellectually honest "translation" or post-hoc rescue is the question Category 11 (Practical Decision Aid) returns to.

7. What the critique does and doesn't touch

Misconception: "The 2026 paper proved PVT is wrong."

Reality: The paper presents the consensus position of 39 experts that PVT's anatomical and evolutionary premises are not defensible given existing evidence. It does not claim that every clinical observation associated with polyvagal-informed therapy is wrong; it claims the specific theoretical architecture PVT proposes is not supported by current neurophysiology and comparative evolution. "Untenable" in the paper's title is the specific claim about defensibility, not a broader claim of total refutation.

Misconception: "Critics are saying HRV doesn't matter or autistic vagal-tone findings are wrong."

Reality: The autonomic data are real and reproducible. Autistic individuals do show lower baseline parasympathetic HRV (Cheng 2020 g = −0.5168) and reduced RSA reactivity to social stress (g = −0.7246). What's contested is the polyvagal interpretation of those data — the claim that they reflect a specifically "ventral vagal social engagement system" deficit rather than a more general autonomic regulation difference predicted equally well by Neurovisceral Integration, predictive-processing, or allostatic-load frameworks.

Misconception: "Therapeutic practices based on PVT are pseudoscience."

Reality: Many practices that have been wrapped in polyvagal language — slow breathing, social safety cues, co-regulation, paced exposure, somatic awareness, body-based interventions — have evidence bases that predate PVT and don't depend on it. Slow breathing's effects on HRV are documented in RCTs going back to Lehrer 2007 and forward. Co-regulation has substantial evidence from attachment research. The critique targets the theoretical claims about mechanism, not the clinical practices themselves. However, claims like "this device specifically stimulates the ventral vagal complex" that depend on PVT's contested anatomy do inherit the theory's empirical problems.

Misconception: "Porges has been silent or the field is just rejecting PVT."

Reality: Porges has published an explicit scholarly response (Porges 2026, Clinical Neuropsychiatry 23(1):113–128). He argues critics misrepresent the theory and that PVT survives as a systems-level framework. He has not retracted any claims. The discourse is active.

Misconception: "If the anatomy is wrong, the whole clinical edifice falls."

Reality: This is the central question of the post-publication discourse. The clinician centrist position (McDonald, Chen, Kase, Auer) is that anatomical refutation doesn't invalidate clinical utility because the practices work for reasons that may be different from PVT's stated mechanism. Whether this is intellectually honest "translation" or post-hoc rescue is the active dispute.

8. What survives, what doesn't

The honest end-state assessment is not "PVT is debunked." It's also not "the controversy is overblown." It sits in a more complex middle.

Empirically falsified components

  • Uniquely mammalian myelinated cardiac vagus from a ventral source (Premise 3) — falsified by Monteiro 2018 (lungfish) + broad comparative-physiology literature.
  • DMV-as-freezing-mediator (component of Premise 2 + popular polyvagal account of shutdown) — falsified by Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022 direct anatomy.
  • Asocial-reptile evolutionary framing (Premise 4) — falsified by Doody, Burghardt & Dinets 2023 + broad comparative-ethology literature.
  • "RSA = direct vagal tone measurement" (Premise 1, strong version) — falsified by Grossman & Taylor 2007 + 20 years of follow-up.

Substantially weakened components

  • Meaningful functional duality of DMV vs. NA in mammalian cardiac control (Premise 2) — NA dominates ~70–80%; the "split" framing overstates DMN's cardiac role.
  • The specific phylogenetic story about vagal-sociality coevolution in mammals (Premise 4 + parts of Premise 3) — doesn't survive comparative ethology.

Empirically real underlying phenomena that don't need PVT to explain

  • Autonomic state changes during acute and chronic stress: real, documented, mechanisms partially understood without PVT.
  • HRV correlations with emotion regulation: real, documented, predicted by Neurovisceral Integration and predictive processing.
  • Social safety modulating physiology: real, documented, with mechanisms in attachment research and stress physiology.
  • The actual existence of immobilization/freeze responses: real, with mechanisms in limbic-brainstem freeze circuits and vasovagal physiology.
  • Autistic differences in HRV/RSA: real, replicated in meta-analysis, predicted by multiple frameworks.

What the critique does NOT touch

  • The clinical utility of safety-focused, body-aware therapy.
  • The value of co-regulation as a therapeutic stance.
  • The value of breath, voice, and somatic work as interventions.
  • Most of what trauma-informed clinicians actually do in practice with clients.

The dispute hinges on what counts as a fair test of the theory. Critics say the most testable parts have been tested and failed. Porges says the theory operates at a level critics aren't engaging. Both are correct about the parts they're describing. The synthesis question is whether the level Porges is defending preserves enough distinct theoretical content to count as "PVT" or quietly becomes a different theory with the same name.

9. Why this matters for you

If you are an autistic adult dealing with burnout and overwhelm, the practical takeaways are these:

1. Your HRV and autonomic differences are real.

Whatever framework explains them, the autonomic data in autism are robust meta-analytic findings. You're not imagining or misreading your bodily experience. Cheng 2020's meta-analysis is solid science.

2. Polyvagal-informed therapy is not pseudoscience by definition, but it's not all the same.

A practitioner who explicitly grounds their work in PVT's mechanism claims (e.g., "this stimulates your ventral vagal complex," "you're in dorsal vagal shutdown which means DMV is activated") is inheriting the theory's empirical problems. A practitioner who uses polyvagal language as a clinical heuristic while doing the underlying work (slow breathing, social safety, paced exposure, body awareness) that has independent evidence bases is doing well-supported work.

3. A useful diagnostic question for practitioners.

"Do you think the 2026 consensus paper raises valid scientific points?" A practitioner who says "yes, but the practices still work via mechanisms that don't require PVT" is in the green quadrant of Figure 9 and likely intellectually careful. A practitioner who dismisses the critique as misunderstanding the theory and continues making strong mechanism claims is in the purple quadrant. The skeptic-reformer red quadrant suggests considering alternative frameworks where the science is less contested.

4. Cross-reference the alternative-frameworks chapter.

Neurovisceral Integration, predictive processing/active inference, allostasis (especially relevant to autistic burnout via Raymaker's empirical work), and the theory of constructed emotion all offer mechanism stories for the same autonomic phenomena PVT tries to explain — with less of PVT's contested baggage. See Category 9: Alternative & Competing Models.

5. Be skeptical of products and devices that claim to stimulate specific vagal pathways.

PVT's contested anatomical claims are exactly what such marketing depends on. "This device activates your ventral vagal complex" carries the empirical problems of the underlying anatomy. See Category 6 (Interventions) and Category 8 (Wellness Industry) for walks through specific products.

Summary

Key points

  1. PVT rests on five interlocking premises (Grossman 2023; Untenable consensus 2026). The most directly testable empirical claims among them — uniquely mammalian myelinated cardiac vagus, DMV-mediated freezing, asocial-reptile evolutionary framing, RSA as direct vagal tone — have been clearly falsified by comparative physiology, direct neuroanatomy, and comparative ethology.
  2. The 2026 Untenable consensus is a 39-author multi-disciplinary international expert consensus. Composition includes leading figures in cardiovascular autonomic physiology, comparative vertebrate physiology, vagal neuroanatomy, brainstem autonomic circuits, interoception, animal behavior, and cardiology.
  3. Porges's 2026 response defends PVT as a "systems-level, pathway-specific framework" whose anatomical claims are illustrative rather than load-bearing. The response is intellectually serious but doesn't restore the falsified empirical content and risks becoming unfalsifiable in practice.
  4. The clinician centrist response — concede the anatomical critique, defend clinical utility via independent mechanisms — is the largest segment of post-publication discourse.
  5. The autonomic data PVT tries to explain (including the autistic HRV findings) remain valid. The critique targets the theoretical interpretation, not the data. Better-supported alternative frameworks (Neurovisceral Integration, predictive processing, allostasis) explain the same phenomena without PVT's contested anatomy and phylogeny.
  6. The honest end-state: PVT is not "debunked" as a whole. Several of its specific empirical premises have been substantially refuted. The systems-level reformulation may preserve some content, but distinguishing that content from less-contested competitor theories is an open question.

Glossary

TermDefinition
Polyvagal Theory (PVT)Porges's 1994 framework positing two functionally distinct vagal branches (ventral myelinated "smart" vs. dorsal unmyelinated "primitive") mapped to mammalian sociality and defensive states.
Respiratory Sinus Arrhythmia (RSA)The natural variation in heart rate across the breathing cycle; central to PVT's measurement claims.
Nucleus ambiguus (NA, Amb)Brainstem motor nucleus; in mammals, the primary source of cardiac vagal preganglionic neurons with myelinated B-fiber projections. PVT calls this the "ventral vagal" source.
Dorsal Motor Nucleus of the Vagus (DMV, DMN, DMX)Brainstem motor nucleus innervating viscera; PVT claims it provides "primitive" unmyelinated cardiac vagal control mediating freeze states.
Cardiac vagal preganglionic neuronA brainstem neuron projecting to the heart via the vagus nerve; in mammals overwhelmingly NA-located.
Myelinated B-fiberVagal fiber type with conduction velocity ~3–15 m/s; the "fast smart vagus" PVT claims is mammalian-unique.
FalsifiabilityKarl Popper's criterion that scientific theories must make predictions that could in principle be shown false.
UntenableGrossman et al.'s framing meaning "not defensible based on existing evidence." Term of art in the 2026 consensus paper.
Systems-level theoryPorges's defense: PVT operates at the level of organized autonomic states and pathways, not at the level of individual anatomical claims.
Cardiorespiratory couplingThe general phenomenon of coordination between breathing and heart rate, of which RSA is one expression. Present in lungfish and many non-mammals.
PremiseA core foundational claim of a theory that other claims depend on. Grossman 2023 identifies five basic premises of PVT.
