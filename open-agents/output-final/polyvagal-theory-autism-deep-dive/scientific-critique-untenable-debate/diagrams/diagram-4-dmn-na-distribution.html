The DMN vs NA Cardiac Control Dispute

PVT requires a meaningful functional split between the dorsal motor nucleus (DMN/DMV) and nucleus ambiguus (NA) in mammalian cardiac control. The empirical anatomy shows NA dominance, not a split.

PVT Premise 2

What PVT claims

Mammalian cardiac vagal control is functionally split between two nuclei with distinct roles:

PVT's implicit framing
NA — "smart vagus"
DMN — "primitive vagus"

Roughly co-equal weighting in popular polyvagal accounts; both nuclei do load-bearing work in the model.

NA branch: myelinated, fast, mammalian-novel, mediates the "Social Engagement System," tied to safety/affiliation.

DMN branch: unmyelinated, slow, "phylogenetically ancient," mediates freeze/shutdown/immobilization defensive states (the "dorsal vagal" state).

This functional split is the polyvagal architecture — the "ladder" of ventral / sympathetic / dorsal states maps onto these two anatomical nuclei.

Comparative anatomy

What direct counts actually show

In mammals, cardiac vagal preganglionic neurons are overwhelmingly in the nucleus ambiguus, with the dorsal motor nucleus contributing a minor share.

Cardiac vagal preganglionic neuron distribution (species-specific)
Cat
NA ~80%
DMN ~20%
Monkey
NA ~60%
DMN ~40%
Human (est.)
NA ~70–80%
DMN ~20–30%

Species-variant; cat values from primary morphometric studies (ScienceDirect Ambiguus Nucleus reference). Human values inferred from comparative data. Cardiac output dominance — DMN does have significant non-cardiac visceral roles (gut, etc.).

Calling this anatomy a meaningful "split between nuclei" — the load-bearing claim of the polyvagal architecture — overstates the DMN's cardiac contribution. The two-state ladder doesn't map cleanly onto an anatomy where one nucleus dominates.

Porges's defense (worth taking seriously): NA and DMN do differ qualitatively beyond numbers — different embryological origin, different myelination patterns, different transcriptomic profile (Coverdell et al. 2019; Jalil et al. 2023; Strain et al. 2024 optogenetic work). His argument: even if NA dominates numerically, the qualitative differentiation is real and the systems-level theory survives.

The critics' rejoinder: qualitative differences between two motor nuclei don't establish that they mediate functionally distinct states in the way PVT requires. Differentiation ≠ duality. NA & DMN are different cells with different projections — this was never disputed. What's disputed is whether the difference carries the PVT clinical story.
The dispute, distilled: "Split between nuclei" requires both nuclei doing comparable cardiac work in opposite functional directions. Direct counts show one nucleus does ~70–80% of the cardiac work in mammals. PVT defenders argue qualitative differences between the nuclei restore the functional duality; critics argue numerical dominance plus the absence of direct evidence for DMN-mediated freeze states (see Diagram 5) makes the polyvagal architecture not survive contact with anatomy.
