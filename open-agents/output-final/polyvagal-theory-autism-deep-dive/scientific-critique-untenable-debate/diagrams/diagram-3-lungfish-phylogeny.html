The Lungfish Problem — Myelinated Cardiac Vagus Across Vertebrates

PVT Premise 3 claims myelinated cardiac vagal fibers from a ventral source are uniquely mammalian. The actual comparative-physiology literature shows them across vertebrate lineages all the way back to sharks. The lungfish result is the cleanest empirical refutation.

Why the lungfish row is highlighted: the South American lungfish (Lepidosiren paradoxa) is a primitive air-breathing fish more closely related to tetrapods than to ray-finned fish. Monteiro et al. (2018) directly demonstrated that this primitive lineage already has the supposedly "uniquely mammalian" features: predominantly myelinated cardiac vagus, B-fiber-range conduction velocities (~10 m/s), and vagal preganglionic neurons in a ventrolateral region interpreted as a primordial nucleus ambiguus. If PVT Premise 3 were correct, this result should have been impossible. The result has stood for over 7 years without published refutation.

How to read this diagram

Green bars indicate presence of myelinated cardiac vagal fibers in that clade. Length of bar approximates the prominence of myelinated fibers where quantified (sharks ~45%; lungfish & mammals predominantly myelinated). Other clades shown as "present" without specific percentage.

Lungfish highlighted because Monteiro 2018 is the cleanest empirical refutation of PVT Premise 3.

Mammals highlighted because they're the clade PVT claims uniqueness for — but their pattern is the same as much older lineages.

Bottom line: Myelinated cardiac vagal fibers are not a mammalian innovation. They appear in primitive air-breathing fish, sharks, bony fish, amphibians, reptiles, and birds. The PVT phylogenetic story — that myelinated "smart vagus" coevolved with mammalian sociality — doesn't survive contact with comparative physiology. The autonomic data PVT tries to explain (e.g., autistic HRV findings) remain real; the evolutionary narrative explaining why mammals are special is what's contradicted.
