Polyvagal Theory (PVT) was introduced by Stephen Porges in his 1994 presidential address to the Society for Psychophysiology. It proposes that the mammalian autonomic nervous system isn’t the two-branch sympathetic-vs-parasympathetic on/off system most textbooks teach, but a three-tier hierarchy: a phylogenetically newer ventral vagal complex supporting social engagement and “felt safety”; the sympathetic nervous system driving fight-or-flight; and a phylogenetically older dorsal vagal complex driving freeze, collapse, and shutdown. The theory introduces neuroception — the nervous system’s continuous sub-conscious scanning for cues of safety or threat — and an integrated social engagement system linking face muscles, voice prosody, middle-ear muscles, and the heart.

Over thirty years, the theory migrated far beyond Porges’s original psychophysiology audience. It is foundational in trauma therapy (Bessel van der Kolk’s Body Keeps the Score), in Deb Dana’s “Rhythm of Regulation” clinical framework, in trauma-informed yoga and Somatic Experiencing, and in autism-focused interventions like Porges’s own Safe and Sound Protocol, sold commercially through Unyte Health. It has also been absorbed into a vast wellness ecosystem: vagus-nerve “hacks,” supplements, devices, social-media educators, and coaching certifications.

The scientific status of the theory is contested at an unusual level of intensity. Paul Grossman’s 2023 paper in Biological Psychology argued that all five core premises of PVT are inconsistent with established comparative neuroanatomy. In early 2026, 39 international experts in vagal physiology and vertebrate evolution co-authored a consensus paper titled “Why the Polyvagal Theory is Untenable” in Clinical Neuropsychiatry, rebutting Porges’s 2025 status update. Porges issued a scholarly response calling the critique itself “untenable” and arguing the critics attack a reconstructed version of his theory. The Journal of Psychiatry Reform (2023) split the difference with an assessment titled “Polyvagal Approaches: scientifically questionable but useful in practice.”

For autistic adults dealing with burnout and overwhelm, the picture is layered. There is real, replicated evidence that autistic people show lower baseline parasympathetic HRV and lower RSA reactivity to social stress (Cheng 2020 meta-analysis: g = −0.52 baseline, g = −0.72 reactivity, n = 34 studies) — a finding that fits polyvagal predictions but doesn’t require PVT to explain. Thayer & Lane’s Neurovisceral Integration Model predicts identical correlations without PVT’s contested evolutionary claims. Allostatic load (McEwen) frames autistic burnout the way Raymaker’s 2020 empirical work actually uses. Predictive processing (Friston, Seth, Barrett) offers a third alternative. So the question isn’t “is polyvagal theory true” vs “is it false” — it’s “is it the best framework, given the data and the competition?” That’s what this deep dive is built to answer.

Meltdown, Shutdown & Autistic Burnout — Three States, Three Epistemic Statuses

Explicitly separates three states polyvagal popularizations tend to blur. Meltdown as sympathetic over-arousal has empirical support (HRV-reactivity data). Shutdown as dorsal vagal collapse is clinical heuristic; no in-vivo measurement. Autistic burnout has a growing empirical literature (Raymaker 2020 / AASPIRE) that explicitly uses an allostatic load framework, NOT polyvagal language. Cross-references existing project work on alexithymia + cumulative overwhelm.
