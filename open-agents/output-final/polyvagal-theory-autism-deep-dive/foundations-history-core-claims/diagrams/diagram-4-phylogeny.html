The Evolutionary Phylogeny Claim: Reptile → Mammal

The pivotal evolutionary claim of polyvagal theory: that the migration of cardioinhibitory neurons from the dorsal motor nucleus to the nucleus ambiguus, producing a myelinated cardiac vagus, is a uniquely mammalian innovation. This is the claim that the critique chapter will examine in depth.

Reptilian Pattern

Older, "ancestral" defense system
Dorsal Motor Nucleus (DMNX) Unmyelinated
Nucleus Ambiguus — myelinated cardiac vagus Absent per PVT
Primary defense Immobilization, freeze, severe bradycardia. The "ancient vagus" responding to threat by shutting down.
Cardiac control Slow, unmyelinated. Sufficient for low-energy ectothermic life. No social-engagement integration.
→
Ventral migration of cardioinhibitory neurons (claimed uniquely mammalian)

Mammalian Pattern

Newer "social" cardiac vagus retained alongside the older
Dorsal Motor Nucleus (DMNX) Unmyelinated
Nucleus Ambiguus (NA) — cardiac vagus Myelinated
What's new (per PVT) Fast, myelinated cardiac vagus integrated with face, voice, middle ear (cranial nerves V, VII, IX, X, XI). The "social engagement system."
Cardiac control Two systems: NA supplies fine, rapid heart-rate modulation (RSA, vagal brake) for social states; DMNX retained for subdiaphragmatic visceral control and dorsal-vagal defense.
What the critique chapter examines. Comparative neuroanatomy in fish, sharks, lungfish, and birds shows myelinated cardiac vagal fibers in non-mammals — contradicting the "uniquely mammalian" claim.
