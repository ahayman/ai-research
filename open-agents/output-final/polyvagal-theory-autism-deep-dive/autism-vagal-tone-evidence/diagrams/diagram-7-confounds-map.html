Confounds Map — What Else Could Be Driving the HRV Signal in Autism Studies

A reported "low HRV in autism" finding has many possible drivers. Studies that don't control for these confounds can misattribute their effects to autism per se.

Reported HRV difference in an autism study

The signal isn't always what it appears. Each spoke is a real, documented confound.

Medications LARGE

SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, stimulants, atypical antipsychotics all alter HRV. TILDA study (PMC4413849) found unmedicated MDD didn't differ from controls; medicated MDD did. Many autistic adults take 1+ of these.

Comorbid anxiety / depression LARGE

~50–70% lifetime prevalence in autism. Both independently lower HRV. Without stratification, autism HRV findings may largely reflect anxiety. Panju 2015 (PMC4676885) shows this within autism.

Respiratory rate LARGE

RSA varies with breathing pattern. Slow breathing (<9 bpm) shifts power out of HF band entirely. Studies that don't control or measure respiration can produce artifactual RSA differences.

Body position MEDIUM

Supine vs seated vs standing changes HRV substantially. Cross-study comparisons require matched protocols. Many studies don't fully report this.

Age MEDIUM

HRV declines with age. Mismatched age distributions between autism and control samples introduce bias. Adult autism studies pool ages 18–65; effects can be lost in the mean.

Sex / gender MEDIUM

HRV differs by sex. Autism research is heavily male-weighted (historical recruitment bias). Adult autistic women's autonomic profile under-characterized.

Aerobic fitness LARGE

Regularly exercising people have markedly higher HRV. Sedentary autistic samples vs active controls produce spurious "autism effect." Few studies stratify by fitness.

Caffeine, food, time of day MEDIUM

All affect baseline HRV. Standardized lab protocols try to control; consumer-wearable self-measurement does not.

Alexithymia VARIES

Bird & Cook's hypothesis: alexithymia (not autism per se) drives many interoceptive and emotion-recognition differences. Co-occurs in ~50% of autistic adults. Plausibly modifies the HRV picture.

Co-occurring dysautonomia / POTS VARIES

Many autistic adults also have POTS, hEDS, or related autonomic disorders. HRV findings in these individuals reflect the dysautonomia primarily, not autism per se.

Sleep / sleep debt MEDIUM

Sleep restriction lowers HRV measurably. Autistic adults have elevated rates of sleep disorders. Pre-test sleep status rarely reported in older studies.

Measurement device / protocol MEDIUM

ECG-gold-standard vs photoplethysmography vs consumer wearables produce different HRV numbers. Frequency-domain vs time-domain methods differ. Cross-study comparison non-trivial.

Confound magnitude:
LARGE Can swamp the autism signal
MEDIUM Material but usually partial
VARIES Depends on the individual
Practical implication for the individual reader: If you measure your own HRV on a wearable and see a low value, it could be your autism, your anxiety, your SSRI, your fitness level, your sleep last night, your breathing rate during measurement, or all six at once. A single HRV reading isn't a diagnosis. The group-level Cheng 2020 finding is real; individual interpretation requires ruling out the confounds.
