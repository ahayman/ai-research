Self-Assessment Decision Tree — Interpreting Your Own Low HRV Reading

If you've seen a low HRV value on a wearable, an HRV biofeedback app, or in lab feedback, here's a framework for sorting what it could mean. None of these steps require a clinician — but acting on the result might.

START · "My HRV reading is lower than I'd like (or lower than typical norms for my age and sex)."
↓
Question 1: Are you currently taking SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, stimulants, atypical antipsychotics, or beta-blockers?
Yes → Medication effects on HRV are well-documented (TILDA, multiple reviews). Your reading is partly — possibly substantially — medication-driven. Do not attribute to autism alone.
No → One major confound ruled out. Continue.
↓
Question 2: Do you have a current diagnosis of anxiety disorder, depression, PTSD, or significant chronic stress?
Yes → Anxiety and depression independently lower HRV. Comorbid prevalence in autism is ~50–70%. Your reading is partly the comorbidity, not necessarily autism per se. Treating the comorbidity may raise HRV.
No → Continue.
↓
Question 3: Was the measurement taken with controlled breathing rate (not slow, not anxious), consistent body position (seated/supine), and minimum 5 minutes resting?
Yes → Methodology confound minimized. Continue.
No → Wearable readings during walking, talking, or with low/variable respiration are methodologically noisy. Re-measure under controlled conditions before drawing conclusions.
↓
Question 4: Is your aerobic fitness level low or sedentary?
Yes → Aerobic fitness markedly raises HRV. Even modest activity (walking, cycling) over weeks can shift HRV upward. Fitness improvement is the highest-confidence HRV-raising intervention.
No → Continue.
↓
Question 5: Are you in chronic sleep debt, recovering from acute illness, in caffeine-withdrawal, or in the late luteal phase (if applicable)?
Yes → All these state factors transiently lower HRV. Re-measure over 1–2 weeks under stable conditions before treating the reading as your "baseline."
No → Continue.
↓

Interpretation after these checks

If most/all confounds are ruled out and your HRV is still consistently lower than age/sex norms: a real autonomic difference may be present. This is consistent with the Cheng 2020 group-level finding in autism, but also consistent with the alexithymia, allostatic-load, and prefrontal-regulatory frameworks (see Diagram 8).

What this is not: proof of a polyvagal-specific "ventral vagal social engagement system deficit." Low HRV is a non-specific marker, not a mechanism-specifying diagnosis.

What this might license trying: HRV biofeedback (Coulter 2024 mini-review), slow paced breathing (~6 bpm; strong independent evidence), aerobic conditioning, sleep optimization, sensory accommodation, and treating any comorbid anxiety / depression. (See Category 6: Interventions.)

What this does not license: Expensive vagus nerve devices without RCT evidence, brain-retraining cure-claims, or interpreting your reading as a fixed biological deficit. (See Category 8: Wellness Industry.)

Honest framing: Your HRV reading is one signal among many. It's worth attending to, especially if persistent and accompanied by lived symptoms (chronic exhaustion, poor stress recovery, sleep disturbance). It is not a diagnostic verdict. The interventions with the best evidence base (paced breathing, fitness, sleep, anxiety treatment) work regardless of which theoretical framework is correct — which is, on net, an encouraging fact for actionability.
