RSA Reactivity to Social Stress — What the Largest Effect Actually Looks Like

The g = −0.72 effect in Cheng 2020 (RSA reactivity to social stress) is the single largest autonomic finding in autism. Here's the typical pattern it describes — and the two interpretations the data leaves open.

What you're seeing: Neurotypical RSA drops substantially when a social stressor arrives (the "vagal brake releases") and recovers afterward. The autistic group shows a smaller drop — this is the g = −0.72 reactivity difference, the largest effect in the meta-analysis. Baseline values are also lower (left side), corresponding to the g = −0.59 baseline RSA difference.

Two interpretations the data leaves open

Interpretation A: Regulatory deficit

The autistic autonomic system is less able to engage vagal withdrawal in response to demand. The "vagal brake" doesn't release properly. This is the polyvagal-theory-aligned reading: a deficit of the ventral vagal social engagement system.

Interpretation B: Under-resourced regulation

Social demand is genuinely more taxing for autistic people (sensory load, decoding load, masking load). Baseline RSA is already lower because physiological reserves are partly engaged. The smaller "drop" reflects there being less room to drop further, not a broken brake.

The data alone doesn't distinguish A from B. Both are consistent with Cheng's findings. Interpretation A inherits PVT's contested anatomical-evolutionary scaffolding; Interpretation B fits the lived experience of autistic adults more directly and is consistent with NVI, allostatic load, and predictive-processing accounts. The autonomic numbers are real; the interpretation is theoretical.
