Cheng 2020 vs Kushki 2022 — A Real Empirical Disagreement

Both papers are peer-reviewed, well-conducted, and right within their own scope. The disagreement is real and partially resolved by recognizing the heterogeneity of autistic autonomic profiles.

Cheng, Huang & Huang 2020
Neurosci Biobehav Rev 118:463–471 · meta-analysis · PubMed 32818581
"Autistic individuals show significant autonomic differences vs neurotypical controls."
Design:
Pooled 34 published studies (mostly pediatric)
Baseline:
Lower parasympathetic HRV (g = −0.52) and RSA (g = −0.59), both highly significant
Reactivity:
Lower RSA reactivity to social stress, g = −0.72, largest effect
Conclusion:
Low HRV (especially RSA-reactivity) is a candidate biomarker for autism
Strength:
Large, multi-study, robust statistical power
Limit:
Heterogeneous methods across studies; no medication / anxiety stratification
Kushki et al. 2022
Frontiers Psychiatry 13:830234 · hypothesis-and-theory paper · PMC8964964
"ASD in children is not associated with abnormal autonomic function at the group level."
Design:
Single-lab pediatric data + targeted review
Baseline:
No significant baseline RSA differences observed
Reactivity:
No significant reactivity differences to most tasks
Subgroup:
Small subgroup (~10–15%) with outlier RSA values, possibly anxiety-linked
Conclusion:
Group-level "autistic ANS dysfunction" claim is overstated
Strength:
Internally consistent methodology, large single-lab dataset
Limit:
Limited generalizability; single recruitment context

What reconciles them?

1. Heterogeneity is real. Hyper-arousal and hypo-arousal autistic subgroups have distinct autonomic profiles. They cancel partially at the group mean.
2. Study composition matters. Cheng pooled across many labs and recruitment streams. Kushki's pool was narrower and may be hypo-arousal-weighted.
3. Statistical power. Cheng's pooled n is much larger than any single-lab dataset, detecting smaller real effects Kushki may have missed.
4. Comorbidity confound. Comorbid anxiety stratifies the autonomic profile within autism (Panju 2015, PMC4676885). Both papers are partially measuring this.
Honest synthesis: At the population level, autistic samples show real autonomic differences from controls — especially in reactivity to social stress. At the individual level, autistic autonomic profiles are highly heterogeneous, with some subgroups very different from controls and others overlapping completely. Neither "all autistic people have low HRV" nor "autism isn't about autonomic function" captures the full picture. Both Cheng and Kushki are partially right.
