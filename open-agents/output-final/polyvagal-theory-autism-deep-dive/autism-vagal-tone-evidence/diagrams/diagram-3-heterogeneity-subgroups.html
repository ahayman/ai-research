Heterogeneity — Hyper-Arousal and Hypo-Arousal Autistic Subgroups

"Autistic autonomic profile" isn't a single thing. At least two distinct subgroups exist with opposite physiological patterns, partly explaining why different studies report different group-level findings.

Autonomic profile axis

Hypo-arousal autistic subgroup

  • Blunted physiological reactivity
  • Sometimes within-normal-range HRV
  • Reduced electrodermal activity
  • Different sensory profile
  • May overlap typical-control range

Neurotypical controls

  • Typical baseline parasympathetic HRV
  • Robust RSA withdrawal during demand
  • Reactivity recovers to baseline
  • Wide normal range

Hyper-arousal autistic subgroup

  • Reduced parasympathetic vagal tone
  • Elevated sympathetic activity
  • Often comorbid anxiety
  • More severe autistic symptomatology
  • Lower global functioning scores

Subgroup typology synthesized from Panju et al. 2015 (PMC4676885, Molecular Autism), Lydon et al. 2016 systematic review on autonomic atypicality in autism, and Patriquin et al. body of work. No field-standard subgroup taxonomy has emerged, but the existence of distinct profiles is consistent across multiple labs.

Why this matters: Cheng 2020 reports a pooled autism mean to the left of the control mean (lower HRV) — the red dashed line. Kushki 2022 reports their single-lab data largely overlapping controls — consistent with a hypo-arousal-weighted sample. The two studies don't contradict each other so much as describe different mixtures of the same underlying subgroup distribution. The same autistic person can be high or low on either subgroup axis; "the autistic autonomic profile" is a population statement, not an individual prediction.
