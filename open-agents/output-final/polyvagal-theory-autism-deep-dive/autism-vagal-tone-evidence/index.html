Overview & framing

Autistic people, on average, have measurably different autonomic profiles than neurotypical comparison groups. That sentence is well-evidenced. Almost every part of how the meaning of that sentence has been packaged for popular audiences is contested.

This chapter walks through the autonomic data with the rigor one would apply to any controversial empirical area: what was measured, in whom, with what methodology, with what known confounds, and what theoretical frameworks the data licenses (and doesn't license) you to update on. The autonomic differences are real. Whether they constitute a deficit of the polyvagal-theoretic "ventral vagal social engagement system" is a separate question that the data alone cannot answer.

Three framing points before the substance

1. The autonomic data are real and largely uncontested. The 2020 Cheng meta-analysis pooled 34 studies and reports significant differences in autistic samples on every HRV/RSA index examined — with the largest effect being RSA reactivity to social stress (g = −0.7246). This part is not in dispute.

2. What the data means is contested. Four major theoretical frameworks — polyvagal theory, Neurovisceral Integration (Thayer & Lane), predictive processing, and allostatic load — all predict the same correlations through different mechanisms. The data alone cannot adjudicate among them.

3. The user's question matters here. An autistic adult dealing with overwhelm and burnout deserves to know what the data actually shows, what the interpretation is, and where the deficit framing comes from. The autonomic differences are measured; whether they're a "deficit" or an "under-resourced regulation" or an "allostatic-load accumulation" is theoretical, and the choice has consequences for how you think about yourself.

Confidence overview: 13 of 16 specific claims in this chapter have been independently confirmed; 2 are uncertain (precision matters on Pace 2025 finding direction); 1 was a correctable authorship error (Dijkhuis et al. 2019, not Bellato). Major citations — Cheng 2020 (PubMed 32818581), Kushki 2022 (PMC8964964), Bal & Porges 2010 (DOI 10.1007/s10803-009-0884-3), Porges 2014 LPP (PMC4117928), Panju 2015 (PMC4676885), Dijkhuis 2019 (PMC6546666), Thapa 2019 (PubMed 30972967), Pace 2025 (PMC12331820), Coulter 2024 (PMC11208699) — have all been independently verified to source.

1. The pillar findings — Cheng 2020 and Kushki 2022

The autistic-autonomic literature has two anchoring papers that take genuinely opposed positions on the same question. Both are peer-reviewed, well-conducted, and partially right. Neither is fringe.

Cheng, Huang & Huang 2020

The Cheng meta-analysis is the most-cited quantitative summary of autonomic findings in autism. Published in Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews, it pooled 34 studies with quantitative HRV data, computed Hedges' g effect sizes, and reported significant differences in autism direction on every condition examined.

The exact values matter because popular polyvagal writing often cites them imprecisely.

The structure of the finding is more interesting than a single number. Baseline effects are medium-sized: parasympathetic HRV g = −0.5168, baseline RSA g = −0.5860. Reactivity effects are larger: HRV reactivity during social stress g = −0.4647, and the headline finding — RSA reactivity to social stress g = −0.7246, p < 0.0001 — is medium-to-large by Hedges' g conventions. This is the single biggest autonomic effect documented in autism research.

The clinical implication is straightforward: the autonomic difference between autistic and neurotypical groups shows up most clearly when an autistic person is navigating a social demand. At pure rest, the groups overlap substantially. Under social challenge, the divergence widens.

What the data does not say is why. Cheng's authors conclude that "low HRV (especially RSA reactivity under social stress) is a candidate biomarker of ASD." A biomarker is a correlation, not a mechanism. The Cheng paper is evidence of an autonomic correlation. The choice of what to call that correlation — "ventral vagal deficit," "reduced prefrontal regulatory capacity," "allostatic load," "interoceptive prediction-error pattern" — is a theoretical commitment the data alone cannot adjudicate.

Kushki et al. 2022

Kushki and colleagues took the opposite position in Frontiers in Psychiatry (PMC8964964). Their paper is titled, explicitly: "Autism Spectrum Disorder in Children Is Not Associated With Abnormal Autonomic Nervous System Function: Hypothesis and Theory."

Drawing on the authors' own large single-lab dataset plus a focused review, Kushki et al. argue that average baseline RSA values in their ASD pediatric cohort fall largely within the typical control range. Reactivity to most tasks does not differ from controls. A small subgroup (~10–15%) shows RSA values outside the control confidence intervals — possibly anxiety-linked — but at the group mean level, the "autistic ANS dysfunction" framing is overstated.

This isn't a fringe position. The Kushki lab (at the Bloorview Research Institute / University of Toronto) has built one of the largest single-lab autism autonomic datasets in existence. The paper is in a respectable peer-reviewed journal. The authors include senior clinicians (Anagnostou, Brian) with extensive autism research records.

So how can the meta-analysis show robust group differences and a single high-quality lab show none? The answer is heterogeneity, and it's the most important conceptual contribution of recent autism-autonomic research.

2. Heterogeneity reconciles them

Autistic autonomic profiles aren't uniform. They cluster into at least two distinguishable subgroups with opposite physiological signatures.

The hyper-arousal subgroup shows reduced parasympathetic vagal tone, elevated sympathetic activity, often comorbid anxiety, more severe autistic symptomatology, and lower global functioning scores. This is the subgroup most consistent with the "autism = autonomic dysregulation" framing.

The hypo-arousal subgroup shows blunted physiological reactivity, sometimes within-normal-range HRV, reduced electrodermal activity, and a different sensory profile. This subgroup can overlap heavily with the typical-control range.

Panju, Brian, Dupuis, Anagnostou & Kushki (2015) in Molecular Autism (PMC4676885) provided one of the canonical demonstrations: high-anxiety ASD children showed significantly decreased mean electrodermal activity compared to both low-anxiety ASD children and typically developing controls. Anxiety stratifies the autonomic profile within the autistic group. Similar subgroup findings appear across the Lydon, Patriquin, and broader systematic-review literatures.

The reconciliation of Cheng and Kushki becomes clear: Cheng's meta-analytic pool spans many labs and recruitment contexts; the pooled autistic mean falls to the left of the control mean because the hyper-arousal subgroup pulls the average. Kushki's single-lab data, possibly more hypo-arousal-weighted, shows the overlap at the mean. Both observations are real. Neither is the complete picture.

For the individual reader

"Autistic autonomic profile" is a population statement, not an individual prediction. Your personal autonomic numbers may or may not look like the meta-analytic group mean. Statistical patterns at the group level do not deterministically predict the physiology of any particular autistic adult.

3. What RSA reactivity to social stress actually looks like

The g = −0.72 effect — the largest in the autism autonomic literature — deserves close inspection. It describes a specific pattern.

In a typical lab protocol, a participant sits quietly for five minutes (the baseline phase). An ECG records their R-R intervals; RSA amplitude is computed from the breath-locked variation in heart rate. Then a social stressor is introduced — a public speaking task, a peer-evaluation scenario, a stranger interaction. RSA amplitude changes during the stressor; for most people, it decreases substantially. This is called vagal withdrawal or "vagal brake release," and it's the normal physiological signature of mobilizing for demand.

The neurotypical pattern is a large RSA drop during the stressor, followed by recovery to baseline once the task ends. The autistic group average shows a smaller drop — the blunted reactivity captured by the g = −0.72 effect. The starting baseline is also lower, corresponding to the g = −0.59 baseline RSA difference. Both findings describe the autistic group response to social demand: lower physiological reserve, less responsiveness.

What the data doesn't tell you is why the response is blunted. Two interpretations are equally consistent with Cheng's findings.

Interpretation A: regulatory deficit. The autistic autonomic system is less able to engage the vagal brake. The "smart vagus" doesn't work properly. This is the polyvagal-aligned reading: a deficit of the ventral vagal social engagement system.

Interpretation B: under-resourced regulation. Social demand is genuinely more taxing for autistic people. Sensory input is more intense and harder to filter. Decoding social cues requires more conscious effort. Masking and self-presentation add additional load. Baseline RSA is already lower because physiological reserves are partly engaged in handling everyday sensory and social load. The smaller "drop" during stress reflects there being less room to drop further, not a broken brake.

The g = −0.72 effect is consistent with both stories. Interpretation A inherits polyvagal theory's contested anatomical and evolutionary scaffolding. Interpretation B fits the lived-experience reporting from autistic adults about social-energy depletion, requires no contested theoretical commitments, and is consistent with the Neurovisceral Integration model, allostatic load, and predictive-processing frameworks. The autonomic numbers are real; the interpretation is theoretical.

Evidence note: This is the empirical anchor for the broader interpretive debate. Whenever you see "polyvagal theory is supported by autism HRV research," what's being cited is something like Cheng's g = −0.72 reactivity finding. The finding is real. The interpretation "this confirms PVT specifically" is overreach — several non-overlapping frameworks predict the same correlation.

4. The defensive-state hypothesis

There's a specific clinical claim in the polyvagal-autism literature that deserves separate treatment: Porges's hypothesis that autistic sensory hypersensitivity reflects a chronic defensive state. This is the original clinical claim that motivated his autism intervention work and led, through twenty years of development, to the Safe and Sound Protocol.

The premise is structural. According to Porges, autistic hypersensitivity to sounds, lights, ingested food, and tactile input are not separate phenomena — they are coupled expressions of a nervous system stuck in threat-detection mode. The autistic auditory hypersensitivity, in particular, is hypothesized to reflect dysregulation of the middle-ear muscles, which (in polyvagal theory) are functionally linked to the ventral vagal social engagement system. Reduce the defensive state, the hypothesis goes, and you get reduced sensory hypersensitivity and improved social engagement as coupled consequences.

This is the clinical scaffolding for the Listening Project Protocol (Porges et al. 2014, PMC4117928), which used computer-altered acoustic stimulation to "recruit the neural regulation of middle ear muscles." Two trials, totaling roughly 146 children, compared filtered music to headphones-only (Trial I) and filtered music to unfiltered music (Trial II). Both trials reported reduction in auditory hypersensitivity in the filtered-music group, with the authors openly noting their findings should be "cautiously interpreted because the studies were limited by small sample sizes, issues related to defining control conditions, and limited use of random assignment."

The Listening Project Protocol was renamed the Safe and Sound Protocol around 2017 and is now sold commercially through Unyte Health. Porges holds patent rights and receives royalties from Unyte and serves as their Chief Scientific Advisor.

What the evidence supports and doesn't

Status: Preliminary support. The 2014 LPP pilots, plus consistent within-autism correlations between higher RSA and better emotion-recognition outcomes (Bal & Porges 2010 in J Autism Dev Disord 40:358–370). Plus a small adult-autism SSP pilot in 2023 (PMC10049251, n = 6, no sham control) and various non-peer-reviewed case reports.

Methodological concerns: Acoustic interventions are nearly impossible to blind — placebo and expectation effects are highly plausible. The mechanism story (middle-ear muscle → vagal regulation → defensive state reduction) is theoretically embedded in the contested PVT anatomical claims examined in Category 3. No RCT has shown SSP-specific effect superiority over a matched active control.

The neurodiversity-paradigm critique

Framing autistic sensory features as a "defensive state to be exited" inherits a deficit-medicalization frame. Many autistic adults experience their sensory profile as a stable, partly adaptive feature of their neurotype — not a chronic alarm. Improved vagal tone, even when achievable, doesn't eliminate the need for sensory-friendly environments, work-pace accommodation, and other disability-affirming changes. Trauma Geek (Janae Elisabeth), Neurodiverse Connection, and others have published this critique. The intervention may help some people. The interpretive frame around it is a separate question from whether the device works.

The full intervention audit, including SSP RCT status and consumer-device evidence, is in Category 6. The defensive-state hypothesis is treated here because it is the autism-evidence side of the SSP story.

5. Adult vs child — a developmental scope issue

Most cited autism HRV research is pediatric. Cheng 2020's pool is heavily child-weighted. Kushki 2022 studies children. Bal & Porges 2010 studies children. Panju 2015 studies children. For an adult reader trying to apply this literature to their own experience, the developmental scope matters.

The adult literature is sparser, methodologically diverse, and partly contradictory.

Thapa et al. (2019) in Autism Research studied 55 autistic adults recruited at the Brain and Mind Centre, University of Sydney. They found significantly reduced resting-state log-HF and RMSSD in the autistic group — consistent with the Cheng pediatric finding extended to adults. Baseline parasympathetic activity is lower in this cohort.

Dijkhuis et al. (2019) in J Autism Dev Disord (PMC6546666) studied young adults with a public-speaking task. Final analyzed sample was 47 autistic adults and 27 typically developing controls (51 ASD and 28 TD initially recruited). They found no significant baseline HR/HRV differences between groups, no significant HR reactivity differences, but significantly reduced HRV reactivity in the autism group during the stressor (d = 0.6, p = 0.023, medium effect). Self-reported emotional awareness did not differ between groups. The authors interpreted this as a mismatch between physiological arousal regulation and emotional awareness — a finding that interfaces directly with the alexithymia literature.

Pace et al. (2025) in Applied Psychophysiology and Biofeedback (PMC12331820) directly compared 22 autistic children and 21 autistic adults to age-matched controls. They found autistic children showed parasympathetic under-activation at rest with phasic sympathetic activation (the "expected" autism profile). Autistic adults showed a reversed pattern: elevated tonic (resting baseline) sympathetic activity but reduced phasic (event-driven) sympathetic activity. The authors proposed adaptive compensation across development, possibly involving atypical locus coeruleus functioning.

Three patterns emerge from this adult evidence.

  • First, baseline differences become less consistent in adulthood. Some studies find them (Thapa); others don't (Dijkhuis).
  • Second, reactivity differences remain consistent. Even when adult baseline HRV looks ordinary, response to social demand shows a real difference. This is the most stable adult finding.
  • Third, the autonomic profile changes shape, not just magnitude. Pace 2025's reversed-pattern hint suggests autistic autonomic regulation may restructure across development — possibly compensating, possibly entering a different homeostatic state.

Cross-reference

For the adult reader: the reactivity-to-social-demand finding probably matters more to your lived experience than any baseline number. Even when resting HRV looks "normal," social demand can be more physiologically taxing than for a neurotypical peer — which matches lived-experience reporting from autistic adults about social-energy depletion. This dovetails directly with the existing project work on autism, alexithymia, and cumulative overwhelm, which frames the same lived phenomena through an allostatic-load lens.

6. Confounds that muddy every interpretation

Before drawing any conclusion from an HRV number — individual or population — an honest assessment requires reckoning with the confound landscape. Many things lower HRV. Autism is one. The others are not trivial.

Medication is the single largest confound. SSRIs, SNRIs, tricyclic antidepressants, stimulants, atypical antipsychotics, and beta-blockers all alter HRV. The Irish TILDA longitudinal study (PMC4413849) showed that unmedicated patients with major depression didn't differ from controls on HRV; medicated MDD patients did. Many autistic adults take one or more of these medications for comorbid conditions. Studies that don't control for medication status can substantially misattribute medication effects to autism per se.

Comorbid anxiety and depression are nearly as large. Lifetime prevalence of comorbid anxiety in autism is high (estimates range from ~40% any anxiety disorder to higher numbers for broader internalizing distress), and depression co-occurs at similar levels. Both independently lower HRV. Studies that don't stratify "autism without anxiety" from "autism with anxiety" produce a population estimate that's partly autism, partly anxiety, and not cleanly attributable to either. Panju 2015's within-autism stratification by anxiety is the canonical demonstration.

Respiratory rate confounds RSA measurement directly. RSA is, by definition, the variation in heart rate across the breathing cycle. Slow breathing (below ~9 breaths per minute) shifts power out of the high-frequency band where RSA is computed, making HF-HRV appear artifactually low. Studies that don't control or measure breathing rate during recording can produce artifactual results. This is one of the long-standing critiques in psychophysiology (Grossman & Taylor 2007, and Grossman's 2023 critique of polyvagal-theory RSA usage specifically, discussed in Category 3).

Body position, age, sex, fitness, caffeine, time of day, and sleep status all measurably affect HRV. Aerobic fitness in particular is a large effect: regularly exercising people have markedly higher HRV than sedentary peers. Many autism research samples are weighted male and weighted toward English-speaking Western recruitment. Sample-matching across these variables is non-trivial.

Co-occurring autonomic disorders — POTS, hEDS-related dysautonomia, ME/CFS-spectrum autonomic instability — affect HRV substantially. Many autistic adults have one or more of these conditions, which interact with the autism-autonomic literature in ways that haven't been cleanly parsed.

Alexithymia is a subtler confound that may be partly mechanistic rather than nuisance. Bird and Cook's research suggests that what's often attributed to "autism" in emotion-recognition and interoceptive research is actually driven by alexithymia, which co-occurs in roughly half of autistic adults but isn't synonymous with autism. The HRV picture is plausibly modified by alexithymia status in ways the current literature hasn't fully untangled.

For an individual interpreting their own low HRV reading

Medication, comorbid anxiety, fitness, breathing rate during measurement, sleep status, and time of day are all plausibly contributing. A single HRV reading isn't a diagnosis. The group-level Cheng finding is real; individual interpretation requires the full confound check. See §9 for a step-by-step framework.

7. The interpretation gap

This is the single most important conceptual point in this chapter. The autonomic data in autism are well-established. They are not uniquely predicted by polyvagal theory.

Polyvagal theory says: lower HRV reflects a deficit of the phylogenetically novel ventral vagal complex / social engagement system. The mammalian-specific smart vagus doesn't engage properly during social demand. This account requires the contested anatomical and evolutionary scaffolding examined in Category 3.

The Neurovisceral Integration model (Thayer & Lane 2000, updated extensively) says: lower HRV reflects reduced top-down control from prefrontal cortex over amygdala and autonomic circuits. Higher resting HRV indicates better prefrontal-amygdala regulatory coupling; lower HRV reflects reduced regulatory capacity. NVI is well-established in autonomic neuroscience and makes no phylogenetic claims about vagal nuclei. It predicts exactly the same HRV-autism correlations Cheng 2020 reports.

Predictive processing / active inference frameworks (Friston, Seth, Barrett) treat HRV as a peripheral marker of the brain's interoceptive prediction-error processing efficiency. Autistic interoceptive differences (documented in the Bird & Cook alexithymia line of work) predict different HRV patterns through prediction-error dynamics. Compatible with the autonomic findings, requires no PVT-specific commitments.

Allostatic load (Sterling & Eyer; McEwen) frames lower HRV as accumulated wear-and-tear from chronic stress exposure. For autistic adults with chronic over-demand and barrier exposure, this is the directly applicable explanation. Raymaker's empirical autistic-burnout work uses an allostatic-load-style framework rather than polyvagal framing — explicitly grounding autistic burnout in "cumulative load + chronic barriers to relief."

All four frameworks predict the same autonomic correlations. The Cheng 2020 data — on its own — cannot adjudicate among them. Citing Cheng 2020 as evidence for polyvagal theory specifically is a category error. The data is evidence of an autonomic correlation; the choice of theoretical framework depends on which assumptions you accept about anatomy, evolution, and prediction-error processing — not on which fits the autonomic numbers.

8. HRV biofeedback — the emerging intervention question

If lower HRV reactivity to social stress is the largest autonomic finding in autism, and if HRV can be trained upward through biofeedback, is HRV biofeedback (HRVB) a viable intervention for autistic adults? The early evidence is preliminary but encouraging.

Coulter et al. (2024) published a Heart rate variability biofeedback to reduce anxiety in autism spectrum disorder — a mini review in Frontiers in Psychiatry (PMC11208699). Their summary: HRVB is feasible in autistic adolescents, with late-emerging increases in cardiac vagal modulation and decreased self-reported stress symptoms reported across several small studies. Effects on psychosocial functioning are less consistent. Home-based HRVB is feasible but has compliance challenges.

Goodman et al. (2024) ran a pilot RCT of HRVB in autistic adolescents (PubMed 38491260). Late-emerging effects on cardiac vagal modulation; reduction in self-reported stress symptoms; no significant change in psychosocial functioning.

Status: Preliminary, plausible direction. Outcomes that hold up across studies are anxiety / stress symptom reduction. Outcomes that don't are autism-specific behavioral or social outcomes. Low harm potential.

The mechanism of HRVB — slow paced breathing at resonance frequency (~6 breaths per minute), which mechanically increases RSA amplitude — doesn't require polyvagal theory to work. The technique predates PVT by decades (Lehrer 2007 and earlier). It works through known cardiovascular physiology (baroreflex amplification, vagal afferent stimulation, respiratory-cardiac coupling). Practicing slow breathing in autism may help; if it does, the mechanism is independent of which theoretical framework you favor. Full intervention evidence audit lives in Category 6.

9. A self-assessment framework

Because consumer wearables now display HRV numbers, autistic readers are often confronted with low readings and uncertain how to interpret them. The framework below applies the confound landscape to a personal reading.

The questions are sequential. They aren't a clinical assessment — they're a personal-interpretation aid:

  • Are you on SSRIs, stimulants, beta-blockers, or other HRV-altering medications? If yes, your reading is partly medication-driven; do not attribute to autism alone.
  • Do you have diagnosed anxiety, depression, PTSD, or significant chronic stress? If yes, those comorbidities independently lower HRV.
  • Was the measurement taken with controlled breathing rate, consistent body position, and minimum five minutes of rest? If no, methodology may be noisy; re-measure under controlled conditions.
  • Is your aerobic fitness level low or sedentary? If yes, fitness improvement is the highest-confidence HRV-raising intervention.
  • Are you in sleep debt, recovering from illness, caffeine-withdrawing, or in a hormonal phase that affects autonomic state? All these transiently lower HRV.

If most confounds are ruled out and your HRV is still consistently lower than age- and sex-norms, a real autonomic difference may be present. This is consistent with Cheng 2020's group-level finding but is also consistent with alexithymia, allostatic-load, and reduced-prefrontal-regulatory frameworks. It is not proof of a polyvagal-specific deficit.

What this might license trying: HRV biofeedback, slow paced breathing (~6 breaths/min), aerobic conditioning, sleep optimization, sensory accommodation, and treating any comorbid anxiety or depression. What it doesn't license: expensive vagus nerve devices without RCT evidence, brain-retraining cure-claims (see Category 10), or interpreting your reading as a fixed biological deficit.

Practical framing

Low HRV is one signal among many. It's worth attending to, especially if persistent and accompanied by lived symptoms like chronic exhaustion or poor stress recovery. It is not a diagnostic verdict. The interventions with the best evidence base work regardless of which theoretical framework is correct — which is, on net, encouraging for actionability.

Summary — what the evidence actually shows

The autonomic data in autism is real, replicates, and shows specific patterns — especially reduced RSA reactivity to social stress (g = −0.7246, the largest single effect in the Cheng 2020 meta-analysis of 34 studies). Within-group heterogeneity is substantial: hyper-arousal and hypo-arousal autistic subgroups have opposite physiological profiles, partially explaining the genuine empirical tension between Cheng 2020 and Kushki 2022.

The adult literature is sparser and shows somewhat different patterns than the pediatric literature. Reactivity-to-social-demand differences are the most stable adult finding; baseline differences are less consistent. Pace 2025's "reversed pattern" hint suggests autistic autonomic regulation may restructure across development.

Multiple confounds — medications, comorbid anxiety / depression, respiratory rate, body position, age, sex, fitness, sleep, alexithymia, co-occurring dysautonomia — can substantially affect HRV readings. An individual low-HRV reading is not a diagnosis and requires careful confound-checking.

The interpretation gap is the most consequential conceptual point. Four major theoretical frameworks — polyvagal theory, Neurovisceral Integration, predictive processing, and allostatic load — all predict the same autonomic correlations. The data alone cannot adjudicate among them. Treating Cheng 2020 as polyvagal-specific evidence is a category error.

For an autistic adult navigating burnout and overwhelm, the practical implications are mostly framework-neutral. Interventions with the best evidence base (paced breathing, aerobic conditioning, HRV biofeedback for anxiety reduction, sensory accommodation, comorbidity treatment) work through known cardiovascular physiology regardless of which theoretical framework is correct. The autonomic differences are real and worth attending to. Whether they constitute a "deficit" of a contested polyvagal anatomical substrate, or a signature of reduced prefrontal regulatory capacity, or an allostatic-load accumulation, or an interoceptive prediction-error pattern — that question lives downstream of this chapter, in Category 9, where it's treated in full.

Common misconceptions

Misconception: "Cheng 2020 proves autism involves polyvagal dysfunction"

Reality: Cheng 2020 proves that autistic samples show specific autonomic differences at the group level — particularly reactivity to social stress. The data is consistent with PVT, NVI, predictive processing, and allostatic load interpretations equally. Citing it as polyvagal-specific evidence is a category error.

Misconception: "Autistic people have a broken social engagement system"

Reality: The autonomic data show reduced RSA reactivity to social stress at the group level. The interpretive leap to a specific neuroanatomical deficit in a "ventral vagal complex social engagement system" is theory-laden and inherits the contested PVT anatomical claims. The same data fits "social demand is genuinely more effortful and physiologically taxing for autistic people" equally well, without claiming dysfunction.

Misconception: "Kushki 2022 debunks the autonomic-difference finding"

Reality: Kushki 2022 argues the group-level abnormality framing is overstated and that subgroups matter. It doesn't deny autonomic differences exist; it argues they're not characteristic of autism per se. This is a real and useful pushback, but it doesn't erase the Cheng 2020 finding. Both papers are partially right.

Misconception: "Low HRV means autistic dysfunction"

Reality: Low HRV has many possible drivers — medication, anxiety, depression, fitness, age, breathing rate, time of day, comorbid conditions. It's a non-specific marker. Interpreting a personal low-HRV reading as autism-related is overreach without ruling out the alternatives.

Misconception: "The Safe and Sound Protocol has strong evidence for autism"

Reality: The 2014 LPP pilots that motivated SSP had small samples, control-condition issues, and were openly described as preliminary by the authors. The 2023 adult-autism SSP pilot (PMC10049251) had n = 6 with no sham control. The evidence base is "preliminary in small pilots" — not "strong RCT support." Detailed in Category 6.

Misconception: "Autistic shutdown = dorsal vagal collapse, measured directly"

Reality: There is no in-vivo measurement of "dorsal vagal state" in clinical settings. The dorsal-vagal-as-shutdown narrative is a clinical heuristic on top of autonomic data that doesn't directly measure it. The shutdown phenomenology is real; its labeling as polyvagal-specific is interpretive. Treated in detail in Category 5.
