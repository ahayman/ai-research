The asymmetry pattern. Academic publication conventions require COI disclosure. Consumer marketing has weaker norms. This is not unique to polyvagal theory — it is a structural feature of how science-based products are marketed. But it is the specific feature the user should be aware of: the same person who carefully discloses commercial relationships in a peer-reviewed paper may not include the same disclosure in a podcast appearance promoting the product. Absence of disclosure does not mean the source is dishonest; it means the consumer-facing communication is operating under different norms than the academic communication, and the user has to do the integration themselves.
Academic publications and consumer-facing materials operate under different disclosure norms. The pattern is: most key actors disclose commercial interests in their academic and clinical publications (where required), and disclose less consistently in podcasts, social media, and product marketing. This is a structural feature of how science-based products are marketed, not a unique flaw of polyvagal theory; but it is the specific feature the user should expect to encounter and check for.
