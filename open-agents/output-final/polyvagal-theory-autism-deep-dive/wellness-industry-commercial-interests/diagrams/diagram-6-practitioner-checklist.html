The Practitioner-Evaluation Checklist

Eight reusable questions to apply to any polyvagal-branded practitioner, product, or claim — print-friendly

A reusable analytical tool. Apply to a coach, therapist, device, supplement, or course. Pass on most questions: take the source seriously. Fail on most questions: look elsewhere.
1
Mechanism: Is the claim a mechanism or an experience?
Good signPractitioner makes experience claims ("clients often feel calmer after slow breathing") with hedging around mechanism.
ConcerningSpecific mechanism claims ("this activates the ventral vagal complex and rewires the social engagement system") without evidence.
2
Citations: Are specific peer-reviewed studies cited?
Good signNames specific studies, authors, journals. Cited studies actually about what is claimed.
ConcerningOnly testimonials, case studies, or vague "research shows" without specifics.
3
FDA status (for devices): Cleared, and for what indication?
Good signSpecific 510(k) clearance cited with explicit indication scope (e.g., gammaCore for cluster headache).
Concerning"FDA-registered" (just a manufacturer registration), "FCC-certified" (not FDA), or "general wellness device" framing presented as clinical efficacy.
4
Scope of practice: Is the practitioner working within their license/credential?
Good signLicensed clinician (therapist, MD, RN, OT, SLP) working within license, OR coach being clear they don't diagnose / treat.
ConcerningNon-licensed coach offering trauma sessions, diagnosing autonomic states, "treating" anxiety/PTSD, or "rewiring" clients.
5
Register: Is "polyvagal-informed" an orientation, or presented as established biological mechanism?
Good sign"I use polyvagal language because clients find it useful for thinking about autonomic experience." Treats it as orientation.
ConcerningTreats the framework as settled biology. No awareness of the 2023–2026 academic controversy.
6
Conflict of interest: Are financial interests disclosed?
Good signPractitioner discloses if they sell products / supplements / courses, or have referral arrangements.
ConcerningRecommends a specific product, course, or device with no disclosure. Affiliate links not flagged. Selling a product they then recommend without acknowledgment.
7
Falsifiability: Could the claim ever be wrong?
Good signCan state conditions under which their claim would be falsified. Acknowledges what evidence would change their mind.
Concerning"You're in dorsal vagal because you can't feel ventral" — circular and unfalsifiable. No evidence could prove it wrong.
8
Engagement with uncertainty: Do they acknowledge the 2023–2026 controversy?
Good signAware of Grossman 2023 / Grossman et al. 2026 critique. Treats polyvagal theory as contested-but-clinically-useful or holds calibrated view.
ConcerningTreats polyvagal theory as settled science. No awareness or dismisses critique as irrelevant.
How to read the answers: Mostly "good signs" doesn't mean the practitioner is automatically effective — therapy is hard to evaluate from credentials alone. Mostly "concerning" answers is grounds to look elsewhere. Two or three concerning answers in combination is a stronger signal than any single one alone.
A reusable tool. Apply to any polyvagal-branded practitioner, course, product, or claim. The checklist is calibrated against the specific overreach patterns documented across the wellness-industry register, but the questions themselves generalize to any wellness or psychiatric claim.
