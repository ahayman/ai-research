Overview & verdict

A theory's epistemic status isn't just about whether its own predictions fit some data. It's also about whether other theories fit the same data — or better. If a competing framework explains the same observations with a less contested mechanism, the original theory's privileged status erodes. The autistic HRV data, the meltdown/shutdown phenomenology, the burnout literature, the interoception findings — these are the data points you care about. The question this chapter asks is: are these data points uniquely explained by polyvagal theory, or are they equally well (or better) explained by frameworks that don't require PVT's contested anatomical and evolutionary claims?

The honest comparative answer is that polyvagal theory is not winning this contest. There are at least five mainstream contemporary frameworks that explain the autonomic, interoceptive, and emotion-regulation data you care about, without invoking PVT's three-tier vagal hierarchy, mammalian-only myelinated vagus, dorsal-vagal-as-shutdown, or neuroception-as-special-mechanism. Two of those frameworks (neurovisceral integration and predictive processing) make essentially identical HRV predictions to PVT and are the dominant academic frameworks in autonomic and computational neuroscience respectively. A third (allostasis) is the framework the empirical autistic-burnout literature actually uses — Raymaker 2020's definition of autistic burnout maps directly onto McEwen's allostatic-load construct. A fourth (Lisa Feldman Barrett's theory of constructed emotion) directly contradicts PVT's architectural premise. A fifth (Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis) captures PVT's "body feels it first" intuition without the contested phylogenetic baggage.

Key takeaways

  • Polyvagal Theory has no unique explanatory advantage on the autism HRV data, the autistic burnout phenomenology, autistic emotion regulation, or autistic sensory hypersensitivity.
  • For autistic burnout specifically, allostasis decisively wins — it's the framework Raymaker 2020 / AASPIRE autistic-burnout research uses.
  • For HRV and emotion regulation, NVI and predictive processing dominate the academic neuroscience literature with higher citation impact and less contested mechanisms.
  • PVT survives as a useful clinical heuristic — the "ladder" metaphor genuinely helps some people name and track their states — but not as a privileged scientific framework.
  • The honest assessment: PVT loses the comparative contest. It's not the right framework when its competitors explain the same data more parsimoniously.

You're autistic and have spent years navigating the polyvagal-saturated wellness and therapy ecosystem. You've encountered PVT framing everywhere: in trauma-informed therapy, in autistic burnout literature, in trauma-sensitive yoga, in social-media nervous-system content, in commercial interventions like the Safe and Sound Protocol. The autistic HRV data stops being evidence for PVT (because it's equally well predicted by NVI and predictive processing). Model 1: Neurovisceral Integration (NVI) — Thayer & Lane

Among the alternatives, neurovisceral integration is the closest direct competitor to PVT. It addresses the same core data — heart-rate variability, autonomic regulation, the brain-heart connection — but does it with established neuroanatomy and without phylogenetic claims that have been empirically contested.

Mechanism

Thayer and Lane's NVI model proposes a hierarchical neural network that integrates the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC), the amygdala, and the brainstem vagal nuclei into a continuous regulatory circuit. The architecture is straightforward: the vmPFC exerts tonic inhibition on the amygdala. The amygdala, when uninhibited, drives autonomic arousal via its projections to brainstem nuclei. The vagal nuclei in turn control heart rate via the vagus. Higher tonic vmPFC inhibition of the amygdala means greater top-down regulatory capacity, which manifests peripherally as higher heart-rate variability (HRV) and higher respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA).

The model thus links a central brain network (cortical – subcortical – brainstem) to a measurable peripheral signal (HRV). HRV becomes a transdiagnostic biomarker of "functional integrity of the central autonomic network." It indexes not just cardiac vagal tone but the overall capacity of the prefrontal – amygdala – vagus circuit for adaptive regulation.

Evidence base

NVI is broadly accepted in autonomic neuroscience. Thayer and Lane's 2000 paper in Journal of Affective Disorders has over 2,679 citations. The 2009 follow-up in Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews ("Claude Bernard and the heart-brain connection") and the 2017 Smith et al. paper on the "hierarchical basis" of NVI have established the model as a unifying framework for HRV-emotion regulation research. The structural overlap between the central autonomic network and prefrontal-amygdala emotion-regulation circuits has been validated in neuroimaging studies. Multiple meta-analyses support the core HRV – regulation link.

In academic autonomic neuroscience, NVI — not PVT — is the standard framework. PVT is more prominent in clinical psychology and the wellness industry; NVI dominates in basic and translational neuroscience.

How NVI explains the autism HRV findings

Here's the critical point: NVI predicts everything PVT predicts about the autistic HRV data, with a mechanism that doesn't require contested phylogenetic claims.

Cheng 2020's meta-analysis of 34 studies found autistic individuals show significantly lower baseline parasympathetic HRV (Hedges' g = −0.52) and lower RSA reactivity to social stress (g = −0.72). NVI's interpretation: autistic individuals show atypical vmPFC structure and function (well-documented in neuroimaging), atypical amygdala reactivity, and atypical resting connectivity in regulatory networks. Reduced vmPFC top-down inhibition of the amygdala produces lower baseline HRV and impaired dynamic regulation under social-stress demand.

The HRV finding is exactly what NVI predicts for any population with reduced top-down regulatory capacity — depression, anxiety, PTSD, autism, alcohol use disorder, schizophrenia. None of these require polyvagal-specific machinery to explain.

Why NVI wins the comparison

When two frameworks predict the same observation, the parsimony tie-breaker favors the framework with less contested mechanism. NVI uses established neuroanatomy. PVT requires contested phylogenetic claims that the 2026 Untenable consensus (39 co-authors) rejected. NVI has higher citation impact in academic neuroscience. PVT has higher visibility in clinical/wellness contexts. If the question is "what is the established scientific framework for HRV-emotion regulation?", the answer is NVI.

Model 2: Predictive Processing / Active Inference

Predictive processing is the framework that has reshaped cognitive and computational neuroscience over the past 20 years. It's the dominant contemporary account of how the brain handles perception, action, and emotion. For our question, it's the framework that absorbs PVT's "neuroception" construct as ordinary predictive inference, and provides a far broader explanatory frame for autism, alexithymia, autonomic regulation, and emotion.

Mechanism

The core idea, originating in Karl Friston's free energy principle and elaborated by Anil Seth, Lisa Feldman Barrett, and many others: the brain is a prediction machine. Rather than passively receiving sensory data and constructing meaning bottom-up, the brain generates probabilistic predictions ("priors") about what it will perceive next — both externally and internally. Sensory input is compared to predictions; the discrepancy is prediction error. The brain minimizes prediction error by (a) updating its predictions (perceptual inference) or (b) acting on the world or body to make sensory data match predictions (active inference).

For autonomic regulation specifically, the brain predicts what physiological state the body will need before it needs it, and pre-emptively adjusts via descending autonomic outputs. This is exactly Sterling's reformulation of allostasis as "predictive regulation" (Model 3 below). HRV, on this view, is the peripheral signature of a regulatory system that's anticipating, weighting predictions against incoming data, and continuously adjusting both predictions and bodily action.

For emotion, the predictive-processing framework was extended in Anil Seth's 2013 Trends in Cognitive Sciences paper ("Interoceptive inference, emotion, and the embodied self"). Emotions, on this view, are not hardwired outputs of evolved circuits but inferences about what the body's interoceptive prediction errors mean in context. The same physiological state might be inferred as "anxiety" before a job interview, "excitement" before a first date, "anger" during an argument — depending on the brain's contextual concept-construction.

Evidence base

Predictive processing is the current dominant framework in computational and cognitive neuroscience. Karl Friston is among the most-cited neuroscientists alive. The framework subsumes predictive coding, Bayesian brain, active inference, and free energy minimization under one umbrella. Anil Seth's interoceptive predictive coding has become foundational in interoception research. Barrett & Simmons 2015 in Nature Reviews Neuroscience established interoceptive predictions as a unifying explanatory frame across emotion, body awareness, and psychiatric disorder.

For autism specifically: Quattrocki & Friston 2014 explicitly framed autism as an interoceptive prediction problem, with developmental oxytocin abnormalities producing atypical precision-weighting on interoceptive signals. Pellicano & Burr 2012 proposed autistic perception involves weaker prior weighting in Bayesian inference ("hypo-priors"). Both have generated robust research programs.

How predictive processing explains the autism + alexithymia + interoception data

The fit is much tighter than PVT's. Where PVT offers a "states" framework (ventral vagal / sympathetic / dorsal vagal), predictive processing offers a mechanism: precision-weighting on interoceptive predictions.

In autism, atypical precision-weighting can manifest as:

  • Hypo-priors (Pellicano & Burr 2012): sensory data over-weighted → veridical detail-focused perception, sensory hypersensitivity, reduced susceptibility to illusions.
  • Atypical interoceptive precision (Quattrocki & Friston 2014): interoceptive signals processed atypically, contributing to alexithymia and emotion-categorization difficulties.
  • Failed predictive control of autonomic state: the regulatory system can't anticipate demands and lurches reactively, producing the autonomic dysregulation captured by HRV/RSA measurements.

This connects to alexithymia in a way PVT cannot. The Bird & Cook 2013 hypothesis — that the interoceptive deficits attributed to autism are actually alexithymia-driven (~50% co-occurrence) — fits cleanly within predictive processing: alexithymia = atypical interoceptive precision-weighting + atypical concept-construction. When alexithymia is controlled for, autism-vs-non-autism interoception differences largely disappear (Shah et al. 2016).

Why predictive processing wins the comparison

Predictive processing is broader, more general, and more empirically constrained than PVT. It absorbs PVT's "neuroception" as ordinary predictive inference (no special mechanism needed); explains autonomic regulation, emotion, interoception, perception, decision, psychiatric disorder, and autism under a unified framework; connects directly to allostasis (Model 3) and constructed emotion (Model 4); and has substantially higher academic impact than PVT in computational and cognitive neuroscience. When PVT and predictive processing make the same predictions, parsimony favors predictive processing. When they differ — most notably on whether neuroception is a special mechanism — predictive processing wins because it's both more general and more empirically constrained.

Model 3: Allostasis & Allostatic Load — Sterling & Eyer; McEwen

If predictive processing is the dominant framework in computational neuroscience and NVI is the dominant framework in autonomic neuroscience, allostasis is the framework that most directly addresses your centrally-stated concern: burnout. The autistic burnout literature is structured around allostasis-style cumulative load. The PVT reframe of burnout as "dorsal vagal shutdown" is metaphor layered on top of an allostatic-load substrate.

Mechanism

Peter Sterling and Joseph Eyer introduced the concept of allostasis in 1988 (chapter in Handbook of Life Stress, Cognition and Health) with a deceptively simple principle: stability through change. Where classical homeostasis posits fixed setpoints the body returns to, allostasis posits predictive adjustment of physiology in anticipation of need. Heart rate, blood pressure, cortisol, glucose, immune function — none have fixed setpoints. All are continuously re-set by the brain based on anticipated demand.

When this anticipatory regulation works, it's adaptive. When it doesn't, costs accumulate. Bruce McEwen's seminal 1998 New England Journal of Medicine paper introduced allostatic load: the cumulative biological wear-and-tear from chronic over-demand on regulatory systems. McEwen identified four patterns that produce load: frequent activation, failure to shut off, inadequate response with compensatory over-activation, and maladaptive adaptation.

Allostatic load damages the cardiovascular system, immune system, brain (particularly hippocampus and prefrontal cortex), and metabolic system over time. It's the biological mechanism linking chronic stress exposure to disease — depression, anxiety, PTSD, cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, cognitive decline, immune dysregulation.

Sterling's 2012 Physiology & Behavior paper reframed allostasis as "predictive regulation," connecting it directly to predictive processing (Model 2). The brain anticipates physiological needs and pre-emptively adjusts; when anticipation fails, load accumulates.

Evidence base

Allostatic load has been operationalized via biomarker indices — combinations of cortisol, blood pressure, inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6), HRV, metabolic markers, and others — and validated in hundreds of studies linking chronic stress exposure to disease risk. McEwen 1998 is one of the most-cited papers in stress biology. The framework has been applied across social-determinants-of-health research, chronic-illness research, aging research, and psychiatric epidemiology. It is, in plain terms, one of the most empirically grounded frameworks in stress biology.

How allostasis explains autistic burnout — the key linkage

Raymaker 2020 / AASPIRE defined autistic burnout as "chronic life stress and a mismatch of expectations and abilities without adequate supports" producing "pervasive, long-term exhaustion, loss of function, and reduced tolerance to stimulus." That definition is operationally identical to allostatic-load-induced exhaustion.

The empirical follow-on work supports this. Higgins & Arnold 2021/2025 found autistic burnout correlates with PTSD symptoms at r ≈ 0.70 — and PTSD is the paradigmatic case of allostatic dysregulation. The autistic burnout literature, from its empirical inception, has used allostatic-load framing rather than polyvagal framing. Raymaker's qualitative work named the central drivers as: masking, sensory environment overload, social demand mismatch, lack of accommodations, repeated unmet recovery needs. Each of these is a chronic allostatic challenge.

"I'm completely on autopilot for things, kind of like robotically going through what I have to do every day in my life... not really able to be a participant in my own life... extreme exhaustion." Raymaker 2020 — AASPIRE autistic-burnout participant quote

No polyvagal claims are needed to explain autistic burnout. Allostasis is the framework the empirical literature uses. PVT's "dorsal vagal shutdown" reframe is metaphor on top of this substrate.

Why allostasis wins for burnout specifically

This is the strongest case for an alternative model dominating PVT on a specific phenomenon. The empirical autistic-burnout literature uses allostatic-load framing, not PVT framing. The biomarker measurements come from the allostatic-load tradition. The intervention implications differ: PVT-derived advice emphasizes "regulating vagal tone." Allostatic-load-derived advice emphasizes demand reduction. The biology recovers when demand is reduced, not when the autistic person learns to "regulate" themselves better. For autistic burnout specifically, allostasis wins decisively. Your centrally-stated concern has allostasis as its native framework.

Model 4: Theory of Constructed Emotion — Lisa Feldman Barrett

Where the previous three frameworks compete with PVT on autonomic regulation and burnout, Barrett's theory of constructed emotion directly contradicts PVT's architectural premise. PVT posits that hardwired biological emotion-states map to specific brainstem vagal substrates. Barrett's framework rejects this entire approach to emotion.

Mechanism

Barrett's central claim, developed across her 2017 book How Emotions Are Made and her 2017 paper in Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience: emotions are not hardwired biological programs. They are constructed in the moment by the brain from three ingredients:

  1. Interoceptive predictions and prediction errors — the body's current state, as inferred by the predictive-processing framework.
  2. Concept knowledge — learned categories like "anger," "sadness," "fear," "excitement." These categories differ across cultures and individuals.
  3. Context — what's happening, who's around, what's expected.

Different brains construct different emotions from similar physiological states. The same elevated heart rate might be inferred as "anxiety" before a job interview, "excitement" before a first date, "anger" during an argument, "love" during intimacy — depending on the concept knowledge and context.

The implication: there is no "fear circuit" in the brain. There are general-purpose prediction and categorization networks that produce instances of fear when interoceptive prediction errors + the concept "fear" + threat context combine in a particular way.

Evidence base

The empirical case for constructed emotion is strong. Lindquist et al. 2012 in Behavioral and Brain Sciences presented a meta-analysis of 91 neuroimaging studies and 1,829 subjects: no discrete brain region maps cleanly to any single emotion category. Emotions emerge from distributed networks active in many tasks, not from emotion-specific modules. Wager et al. 2015 used pattern-classifier neuroimaging analysis: emotion-specific patterns exist but they're distributed across many brain regions. Emotional granularity research (Barrett 2004 and subsequent) shows people who construct more differentiated emotion categories have better emotion regulation, fewer mental health problems, lower physical health risk. Cross-cultural research shows emotion categories vary substantially between cultures.

The direct anti-PVT implication

If constructed emotion is right, PVT's "ladder" architecture is at best a useful clinical metaphor and at worst a category error. PVT requires that biological states (ventral vagal, sympathetic, dorsal vagal) map onto discrete emotional states. Constructed emotion denies this mapping exists.

In practice, this means: "being in dorsal vagal" doesn't have a fixed emotional signature — it depends on concept and context. Two autistic people experiencing the same physiological exhaustion may construct different emotions from it depending on their concept knowledge: "I'm in shutdown" vs. "I'm sick" vs. "I'm sad" vs. "I'm done caring." Interventions that build emotional granularity (emotion-labeling practice, concept-construction) should improve regulation independent of any vagal-tone intervention.

How constructed emotion explains autistic emotional experience

The framework fits autistic experience well, particularly through the alexithymia connection. Alexithymia involves low emotional granularity — fewer differentiated concepts to construct from. The phenomenology "I just feel bad" or "I can't tell what I'm feeling" is exactly what low granularity predicts. Sensory overload looks like cascading interoceptive prediction error that can't be efficiently categorized into emotion concepts. Treatment implications: building emotional granularity through concept-learning interventions should improve regulation, independent of any vagal-tone framework.

Model 5: Somatic Marker Hypothesis — Damasio

Damasio's framework is older but enduring. It captures the same "body feels it first" insight that makes PVT intuitively appealing, without requiring the contested phylogenetic claims. Somatic markers is the framework with cleaner empirical anchoring for the PVT intuition.

Mechanism

Antonio Damasio's 1994 Descartes' Error introduced the somatic marker hypothesis. The mechanism: when a person faces a decision, the brain rapidly previews possible outcomes. The body responds to each option with somatic markers — gut feelings, heart rate changes, skin conductance shifts — that signal whether the option is advantageous or risky, based on past experience. These body-state signals are processed by the vmPFC and bias decision-making toward advantageous options before conscious deliberation completes.

Damasio's central claim: rationality requires emotion. Without body-state signals integrated by vmPFC, decisions become disconnected from their consequences. The famous evidence: Iowa Gambling Task studies showing that patients with vmPFC damage fail to develop anticipatory skin conductance responses and continue making disadvantageous choices long after non-damaged controls have learned to avoid them.

Where Damasio overlaps and differs from PVT

Overlap. Both Damasio and Porges emphasize body-to-brain signals in emotion and decision. PVT's "neuroception" is conceptually adjacent to Damasio's "somatic markers as pre-conscious decision-guidance." Both share the broad framing: the body is upstream of cognition.

Differences. No three-tier vagal hierarchy. No phylogenetic claims about vagal nuclei. No claim that "social engagement" has a dedicated brainstem substrate. Centered on vmPFC + interoceptive cortex (insula), not brainstem nuclei. Updated framing (Damasio & Carvalho 2013) converges with predictive processing and constructed emotion.

For the "body feels it first" insight that motivates much of PVT's intuitive appeal, Damasio's framework is older, better-anchored in established neuroanatomy, and doesn't require the contested phylogenetic claims. It's the cleaner version of the PVT intuition.

The Interoception Stack — Craig, Garfinkel, Bird & Cook

Beyond the five main competing models, three interoception-specific frameworks are worth understanding because they target the same data PVT targets — body awareness and emotion — but with finer-grained mechanisms.

Craig — the insular substrate

Bud Craig's 2002 paper ("How do you feel? Interoception") and 2009 follow-up ("How do you feel — now? The anterior insula") established the insular cortex as the cortical hub of interoception. Body signals are first represented in the posterior insula, re-represented in the mid-insula, and integrated in the anterior insula, where activation corresponds to subjective feeling. Craig 2002 has 5,400+ citations; Craig 2009 has 6,200+. The insula's role in interoception is one of the most-replicated findings in cognitive neuroscience.

Garfinkel — the three dimensions

Sarah Garfinkel et al.'s 2015 paper in Biological Psychology showed that interoception is not one thing. It has three dissociable dimensions:

  • Interoceptive accuracy — objective performance on tasks like heartbeat detection.
  • Interoceptive sensibility — self-reported subjective awareness.
  • Interoceptive awareness (metacognitive) — correspondence between accuracy and sensibility.

These dimensions can dissociate within individuals. Garfinkel et al. 2016 found autistic adults showed higher sensibility but lower accuracy than non-autistic adults — an interoceptive awareness mismatch, not simple deficit. PVT's framework cannot represent this nuance.

Bird & Cook — the alexithymia hypothesis

Geoff Bird and Richard Cook's 2013 Translational Psychiatry paper proposed that the interoceptive and emotional difficulties often attributed to autism are actually attributable to co-occurring alexithymia, which is present in roughly 50% of autistic adults but is conceptually distinct from autism. The subsequent empirical work (Shah et al. 2016) supports this: when alexithymia is controlled for, autistic-vs-non-autistic interoceptive performance differences largely disappear. A central appeal of PVT-for-autism is that it offers a story about why autistic people have emotional/regulatory differences. If the relevant differences are alexithymia-driven rather than autism-per-se driven, PVT's autism-specific story is targeting the wrong construct.

Pellicano & Burr — autism as hypo-priors

A final framework worth mentioning: Pellicano & Burr's 2012 Trends in Cognitive Sciences paper proposing autism involves weaker prior weighting in Bayesian sensory inference ("hypo-priors"). Sensory data is weighted too heavily relative to expectations, producing veridical detail-focused perception, sensory hypersensitivity, and reduced susceptibility to perceptual illusions. This competes directly with PVT on sensory hypersensitivity, with the advantage of making testable psychophysics predictions that PVT's "defensive state" account doesn't.

The Convergence/Divergence Map — central analytical anchor

This is the chapter's analytical centerpiece. For each phenomenon you care about, which frameworks predict it? Where do they converge and where do they diverge?

Phenomenon 1: Low baseline HRV in autistic adults (Cheng 2020, g = −0.52)

Five frameworks predict this: PVT (reduced ventral vagal); NVI (reduced vmPFC inhibition of amygdala); predictive processing (atypical precision-weighting); allostasis (cumulative load); Bird & Cook (alexithymia-driven). PVT has no unique explanatory advantage.

Phenomenon 2: Reduced RSA reactivity to social stress (Cheng 2020, g = −0.72)

PVT (vagal brake disengages); NVI (vmPFC fails to dynamically regulate); predictive processing (failed predictive autonomic control); allostasis (insufficient anticipatory regulation). PVT has the most direct ladder-shaped story, but predicts the same outcome as competitors.

Phenomenon 3: Autistic burnout (Raymaker 2020 — chronic exhaustion)

PVT ("dorsal vagal shutdown" — metaphor, no in-vivo measure); NVI (chronic regulatory failure); predictive processing (failed allostatic anticipation); allostasis (direct fit — framework Raymaker uses). Allostasis wins decisively.

Phenomenon 4: Autistic emotion regulation difficulties

Five frameworks predict this: PVT, NVI, predictive processing, constructed emotion, Bird & Cook (alexithymia drives the effects). PVT is one of several competing accounts, arguably the least well-supported on mechanism specificity.

Phenomenon 5: Autistic sensory hypersensitivity

PVT (defensive state); Pellicano & Burr (hypo-priors); allostasis (cumulative overload). Pellicano & Burr's account is more empirically constrained and testable.

Where PVT makes unique predictions that others don't

PVT does make some unique predictions:

  • The dorsal motor nucleus mediates freezing/shutdown specifically — rejected by Neuhuber & Berthoud 2022 and the 2026 Untenable consensus.
  • Myelinated cardiac vagal fibers are mammalian-only — rejected by Grossman 2023 with lungfish/reptile/bird evidence.
  • Three discrete biological states ladder under threat — not directly measurable in vivo; treated as clinical heuristic by most users.
  • Neuroception is a distinct non-conscious detection mechanism — no clear empirical separation from ordinary predictive inference.

The pattern: PVT's unique predictions are precisely the ones that the 2026 Untenable consensus rejected. The predictions PVT shares with other models are the ones supported by autism HRV data — but those don't privilege PVT over its competitors.

The Practical Decision: When PVT Adds Value vs. When It Just Relabels

The honest assessment is layered. PVT loses the scientific comparison decisively on multiple fronts. But it may still add value in specific contexts as a clinical vocabulary or self-narrative tool.

When PVT genuinely adds value

  • As a clinical vocabulary. If the "ladder" metaphor (ventral → sympathetic → dorsal) helps you name and track your states, the framework provides usable scaffolding. This is the Journal of Psychiatry Reform 2023 stance: "Polyvagal Approaches: scientifically questionable but useful in practice."
  • As a meta-permission. PVT gives socially-acceptable language for needing rest, safety, and accommodation — that's real benefit.

When PVT doesn't add value and other frameworks dominate

  • HRV and emotion regulation → use NVI. Mainstream framework in autonomic neuroscience.
  • Burnout, chronic exhaustion → use allostasis. The framework empirical autistic-burnout research uses.
  • Emotion construction, alexithymia, emotional granularity → use constructed emotion or predictive processing. Finer mechanism than PVT's states.
  • Interoception, body awareness → use Craig + Garfinkel + Bird & Cook. Decompose interoception; recognize alexithymia drives the autism findings.
  • Sensory hypersensitivity → use Pellicano & Burr / predictive processing. More testable than PVT's defensive-state account.
  • Comparative anatomy claims → reject. The 2026 Untenable consensus has rejected PVT's core anatomical claims.

The final analytical takeaway

Is polyvagal theory the right framework for understanding autistic HRV, burnout, emotion regulation, and overwhelm?

No. The honest comparative assessment is that polyvagal theory loses the contest.

Five mainstream frameworks predict the same data with mechanisms that are either better-established (NVI, predictive processing), specifically empirically anchored to your centrally-stated concern (allostasis for burnout), or directly contradictory to PVT's architectural premises (constructed emotion). For every observation you care about — the autistic HRV data, the autistic burnout phenomenology, autistic emotion regulation difficulties, autistic sensory hypersensitivity — at least one alternative framework explains it more parsimoniously than PVT does.

PVT's unique predictions have been empirically rejected by the 2026 Untenable consensus. The predictions PVT shares with other models are the ones supported by the autism data — but shared predictions don't privilege PVT.

Where PVT survives — and this matters — is as a useful clinical vocabulary and self-narrative tool. The "ladder" metaphor genuinely helps some autistic people name and track their states. The Journal of Psychiatry Reform 2023's framing captures this: "scientifically questionable but useful in practice." Heuristic value is real. But heuristic value isn't theoretical privilege.

For the user's question — "is polyvagal the right framework?" — the answer is: not really, and especially not for the burnout question that matters most. For autistic burnout, allostasis is the framework that empirical autistic-burnout research actually uses. For HRV and emotion regulation, NVI and predictive processing dominate the academic neuroscience literature. For emotion construction and alexithymia, constructed emotion and the Bird & Cook hypothesis offer finer mechanisms. PVT can be used as scaffolding where it's genuinely useful; it should not be imported as if its mechanistic claims were established science.

The autistic HRV data is not strong evidence for polyvagal theory. It's evidence for any framework that links central regulatory networks to peripheral autonomic output — which is the entire post-1990 autonomic neuroscience literature. The question shifts from "is PVT supported?" to "is PVT the best framework available?" and the answer there is: no.

Glossary

TermDefinition
Active inferenceFriston's formalization in which agents act on the world (or body) to make sensory input match predictions, rather than only updating the predictions.
AllostasisStability through change. Predictive regulation of physiology in anticipation of need (Sterling & Eyer 1988; Sterling 2012).
Allostatic loadCumulative biological wear-and-tear from chronic over-demand on regulatory systems (McEwen 1998).
Constructed emotionBarrett's theory that emotions are constructed from interoceptive prediction + concept + context, not biological programs.
Emotional granularityThe fineness of distinctions a person can make between emotion categories. Higher granularity is associated with better regulation outcomes.
Free energy principleFriston's unified theory that all living systems strive to minimize free energy (a measure of prediction error).
Hypo-priorsPellicano & Burr's hypothesis that autistic perception involves weaker prior weighting in Bayesian sensory inference.
InteroceptionThe sense of the body's internal physiological condition.
Interoceptive accuracyObjective performance on interoception tasks (e.g., heartbeat detection). One of Garfinkel's three dimensions.
Interoceptive awareness (metacognitive)Correspondence between interoceptive accuracy and sensibility. The third Garfinkel dimension.
Interoceptive inferencePredictive processing applied to interoception. Emotion as inference about interoceptive prediction errors (Seth 2013).
Interoceptive sensibilitySubjective self-reported attention to body signals. One of Garfinkel's three dimensions.
NVINeurovisceral integration. Thayer & Lane's model linking vmPFC, amygdala, vagus, and HRV.
Predictive processingFramework treating the brain as a hierarchical prediction machine that minimizes prediction error.
Somatic markersDamasio's body-state signals that guide decision-making via vmPFC.
vmPFCVentromedial prefrontal cortex. Central integrator across multiple frameworks for top-down regulation.
