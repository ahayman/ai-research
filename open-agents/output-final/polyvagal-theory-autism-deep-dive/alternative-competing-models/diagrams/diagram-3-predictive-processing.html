Predictive Processing — Emotion as Interoceptive Inference

Seth's framework (extending Friston): the brain generates predictions about body state, compares them to interoceptive signals, and updates either the predictions (perceptual inference) or the body (active inference) to minimize prediction error. Emotion is the brain's inference about what its interoceptive prediction errors mean. This is the dominant contemporary framework in computational neuroscience.

How predictive processing replaces PVT's "neuroception." PVT proposes neuroception as a special non-conscious detection mechanism for safety/threat cues. Predictive processing absorbs this construct as ordinary predictive inference — predictions about safety/threat are generated continuously, weighted by precision, and updated by sensory data. No need for a separate special-purpose detection system. The framework is broader, more general, and more empirically constrained than PVT.
How it explains autism + alexithymia. Quattrocki & Friston (2014) framed autism as an interoceptive prediction problem with developmental oxytocin abnormalities causing atypical precision-weighting on interoceptive signals — producing alexithymia and difficulty representing emotional states. Pellicano & Burr's (2012) hypo-priors account explains autistic perception in the same framework. Bird & Cook's (2013) finding that alexithymia (not autism) drives interoception deficits fits cleanly: alexithymia = atypical interoceptive precision-weighting. PVT cannot represent this fine-grained mechanism.
