Allostatic Load Curve — The Empirical Framework for Autistic Burnout

Sterling & Eyer's allostasis (1988) + McEwen's allostatic load (1998) describe cumulative biological wear-and-tear from chronic over-demand. This is the framework Raymaker 2020 / AASPIRE autistic-burnout research actually uses — not polyvagal theory. The "dorsal vagal shutdown" reframe is metaphor layered on top of an allostatic-load substrate.

Why this matters. Raymaker 2020 defined autistic burnout as "chronic life stress and a mismatch of expectations and abilities without adequate supports." Higgins/Arnold 2021 found autistic burnout correlates with PTSD symptoms at r ≈ 0.70 — the signature of chronic allostatic dysregulation. The empirical autistic-burnout literature uses allostatic-load framing, not polyvagal framing. Calling burnout "dorsal vagal shutdown" is metaphor, not mechanism — and PVT's metaphor doesn't change the recovery strategy: reduce demand, restore reserves, build accommodations. The biology recovers when load decreases.
The PVT-allostasis split on burnout recovery. PVT-derived advice emphasizes building "ventral vagal tone" (SSP, breathwork, co-regulation). Allostatic-load-derived advice emphasizes reducing cumulative demand (sensory accommodation, masking reduction, demand-matching, restorative time). The advice overlaps where it matters (rest, safety, sensory accommodation) but diverges where it counts: PVT-derived advice can subtly imply you're broken and need to "regulate" yourself back to a state; allostatic-load-derived advice acknowledges that the regulatory system is overwhelmed by external load and the fix is upstream.
