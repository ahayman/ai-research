Pellicano & Burr — Autism as Predictive Coding Difference

A predictive-processing account of autistic perception that competes directly with PVT-style "defensive state" explanations of sensory hypersensitivity. Pellicano & Burr (2012) argue autistic perception involves weaker prior weighting — sensory data is weighted too heavily relative to expectations. This explains sensory hypersensitivity, atypical perceptual differences, and detail-focused processing without invoking PVT's defensive-state architecture.

Non-autistic (typical) Bayesian inference

P(world|sensory) ∝ P(world) × P(sensory|world)
(prior × likelihood = posterior)
Perception is heavily influenced by context. Optical illusions work. People see what they expect. Familiar objects perceived faster than novel.

Autistic — hypo-priors / weak-priors

P(world|sensory) ∝ weak P(world) × P(sensory|world)
(weak prior × strong likelihood = posterior dominated by sensory data)
Perception is veridical, detail-focused. Less susceptible to illusions. Sensory hypersensitivity. Context-poor perception. The "intense world" effect.

How Pellicano & Burr competes with PVT on sensory hypersensitivity

PVT explanation: Autistic sensory hypersensitivity is the result of a "defensive state" — the autistic nervous system is dispositionally tilted toward sympathetic/dorsal vagal activation, biasing sensory processing toward threat detection. This is the original Listening Project Protocol / SSP motivation.

Pellicano & Burr explanation: Autistic sensory hypersensitivity is the result of weaker prior weighting in Bayesian sensory inference. Without strong priors to "explain away" sensory input, every signal feels equally informative — hence overwhelming. No defensive state required.

Why Pellicano & Burr arguably wins. The hypothesis makes testable psychophysics predictions (autistic people should be less susceptible to specific illusions; should show different adaptation curves; should show different attention-to-detail patterns). These predictions have been partially supported in subsequent research. Van de Cruys et al. 2013 has proposed a refinement ("overfitted predictions") rather than rejecting the framework. The 10-year-review of Bayesian theories of autism (2023) shows this framework has grown into a robust research program. PVT's defensive-state hypothesis hasn't generated comparable testable predictions.
