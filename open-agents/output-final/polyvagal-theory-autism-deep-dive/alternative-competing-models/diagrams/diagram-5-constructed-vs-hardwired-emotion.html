Constructed Emotion vs. Hardwired Emotion Programs

Polyvagal Theory and Barrett's Theory of Constructed Emotion are directly architecturally incompatible. PVT says discrete biological emotion-states map to brainstem vagal substrates. Constructed emotion says no such mapping exists — and meta-analytic neuroimaging supports the constructed view.

Polyvagal Theory — hardwired emotion programs

Threat detection → biological state
(ventral vagal / sympathetic / dorsal vagal)
→ emotion experience
  • Three discrete states with brainstem substrates
  • Ventral vagal = "social engagement, safety"
  • Sympathetic = "fight/flight"
  • Dorsal vagal = "freeze/shutdown"
  • Hierarchical recruitment under threat (dissolution)
  • States are universal evolutionary outputs
Evidence problems. The "discrete biological emotion programs" view has been steadily losing ground in cognitive neuroscience. Lindquist 2012 meta-analysis (91 studies, 1,829 subjects) found no discrete brain region maps cleanly to any single emotion category. Wager et al. 2015 found emotion-specific patterns are distributed across many regions, not localized to "emotion circuits."

Constructed Emotion — emotions built on the fly

Interoceptive prediction
+ concept knowledge
+ context
= constructed emotion (in the moment)
  • No discrete emotion circuits
  • Same physiological state → different emotions in different contexts (e.g., "excitement" vs. "anxiety")
  • Concept knowledge is learned, culture-dependent
  • Emotional granularity varies among people
  • Brain uses general-purpose prediction networks, not emotion-specific modules
  • Built on active inference (Friston framework)
Evidence base. Lindquist 2012 meta-analysis supports constructionist view. Cross-cultural research shows emotion categories vary substantially. Behavioral work shows people with higher emotional granularity have better emotion regulation, fewer mental health problems, lower physical health risk. Wager pattern-classifier work shows emotion patterns are distributed, not localized.
Where they conflict — and which wins. The two frameworks make incompatible predictions about how emotion is generated. PVT requires that specific biological circuits produce specific emotion-states; constructed emotion denies this. The empirical brain-imaging meta-analyses support constructed emotion. If constructed emotion is correct (and the empirical evidence increasingly supports it), then PVT's "ladder" (ventral vagal → sympathetic → dorsal vagal as a state-hierarchy) is at best a clinically useful metaphor for a more complex underlying process — not a literal description of how the brain produces emotion. For autistic experience specifically: low emotional granularity (= alexithymia, ~50% of autistic adults) fits the constructed-emotion account cleanly. The intervention implication is concept-construction practice (emotion-labeling, granularity training) rather than vagal regulation.
