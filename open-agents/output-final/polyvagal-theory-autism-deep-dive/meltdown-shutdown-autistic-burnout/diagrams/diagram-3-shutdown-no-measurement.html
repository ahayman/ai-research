Shutdown — Real Phenomenology, Contested Mechanism

Autistic shutdown is a recognisable withdrawal pattern that needs different support than meltdown. But the popular polyvagal framing — "dorsal vagal collapse" — sits on weak empirical ground at the mechanism level. The phenomenology is real; the proposed neural mechanism is inferred, not measured, and may be anatomically wrong.

Phenomenology — Well Described

1

Mounting overload past mobilisation capacity

The system has exhausted its capacity to remain in active engagement OR has neurocepted that mobilisation is unsafe (e.g., trapped in long demand, no escape, social contagion of distress).

2

Motor inhibition begins

Slowed or halted movement. Voice quietens or stops. Eye contact drops. Facial expression flattens. The person may not notice this happening to them.

3

Cognitive engagement narrows

Difficulty making decisions. Difficulty processing speech. Difficulty initiating speech or movement. The world feels distant.

"Shutdown is just...usually 'cause of stress, I'll just completely freeze up, I can't really talk, I'll stutter." — autistic youth, Phung et al. 2021

4

Full shutdown — internal withdrawal

Partial or total mutism. Social withdrawal. Reduced response to stimuli. Sometimes mistaken for catatonia. Distinct from sleep — the person is awake but unable to engage.

"Just feeling drained, like I don't have the energy to get up." — autistic youth, Phung et al. 2021

5

Recovery — quiet, no demands

10 minutes to two hours typical. Needs uninterrupted quiet, often solo. Premature pressure to engage prolongs the shutdown or pushes into meltdown.

The Mechanism Claim — Examined

The popular framing

"Shutdown = dorsal vagal collapse." The nervous system has neurocepted life-threat or overwhelm beyond mobilisation capacity and triggered the older immobilisation circuit (dorsal motor nucleus → unmyelinated vagal pathway → parasympathetic over-engagement → freeze).

Problem 1: No in-vivo measurement

The dorsal motor nucleus innervates sub-diaphragmatic viscera. It is not amenable to specific non-invasive assay in humans. HRV indices (RSA, RMSSD, HF-HRV) reflect cardiac vagal tone via the nucleus ambiguus pathway, NOT the dorsal motor nucleus. "Dorsal vagal state" is inferred from behaviour and self-report, not measured.

Problem 2: Anatomy critique

Neuhuber & Berthoud (2022, Biol Psychol 174:108425) reviewed comparative neuroanatomy of vagal nuclei. Their conclusion: emotional freezing in mammals appears to be primarily NA-mediated (ventrally located), not DMV-mediated. The proposed polyvagal mechanism for shutdown may be anatomically wrong.

What still holds

Shutdown as a phenomenological cluster is real, recognisable, and clinically useful. It needs different support than meltdown. The framework can be useful as a description and as a way of explaining the experience to other people — even if the proposed mechanism is wrong in detail.

Verdict: clinical heuristic, not measured mechanism. Use "shutdown" to describe the experience. Treat "dorsal vagal" as a clinical/self-narrative label, not as a confirmed neural state. The phenomenology is solid; the mechanism explanation has known problems.
