Burnout × PTSS × Repeated Meltdowns — How Much Do They Overlap?

In autistic adults, autistic burnout, post-traumatic stress symptoms, and repeated meltdowns frequently co-occur. The empirical correlations are substantial (r = 0.61–0.70) but not perfect — these are overlapping but distinct constructs, not the same thing. Disentangling which is "primary" may be impossible in any individual case; treating each strand separately is often more useful than choosing one.

AUTISTIC BURNOUT (alone)

Definition: chronic exhaustion + skill loss + reduced stimulus tolerance from cumulative load (Raymaker 2020).

Other correlations:

× Depression r = 0.61 × Anxiety r = 0.67

Cross-comorbidity with depression and generalised anxiety is substantial — but burnout is distinct (Bougoure 2026 AUC = 0.92).

PTSS (alone)

Definition: post-traumatic stress symptoms (intrusions, avoidance, hyperarousal, negative cognitions) measured by PCL-5.

Autistic-adult prevalence (Rumball 2020):

DSM-5 trauma → 61% lifetime probable PTSD Non-DSM-5 → 63% lifetime probable PTSD

"Social misperceptions, bullying, aversive sensory experiences" as often associated with PTSS as Criterion-A events.

REPEATED MELTDOWNS (alone)

Definition: recurring involuntary overwhelm episodes with sympathetic-over-arousal substrate (Cheng 2020 RSA reactivity g = −0.72).

Theorised pathway: repeated overwhelm contributes to allostatic load → burnout; can also be experienced as traumagenic if not contextualised — feeding the PTSS pattern in the cumulative-overwhelm route.

Cross-link existing alexithymia × cumulative-overwhelm chapter for the full causal-cascade analysis.

The interpretive takeaway Substantial overlap is not identity. r = 0.70 means burnout and PTSS share ~49% of their variance — close but distinct. Many autistic adults have burnout AND PTSS simultaneously; some have one without the other. Treatment that addresses each strand on its own merits (demand reduction for burnout; trauma-informed care for PTSS; sensory pacing for repeated meltdowns) is usually more useful than collapsing all three into one label.
