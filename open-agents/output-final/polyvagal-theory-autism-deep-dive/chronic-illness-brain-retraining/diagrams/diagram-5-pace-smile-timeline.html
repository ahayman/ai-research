The PACE & SMILE Trials — A Case Study in Outcome-Switching

Why ME/CFS advocacy is sharply suspicious of behavioral interventions for chronic illness. Two large, well-funded trials. Both had outcome-switching after data collection began. Both were ultimately reversed — but only after years of advocacy and FOI requests.

PACE trial (adult ME/CFS)

White, Sharpe, Chalder et al. · UK · £5M (incl. DWP funding) · n=641 · CBT, GET, Adaptive Pacing, Specialist Medical Care
2011Lancet publishes initial PACE results. CBT and GET reported as "moderately effective." Recovery rates initially reported as 22%.
2013Psychological Medicine publishes "recovery" paper with revised, lower thresholds — recovery threshold for physical functioning lowered from ≥85 to ≥60 (below the trial's entry criterion).
2015–16Patient advocate Alem Matthees files FOI request. PACE investigators resist. Information Tribunal orders data release on 9 September 2016 (dismissing PACE team's harassment claims as "grossly exaggerated").
2017J Health Psychology special issue: Geraghty "Further commentary on the PACE trial: Biased methods and unreliable outcomes." Multiple co-published critiques.
2018Wilshire et al. reanalysis in BMC Psychology. Recovery rate using original protocol: ~7–8% across all arms. PACE claims "not statistically reliable."
2021NICE NG206 ME/CFS guideline no longer recommends CBT or GET. CDC removes endorsement.
Recovery rate, before vs after reanalysis:
22% → 7–8%

SMILE trial (pediatric CFS / Lightning Process)

Crawley et al. · UK · Bristol/Bath · n=100 planned · Lightning Process vs Specialist Medical Care
2010Trial proposed. ME Association, Young ME Sufferers Trust, Invest in ME, Robin Gill (ethics) raise concerns about pediatric subjects and the unproven intervention.
2011Ethical approval granted after prolonged protest. Housebound (more severely affected) children excluded — biases sample toward less-severe cases.
~2014Primary outcome switched mid-trial after more than half of full-trial sample had been recruited: school attendance (objective) → SF-36 physical function (self-report subjective).
2017SMILE results published in Archives of Disease in Childhood: positive results based on switched (subjective) endpoint. Original (school attendance) endpoint reported as null.
2017–18David Tuller (Virology Blog), 22+ academic signatories open letter raises "serious issues and anomalies."
2019Arch Dis Child editor Nick Brown issues lengthy editorial correction acknowledging paper was "not fully ICMJE compliant" re: undisclosed primary outcome change.
2021NICE NG206: "do not offer the Lightning Process, or therapies based on it, to people with ME/CFS."
The endpoint switch:
School attendance (objective) → SF-36 physical function (subjective)
Why this matters for brain retraining. The PACE and SMILE histories taught the ME/CFS and long COVID community that behavioral-cognitive interventions, when marketed enthusiastically and tested with subjective endpoints in unblinded trials, can survive publication and clinical recommendation for years before independent reanalysis reveals the methodological failures. The brain-retraining industry uses the same study-design shortcuts — small samples, no blinding, subjective endpoints, founder-coupled researchers. The history says scrutiny is warranted before the next decade goes by.
