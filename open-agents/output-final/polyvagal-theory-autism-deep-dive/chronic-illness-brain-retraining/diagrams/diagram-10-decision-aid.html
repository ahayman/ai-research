Decision Aid — Should I Try Brain Retraining?

A practical walk-through for an autistic adult considering a brain-retraining program. Not a yes/no — a structured way to think through the specific situation.

Start here

You're an autistic adult with chronic illness symptoms. You're considering a brain-retraining program. Walk through these questions in order.

Question 1
Do you have a clear diagnostic workup (ruling out treatable conditions: thyroid, anemia, sleep apnea, Lyme co-infections, vitamin deficiencies, autoimmune)?
Yes Proceed to Q2.
No Do the workup first. Brain retraining can't cure treatable underlying causes — and you may not need a program if a treatable cause is found.
Question 2
Have you tried the free / low-cost evidence-based practices first? (pacing within energy envelope, slow-paced breathing ~6 breaths/min, basic HRV biofeedback with a free app, sleep optimization, sensory accommodation, demand reduction)
Yes, with adequate trial Proceed to Q3.
No / not consistently Try those first for 8–12 weeks. They have stronger evidence, cost nothing, and many people don't need a paid program after consistent application of the basics.
Question 3
Do you have prominent post-exertional malaise (PEM) — symptoms that worsen for 24+ hours after physical, cognitive, or emotional exertion?
No Lower (but not zero) risk from brain-retraining programs. Proceed to Q4.
Yes Major caution. Programs that encourage "expanding your safety zone" or "pushing through" are exactly the wrong framing for PEM. Crashes can last days to weeks. If you proceed, do so with strict HR-based pacing and a hard PEM-monitoring discipline.
Question 4
Is the program cost feasible without straining your financial situation, AND do you have time/energy for daily practice for 3–6+ months?
Yes Proceed to Q5.
No Don't go into debt for an unproven program. The expected return is uncertain; the cost is certain. Continue with free/low-cost practices.
Question 5
Have you applied the 10-question evaluation framework (Diagram 7) to your specific program? Does it score "good" or "partial" on at least half?
Yes (mostly partial/good) Proceed to Q6.
No (mostly poor/absent) Reconsider. The program is unlikely to be more effective than the basics you can do for free. The DNRS / Primal Trust evidence base falls into this category.
Question 6
Can you set pre-defined evaluation endpoints (specific symptoms / function measures, monitored weekly) and a defined trial period (e.g., 3 months) with an explicit non-judgmental exit plan if it doesn't help?
Yes Proceed with caution. See "Cautious proceed" outcome below.
No Don't start until you can. Without pre-set endpoints, sunk-cost reasoning will keep you in a non-working program longer. Set the exit conditions before paying.

Outcome: Cautious proceed

If you reached this point with mostly "yes" answers: you have a workup, you've tried basics, no severe PEM, the cost is affordable, the program scored at least partial on the evaluation framework, and you have an exit plan. You can proceed with the program. Treat it as an experiment with a defined trial window. Monitor weekly. Stop if it's not helping by the trial endpoint. If it is helping, you may still want to taper rather than continue indefinitely. And remember: the most plausible source of benefit is probably the embedded basics (breathing, pacing, attention), not the program's unique framework.

Outcome: Don't pay yet — try the basics

If you reached this point because basics haven't been tried adequately: invest 8–12 weeks in slow-paced breathing, pacing, HRV biofeedback (free app + cheap sensor), sleep work, and sensory accommodation. Many people don't need a paid program after this. If symptoms persist, return to Q3.

Outcome: Don't proceed with this program

If you reached this point because of severe PEM without strict pacing discipline, or because the program scored poorly on evaluation, or because you couldn't set evaluation endpoints: the risk-to-benefit ratio is unfavorable. The Lightning Process has NICE-NHS "do not offer" status. DNRS and Primal Trust have no published RCT evidence at all. Gupta has weak evidence. This is the right time to not pay — and to do the cheaper, safer, more-evidence-based practices instead.

Bottom line. The point of this decision aid is not to tell you "never try brain retraining." It is to help you try in a way that minimizes wasted money, avoidable PEM crashes, and the kind of failure-experience that gets blamed on the patient rather than on the program's overclaim. If you proceed, proceed with measurement, an exit plan, and your own judgment as the final authority — not the program's encouragement.
