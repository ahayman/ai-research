The "wandering nerve" (Latin vagus) descends from the medulla through neck, thorax, abdomen, sending branches to multiple organ systems.

Figure 4. The vagus is the most-traveled cranial nerve. It exits the skull at the jugular foramen and descends bilaterally, branching to pharynx, larynx, carotid sinus, heart, lungs, esophagus, and most of the GI tract above the splenic flexure of the colon. Distal colon and rectum get parasympathetic supply from sacral spinal nerves (S2–S4), not vagus. The small auricular branch to the external ear is the anatomical basis for transcutaneous auricular vagus nerve stimulation (taVNS).
