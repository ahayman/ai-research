Vagus Nerve Fiber Composition (textbook approximation)

The popular "vagal brake" describes a minority subset of vagal fibers. The majority are sensory and unmyelinated.

Important caveat — these percentages are textbook approximations, not measured constants. The 80/20 afferent/efferent ratio comes from Foley & DuBois (1937) in cats and has been widely propagated since. The 90%/70% C-fiber breakdown is similar tradition. Modern morphometry (Settell et al. 2020 in pigs) reports the cervical vagus contains ~47 ± 7% myelinated fibers, suggesting some species and cervical-level variation. The substantive claim — vagus is afferent-dominant and predominantly unmyelinated — is robust within ±5–10%. What it should change: a healthy skepticism about treating "exactly 80%" or "exactly 90%" as precision figures.
Figure 3. The teaching takeaway: when popular polyvagal writing emphasizes the myelinated efferent "vagal brake" as the vagus, that's roughly 6% of vagal fibers. The other 94% are doing other things — mostly sensing the body and modulating the gut. The "vagal brake" is real and important, but it's a slice of a much larger nerve.
