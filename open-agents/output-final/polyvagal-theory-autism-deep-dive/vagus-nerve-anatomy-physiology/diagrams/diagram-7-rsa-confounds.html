RSA Confounds — Why "Vagal Tone" Readings Can Mislead

RSA is a real, informative biomarker. It is also surprisingly easy to push around. These are the major factors that change RSA without changing underlying parasympathetic function.

Confound Effect on RSA Why it matters
Breathing rate Slower breathing → larger RSA Even voluntary changes in pacing can dramatically alter RSA. Six breaths/min (the "resonance frequency") produces maximal RSA amplitude. A "vagal tone" reading taken during slow paced breathing isn't comparable to one taken during normal breathing.
Tidal volume Deeper breathing → larger RSA Supine RSA roughly doubles when tidal volume doubles. Asking someone to "breathe deeply" before a reading mechanically inflates the number.
Body position Supine > sitting > standing Postural shifts move the autonomic operating point. RSA measured while lying down is systematically higher than the same person standing. Head-down tilt produces extreme RSA.
Age Declines steadily with age RSA peaks in young adulthood and decreases roughly monotonically through midlife and older age. A 65-year-old's "low" RSA may be perfectly normal for age.
Fitness Endurance training raises resting RSA Aerobically trained adults have higher baseline parasympathetic indices. A "high vagal tone" reading can reflect fitness rather than emotional regulation capacity.
Sex and menstrual cycle Modest effects Young adult women generally show slightly higher resting parasympathetic HRV than men; menstrual cycle phase produces small modulations.
Medications Variable, often substantial Beta-blockers, atropine, anticholinergics, SSRIs, SNRIs, anti-anxiolytics, and several other commonly prescribed psychiatric meds all affect RSA. Any HRV reading must be interpreted in light of medication status.
Voluntary respiratory control Can artificially inflate RSA If the subject knows their breathing is being measured (or even that HRV is being recorded), they may breathe more deliberately. This alone changes the reading.
Recent meal, caffeine, alcohol Modest acute effects Post-prandial state, recent caffeine intake, and alcohol all shift autonomic baseline. Wearable readings vary depending on what you ate or drank that day.
Coughing, talking, swallowing Transient disruption These produce artifacts that contaminate short HRV recordings; need to be filtered out or controlled.
Sleep stage / time of day Diurnal variation RSA varies across the 24-hour cycle and across sleep stages. Comparison across times of day needs care.
Key takeaway. A single "low vagal tone" reading from a consumer wearable sitting on the couch is not a reliable diagnostic. Most of the confounds above can shift RSA by a factor of two or more without anything changing about your underlying parasympathetic nervous system. The metric is real and useful when measured carefully; it's misleading when treated as a precision biomarker in uncontrolled conditions.
Practical guidance for getting meaningful HRV data.
  • Compare same person to themselves over time, not to population norms.
  • Take readings at the same time of day, in the same position, in the same recent-meal/medication state.
  • Pay more attention to trends across weeks than to single readings.
  • If using paced breathing, use the same pace every time so RSA modulation by breathing rate is held constant.
  • Be cautious about interpreting baseline RSA. As Beauchaine 2015 notes, reactivity (change during task) is more clinically informative than baseline level.
  • Don't take consumer-wearable HRV labels too literally — read them as "this metric of this person at this moment under uncontrolled conditions," not as a diagnostic measure of vagal function.
