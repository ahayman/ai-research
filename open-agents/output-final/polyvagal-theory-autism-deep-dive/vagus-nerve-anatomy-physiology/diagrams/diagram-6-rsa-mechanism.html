RSA Mechanism — How Breathing Modulates Heart Rate

Heart rate accelerates slightly on inhale and decelerates on exhale, driven by rhythmic gating of cardiac vagal output by the brainstem respiratory rhythm generator.

The mechanism, step by step

1. Respiratory rhythm generator (brainstem) fires inspiratory commands Pre-Bötzinger complex and nearby nuclei in the medulla generate the breathing rhythm. The inspiratory burst sends signals to the diaphragm but also sends an inhibitory signal to cardiac vagal preganglionic neurons in the nucleus ambiguus.
2. Cardiac vagal output is gated by respiration During inhalation, the nucleus ambiguus cardio-inhibitory neurons are silenced. The vagal brake is temporarily released. The sinoatrial node's intrinsic pacemaker rate (≈100–110 bpm in adults) shows through, so heart rate speeds up briefly.
3. Exhalation restores the vagal brake When inspiratory drive stops, cardiac vagal neurons resume firing. Acetylcholine release at the sinoatrial node slows pacemaker firing. Heart rate decelerates.
4. The rhythmic in-and-out generates RSA Repeat this cycle every 3–6 seconds and you get the characteristic sinusoidal modulation of heart rate that the high-frequency band of HRV captures.
5. Baroreflex makes a secondary contribution Breathing-related changes in venous return and blood pressure trigger baroreceptor reflex modulation that compounds with the central gating. The relative weight of central feedforward vs. peripheral feedback contributions is still debated, but the central gating is dominant in most healthy adults.
Figure 6. RSA is real, robust, and reflects vagal modulation of heart rate. It is also state-dependent: amplitude scales with breathing rate (larger at slow breathing) and tidal volume (larger at deep breathing). At very slow paced breathing (around 6 breaths/min, the "resonant frequency"), RSA reaches maximum amplitude — which is the physiological reason HRV biofeedback uses paced breathing at that rate. Whether artificially maximizing RSA via paced breathing actually improves tonic vagal control over time is a separate empirical question covered in the Interventions category.
