The Critical-Appraisal Checklist

A reusable scaffold for evaluating any "polyvagal" or vagus-nerve intervention claim. Walk through all eight before accepting marketing at face value.

How to use this. When you encounter a new product, protocol, or "vagal tone" claim, run it through every question below. The number of "no / unclear / fails" responses scales inversely with the credibility of the claim. Three or more red flags → treat as Tier D unless the cost and risk profile are zero.
1

Is there a sham control?

A sham is a fake version of the intervention that matches sensation, time commitment, and participant attention but lacks the active mechanism — for example, off-frequency stimulation or unfiltered music in place of SSP. Sham controls separate specific effects from placebo and expectancy.

Red flag: Most consumer-device studies do not include sham controls.
2

Is it an RCT?

Pilot, feasibility, open-label, and pre-post designs are all weaker than randomized controlled trials. RCT with concealed allocation and intention-to-treat analysis is the gold standard.

Red flag: The published SSP autism literature is dominated by open-label pilots with n < 30.
3

Was the primary endpoint pre-registered?

Outcomes "discovered" after data collection are exploratory. Only pre-specified primary endpoints count for efficacy claims. Pre-registration prevents post-hoc cherry-picking of whichever measure happened to reach significance.

Red flag: Most consumer wellness device studies do not pre-register endpoints.
4

Is the sample size adequate?

A pilot of n = 6 cannot demonstrate efficacy. It can demonstrate feasibility, tolerability, and signal-to-warrant-larger-trial. Adequate sample size for moderate effects is typically n ≥ 50 per arm. Larger for smaller expected effects.

Red flag: The Kawai 2023 SSP adult pilot is n = 6.
5

Are conflicts of interest disclosed?

Financial relationships — patent holdings, royalty arrangements, advisory roles, manufacturer-funded research — should be disclosed. Disclosure doesn't invalidate findings but it shifts the prior probability you should attach to manufacturer-friendly outcomes.

Red flag: Porges holds patents and receives royalties from Unyte (which sells SSP); Apollo Neuro, Pulsetto, Sensate fund the majority of their own studies.
6

Is the mechanism plausible?

"Vibration of vocal folds activates the laryngeal vagal afferents" maps onto real anatomy. "Wrist vibration activates the vagus nerve" requires substantially more theoretical bridge-building. "Magnesium increases vagal tone" is several steps removed from a measurable vagal pathway.

Red flag: Claims that invoke vagal pathways without a clear anatomical route are speculative.
7

What is the cost vs. benefit?

Slow breathing: free. SSP via licensed provider: hundreds of dollars plus device cost. Cold plunge tub: $5,000+. Compute cost-per-effect-size, not absolute cost. A $0 intervention with Tier A evidence dominates a $500 intervention with Tier C evidence on every dimension that matters to your wallet.

Red flag: High-cost interventions with weak evidence + manufacturer-funded research.
8

What harms have been reported?

Many wellness interventions market themselves as "risk-free." Hyperventilation combined with water immersion has killed people. "Limbic retraining" cure-claims for ME/CFS have provoked active harm-reduction advocacy from the patient community. Risk profiles must be characterized — absence of risk reporting is not the same as absence of risk.

Red flag: Practices that combine breath-hold with cold immersion; cure-claims for serious illness without standard-of-care collaboration.

Quick scoring rubric

0–1 red flags: Likely Tier A or B. Trust but verify autism-specific data.
2 red flags: Tier C territory. Worth trying as an n=1 personal experiment if cost and risk are low.
3+ red flags: Tier D. Treat marketing claims skeptically; expect no benefit beyond placebo.
Documented harm + cure-claims for serious illness: Tier E. Avoid or use only with informed clinical guidance.
