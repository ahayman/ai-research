The Evidence-Tier Framework (A–E)

A reusable critical-appraisal tool. Apply each criterion to any wellness or psychiatric intervention claim before accepting it at face value.

A
Strong
Criteria
  • Multiple independent randomized controlled trials
  • Adequate samples (typically n ≥ 50 per arm)
  • Sham or active control comparison
  • Pre-registered, pre-specified primary endpoint
  • Replicated by groups independent of original developer
  • Meta-analyses or systematic reviews show effect
  • Mechanism plausible and consistent with established physiology
  • No serious conflict-of-interest concerns in the evidence base
Examples in this chapter
Slow breathing at ~6 bpm (HRV, stress); cold face splash via diving reflex; humming/Bhramari (nasal NO); gammaCore (cleared headache indications only)
B
Moderate
Criteria
  • At least one well-controlled RCT with adequate power, or multiple smaller controlled trials
  • Effects replicated in at least two independent labs
  • May lack consistent meta-analytic synthesis
  • Mechanism plausible but not fully established
  • Some conflict-of-interest exposure but not exclusive
Examples in this chapter
Trauma-sensitive yoga in PTSD (van der Kolk 2014); alternate-nostril breathing; slow breathing for autism-specific anxiety endpoints
C
Preliminary
Criteria
  • Open-label pilots, feasibility studies, small uncontrolled trials
  • No sham comparison or wait-list only
  • Mechanism plausible
  • Insufficient to support strong efficacy claims; supports doing larger trials
Examples in this chapter
SSP in autism (n=6 adults, n~36 children, no sham RCT); taVNS in autism (feasibility, RCT protocol pending); Apollo Neuro (more independent data than competitors but still preliminary)
D
Insufficient
Criteria
  • Manufacturer-funded, often unpublished or non-peer-reviewed
  • Marketing claims outrun the data
  • No sham; indistinguishable from placebo/expectancy
  • Mechanism claim invokes a contested or speculative pathway
  • Evidence base controlled by entity with financial stake
Examples in this chapter
Sensate, Pulsetto, Truvaga (all not FDA-cleared as medical devices); vagus-nerve supplements; "polyvagal massagers"; vagus-nerve oils
E
Risk
Criteria
  • Documented adverse events in vulnerable populations
  • Risk profile not adequately characterized
  • Strong cure-claims where iatrogenic harm has been reported
  • Used in place of standard care for serious conditions
  • Examples: hyperventilation + water immersion, "limbic retraining" cure-claims for ME/CFS-spectrum illness
Examples in this chapter
Wim Hof Method when combined with water immersion (32+ reported deaths as of 2024); holotropic breathwork in unscreened populations; "brain retraining" cure-claims for ME/CFS / long COVID
How to read this. Tier is not equivalent to "good or bad." A Tier C intervention may still be worth trying as an n=1 personal experiment if cost and risk are low. A Tier A intervention can still fail to help an individual. The tiers describe the strength of the published evidence for the *specific claim* being made — they do not predict whether any given intervention will work for you.
