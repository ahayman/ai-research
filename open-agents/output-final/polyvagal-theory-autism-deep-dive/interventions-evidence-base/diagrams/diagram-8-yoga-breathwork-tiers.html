Yoga & Breathwork Ecosystem Evidence Tiers

A broad swath of practices is marketed as "polyvagal" or "vagal regulation." The evidence quality across this ecosystem varies by orders of magnitude. Pay particular attention to the Tier C/E boundary — that's where the safety story changes.

Generic yoga ≠ TSY. Instructor must be specifically trained in TCTSY or analogous trauma-informed approach.

Highly variable. No standardized evidence base. Practices may be effective; PVT framing is largely a descriptive overlay on standard somatic work.

C/EConditional
Wim Hof Method
Cyclic hyperventilation + cold + mindset
PMC10936795 PLOS One systematic review: mixed results. Some positive immune and stress-perception findings; pain perception reduction documented. 7-week chronic SCI RCT: no mental-health improvement; some respiratory and metabolic benefits.
Tier E when combined with water: 32+ reported drowning deaths as of 2024; cardiology data shows 1–3% arrhythmia in cold immersion alone, up to 63% with pre-immersion breath-hold. Wim Hof's own materials warn never to do the breathing protocol in or near water.
C/EConditional
Holotropic Breathwork
Grof method · 2–3 hour sessions
Mixed and limited published evidence. Designed to induce altered states. Some self-report benefit data; mechanisms unclear.
Adverse events documented: hyperventilation hypocapnia produces tetany, paresthesias, dizziness; in vulnerable individuals can precipitate panic, dissociation, rare seizure. Contraindications: pregnancy, cardiovascular disease, glaucoma, detached retina, epilepsy, serious psychiatric instability. Findhorn Foundation suspended its breathwork program after a 1993 Scottish Charities Office report.
The Tier C/E boundary. The decisive distinction in this ecosystem is not whether a practice has "evidence" or "not" — it's whether the risk profile has been adequately characterized for the population using it. Slow breathing has near-zero risk. Wim Hof breathing has documented deaths when combined with water. Holotropic breathwork has documented psychiatric adverse events in unscreened populations. The cure-claim "limbic retraining" programs for ME/CFS adapt these techniques into something the patient community has actively organized against. For autistic burnout, the Tier A and Tier B practices cover the use cases without needing to enter Tier C/E territory.
