SSP: What the Trials Show vs. What's Marketed

A side-by-side audit of the published evidence base for the Safe and Sound Protocol versus the typical claims encountered in consumer-facing marketing and provider testimonials.

What the published trials actually show
Kawai / Domínguez-Lara et al. 2023 (PMC10049251)
Adult ASD pilot · n = 6 adults · ages 21–44
Five daily 60-min sessions, 1-month follow-up. Exploratory. No sham, no control group. Only the Social Awareness subscale of the SRS-2 Family-Report reached statistical significance. Authors explicitly called for multicenter RCTs.
Porges et al. 2014 (Listening Project Protocol)
Predecessor of SSP · ~36 children · two trials
Reported decreased auditory hypersensitivity in ASD children vs. controls. Open-label, small. Forms the foundational evidence base SSP marketing inherits.
Heilman et al. and assorted pilots
Various small autism pilots
Mixed methodology, varied populations, most uncontrolled.
AOTA 2024 exploratory study
Young children · SSP adjunct to OT
Uncontrolled adjunct study; difficult to attribute effects to SSP specifically.
PMC12163202 — Sound-based interventions systematic review
Two SSP papers included
Evidence rated mixed; no definitive efficacy verdict.
What marketing emphasizes
Transformation narrative Provider testimonials and case studies featuring dramatic before/after stories of emotional regulation, language gains, social engagement breakthroughs.
"Polyvagal" mechanism framing The acoustic filter activates the social engagement system via middle-ear muscle tone modulation through the ventral vagal complex — a mechanism claim that inherits all the uncertainties of polyvagal theory itself.
Broad indication implication Marketing materials suggest applicability to autism, trauma, anxiety, attachment, language disorders, sensory processing, ADHD, and more — far broader than any controlled trial has actually tested.
"Backed by research" language Generic appeals to "decades of polyvagal research" without distinguishing PVT research from SSP-specific efficacy trials.
Provider gating Sold through Unyte Health to licensed providers, which adds a cost layer and a perceived authority signal without changing the underlying evidence base.
Conflict-of-interest disclosure
Stephen Porges holds patent rights on SSP-related technology and receives royalties from Unyte / Integrated Listening Systems. He serves as Chief Scientific Advisor at Unyte Health, the company that sells SSP. He co-founded the Polyvagal Institute (501(c)(3)), which offers paid courses and certifications. He is also an author for W. W. Norton and receives royalties from book sales. These disclosures appear in his academic publications but are not prominently surfaced in SSP marketing materials or in many provider-facing communications. None of this invalidates SSP — but it is relevant institutional context for evaluating evidence quality and the direction of incentive pressure on the research base.
