Origin Story

The story of Cboard begins not in a corporate boardroom but in a rehabilitation center in Cordoba, Argentina. In 2017, Martin Bedouret, a software developer with more than 20 years of experience, was diagnosed with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) at the age of 39. As he began working with a speech therapist, he discovered a troubling reality: the tools designed to help people with speech impairments communicate were prohibitively expensive, available in only a handful of languages, and locked behind proprietary platforms.

Rather than building yet another proprietary tool, Bedouret partnered with Shay Cojocaru, an Israeli developer serving as Head of Engineering at CIREHA, a rehabilitation center in Cordoba. Cojocaru had been searching online for programmers willing to contribute to an open-source AAC project. Together with early contributors Akshat Gupta and Amberley Romo, they formed the nucleus of what would become a global community.

"Open Source was the way it all came about."
--- Martin Bedouret, Co-Founder of Cboard

The project attracted the attention of UNICEF's Innovation Fund, which provided early investment through CIREHA to develop Cboard into a scalable, affordable AAC solution. UNICEF estimated that approximately 840,000 children in Europe and Central Asia alone could benefit from AAC solutions --- and the vast majority had no access to any communication tools at all.

How It Works

Unlike most AAC apps that require downloading a platform-specific application, Cboard runs in any modern web browser. A family in rural Montenegro does not need to buy an iPad or a dedicated speech device --- any tablet, phone, laptop, or desktop computer with a browser can become a communication device.

At its core, Cboard uses the browser's native Speech Synthesis API to convert symbol selections into spoken words. When a user taps a pictographic symbol on their communication board, the browser generates speech in the user's language.

Language Coverage

Perhaps Cboard's most striking competitive advantage is its language coverage. While most commercial AAC apps support between 6 and 25 languages, Cboard is available in over 40 languages --- making it one of the most linguistically diverse AAC platforms in existence. Languages are community-translated via Crowdin, with recent additions including Persian (Farsi) and custom voices like the Macedonian "Suze."

AI-Powered Features

Cboard has embraced AI to solve real problems in AAC. From automated board creation to predictive communication, these features represent some of the most innovative work in the free AAC space.

🤖

CBuilder (AI Board Creator)

Type a prompt like "daily routine for a 5-year-old" and CBuilder generates a complete communication board using OpenAI's API. Published as the cboard-ai-engine npm package.

💡

Predictive Text

AI learns from user communication patterns, offering increasingly accurate word and phrase suggestions over time based on context and usage history.

💬

AI Sentence Creation

Launched October 2023 --- analyzes user inputs and message context to suggest complete phrases based on pictogram combinations, moving beyond simple word prediction.

🎤

Voice Cloning (ElevenLabs)

Personalized synthetic voices created from old recordings, preserving user identity for those with ALS, vocal cord paralysis, laryngeal cancer, and other conditions.

The Price Revolution

In a market where AAC apps routinely cost $250-$300, Cboard's zero price point is not merely a competitive advantage --- it is a moral statement. The OTTAA Merger

In a move that illustrates the unique advantages of open-source development, Cboard merged with OTTAA, another AAC project from UNICEF's Innovation Fund portfolio. OTTAA brings an AI-powered platform that uses contextual data --- time of day, user age, gender, and location --- to pre-select four pictograms from its database of 18,000 symbols, helping users communicate quickly in real-time.

"We had the same end goal: to return the voice to those who have lost it."
--- Cboard & OTTAA Teams, on the merger

Because both projects were open source, the collaboration could happen without legal bureaucracy, non-disclosure agreements, or discussions over Intellectual Property. The two projects now share marketing resources, technology platforms, and development tools. This merger exemplifies what open-source advocates have long argued: that freely licensed software enables a kind of collaboration that is simply impossible in the proprietary world.

Strengths & Limitations

▲ Strengths

  • Completely free --- zero cost barrier, critical for families and developing countries
  • 40+ languages --- largest language coverage in the AAC space
  • UNICEF backing --- institutional credibility and global deployment infrastructure
  • Open source (GPL-3.0) --- transparent, forkable, community-driven development
  • Cross-platform --- works on any device with a modern browser, plus Android and iOS apps
  • AI-powered CBuilder --- dramatically reduces board setup time with OpenAI integration
  • 20,000+ symbols --- access to Mulberry, ARASAAC, and Global Symbols libraries
  • ElevenLabs voice cloning --- preserves user identity through personalized voices
  • Microsoft grant --- Azure Neural TTS for high-quality speech synthesis
  • Active community --- 152+ GitHub contributors, Discord server, Open Collective funding

▼ Limitations

  • Less polished UI --- interface functional but not as refined as commercial apps
  • Browser-dependent TTS --- voice quality varies significantly across browsers/OS
  • Limited offline support --- full offline only in Chrome; other browsers restricted
  • Machine translations --- many language packs need proofreading by native speakers
  • Online board editing --- board customization still requires internet connection
  • No eye tracking --- lacks specialized access methods some commercial apps offer
  • Cross-platform variance --- feature availability differs between web, Android, iOS
  • Stability reports --- Nov 2025 review noted disappearing folders and layout issues
  • Small core team --- active maintainer team smaller than commercial competitors
  • Grant-dependent --- sustainability relies on UNICEF, Microsoft, and donations

Frequently Asked Questions

Yes. Cboard is 100% free to use and is licensed under GPL-3.0, which ensures it will remain free forever. There are no premium tiers, no in-app purchases, and no subscription fees. Development is funded through UNICEF's Innovation Fund, Microsoft's AI for Accessibility grant, and community donations via Open Collective.
Cboard works on virtually any device with a modern web browser --- desktops, laptops, tablets, and smartphones. It is also available as a native app on Android (Google Play) and iOS (App Store). The web version is a Progressive Web App (PWA) that can be installed on your home screen.
Cboard has offline capabilities through Service Worker API, but this currently works fully only in Google Chrome (desktop and Android). Other browsers have more limited offline support. Board customization still requires an internet connection. The native Android and iOS apps offer better offline functionality.
CBuilder uses OpenAI's API to generate complete communication boards from simple text prompts. For example, a speech therapist can type "daily routine for a 5-year-old" and the AI will suggest appropriate symbols, categories, and board layouts. It can also create new pictograms when existing symbol libraries don't have what's needed. The engine is open source and available as an npm package (cboard-ai-engine).
There are several ways to contribute: (1) Code contributions via the GitHub repository (cboard-org/cboard), (2) Translation proofreading through the Crowdin platform, (3) Financial donations via Open Collective, (4) Bug reports and feature suggestions through GitHub Issues, and (5) Community support via the Discord server. All contributions are welcome, regardless of technical skill level.
OTTAA was another UNICEF Innovation Fund-backed AAC project that used AI to predict pictogram selections based on context (time of day, user age, location). Both teams realized they shared the same goal, and because both projects were open source, they could merge without legal barriers. The combined project now shares marketing, technology platforms, development tools, and OTTAA's database of 18,000 pictograms.

Key Takeaways

Cboard occupies a unique position in the AAC market. It does not directly compete with premium commercial apps on features or polish, but it competes on access. For the estimated 840,000 children in Europe and Central Asia alone who could benefit from AAC, the choice is often not between Cboard and Proloquo2Go --- it is between Cboard and nothing.

What Cboard has demonstrated is that the open-source model can work for assistive technology. A project born from one developer's personal experience with ALS has grown into a tool used by thousands of children across multiple continents. It has attracted the support of UNICEF, Microsoft, and ElevenLabs. It has 152 contributors on GitHub, an active Discord community, and a clear mission that continues to attract talented people willing to donate their time.

In a world where communication is a fundamental human right, Cboard is making a compelling case that the technology to exercise that right should not come with a price tag.
