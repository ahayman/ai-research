Mechanics & Process

Unblending, witnessing, unburdening, and the 6 Fs — what the procedure actually requires of the client, and which steps depend on interoception.

Overview

Underneath "no bad parts" and "befriend your inner child" is a fairly stable procedure. The IFS clinical method has a sequence — taught in IFS Institute Level 1 trainings, codified in textbooks (Schwartz & Sweezy 2020), and reproduced consistently across clinician how-to writing. This page describes the procedure as concretely as possible: what the therapist actually says, what the client is being asked to perceive, and what each step requires of the perceptual machinery.

This matters because the mechanics meet the load-bearing question. IFS procedure assumes — at multiple steps — that the client can notice something happening inside their body and report on it. That assumption is the model's biggest interaction problem with alexithymia and low interoception. A clear picture of which steps depend on what perceptual capacity is the difference between knowing IFS as marketing copy and knowing it as a tool you can evaluate against your own profile.

The procedure has three nested layers. Unblending is the always-active background practice of separating Self from a flooded part. The 6 Fs are a six-step interview for getting to know a protector part. The exile sequence — witnessing, do-over, retrieval, unburdening, integration — is what happens after a protector gives permission to meet the wounded part it's been protecting. A typical session usually moves through unblending into 6-F territory and stops there. Reaching unburdening usually requires many sessions of protector work first.

Key Takeaways

  • The 6 Fs are a protector protocol; exile work uses a different sequence with witnessing, do-over, retrieval, unburdening, and integration.
  • The "Feel toward" question (the fourth F) is a diagnostic gateway. A non-C answer ("frustrated," "scared") tells the therapist another part is blended; the protocol pivots to that part rather than failing.
  • IFS has two access modes — in-sight (client perceives parts internally) and direct access (therapist speaks to the part directly). Both are canonical Schwartz-approved IFS. Direct access is the procedural lifeline when in-sight access isn't available.
  • The unburdening ritual standardly releases burdens to one of five elements (light, water, fire, earth, wind), but the canonical instruction includes "or some other way of letting go" — the elements are a default menu, not a requirement.
  • Different steps require different perceptual capacities. The interoception map below is the key reference for low-interoception readers.

Prerequisites

This page assumes you have read Cat 1 (The Parts Model) and have the basic vocabulary — managers, firefighters, exiles, Self, and the 8 Cs. If those words feel unfamiliar, start there.

For low-interoception readers especially, the procedure described here often gets explained in interoception-saturated language by practitioners ("feel into," "sense into," "drop into"). This page deliberately uses precise procedural language and marks where interoception is and isn't required.

Walking Through the 6 Fs

The 6 Fs — Find, Focus, Flesh out, Feel toward, beFriend, Fears — are the canonical IFS interview procedure for getting to know a protector. The mnemonic is now standard across IFS Institute training, PESI continuing-education, and clinician how-to writing.

Provenance note. The exact "6 Fs" name appears to have been codified during IFS Institute training development, not in Schwartz's 1995 first-edition textbook. The procedure itself is unquestionably Schwartz's; the naming is a teaching device.

Step 1: Find

The therapist asks: "What do you notice if you turn your attention inward? Is there a section of your body where you feel this more? Is there anything in or around your body that doesn't fully feel like you?"

The client is being asked to locate the part. Standard answers might be "tightness in my chest," "a heaviness in my legs," "pressure behind my eyes," or "something off to the right of me." The standard adaptation when a body location doesn't surface is to widen to "in front of, behind, beside, above" — to relax "in the body" into "in the space around the body."

Required capacity. Interoceptive and/or spatial. This is the first interoception gate. A client with strong alexithymia may report "I don't know" or "nothing." That is not a failure of the protocol — it's a signal that the standard prompt isn't working. The clinical move is to widen the prompt, drop into spatial location, or shift to direct access where the body-location question isn't required.

Step 2: Focus

"Can you stay with whatever is making itself known?" "Spend some time recognizing that there is a part of you here."

The client is being asked to hold attention on the located part. This is less about new perception and more about not turning away.

Required capacity. Sustained inward attention. This is more about attention than interoception, and is generally accessible. For monotropic readers (often a feature of autistic cognition), this step can actually be an asset — sustained narrow focus on one part is what the model wants.

Step 3: Flesh Out

"What does it look like? Sound like? How old does it seem? What's it wearing? What's its facial expression? What's it doing?"

The client is being asked to see or sense the part as a figure. This is the most aphantasia-affected step. About 2–4% of the general population has aphantasia (the absence of voluntary mental imagery), and the rate may be higher among autistic populations.

Required capacity. Visual-imaginative, ideally. A common adaptation: substitute words and concepts. "It feels heavy. Old. Quiet. Like an animal that's been hiding." Or write a description rather than picture one. The figure does not have to be visual — it has to be characterized. Schwartz/Sweezy 2020 explicitly notes that some clients work in words, sounds, or felt qualities rather than images.

Step 4: Feel Toward — the Diagnostic Gateway

This is the diagnostic gateway. The therapist asks: "How do you feel toward this part?"

The answer is interpreted through the 8 Cs (curiosity, calm, compassion, clarity, courage, confidence, creativity, connectedness):

  • If the answer is in the C neighborhood — "curious," "I want to know it," "compassionate," "I feel for it," "I care about it" — Self is present and the work proceeds to beFriend.
  • If the answer is not in the C neighborhood — "frustrated," "annoyed," "scared of it," "I want it to go away," "I hate it" — another part has blended in. The protocol pivots: ask the blending part to step back, or make it the new target and run the 6 Fs on it instead.
  • If the answer is "nothing," "neutral," or "I don't know" — ambiguous. Could be Self's calm openness; could be protective dissociation. The therapist takes time to discern.

PESI clinical training calls Feel toward "a Geiger Counter for Self-energy." That framing captures the diagnostic role exactly.

Required capacity. Interoceptive emotional read. This is the single largest interoception bottleneck in the protocol. The whole step asks the client to notice an internal feeling state and label it — exactly what alexithymia disrupts. The 2016 Shah et al. Cortex paper found that alexithymia (not autism per se) drives interoceptive impairment; for the ~50% of autistic adults who also meet alexithymia criteria, this step is structurally hard.

There are partial workarounds. Sometimes the read is approached behaviorally rather than felt-sensed: "If this part were here, what would I want to do — approach it or move away from it? Lean toward or pull back?" That converts an interoceptive question into a relational/spatial one. Direct access bypasses this step entirely, since the therapist talks to the part regardless of whether the client can locate Self-energy. See Cat 6 for the deep treatment.

Step 5: beFriend

"Is the part aware of you? Is it willing to connect? Let it know you're here."

This is the relational/dialogic step. Self introduces itself; the part may be wary, hostile, hopeful, or surprised. The work is to build a working relationship — not to fix anything yet.

Required capacity. Relational/dialogic. The capacity to imagine or sense reciprocal exchange. Less interoception-dependent than Find or Feel toward; more like having a conversation with an imagined figure. For a literal-thinking client, this step can be approached as "what would I say to someone like this if they showed up in my life? What might they say back?" — concretely dialogic rather than felt-sensed.

Step 6: Fears

"What does this part fear would happen if it stopped doing its job? What does it achieve by doing what it does?"

The part articulates its protective logic. Almost always, the answer points to an exile. "If I stopped, you'd feel worthless again." "If I stopped, you'd feel the loneliness." "If I stopped, the kid would be hit."

Required capacity. Cognitive/dialogic. This is the most accessible step in the protocol for low-interoception, literal-thinking clients. It's explicit, sentence-shaped, and asks the part for its reasoning — which a thinking-style client often handles with ease.

By the end of the Fears step, you usually know which exile the protector is protecting. That's the bridge to the exile sequence — but only with permission.

Unblending in Detail

Unblending is the always-active background practice. Blended means the client is a part rather than relating to it; the part's perspective feels like absolute truth ("I'm worthless," not "a part of me feels worthless"). Unblended means the client can notice the part as separate from Self and relate from a slightly more spacious position.

The standard unblending move:

  1. Notice the part — body tension, charged emotion, rigid thought pattern.
  2. Name it — "a part of me is anxious," not "I am anxious."
  3. Ask the part to step back — "would you give me some space so I can get to know you?"
  4. Check for Self-energy — do I now have access to some of the 8 Cs?
  5. Relate from there — once Self is present, the work can proceed.

This is also exactly what's happening at the Feel toward step. "How do you feel toward this part?" is the recurring unblending check — running silently in the background of every move. A non-C answer means the unblending isn't complete and another part is in the way.

When a part won't step back, it becomes the new target. This is not a failure — it's the procedure. That blending part now gets its own 6-F interview ("Find the part that won't let me work with the original target. Where is it? What does it look like? How do you feel toward it?"), and the original target gets revisited later when the system is more available.

When the client cannot notice the part as separate at all — fully fused, can't get any distance — the therapist switches to direct access.

A Theoretical Note on Self-Energy

Self-energy is operationally defined by its effects (the 8 Cs), and its absence is interpreted as "a part is in the way." This makes the construct hard to disconfirm. A clinician with this lens cannot encounter a session in which Self is genuinely unavailable — only sessions in which a part is masking it. The procedure works either way, which is one reason the model is hard to falsify.

Evidence note. Self-energy as a construct is internally consistent and clinically useful, but is not an independently measurable variable. Its diagnostic loop ("if not present, then a part is in the way") is part of why the model has been criticized for unfalsifiability. Cat 5 of this deep dive treats this critique in depth. Knowing the procedural limit doesn't invalidate the procedure — but it does mean "I felt Self was present" and "I felt Self was not present" are interpretations through the model's frame, not raw observations of an external thing.

In-Sight Access vs. Direct Access

IFS has two procedural modes for engaging parts. Both are canonical — taught at Level 1, defined in the IFS Institute glossary, used by Schwartz himself.

In-sight access is what most public IFS material describes. The client looks inward, perceives the part, reports back to the therapist, and a Self-to-part conversation happens internally. This is what the 6 Fs as written above assume. It depends on the client being able to perceive parts inside themselves — through felt sense, imagery, inner voice, or some combination.

Direct access is the IFS method in which the therapist speaks directly to the client's part. The client may explicitly hand over the floor ("can the part talk to you directly?" — explicit direct access) or simply be speaking from the part already without contracting it ("I can hear that the cynical part is here right now" — implicit direct access). Either way, the therapist interviews the part as if speaking to a person.

Direct access is the right choice when:

  • The client is so blended that no Self is available;
  • The client lacks visualization or interoceptive access;
  • The part refuses to step back;
  • The client is in crisis or severely dissociated;
  • The client has learned aversion to "imagining" or "feeling into" things;
  • The client is a child who doesn't easily map onto in-sight framing.

For clients with alexithymia or aphantasia, direct access may be the primary procedural mode, not a workaround. The IFS literature has historically led with in-sight in public-facing material, but Fran Booth's IFS Institute training materials describe direct access as "an essential IFS skill," and Schwartz's own teaching treats it as a core mode. The Schwartz/Sweezy 2nd edition (2020) includes substantial procedural detail on direct access that is harder to find in trade-press IFS books.

This is operationally important for the user's profile. When public IFS marketing implies that "feeling into the body" is the only way IFS works, that is misleading. Direct access exists, is canonical, and produces the same kind of work — Self-to-part relationship building, protector consent, witnessing — without requiring the client to access most of the interoception-gated steps.

Witnessing the Exile

Once a protector has given permission to meet the exile, the work crosses into different terrain. The 6 Fs interview a protector; witnessing is what Self does with an exile.

The therapist asks: "What does the exile want you to know? Let the exile show you what happened. Stay with it."

The exile shows — through memory, image, body sensation, words, or some mixture. Self stays present. The crucial discipline is what Self doesn't do: not fix, not reassure, not rush past, not interpret. Self listens. The Way of Belonging summary describes it as "being with, acknowledging the pain and offering tenderness." Therapy with Alessio's tradition emphasizes "the exile feels seen." Until that seen-ness lands, unburdening typically won't take.

This is also the most procedurally sensitive step. There's a known failure mode where the client becomes the exile rather than relating to it — Self drops out, flooding starts, the original wound is being re-experienced rather than witnessed. That is retraumatization, not therapy. The therapist's job in witnessing is to keep Self present and to slow things down or pause if flooding begins.

A second concern lives here too. In the witnessing process, an exile may show "memories" of events the client doesn't otherwise remember. The IFS literature does not, in general, treat these as veridical autobiographical recall — they're treated as how the part is presenting its experience. But in some clinical hands, witnessing can shade into uncritical acceptance of recovered material, which is the failure mode at the heart of older recovered-memory critiques (and a frequent reader-confusion vector — note that the Castlewood lawsuits involved Mark Schwartz, no relation to Richard Schwartz; see Cat 5 for the disambiguation). The procedural safeguard is the same as for retraumatization: stay in Self, take it slowly, don't proceed faster than the system allows. See Cat 9 for the full catalogue of failure modes.

Unburdening with Elements; Do-Overs and Retrieval

After witnessing, the exile is no longer alone in its original scene. The next moves — interleaved more than strictly sequenced — are do-over, retrieval, and unburdening.

Do-over (or "redo") is a corrective experience in imagination. Self goes into the original scene and offers what was missing — protection, words the exile never heard, intervention against the harm. "What would you have needed back then? Can you go offer that now?" This is sometimes framed as memory reconsolidation, the proposed neural mechanism by which a corrective experience updates the encoded prediction the exile had been holding. (The reconsolidation framing is suggestive but not directly empirically tested in IFS contexts — it remains a proposed mechanism, not an established one.)

Retrieval is the move of bringing the exile from the past scene to a safe present-day location. The exile decides where; common destinations include a meadow, beach, room in a sanctuary, the body, or some imagined safe place. Way of Belonging notes that retrieval can happen at any point in the healing process or not at all — the decision rests with the exile.

Unburdening is the formal release ritual. The structure:

  1. Identify the burden. "What do you carry?" "Shame." "Worthlessness." "Fear of being left." "Heaviness in the chest."
  2. Locate it in the body. Where does the exile carry the burden? Chest, throat, head, surrounding the figure. (This step is interoception-gated.)
  3. Choose an element. Light, water, fire, earth, wind/air. The canonical instruction is "or some other way of letting go" — the elements are a default menu, not a requirement. Some clinicians add space, gravity, breath. Some clients prefer non-elemental release.
  4. Release. The exile gives the burden to the chosen element — into the wind, washed away in water, burned in fire, buried in earth, dissolved in light. The client visualizes or senses this happening.
  5. Notice what arises in the burden's place. A quality the exile couldn't express: joy, playfulness, curiosity, calm. This is sometimes called the "invitation."
  6. Integration. The unburdened part — now with its restored qualities — is welcomed back into the system. The protector that was protecting this exile is renegotiated: "now that your old job isn't needed, what would you like to do?"

When unburdening fails, the most common reason is that one of the prerequisites was skipped. The exile didn't feel witnessed. The protector didn't consent. Self wasn't actually present and a part was leading the unburdening. In all of these cases, the system reasserts the burden — not because the procedure didn't "work" but because the system isn't yet in a state where release is appropriate. Premature unburdening is one of the most-named failure modes in the IFS clinical literature (see Cat 9).

The Interoception Map

This is the section the page exists to deliver. Here is a step-by-step map of which IFS procedural moves require what perceptual capacity, with notes on substitutions for low-interoception clients.

Step Required Capacity Difficulty for low interoception Substitutions
Find Interoceptive or spatial — locate part in/around body High (interoception-gated) Widen from "in body" to "around body / in front / behind / above"; locate via behavior ("when this part is active, what do I do?"); use direct access
Focus Sustained inward attention Generally accessible None usually needed; can be an asset for monotropic clients
Flesh out Visual-imaginative High for aphantasia Substitute words, sound, conceptual sketch; written description of the part; characterize without picturing
Feel toward Interoceptive emotional read Highest barrier — the bottleneck Approach behaviorally ("approach or avoid?"); use direct access (bypasses); accept "I don't know" without forcing a feeling
beFriend Relational / dialogic Generally accessible Frame concretely as conversation; written dialogue between Self and part
Fears Cognitive / dialogic Accessible — often easiest for thinking-style clients Asks for reasoning; works fine in writing
Witnessing (exile) Sustained Self-presence with felt receptivity Hard if interoception is low — but mostly an attention/intention problem Lean on the intention of presence rather than the felt sense; use external markers (therapist's presence, written reflection)
Do-over Visual-imaginative + symbolic Hard for aphantasia and literal-thinking clients Reframe as "what does the part need to know now, with you here?"; written do-over (Self writes a letter to the exile); speak it aloud
Retrieval Visual-imaginative Hard for aphantasia Use a real location the client knows; substitute description for imagery; can be skipped if exile doesn't request it
Unburdening — locate in body Interoceptive High for alexithymia Substitute "where in the figure" rather than "where in your body"; use behavioral location; sometimes verbal characterization suffices
Unburdening — element release Visual / symbolic Hard for aphantasia "Any way that feels right" — words, breath, just letting it go; non-visual release
Integration Cognitive / relational Generally accessible Written reflection; verbal renegotiation with the protector
Direct Access (alternate mode) Cognitive / dialogic Most accessible for low-interoception clients This is the systemic substitute. Whole sessions can be conducted in direct access.

A reading of this table for the user's profile: the steps that work most cleanly are Focus, Fears, beFriend, Integration, and the entire direct-access mode. The steps that need explicit adaptation are Find (use spatial/behavioral substitutes), Flesh out (use words), Feel toward (use behavioral approach-or-avoid, or accept "I don't know" without forcing), do-over and retrieval (consider written or behavioral versions), unburdening's body-location and element-visualization steps. Witnessing sits in the middle — most of it is intention and attention, both accessible.

The takeaway is not "IFS doesn't work for low-interoception clients." The takeaway is that the procedure has both interoception-gated and non-interoception-gated paths, and a clinician working with a low-interoception client should be deliberately leaning on the non-gated paths — which means leaning on direct access, on cognitive/dialogic steps, and on substituting written or behavioral access where the standard prompts assume internal sensing.

Session Arc and Pacing

A typical session opens with check-in — body scan, "what's coming up for you," or following whatever the client brings. A target part surfaces, usually a protector that's been active in the client's recent life. Unblending begins (often happens silently as the client describes the part). The 6 Fs unfold around the target. By the end of the Fears step, the exile being protected is usually identifiable.

From there, two paths. Most sessions stay with the protector — building trust, hearing fears, negotiating. This is normal. The exile sequence has many prerequisites, and rushing them is the canonical failure mode. Some sessions move into the exile sequence (witnessing → do-over → retrieval → unburdening → integration), often after weeks or months of protector work has built sufficient consent. Most sessions don't unburden, and that doesn't mean the session was unproductive.

Closing matters. The standard close: thank the parts who showed up, name where the work paused (so it can be picked up next session), ground the body (orient to the room, breath, posture), and signal a gentle return to outside life. Some clinicians assign homework — journaling, parts check-ins, written dialogue between sessions. Self-led journaling-based parts work is a substantial topic and gets full treatment in Cat 10.
