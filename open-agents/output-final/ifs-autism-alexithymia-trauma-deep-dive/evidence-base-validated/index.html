What "evidence-based" really means — and why it matters here

"Evidence-based" is one of the most overworked phrases in modern psychotherapy marketing, and IFS sits in the middle of the linguistic mess. In contemporary clinical research, the phrase is a spectrum, not a binary. At the minimal threshold, "evidence-based" can mean: at least one peer-reviewed trial showing that the intervention does something on at least one outcome in at least one population. At the strong threshold, it means: replicated randomized controlled trials, in independent labs, showing between-arm superiority over credible active comparators across multiple conditions, ideally backed by meta-analysis.

These are different planets. CBT for depression sits at the strong end — hundreds of RCTs over decades, multiple meta-analyses, replicated findings. EMDR for PTSD sits there too. IFS, as of April 2026, sits at the minimal end — and only for one or two conditions.

That's not an attack. It's a calibration. Some genuinely useful clinical models start out with thin evidence and accumulate it as they age. Some never do. IFS is currently somewhere in the middle of that arc: the model has been around for roughly 40 years, has been adopted at industrial scale (45,764 therapists self-listed it on Psychology Today as of April 2024), and has produced two RCTs and a handful of pilot studies. That ratio of adoption to evidence is the central thing this page exists to make visible.

How to use this page The reader's job here isn't to decide whether IFS "works." It's to know, study by study, what each piece of evidence actually does and does not establish — so that downstream marketing claims (and downstream critiques) can be weighed against the actual data.

Shadick et al. 2013 — the rheumatoid-arthritis RCT (the strongest single trial)

Shadick NA et al. (2013). A Randomized Controlled Trial of an Internal Family Systems-based Psychotherapeutic Intervention on Outcomes in Rheumatoid Arthritis: A Proof-of-Concept Study. Journal of Rheumatology 40(11): 1831–1841.

Design
RCT, proof-of-concept
Sample
n=79 (39 IFS, 40 control)
Comparator
Mailed education
Funding/COI
Schwartz senior author; Sweezy co-author; Harvard infrastructure

The 2013 Shadick et al. paper in the Journal of Rheumatology remains the most-cited and methodologically strongest IFS trial. It was a two-arm randomized controlled trial conducted at Brigham and Women's Hospital / Harvard Medical School. Seventy-nine patients with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) were randomized to either a 9-month IFS group intervention (n=39) or an education control (n=40) that received mailed materials about RA symptoms and management. Sixty-eight completed assessments. The authors describe the study explicitly as "proof-of-concept."

At posttreatment, the IFS arm showed greater improvement than the education arm in overall pain (mean change −14.9, p=0.04) and physical function (+14.6, p=0.04). At 1-year follow-up — the more impressive number — the IFS arm showed sustained benefits in self-assessed joint pain (−0.6, p=0.04), self-compassion (+1.8, p=0.01), and depressive symptoms (−3.2, p=0.01). What didn't change: anxiety, self-efficacy, and the objective biological markers of RA disease activity (DAS-CRP4) measured by blinded rheumatologists. The IFS effect, in this trial, was on patient-reported psychological and pain-related outcomes — not on the disease itself.

Honest sentence IFS has one proof-of-concept RCT showing sustained 1-year benefits in pain, depression, and self-compassion in rheumatoid arthritis, with a deliberately weak comparator (mailed education) and partial IFS-Institute authorship. The senior author is the IFS founder; the Harvard rheumatology infrastructure is independent.

What it establishes: IFS produces clinically meaningful, sustained benefits on patient-reported pain and depression in a chronic-illness population, vs. a weak control.

What it does not establish: That IFS works for any other condition. That IFS outperforms an active psychotherapy comparator. That it changes biological disease activity. That the effects generalize to populations less like the trial sample.

Haddock et al. 2016/2017 — the depression pilot RCT (equivalence, not superiority)

Haddock SA, Weiler LM, Trump LJ, Henry KL (2017). The Efficacy of Internal Family Systems Therapy in the Treatment of Depression Among Female College Students: A Pilot Study. Journal of Marital and Family Therapy 43(1): 131–144 (online 2016).

Design
Pilot RCT
Sample
n≈32 (IFS=17, TAU=15)
Comparator
TAU = CBT or IPT
Finding
No significant between-group difference

The 2017 Haddock paper in the Journal of Marital and Family Therapy is the second of IFS's two RCTs. It was a small pilot study of female college students with depression, randomized to IFS (n=17) or treatment-as-usual (TAU, which meant CBT or IPT) (n=15). The analytic sample was n≈32; some sources cite 37 enrolled. Each arm was supposed to receive 16 sessions; many participants completed 11–15.

The headline finding: both arms showed similar declines in depressive symptoms. The authors found no significant between-group differences in either the magnitude or rate of change. Their conclusion: "preliminary evidence for the efficacy of IFS in the treatment of depressive symptoms."

Equivalence vs. superiority — the key distinction "Preliminary evidence for efficacy" and "no significant difference between IFS and CBT/IPT" are both technically true sentences about the same data. But the second is what statistical inference actually supports; the first invites the reader to round up. The Haddock study was an underpowered superiority trial that failed to find a difference. With n=32, the trial cannot confidently distinguish "the two treatments are equivalent" from "the trial was too small to detect a difference if one exists." A real equivalence trial would have pre-specified an equivalence margin and required substantially more power than a superiority test of the same size.

Imbalances worth tracking: 4 of 5 IFS therapists had less than 1 year of experience; the IFS group was effectively learning on the job. More than half of the TAU participants were on antidepressants concurrently; none of the IFS participants were. These imbalances cut both ways.

Honest sentence A small pilot RCT (n≈32) of depressed female college students found no significant difference between IFS and TAU (CBT or IPT). The trial was too small to confirm equivalence; it found no detectable difference, with caveats about therapist experience and concurrent medication use.

Hodgdon et al. 2021 — the PTSD pilot (the marketing number — and the methodological asterisks)

Hodgdon HB et al. (2021/2022). Internal Family Systems Therapy for PTSD Among Survivors of Multiple Childhood Trauma: A Pilot Effectiveness Study. Journal of Aggression, Maltreatment & Trauma 31(1): 22–43.

Design
Uncontrolled, single-arm, within-subject
Sample
n=17 (13 completers)
Reported effect size
CAPS d = −4.46; DTS d = −3.05
Funding
Foundation for Self Leadership

The 2021 Hodgdon et al. paper in the Journal of Aggression, Maltreatment & Trauma is the source of the most-marketed IFS statistic. It's also the one that, when you read it carefully, requires the most calibration.

The design first. This was an uncontrolled, open-label, single-arm pilot effectiveness study with within-subject (pre/post) design. There was no control group. There was no randomization. Seventeen adult survivors of multiple childhood traumas with current PTSD diagnoses received 16 weekly 90-minute IFS sessions. Of the 17, 13 were completers in some analyses. Funding came from the Foundation for Self Leadership — the IFS Institute's research-funding nonprofit arm. The lead author was at the Trauma Center / Justice Resource Institute; co-authors include Frank Anderson and Schwartz, both senior IFS-Institute figures.

The headline numbers: at 1-month follow-up, 92% of completers (12 of 13) no longer met criteria for PTSD. The within-subject Cohen's d for the Clinician-Administered PTSD Scale (CAPS) was reported as d = −4.46. The companion Davidson Trauma Scale d was −3.05.

Why d = −4.46 is implausibly large A within-subject Cohen's d of −4.46 means the post-treatment mean is roughly four-and-a-half standard deviations below the pre-treatment mean. In the credible PTSD treatment literature — including for trauma-focused therapies that are the field's gold standards (Prolonged Exposure, Cognitive Processing Therapy, EMDR) — within-group effect sizes in well-conducted RCTs typically land in the d ≈ 0.8–1.5 range. Between-group effects (vs. waitlist) usually fall in the d ≈ 0.5–1.0 range. A within-group d of −4.46 is several times larger than anything credible the field has produced.

The authors themselves acknowledge this in the limitations section. They write that "this was an open, uncontrolled trial using a within subjects design, which likely inflated the effect size to some degree" and that "these effect sizes are larger than what has been observed in the PTSD treatment literature." That admission, buried in limitations, doesn't usually make it into IFS marketing materials, where "92% no longer met PTSD criteria" travels alone.

Honest sentence An uncontrolled pilot of 17 adult survivors of childhood trauma with PTSD found that 92% of completers no longer met PTSD criteria at 1-month follow-up after 16 sessions of IFS. The reported within-group effect size (d = −4.46) is several times larger than anything in the credible PTSD treatment literature; the authors themselves acknowledge the design "likely inflated the effect size to some degree." Treat as a feasibility signal pending a real RCT.

Comeau et al. 2024 — the second PTSD pilot (more credible numbers, same single-arm caveat)

Comeau A, Smith LJ et al. (2024). Online group-based internal family systems treatment for posttraumatic stress disorder: Feasibility and acceptability of the program for alleviating and resolving trauma and stress. Psychological Trauma: Theory, Research, Practice, and Policy.

Design
Single-arm proof-of-concept
Sample
n=15 (13 responders)
Reported effect size
CAPS-5 d = −0.7 (wk 16); −0.9 (wk 24)
Funding
Foundation for Self Leadership

In 2024, a different research group published a second IFS PTSD pilot in Psychological Trauma — the PARTS (Program for Alleviating and Resolving Trauma and Stress) study, led by Comeau et al. at Cambridge Health Alliance / Harvard. This one had n=15 (13 responders), was also single-arm and Foundation-funded, and tested an online group format (16 weekly group sessions). Attendance was 73% completion of 12 or more sessions; 92% of responders said they would recommend it; 100% found it helpful.

The clinically interesting numbers: CAPS-5 PTSD severity reduction d = −0.7 at week 16, d = −0.9 at week 24. 53% achieved a clinically meaningful response (defined as ≥10-point CAPS-5 reduction). Secondary improvements in decentering, self-compassion, and emotion regulation.

The contrast that's actually informative Putting Hodgdon 2021 (d = −4.46) and Comeau 2024 (d ≈ 0.7–0.9) side by side is itself the signal. The smaller, more measured Comeau numbers are probably closer to what IFS actually does for PTSD; the Hodgdon number is likely an artifact of the design. Both are single-arm and Foundation-funded; neither is a real RCT. But the Comeau pilot suggests that when IFS for PTSD is studied carefully, the effects look like real psychotherapy effects rather than miracles. The authors of Comeau themselves note: "well-controlled efficacy research is needed."

Ally et al. 2025 — the PARTS-SUD pilot (substance use, the newest data point)

Ally D, Tobiasz-Veltz L, Tu K et al. (2025). A pilot study of an online group-based Internal Family Systems intervention for comorbid posttraumatic stress disorder and substance use. Frontiers in Psychiatry 16: 1544435.

Design
Single-arm pilot
Sample
n=10; 70% retention
Outcomes
PCL-5 −1.7 pts/wk (p=0.002); craving −0.25/wk (p=0.014)
Funding
Schwartz Research Fellowship; Foundation

The 2025 Frontiers in Psychiatry paper by Ally et al. extended the PARTS framework to comorbid PTSD and substance use disorder (PARTS-SUD). It's a single-arm pilot with n=10. Twelve weekly 90-minute group sessions plus six individual 50-minute sessions over 12 weeks; online/telehealth format. The sample was diverse (40% White, 40% Hispanic/Latinx, 20% Asian; 40% male, 40% female, 20% non-binary). Foundation-funded, with the Richard C. Schwartz Research Fellowship.

Acceptability metrics were strong: 86% general acceptability, 92% willingness to recommend, 70% retention at 12 weeks. The exploratory clinical outcomes: PCL-5 PTSD severity dropped 1.7 points/week (95% CI −2.45 to −0.93, p=0.002), and craving dropped 0.25 points/week (95% CI −0.45 to −0.06, p=0.014). 54% achieved a clinically meaningful difference on the PCL-5.

Honest sentence A 2025 single-arm Foundation-funded pilot of online group IFS for n=10 adults with comorbid PTSD and substance use found high acceptability and within-subject reductions in PTSD severity and craving. Preliminary feasibility/acceptability signal; not efficacy.

Buys 2025 — the scoping review (the most calibrating single source)

If you read only one paper on IFS evidence, read the 2025 Buys scoping review in Clinical Psychologist. It searched Medline, Scopus, PsycINFO, and Google Scholar for peer-reviewed IFS research and applied scoping-review inclusion criteria (broader than systematic-review criteria). The result: 27 studies met inclusion. The breakdown:

  • 2 RCTs (~7%): Shadick 2013 (RA), Haddock 2016/2017 (depression)
  • 5 quasi-experimental studies (~19%)
  • 3 qualitative exploratory studies (~11%)
  • 17 case studies (~63%)

That ratio is the single most useful fact you can carry away from this page. After roughly 40 years of model development, two-thirds of the IFS-research literature is case studies — the lowest tier of clinical evidence. Seven percent is RCT. Buys's synthesized conclusion is that the existing evidence is "promising" for chronic pain, depression, PTSD, and self-compassion development, but that "larger fully-powered clinical trials are needed for each clinical indication" and — importantly — that adverse-event monitoring is largely absent across the IFS evidence base.

The adverse-event gap Most published IFS studies don't systematically report harms. This isn't unique to IFS — psychotherapy trials in general are bad at reporting harms — but for a complex-trauma reader, the absence matters: premature unburdening, over-flooding, and retraumatization are documented failure modes (see Category 9), and a research base that doesn't monitor for them gives you no data on how often they happen.

SAMHSA NREPP — the correction that matters

This is the single most-misleading line in IFS marketing, and it deserves slow handling.

In November 2015, IFS was added to SAMHSA's National Registry for Evidence-Based Programs and Practices (NREPP), based on an application submitted in April 2014. The NREPP rating, after independent SAMHSA review, was: "effective" for general functioning and well-being, and "promising" for depression, anxiety, physical health, and self-concept. The single underlying study was Shadick 2013. Note carefully: the rating was for "general functioning and well-being." It was NOT for PTSD, was NOT for any specific clinical condition, and was based on one trial in rheumatoid arthritis.

Correction — NREPP no longer exists
  • The administering contract was terminated for "the convenience of the government" on December 28, 2017.
  • The website was effectively shuttered and phased out in early 2018.
  • SAMHSA Assistant Secretary Elizabeth McCance-Katz publicly characterized NREPP's standards as inadequate and the registry's coverage as "skewed."
  • In April 2018, SAMHSA launched a different "Evidence-Based Practices Resource Center" that explicitly does not replace NREPP.
  • The "samhsa-nrepp.foundationifs.org" URL that IFS marketing currently links to is a private archive maintained by the IFS Foundation. It mirrors the 2015 NREPP entry. It is not a federal site. It is not a current endorsement.

When IFS marketing copy says "IFS is on the SAMHSA registry," translate that as: "IFS was added to a SAMHSA registry in 2015 for general functioning and well-being. That registry was discontinued by SAMHSA in 2018. The current archive is hosted by an IFS-affiliated foundation, not by the federal government."

Treat the NREPP claim, in 2026, as a historical fact about a registry that no longer exists — not as a current institutional credential.

The funding-source pattern

A separate concern, related but distinct: as of April 2026, every IFS clinical trial and pilot in the published peer-reviewed literature is either funded by the Foundation for Self Leadership (the IFS Institute's research nonprofit arm) or has authorship including the IFS founder or other senior IFS-Institute figures. This is a pattern across:

  • Shadick 2013 — Schwartz senior author, Sweezy co-author; Harvard infrastructure mitigates partially
  • Haddock 2016/2017 — Foundation-supported per Foundation press materials
  • Hodgdon 2021 — Foundation funded
  • Comeau 2024 — Foundation funded
  • Ally 2025 — Foundation funded plus Richard C. Schwartz Research Fellowship

The Foundation publicly states it operates at arm's length, has independent review processes, and "will not attempt in any way to influence the results of the research." That is the standard COI mitigation language. It is not the same as evidence produced by funders with no stake in the outcome.

This concentration is not, by itself, a verdict. Foundation funding has produced what we have, and what we have isn't nothing. But it is the reason why the most-needed missing piece in the IFS evidence base is independent, non-IFS-affiliated replication of any of the existing signals. Until that happens, "evidence-based IFS" will keep meaning, in practice, "evidence produced or funded by the people who teach and certify and sell IFS."

Allegiance effects in psychotherapy research are well-documented across modalities (researchers' allegiance to a treatment correlates with the size of the effects they report). That's a general background fact, not an IFS-specific accusation. It just argues for putting IFS effect-size estimates in a slightly tighter prior than their face-value numbers would suggest, and weighting the most credible-looking non-extreme estimates (Comeau d ≈ 0.7–0.9) more than the implausible-looking ones (Hodgdon d = −4.46).

For a calibrated user — particularly one with a complex-trauma history, suspected alexithymia, and an autistic neurotype — the cleanest read is this: IFS has real preliminary evidence in chronic-illness adjunctive therapy (RA) and in trauma populations (small pilots). It has effectively no evidence in your specific intersectional population. Whether that means "use cautiously, with adaptations, knowing the data is thin" or "wait for evidence before committing" is a judgment call that depends on what alternatives are available and what your therapist thinks.

The honest framing Neither "IFS is unsupported quackery" nor "IFS is evidence-based and proven." Somewhere in the middle — and exactly where in the middle is what this page is for.
