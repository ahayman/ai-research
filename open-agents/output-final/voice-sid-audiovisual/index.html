Audio-Visual Multimodal Speaker Diarization

Speaker diarization answers "who spoke when" in a recording. While traditional systems rely on audio alone, multimodal approaches incorporate visual and semantic signals from video to dramatically improve accuracy -- especially in challenging real-world conditions with overlapping speech, background noise, and multiple participants.

Key Takeaways
  • Multimodal diarization fuses audio (speaker embeddings), visual (face/lip detection), and semantic (text content) signals to determine who is speaking.
  • Visual cues are especially valuable for resolving overlapping speech -- the single biggest source of diarization errors.
  • The ACL 2025 tri-modal approach using joint pairwise constraint propagation consistently outperforms audio-only and two-modality baselines.
  • Audio-visual synchronization models like SyncNet and TalkNet match lip movements to voice activity for active speaker detection.
  • Real-world challenges remain: occlusion, off-camera speakers, poor lighting, and computational cost.

Introduction

Speaker diarization is the task of partitioning an audio (or audio-visual) stream into speaker-homogeneous segments. The output is a timeline annotated with speaker labels -- enabling downstream tasks like meeting transcription, subtitle generation, and conversation analytics.

Traditional audio-only diarization pipelines follow a well-established sequence: Voice Activity Detection (VAD) identifies speech regions, speaker embeddings are extracted for each segment, and clustering groups segments by speaker identity. While this works well in controlled environments, it struggles badly with overlapping speech, noisy backgrounds, and high speaker counts -- exactly the conditions found in real meetings, broadcasts, and movies.

Audio-visual multimodal diarization augments this pipeline with visual and semantic information, exploiting the fact that video recordings contain rich complementary signals: who is visible, whose lips are moving, and what is being said.

Why Multimodal Approaches Improve Diarization

Each modality provides unique information that compensates for the limitations of others:

🎵 Audio

Speaker identity from vocal characteristics. Works even when speaker is off-camera.

  • Robust speaker discrimination via embeddings
  • Fails with overlapping speech
  • Sensitive to noise/reverberation

👁 Visual

Active speaker detection from face and lip movements. Resolves overlaps when speakers are visible.

  • Directly shows who is speaking
  • Handles overlapping speech well
  • Fails when speaker is off-camera

💬 Semantic

Speaker turn cues from transcribed text. Exploits conversational structure and named entities.

  • Detects speaker changes from context
  • Identifies roles (interviewer, guest)
  • Depends on ASR accuracy

The fundamental insight is that modalities fail in complementary ways. When two speakers talk simultaneously, audio clustering collapses, but video can show two moving mouths. When a speaker is off-camera, visual detection fails, but audio embeddings still capture their voice. When acoustic conditions are poor, semantic analysis of what was said can reveal speaker changes through discourse structure.

Evidence from MISP 2025 Challenge: The best-performing audio-visual speaker diarization systems at the MISP 2025 Challenge achieved a Diarization Error Rate (DER) of 8.09% -- an improvement of 7.43 percentage points over the audio-only baseline. This dramatic reduction demonstrates the value of incorporating visual information.

Audio Modality: Speaker Embeddings

The audio branch of multimodal diarization focuses on extracting speaker embeddings -- fixed-dimensional vector representations that capture the unique vocal characteristics of each speaker. These embeddings form the backbone of the clustering process that ultimately assigns speaker labels.

X-Vectors

The x-vector architecture, introduced by Snyder et al. (2018), established the deep-learning paradigm for speaker embeddings. It uses a Time Delay Neural Network (TDNN) that processes variable-length speech segments through frame-level layers, applies statistics pooling (computing mean and standard deviation across frames), and projects the result into a fixed-dimensional embedding through segment-level layers.

X-vectors replaced earlier i-vector representations and became the de facto standard for speaker verification and diarization, demonstrating strong performance in the VoxSRC and DIHARD challenges.

ECAPA-TDNN

The Emphasized Channel Attention, Propagation and Aggregation TDNN (ECAPA-TDNN) by Desplanques et al. (2020) significantly improved upon the x-vector architecture with several enhancements:

  • Res2Net modules: Frame-level layers are restructured into 1D Res2Net blocks with multi-scale skip connections, enabling the model to capture patterns at multiple temporal resolutions.
  • Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) blocks: Borrowed from face verification, these blocks model channel interdependencies to emphasize more discriminative feature channels.
  • Channel-dependent frame attention: The statistics pooling layer is upgraded with attention that lets the network focus on different subsets of frames for different channels, improving how the model aggregates temporal information.

ECAPA-TDNN embeddings have proven particularly robust for diarization, performing well under both close-talking and far-field (distant) microphone conditions. The pyannote.audio toolkit (version 3.1) uses ECAPA-TDNN embeddings within its two-stage EEND-VC pipeline, where local end-to-end neural diarization produces frame-level speaker activity predictions that are then consolidated through embedding-based clustering.

Audio Embedding Pipeline
  • Input: Short speech segments (typically 1-3 seconds) from VAD output
  • Feature extraction: Mel-frequency filterbanks or similar acoustic features
  • Model: ECAPA-TDNN or similar deep network
  • Output: Fixed-dimensional embedding vector (typically 192 or 256 dimensions)
  • Clustering: Agglomerative hierarchical clustering or spectral clustering on cosine similarity matrix

Visual Modality: Face Detection, Lip Movement, Active Speaker Detection

Visual information provides a powerful complementary signal to audio. The visual pipeline typically involves three stages: detecting faces, tracking lip movements, and determining which visible person is actually speaking.

Face Detection and Tracking

The visual pipeline begins with detecting and tracking faces across video frames. Modern face detectors (such as RetinaFace or MTCNN) locate all visible faces in each frame, and multi-object trackers (like SORT or DeepSORT) maintain consistent identity tracks across time. Each face track produces a sequence of cropped face images that can be analyzed for speaking activity.

Face recognition models can additionally generate face embeddings that, analogous to speaker embeddings on the audio side, provide a discriminative representation of visual identity. These are particularly useful for re-identifying speakers who leave and re-enter the frame.

Lip Movement Analysis

Lip movement is one of the most direct visual indicators of speaking activity. Two main approaches are used:

  • Geometric approaches: Track facial landmarks around the mouth region (typically 20 landmarks) and measure displacement over time. A lip distance threshold (commonly around 10 pixels) can distinguish speaking from silence.
  • Appearance-based approaches: Use 3D convolutional networks (e.g., ResNet-18 with 3D convolutions) to learn speaking patterns directly from sequences of mouth-region crops, capturing subtle movements that geometric methods might miss.

Active Speaker Detection (ASD)

Active Speaker Detection is the task of determining, for each visible face at each moment, whether that person is currently speaking. This is the critical bridge between visual and audio modalities.

TalkNet (ACM MM 2021)

TalkNet is a landmark architecture for active speaker detection that uses separate audio and visual temporal encoders to process their respective inputs, an audio-visual cross-attention mechanism for inter-modality interaction, and a self-attention mechanism for capturing long-term speaking patterns. Unlike earlier RNN-based approaches, TalkNet uses attention to model both short-term and long-term temporal context.

The architecture achieved 3.5% improvement on the AVA-ActiveSpeaker dataset over prior systems. An extension called TS-TalkNet (Target Speaker TalkNet) further incorporates a pre-enrolled speaker embedding to detect whether a specific target speaker is speaking, useful for scenarios where you are tracking a particular individual.

How ASD Helps Diarization: When audio detects speech but cannot determine the speaker (e.g., during overlap), ASD can identify which of the visible faces has moving lips synchronized with the audio. This directly resolves the overlap problem without needing to separate the mixed audio signal.

Semantic Modality: Text-Based Speaker Cues

The semantic modality extracts speaker-related information from the transcribed content of speech, providing cues that are completely orthogonal to acoustic and visual features. This is the newest of the three modalities to be integrated into diarization systems, and was a key contribution of the ACL 2025 tri-modal paper.

Types of Semantic Cues

  • Speaker change detection: NLP models identify points in the transcript where the speaker likely changes, based on discourse markers ("Well, I think..." vs. "No, the issue is..."), topic shifts, and turn-taking patterns.
  • Role profiling: In structured conversations (interviews, medical consultations, customer service), the vocabulary and phrasing used can identify speaker roles. An interviewer asks questions; a doctor uses medical terminology.
  • Named entity references: If a transcript contains "As Dr. Smith mentioned...", the semantic system can infer that a different speaker (not Dr. Smith) is currently talking and link a previous segment to Dr. Smith.
  • Conversational coherence: Consecutive utterances by the same speaker tend to be topically coherent, while speaker changes often correlate with topic or perspective shifts.

From Text to Pairwise Constraints

Semantic cues are typically structured as pairwise constraints between speech segments: a "must-link" constraint indicates two segments are likely from the same speaker, while a "cannot-link" constraint indicates they are likely from different speakers. These constraints are derived from:

  • Segments with very similar vocabulary/topic receive must-link constraints
  • Segments on opposite sides of a detected speaker change receive cannot-link constraints
  • Segments referencing different named roles receive cannot-link constraints

These constraints are then integrated into the clustering process alongside audio and visual constraints, as described in the joint pairwise constraint propagation section below.

Fusion Strategies

How and when to combine information from different modalities is one of the most consequential design decisions in multimodal diarization. Three main strategies have been explored.

Early Fusion (Feature-Level)

In early fusion, features from each modality are concatenated into a single joint representation before any classification or clustering. For example, audio embeddings, visual face features, and text embeddings might be concatenated into a single vector for each speech segment.

Advantages: Can learn cross-modal correlations at the lowest level. A single model learns joint representations that capture synergies between modalities.
Disadvantages: Suffers from the curse of dimensionality. Different modalities operate at different temporal resolutions and scales, making alignment challenging. Missing modalities (e.g., no video for an off-camera speaker) cause the entire fused representation to degrade.

Late Fusion (Decision-Level)

Late fusion trains separate models for each modality, producing independent diarization outputs that are combined at the decision level through techniques like weighted voting, score averaging, or learned combination functions.

A notable example is the late audio-visual fusion system by Chung et al. (2022), which combines an audio-only sub-system (using ECAPA-TDNN embeddings and spectral clustering) with a visual-centric sub-system (using face tracking and lip-sync scores). The final speaker labels are produced by merging the two outputs with rules that prefer the visual system during detected overlap regions.

Advantages: Each modality can be optimized independently. Robust to missing modalities -- if video is unavailable, the audio sub-system operates alone. Easier to debug and interpret.
Disadvantages: Cannot capture fine-grained cross-modal interactions. The combination rules may be brittle. Each sub-system misses information that would be available through earlier integration.

Hybrid Fusion

Hybrid approaches perform fusion at intermediate levels of the processing pipeline, combining the benefits of both early and late fusion. For example, audio and visual features might be fused at the embedding level (early-ish), while semantic constraints are applied at the clustering level (late-ish).

The ACL 2025 tri-modal system exemplifies hybrid fusion: it extracts modality-specific features independently, converts visual and semantic information into pairwise constraints, and then integrates these constraints into the clustering stage of an otherwise standard audio diarization pipeline. This preserves the well-tuned audio pipeline while injecting multimodal information where it matters most.

Joint Pairwise Constraint Propagation (ACL 2025)

The most significant recent advance in multimodal diarization is the tri-modal framework presented at ACL 2025 by Cheng, Wang, Deng et al. ("Integrating Audio, Visual, and Semantic Information for Enhanced Multimodal Speaker Diarization on Multi-party Conversation"). This is the first systematic effort to integrate all three modalities -- audio, visual, and semantic -- into a unified diarization system.

Core Idea

Rather than directly fusing features, the system converts visual and semantic information into pairwise constraints on speaker segments, then uses these constraints to refine the acoustic affinity matrix before clustering. This elegant formulation preserves the proven audio diarization pipeline while injecting multimodal knowledge at the most impactful point.

Algorithm Steps

  1. Audio affinity computation: Extract speaker embeddings using ECAPA-TDNN and compute a cosine similarity affinity matrix A between all speech segments.
  2. Visual constraint generation: Use face recognition and active speaker detection to build visual pairwise constraints. If two segments show the same active face, generate a "must-link" constraint. If they show different active faces, generate a "cannot-link" constraint.
  3. Semantic constraint generation: Use NLP models on ASR transcripts to detect speaker changes and role profiles. Segments on the same side of a change boundary get must-link constraints; segments across boundaries get cannot-link constraints.
  4. Constraint propagation (E2CP): The Exhaustive-and-Efficient Constraint Propagation algorithm takes the initial affinity matrix A and the set of pairwise constraints, then propagates these constraints through the graph to estimate a refined affinity matrix A'. This step spreads the information from a relatively small number of confident constraints to the entire affinity matrix.
  5. Conflict resolution: When visual and semantic constraints disagree, a weighted combination informed by audio data fidelity resolves the conflict. The system trusts constraints that are consistent with the acoustic evidence more heavily.
  6. Final clustering: Standard spectral clustering or agglomerative hierarchical clustering is applied to the refined affinity matrix A' to produce final speaker labels.
Results
  • The tri-modal system consistently outperforms audio-only and two-modality baselines across multiple datasets.
  • Even incorporating just one additional modality (audio + visual or audio + semantic) improves results, but the full three-modality combination achieves the best performance.
  • The approach largely preserves the overall system framework of standard audio diarization, making it practical to integrate into existing pipelines.
  • The constraint propagation mechanism is robust to sparse constraints -- even a small number of high-confidence multimodal constraints can significantly improve the affinity matrix.

Audio-Visual Synchronization Models

Audio-visual synchronization is the task of determining whether a video stream of a person's face is temporally aligned with an audio stream. This capability is fundamental to active speaker detection: if a face's lip movements are synchronized with the audio, that person is likely speaking.

SyncNet

SyncNet (Chung & Zisserman, 2017) was the first end-to-end lip synchronization network. It uses a two-stream architecture with separate audio and visual encoders that project their inputs into a shared embedding space. The model predicts whether audio and video are in sync by computing the similarity between audio and visual embeddings.

For speaker diarization, a pre-trained SyncNet analyzes each detected face track alongside the audio, producing a synchronization score over time. Faces with high sync scores during speech activity are identified as active speakers.

Multi-Speaker Extensions

In multi-speaker scenarios, the sync model is applied to each face track independently against the full audio mix. The system produces a synchronization score for every (face, time) pair, creating a matrix that indicates which faces are speaking at each moment. When combined with speaker embeddings, this enables joint audio-visual clustering that is far more robust than either modality alone.

Key implementation details from the literature:

  • Temporal windows: Sync scores are computed over short windows (typically 0.2-0.5 seconds) and smoothed to avoid flickering.
  • Confidence calibration: Raw sync scores are calibrated so that the threshold for "active speaker" can be set consistently across different videos and lighting conditions.
  • Multi-scale analysis: Some systems compute sync at multiple temporal scales to capture both rapid lip movements and longer-term speaking patterns.

Handling Occlusion and Off-Camera Speakers

One of the most significant challenges for audio-visual diarization is dealing with situations where the visual modality provides incomplete or misleading information.

Types of Visual Failure

Scenario Problem Mitigation Strategy
Off-camera speaker Speaker is audible but not visible in any frame Fall back to audio-only diarization for unmatched segments; visual constraints only apply to visible speakers
Partial occlusion Face partially blocked by objects, hands, or other people Use robust face detectors; reduce confidence weight for occluded detections; rely on audio for those segments
Head turn Speaker turns away from camera, hiding lip region Track face identity across pose changes; use head pose estimation to detect non-frontal frames
Poor lighting Low light or harsh shadows make face/lip detection unreliable Image enhancement preprocessing; reduce visual constraint weight when detection confidence is low
Blurred video Motion blur or low resolution corrupts lip movement features Quality-aware weighting: assign lower confidence to visual features from low-quality frames
Multiple cameras Speaker visible in some camera views but not others Multi-view fusion: aggregate visual evidence across all available camera streams

Modality Masking

A key technique is modality masking, introduced in the AVR-Net architecture for the AVA-AVD dataset. When visual features are unreliable (low face detection confidence, poor lighting, extreme pose), the system learns to mask or down-weight the visual contribution and rely more heavily on audio. This is implemented as a learned gating mechanism that dynamically adjusts the balance between modalities based on signal quality.

Design Principle
  • Robust multimodal systems must gracefully degrade to audio-only operation when visual information is unavailable or unreliable.
  • The visual modality should help when it can (visible, well-lit, frontal faces with moving lips) and step aside when it cannot (occlusion, off-camera, blur).
  • Confidence-weighted fusion is essential: never force visual information into the pipeline when it has low reliability.

Applications

Video Meetings

Video conferencing platforms (Zoom, Teams, Meet) are a natural fit for audio-visual diarization. Each participant has a dedicated video tile with a frontal face view, creating ideal conditions for lip-sync-based active speaker detection. Meeting transcription with speaker attribution is one of the most commercially valuable applications, enabling automated meeting minutes, action item extraction, and speaking time analytics.

Broadcast News and Talk Shows

Broadcast content features multiple speakers with varying amounts of screen time, frequent camera switches, and both on-camera and off-camera narration. Audio-visual diarization improves automatic indexing and search of broadcast archives, enables speaker-attributed subtitling, and supports content analysis (e.g., measuring speaking time for political candidates).

Movies and Entertainment

Movies present the most challenging scenarios: rapid camera changes, background music and sound effects, artistic lighting, characters speaking off-screen, and large casts. The AVA-AVD dataset specifically targets this domain. Applications include automatic subtitle alignment, character analysis, and accessibility tools that identify speakers for hearing-impaired viewers.

Surveillance and Security

In surveillance scenarios, audio-visual diarization can attribute speech to individuals captured on security cameras. This raises significant privacy concerns but has applications in forensic analysis and access control.

Healthcare and Education

Medical consultations and classroom lectures benefit from speaker-attributed transcription. In clinical settings, distinguishing doctor from patient speech enables automated documentation. In education, tracking teacher vs. student speech supports engagement analysis.

Evaluation Metric: Diarization Error Rate (DER)

DER is the standard metric for speaker diarization, defined as the sum of three error types divided by total speech duration:

  • False alarm (FA): Non-speech classified as speech
  • Missed speech (MS): Speech classified as non-speech
  • Speaker confusion (SC): Speech attributed to the wrong speaker

DER = (FA + MS + SC) / Total Speech Duration

Lower DER is better. State-of-the-art systems achieve DER below 10% on most benchmarks, with the best MISP 2025 systems reaching 8.09%.

Strengths and Weaknesses

Strengths

  • Overlap resolution: Visual cues directly resolve overlapping speech, the largest error source in audio-only systems.
  • Complementary failure modes: When one modality fails (e.g., noise degrades audio), another often compensates (e.g., visual still works).
  • No enrollment needed: Unlike speaker verification, diarization does not require pre-enrolled speaker profiles.
  • Rich applications: The same pipeline serves meeting transcription, broadcast indexing, and media analysis.
  • Improving rapidly: MISP 2025 results show significant year-over-year DER improvements as models and fusion strategies mature.
  • Modular architecture: Constraint-based approaches (like the ACL 2025 method) allow multimodal upgrades to existing audio pipelines.

Weaknesses

  • Off-camera speakers: Visual modality is useless when speakers are not visible, requiring graceful degradation.
  • Computational cost: Running face detection, lip analysis, ASD, and audio embedding models simultaneously is expensive.
  • Video quality dependence: Poor lighting, low resolution, and motion blur can cause visual features to hurt rather than help.
  • Privacy concerns: Face detection and tracking in video raises significant privacy and ethical questions.
  • Data scarcity: Large-scale annotated audio-visual diarization datasets are expensive to create and relatively few exist.
  • Domain gap: Models trained on meetings may not generalize to movies or surveillance footage.
  • Real-time constraints: Full multimodal pipelines are difficult to run in real-time, limiting live applications.
  • ASR dependency: The semantic modality requires accurate speech recognition, which may itself be degraded by the same noise that motivates multimodal diarization.
