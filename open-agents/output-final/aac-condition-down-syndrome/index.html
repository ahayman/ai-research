Overview

Down Syndrome (DS) is the most common genetic cause of intellectual disability worldwide, affecting approximately 1 in every 700 live births. Named after John Langdon Down, who first described the condition in 1866, Down Syndrome is caused by the presence of a full or partial extra copy of chromosome 21, which is why it is also referred to as Trisomy 21.

While individuals with Down Syndrome share certain physical features and developmental patterns, there is enormous variation in abilities, personality, and outcomes. One of the most significant areas of challenge -- and one of the most important for quality of life -- is communication. The gap between what a person with DS understands (receptive language) and what they can express verbally (expressive language) is often wide, sometimes dramatically so.

This is where Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) plays a transformative role. AAC encompasses all forms of communication other than oral speech that are used to express thoughts, needs, wants, and ideas. For individuals with Down Syndrome, AAC is not a replacement for speech -- it is a powerful bridge that supports language development, reduces frustration, and unlocks the full communicative potential that might otherwise remain hidden behind articulation difficulties.

💡 Key Insight

Receptive language (understanding) is consistently stronger than expressive language (speaking) in individuals with Down Syndrome. This means they often understand far more than they can say -- AAC helps close that gap.

🧬

Genetics & Prevalence

Down Syndrome is a chromosomal condition that occurs when an individual has a full or partial extra copy of chromosome 21. This additional genetic material alters the course of development and causes the characteristics associated with the syndrome. There are three genetic variations that can cause Down Syndrome:

Types of Down Syndrome

🧬

Trisomy 21 (Nondisjunction)

The most common form, accounting for approximately 94% of cases. Every cell in the body has three complete copies of chromosome 21 instead of the usual two. This results from a failure of chromosome 21 to separate during cell division (nondisjunction), which is typically maternally derived. Trisomy 21 is not hereditary.

🔀

Translocation

Accounts for about 4% of cases. Part of chromosome 21 breaks off and attaches (translocates) to another chromosome, usually chromosome 14. While the total number of chromosomes remains 46, the extra material from chromosome 21 causes Down Syndrome features. This is the only form that can be inherited from a parent.

🎲

Mosaic Down Syndrome

The rarest form, accounting for about 2% of cases. Only some cells have the extra chromosome 21 while others have the typical two copies. Individuals with mosaicism may show fewer characteristics of DS, as they have a mixture of normal and trisomic cells. The degree of expression varies considerably.

The incidence of Down Syndrome increases with maternal age: the risk rises from less than 0.1% at age 20 to approximately 1% at age 40 and 3% at age 45. Down Syndrome occurs across all races, ethnicities, and socioeconomic groups worldwide.

📊 Global Impact

Down Syndrome is the most common chromosomal condition diagnosed in the United States and affects people of every race, nationality, and economic level. Worldwide, it is estimated that approximately 1 in 1,000 to 1 in 1,100 live births result in Down Syndrome, with some regional variation based on maternal age demographics and prenatal screening practices.

Associated Medical Conditions

Individuals with Down Syndrome have an increased risk for several medical conditions that can also impact communication and AAC use:

  • Congenital heart defects -- Present in approximately 50% of babies with DS; the most common is atrioventricular septal defect (42%)
  • Hearing loss -- 80-90% experience some degree of conductive hearing loss, which directly impacts speech and language development
  • Vision problems -- Over 60% have vision issues requiring correction, though visual learning remains a strength
  • Thyroid disorders -- Hypothyroidism affects 15-20% and can impact energy and cognitive function
  • Hypotonia (low muscle tone) -- Present at birth in virtually all infants with DS, affecting oral motor function
  • Increased susceptibility to infections -- Including frequent ear infections, which contribute to hearing loss
  • Atlantoaxial instability -- Present in 10-20%, an instability of the upper cervical spine

How Down Syndrome Affects Communication

Communication is one of the most significantly affected developmental domains in Down Syndrome. The challenge is not simply one of delayed development; rather, there is a distinctive communication profile that characterizes DS, with specific strengths and weaknesses that have important implications for intervention strategies.

The Receptive-Expressive Gap

Perhaps the most defining characteristic of communication in Down Syndrome is the significant gap between receptive and expressive language abilities. Individuals with DS typically understand considerably more than they can express verbally. This gap means that:

  • A child may comprehend complex instructions but be unable to articulate a response
  • Vocabulary comprehension often far exceeds vocabulary production
  • Social understanding and desire to communicate are often strong, but the motor and cognitive mechanisms needed for clear speech lag behind
  • Frustration, behavioral challenges, and social withdrawal can result when individuals cannot express what they know
⚠ Common Misconception

Because individuals with Down Syndrome may have difficulty expressing themselves verbally, their cognitive abilities are frequently underestimated. Difficulty with speech does not equal difficulty with understanding. AAC helps reveal the true communicative competence that may be masked by speech limitations.

Speech Development Timeline

This is not a reflection of intelligence but rather the combined effects of hearing difficulties, motor planning challenges, and differences in auditory processing and memory.

Expressive Language Characteristics

Research has identified several consistent patterns in the expressive language of individuals with Down Syndrome:

  • Phonological difficulties -- Challenges with sound production patterns, including consonant cluster reduction, final consonant deletion, and fronting
  • Syntactic challenges -- Sentence structure is often simplified; grammar and morphology are particular weaknesses
  • Shorter mean length of utterance (MLU) -- Sentences tend to be shorter with fewer grammatical markers
  • Vocabulary relative strength -- Vocabulary, while delayed, is often a relative strength compared to grammar
  • Pragmatic strengths -- Social use of language and nonverbal communication (gestures, facial expressions) are often relative strengths
⚠

Communication Challenges

The communication difficulties experienced by individuals with Down Syndrome arise from a complex interplay of anatomical, neurological, and sensory factors. Understanding these specific challenges is essential for selecting and implementing effective AAC strategies.

Oral-Motor Weakness & Anatomical Differences

Hypotonia (low muscle tone) is present in virtually all individuals with Down Syndrome and significantly affects the muscles used for speech. The oral-motor challenges include:

  • Weak oral muscles -- Reduced strength and control of the lips, tongue, and jaw make precise articulation difficult
  • Small oral cavity -- A smaller upper jaw and relatively large tongue (not actually enlarged, but relatively large for the oral space) affect tongue placement for sound production
  • High arched palate -- Changes the acoustic properties of the mouth and affects tongue positioning
  • Open mouth posture -- Low muscle tone can lead to habitual open mouth breathing, which further impacts articulation
  • Feeding difficulties in infancy -- The same oral-motor weaknesses affect early feeding, which is connected to later speech development
📊 Key Statistic

100% of speakers with Down Syndrome showed atypical overall speech quality in research studies, and 90% showed reduced intelligibility. Additionally, childhood verbal apraxia co-occurred with oral motor skill difficulty in 98% of children surveyed.

Hearing Loss

Hearing difficulties are extremely prevalent in Down Syndrome and represent one of the most significant barriers to speech and language development:

  • 80-90% of children with DS experience some degree of conductive hearing loss
  • 46% of infants do not pass newborn hearing screening
  • Of those identified, 88% have conductive hearing loss, often related to the anatomy of the ear canal and eustachian tubes
  • Chronic otitis media (middle ear infections) is extremely common due to narrow ear canals and eustachian tube dysfunction
  • Even mild hearing loss has a cascading effect on speech perception, phonological development, and vocabulary acquisition
  • Sensorineural hearing loss may develop over time, and hearing should be monitored regularly throughout life

Childhood Apraxia of Speech (CAS)

Childhood Apraxia of Speech is a motor speech disorder that affects the planning and programming of speech movements. In Down Syndrome:

  • Approximately 15% have been formally diagnosed with CAS, though many more show clinical symptoms
  • CAS significantly compounds the intelligibility challenges already present from anatomical differences
  • Key features include: inconsistent speech errors, difficulty sequencing sounds, and reduced intelligibility with increased utterance length
  • 80% of individuals with both dysarthria and CAS showed moderate or low intelligibility

Speech Intelligibility

Perhaps the most functionally significant challenge is overall speech intelligibility -- how well others can understand the speaker. Even when individuals with DS develop speech, intelligibility often remains a lifelong challenge:

👤

Familiar Listeners

Family members and regular communication partners typically understand 50-80% of what a person with DS says, depending on context. Familiarity, shared knowledge, and nonverbal cues help compensate.

👥

Unfamiliar Listeners

Strangers or new communication partners may understand as little as 25-50% of speech. This dramatically limits social participation, independence, and access to services and opportunities.

Additional Factors

  • Verbal working memory -- Auditory short-term memory is a particular weakness, making it harder to process and reproduce sequences of sounds
  • Fluency differences -- Disfluencies (stuttering-like behaviors) and atypical speech rate are common
  • Voice quality -- Often described as breathy, hoarse, or rough due to hypotonia of the vocal folds
  • Prosody differences -- Intonation, rhythm, and stress patterns may differ from typical speech

How AAC Helps

Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) encompasses a wide range of strategies and tools that support or supplement spoken communication. For individuals with Down Syndrome, AAC serves as a crucial bridge between their often strong receptive language and social abilities and their challenged expressive speech. The benefits are wide-ranging and well-documented.

Closing the Receptive-Expressive Gap

The single most important contribution of AAC for individuals with Down Syndrome is that it allows them to express what they already understand. By providing alternative output channels -- whether through pictures, symbols, signs, or speech-generating devices -- AAC gives individuals a way to demonstrate their knowledge, share their thoughts, and participate in conversations that would otherwise be inaccessible to them.

✅ Research Finding

A systematic review of AAC in children with Down Syndrome found improvements across multiple domains: speech production, cognitive development, socialization, independence, and self-esteem. Children using speech-generating devices showed improved communication in speech, cognition, and social interaction.

Core Benefits of AAC

🗣

Immediate Communication

AAC provides an immediate means of expression while speech is still developing. A child who cannot say "I want juice" can press a button or point to a symbol to communicate the same message, reducing frustration and building confidence.

📖

Language Development

Using AAC systems exposes children to language models and structures. Seeing words and symbols organized into categories and sentences supports vocabulary growth, grammar understanding, and overall language competence.

👪

Social Participation

AAC enables meaningful social interaction with peers, family, teachers, and the wider community. It supports friendships, classroom participation, and reduces the social isolation that can result from unintelligible speech.

💪

Reduced Frustration & Behavior

When individuals can communicate their needs, wants, and feelings, challenging behaviors driven by frustration decrease significantly. AAC provides an acceptable, effective outlet for communication that might otherwise manifest as behavior.

📚

Literacy Support

AAC systems that incorporate written words alongside symbols support literacy development. Research shows that AAC technology features can help individuals with DS build decoding skills, opening doors to reading and writing.

🎯

Self-Determination

AAC empowers individuals to make choices, express preferences, advocate for themselves, and participate in decisions about their own lives -- fundamental rights that depend on effective communication.

Visual Learning & AAC

One of the most significant and well-documented cognitive strengths of individuals with Down Syndrome is their ability to learn through visual channels. This strength is critically important in AAC because most AAC systems are fundamentally visual in nature.

Visual Learning Strengths in Down Syndrome

Research consistently shows that individuals with Down Syndrome demonstrate relative strengths in:

  • Visual-spatial processing -- The ability to process and remember visual information, spatial relationships, and visual patterns
  • Visual memory -- Better retention of information presented visually compared to auditory presentation
  • Visual motor skills -- Motor and visual perceptual skills are areas of relative strength for young children with DS
  • Incidental visual learning -- The ability to pick up information from visual environments without explicit instruction
  • Social observation -- Strong observational skills and ability to learn from watching others
📚 Visual Learning Advantage

Because children with Down Syndrome are strong visual learners who rely on visual memory to recall what they have learned, AAC systems that use pictures, symbols, and written words align naturally with their cognitive strengths. This is why AAC is often more effective than speech-only approaches for early communication intervention.

How AAC Leverages Visual Strengths

🎨

Picture Communication Symbols (PCS)

Colorful, clear illustrations that represent words and concepts. PCS leverages the strong visual recognition abilities of individuals with DS, making language concrete and accessible. Symbols are organized by category and color-coded for easy navigation.

📺

Video Visual Scene Displays (VSDs)

An innovative approach that uses video clips with embedded communication "hotspots." VSDs combine the dynamic nature of real-world interaction with easily recognized visual scenes, which are especially effective for individuals who learn through visual context.

📄

Visual Schedules & Boards

Visual supports that use pictures and symbols to represent daily routines, choices, and activities. These boards serve as both communication tools and organizational aids, leveraging visual processing strengths to support independence.

💻

High-Tech AAC Displays

Tablet and device-based AAC apps use grid layouts of visual symbols that can be organized, customized, and expanded. Research shows that the layout, organization, color, and spacing of AAC displays significantly impact visual search efficiency and communication performance.

Optimizing AAC Displays for Visual Processing

Research has shown that how symbols are arranged on an AAC display significantly affects how efficiently individuals with Down Syndrome can locate and select them. Key findings include:

  • Judicious arrangement of symbols optimizes visual attention -- the spatial organization of symbols matters as much as the symbols themselves
  • Color coding by word type (nouns, verbs, adjectives) helps with visual scanning and language learning
  • Consistent placement of frequently used symbols supports motor learning and speed
  • Appropriate symbol density -- starting with fewer symbols and gradually increasing as proficiency grows
  • High contrast and clear visual boundaries between symbols reduce visual confusion

AAC as a Bridge to Speech

One of the most persistent and damaging myths about AAC is that it will prevent or delay speech development -- that if children are given an alternative way to communicate, they will have no motivation to learn to speak. Decades of research have thoroughly debunked this myth. In fact, the evidence shows the opposite: AAC supports and often accelerates verbal speech development.

✅ Evidence-Based Conclusion

There is general scientific agreement that AAC use does not hinder the development of spoken language but may actually promote its development. AAC does not stop the development of speech or someone's motivation to use their speech. It may enhance the development of natural speech and language when intervention is multimodal and incorporates forms of AAC.

How AAC Promotes Speech Development

There are several mechanisms through which AAC use actually supports the development of verbal speech in individuals with Down Syndrome:

  • Reduces communication pressure -- When a child has a reliable way to communicate (AAC), the anxiety and pressure around speaking is reduced. This more relaxed state is actually more conducive to speech attempts.
  • Provides language models -- AAC systems provide consistent, clear language models. When a child presses a button and hears "I want water," they receive an auditory model of correct speech while simultaneously communicating successfully.
  • Builds language foundations -- AAC helps children develop vocabulary, grammar, and sentence structure, all of which are prerequisites for meaningful speech. Speech without underlying language competence is of limited use.
  • Increases communication opportunities -- When communication is successful and rewarding, children are motivated to communicate more. This increased practice benefits all modes of communication, including speech.
  • Supports multimodal learning -- Seeing a symbol, hearing the spoken word, and associating it with meaning creates multiple neural pathways for language learning.

A critical finding from controlled research: children in speech-generating device (SGD) groups used more targeted spoken words than children in the speech-only group. This means that children who had access to AAC actually spoke more than children who received only traditional speech therapy. The implication is clear: AAC does not compete with speech -- it supports it.

The Signing Bridge

Sign language, particularly simplified systems like Makaton and baby sign, has a particularly strong evidence base as a bridge to speech for children with Down Syndrome:

  • Baby signs predict later vocabulary -- Research shows that baby signs produced by children with DS predicted their spoken vocabulary size one year later
  • Children naturally transition -- Many individuals naturally drop signs at their own pace as speech develops; the signs serve as a temporary scaffold
  • Motor advantage -- Gross motor skills (hand movements for signs) develop before fine motor skills (oral movements for speech), giving children an earlier communication channel
  • Visual reinforcement -- Signs are visual, aligning with the visual learning strengths of individuals with DS
  • Makaton supports speech -- Every Makaton sign is accompanied by spoken words, so children receive simultaneous auditory and visual language input
💡 Multimodal Communication

The most effective approach for individuals with Down Syndrome is multimodal communication -- using a combination of speech, signs, symbols, devices, and gestures as appropriate for the situation. Most people who cannot rely solely on speech use multiple means to communicate depending on the communication partner, context, and message. This is natural and should be encouraged, not seen as a failure to develop speech.

Lifelong Multimodal Communication

While many individuals with Down Syndrome develop functional speech, some will use AAC throughout their lives as part of a multimodal communication system. This is not a failure -- it is a practical, effective approach to communication. Key points include:

  • Some individuals use speech for familiar listeners and AAC for unfamiliar communication partners
  • AAC can serve as a backup when speech is not understood, reducing communication breakdowns
  • As individuals age, AAC needs may change -- what works at age 5 may differ from what works at age 25
  • Recent research (2025) emphasizes that AAC must support personal identity expression and meaningful participation in society across all life domains
  • Providing AAC language intervention can be a critical step to support ongoing expressive language development and use as well as overall functional communication throughout life
🛠

Types of AAC Used

A wide variety of AAC tools and strategies are used with individuals with Down Syndrome. These range from no-tech approaches that require no equipment to high-tech dedicated devices and sophisticated tablet applications. The best AAC solution is often a combination of approaches tailored to the individual's abilities, needs, and contexts.

No-Tech & Low-Tech AAC

Sign language systems are among the most commonly used and well-researched AAC approaches for children with Down Syndrome. Makaton, a simplified sign system designed for individuals with learning disabilities, is particularly popular.

Key advantages:

  • Always available -- no equipment needed
  • Leverages stronger motor skills (hands develop before oral muscles)
  • Visual nature aligns with visual learning strengths
  • Research shows improvements in language development with Makaton use
  • Signs are always used alongside speech, providing dual-modality input
  • Children naturally transition away from signs as speech develops

Limitations: Communication partners must also know signs; less effective with unfamiliar people; not accessible to individuals with significant fine motor impairments.

PECS is a structured, evidence-based system that teaches individuals to initiate communication by exchanging picture cards. It follows a structured six-phase protocol from simple single-picture exchanges to commenting and sentence construction.

Key advantages:

  • Teaches initiation of communication -- the child starts the interaction
  • 44% of children show marked increases in spoken language after using PECS
  • No complex technology to manage
  • Highly structured and evidence-based
  • Portable and relatively inexpensive
  • Improves both language and social communication

Limitations: Requires physical cards that can be lost; limited vocabulary compared to high-tech options; requires physical proximity to the communication book.

Communication boards are physical or printed displays of symbols, pictures, or words arranged by topic or category. They can range from simple choice boards (with 2-4 options) to comprehensive boards with hundreds of symbols.

Key advantages:

  • No technology required -- low cost and durable
  • Can be customized for specific activities (mealtimes, playground, classroom)
  • Context-specific boards reduce visual clutter
  • Easy for communication partners to learn and use
  • Useful as backup when technology is unavailable

Variations: Choice boards, topic boards, visual schedules, first-then boards, social stories with visual supports.

High-Tech AAC

Speech-Generating Devices and tablet-based AAC applications have revolutionized communication for individuals with Down Syndrome. These systems produce clear, synthetic speech output when the user selects symbols, words, or letters.

Popular AAC apps used with DS:

  • Proloquo2Go -- The most widely used symbol-based AAC app; robust vocabulary system with customizable displays; used extensively by people with DS
  • TouchChat HD with WordPower -- Combines symbols with word-based vocabularies; good for individuals transitioning toward literacy-based communication
  • LAMP Words for Life -- Based on Language Acquisition through Motor Planning; consistent motor patterns support motor learning
  • TD Snap -- Tobii Dynavox's flagship app; includes Snap Core First vocabulary for symbol users
  • Avaz AAC -- Specifically designed for individuals with communication difficulties including Down Syndrome
  • CoughDrop -- Open-source, cloud-based AAC with collaborative features

Key advantages of high-tech AAC:

  • Clear speech output that anyone can understand
  • Vast, expandable vocabularies
  • Customizable to individual needs and abilities
  • Support literacy development with text alongside symbols
  • Social acceptability -- tablets are mainstream devices
  • Data tracking for monitoring progress

Dedicated speech-generating devices from companies like Tobii Dynavox, PRC-Saltillo, and Forbes AAC are purpose-built communication devices. Unlike consumer tablets running AAC apps, these are ruggedized, medically approved devices designed specifically for AAC use.

Advantages over tablet-based AAC:

  • More durable construction designed for daily use
  • Often covered by insurance or funding programs as medical devices
  • Dedicated function -- cannot be distracted by games or other apps
  • May include specialized access methods (eye tracking, switch access)
  • Better speaker quality for noisy environments

When to consider: Dedicated devices are often recommended when AAC is a primary means of communication, when a more robust device is needed, or when insurance/funding makes them accessible.

The most current research and clinical practice emphasizes multimodal communication -- the integrated use of multiple communication methods. Recent developments include:

  • Video Visual Scene Displays (VSDs) -- Use video with embedded communication hotspots; leverage dynamic visual learning
  • Digital interactive boards -- Research shows significant benefits in socialization, independence, and self-esteem
  • AAC with literacy decoding features -- Technology that provides models of decoding upon symbol selection supports reading development
  • Generative language intervention (GLI) with AAC -- A 2025 randomized controlled trial demonstrated that AAC-GLI improves aided expressive grammar skills in young children with DS
  • Aided Language Stimulation (ALS) -- Communication partners model AAC use during natural interactions, one of the most valuable tools for learning AAC

Choosing the Right AAC System

Selecting the appropriate AAC system for an individual with Down Syndrome should be a collaborative process involving the individual, family, speech-language pathologist, and other team members. Key considerations include:

Factor Considerations
Age & developmental stage Young children may start with signs and simple boards; older children may benefit from robust high-tech systems
Motor abilities Fine motor skills, hand control, and visual-motor coordination affect which access methods are appropriate
Cognitive level Vocabulary size, symbol understanding, and ability to navigate multi-page systems vary widely
Communication environments Different settings (home, school, community) may require different tools or configurations
Communication partners Familiar vs. unfamiliar listeners; whether partners can learn signs or need speech output
Current speech abilities AAC should complement existing speech, not replace it; the system should grow with the individual
Family preferences & support Family buy-in and willingness to learn and model AAC use is critical for success

Communication & AAC Developmental Timeline

Understanding the typical communication development trajectory in Down Syndrome helps families and professionals plan AAC introduction and intervention strategies. The following timeline represents general patterns -- individual variation is considerable.

Birth - 6 Months
Early Communication Foundations
Infants with DS begin cooing and making eye contact, though onset may be slightly delayed. Hypotonia is evident. Hearing screening is critical -- 46% do not pass newborn hearing screening. Early intervention should begin as soon as diagnosis is confirmed.
6 - 12 Months
Babbling & Early Gestures
Babbling emerges but may be less varied. Pointing and reaching gestures develop. This is an ideal time to introduce baby signs and Makaton. Sign language can begin as soon as the child can bring hands together. Simple signs like "more," "eat," and "help" provide early communication success.
12 - 24 Months
First Signs & Emerging Words
Children begin using signs meaningfully. First spoken words may emerge around 16-24 months but many children rely primarily on signs and gestures. Low-tech AAC boards can be introduced alongside signing. PECS training may begin. Total communication approach (combining signs, words, and pictures) is ideal.
2 - 4 Years
Vocabulary Growth & AAC Expansion
Spoken vocabulary grows slowly but steadily. Many children begin combining words into two-word utterances. High-tech AAC introduction is often appropriate at this stage. Speech-generating devices or tablet AAC apps with symbol-based vocabulary can dramatically expand communicative capacity. Aided Language Stimulation (modeling) by communication partners is essential.
4 - 7 Years
Sentence Development & Social Communication
Children begin producing multi-word sentences, though grammar remains challenging. Speech intelligibility is a significant concern for school participation. AAC supports classroom inclusion and peer interaction. Literacy-based AAC features become increasingly important as reading instruction begins. Vocabulary in AAC systems should be expanded to support academic participation.
7 - 12 Years
Consolidation & Literacy Integration
Language continues to develop. Many children develop intelligible speech for familiar listeners while using AAC as a supplement for unfamiliar partners or complex messages. AAC systems should support increasingly sophisticated communication including storytelling, opinion expression, and academic language. Spelling and text-based AAC may become options.
12+ Years & Adulthood
Lifelong Multimodal Communication
Communication needs evolve through adolescence and adulthood. AAC supports vocational communication, community participation, relationship building, and self-advocacy. Many adults use a multimodal approach -- combining speech, AAC, gestures, and context-dependent strategies. AAC systems should support personal identity expression and meaningful participation in all life domains.
📌 Early Intervention is Key

Research consistently shows that introducing AAC early -- not waiting until speech "fails" -- leads to better outcomes in both AAC use and speech development. AAC should be introduced alongside speech therapy, not as a last resort. The earlier children have access to effective communication, the better their language, social, and emotional development.

Evidence & Research

The evidence base for AAC use with individuals with Down Syndrome has grown substantially over the past two decades, with multiple systematic reviews, randomized controlled trials, and longitudinal studies contributing to our understanding of best practices.

Systematic Reviews

A comprehensive systematic review published in BMC Pediatrics (2018) examined AAC interventions in children with Down Syndrome. The review found:

  • The Picture Exchange Communication System (PECS) led to improvements in language and social communication
  • The Makaton sign language system produced improvements in language development
  • Speech-generating devices improved communication in the domains of speech, cognition, and socializing
  • Digital interactive boards yielded significant benefits in socialization, independence, and self-esteem
  • The supported introduction of sign language in schools facilitated inclusive educational practices, reduced frustration, and facilitated peer interaction

Key Research Findings

🔬

Randomized Controlled Trial (2025)

A landmark RCT on Generative Language Intervention with AAC demonstrated that young children with DS improved their aided expressive grammar skills through structured AAC-based intervention, providing strong experimental evidence for AAC effectiveness.

📈

SGD vs. Speech-Only

Children in speech-generating device groups used more targeted spoken words than children in speech-only groups -- directly demonstrating that AAC does not inhibit but rather promotes spoken language development.

📑

Signing Predicts Vocabulary

Baby signs produced by children with DS predicted their spoken vocabulary size one year later, showing that early signing creates a foundation for later verbal language rather than replacing it.

📖

Literacy Through AAC

AAC technology with decoding features can support individuals with DS in building decoding and encoding skills, demonstrating that AAC benefits extend beyond spoken communication to literacy development.

Evidence Quality & Gaps

While the evidence for AAC in Down Syndrome is consistently positive, there are important notes about the state of research:

⚠ Research Considerations

Strengths: Multiple systematic reviews confirm benefits across AAC types; recent RCTs provide high-quality experimental evidence; findings are consistent across different research groups and countries.

Gaps: More large-scale randomized controlled trials are needed; long-term longitudinal studies tracking AAC outcomes into adulthood are limited; research on optimal timing of AAC introduction is ongoing; there is a need for more research on adults with DS who use AAC.

Emerging Research Directions (2024-2025)

  • Visual-perceptual features of AAC displays -- 2025 research examines how display design affects motor reaching efficiency in DS, informing more accessible AAC layouts
  • Meaningful adult participation -- New research focuses on how AAC supports personal identity, community participation, and quality of life for adults with DS
  • Multimodal AAC across the lifespan -- A comprehensive book published by Brookes Publishing covers AAC strategies for every life stage in DS
  • AAC modeling interventions -- Studies on dialogic reading with AAC modeling show promise for combined literacy and communication intervention
  • Visual fixation patterns -- Research correlating eye tracking with motor selection on AAC displays is informing next-generation AAC design

Key Considerations

Successfully implementing AAC for an individual with Down Syndrome requires attention to several critical factors. The following considerations can make the difference between an AAC system that transforms communication and one that sits unused.

For Families

💧

Start Early, Start Simply

Do not wait until speech "fails" to introduce AAC. Begin with simple strategies (signs, choice boards) and build complexity over time. Early AAC introduction supports both communication and speech development.

🗣

Model, Model, Model

The single most effective strategy for teaching AAC is modeling -- using the AAC system yourself when communicating with the child. Point to symbols as you speak, use the device to narrate activities, and make AAC a natural part of daily interactions.

📈

Expect Slow but Steady Progress

Just as speech develops slowly in DS, AAC proficiency takes time. Celebrate small victories. A child who learns to request one thing using AAC has taken an enormous step toward communicative independence.

👥

Involve Everyone

AAC only works if it is used consistently across all environments. Ensure that teachers, therapists, siblings, extended family, babysitters, and peers know how to communicate using the chosen AAC system.

For Professionals

  • Comprehensive assessment -- Evaluate not just speech but hearing, motor skills, cognition, visual processing, and current communication strategies before recommending AAC
  • Leverage visual strengths -- Design AAC interventions that capitalize on the strong visual learning abilities characteristic of DS
  • Address hearing loss -- Ensure hearing aids or other amplification are optimized; hearing loss compounds all communication challenges
  • Multimodal approach -- Recommend and support the use of multiple communication modalities (signs + device + speech) rather than a single system
  • Family training -- Invest heavily in training families to use AAC; the most sophisticated device is useless without skilled communication partners
  • Ongoing monitoring -- AAC needs change over time; regularly reassess and adjust the system as the individual grows and develops
  • Presume competence -- Always assume that the individual can learn more and communicate more than they currently demonstrate; provide access to robust vocabulary

Common Myths vs. Facts

Myth Fact
"AAC will prevent my child from learning to talk" Research consistently shows AAC supports and often accelerates speech development. Zero studies show AAC hindering speech.
"We should wait to see if speech develops first" Waiting means missing critical early language learning periods. Early AAC introduction leads to better outcomes in both AAC and speech.
"AAC is only for people who can't talk at all" AAC is for anyone whose speech alone does not meet their communication needs. Many AAC users have some speech and use AAC to supplement it.
"Children need certain prerequisites before using AAC" There are no prerequisites for AAC. You don't need a certain cognitive level, age, or skill set to begin. Everyone communicates, and everyone can benefit from AAC support.
"Once they use a device, they'll become dependent on it" Most individuals naturally integrate AAC with speech, using each in appropriate contexts. Just as wearing glasses is not a sign of dependence but a tool for better functioning, AAC is a tool for better communication.
"AAC is too complicated for children with Down Syndrome" AAC systems range from simple to complex. Many individuals with DS successfully use sophisticated AAC systems with proper support and training.

Considerations for Inclusive Education

AAC plays a critical role in supporting educational inclusion for students with Down Syndrome:

  • Classroom participation -- AAC enables students to answer questions, participate in discussions, and engage with curriculum
  • Peer interaction -- AAC supports friendships by providing a way to communicate with classmates who may not understand the student's speech
  • Academic access -- AAC vocabulary should include academic language relevant to the student's grade level and subjects
  • Staff training -- All staff who interact with the student should receive training on the AAC system, including paraprofessionals, specials teachers, and lunchroom staff
  • Aided Language Stimulation in the classroom -- Research shows that AAC modeling during small group activities like dialogic reading improves both communication and literacy outcomes
