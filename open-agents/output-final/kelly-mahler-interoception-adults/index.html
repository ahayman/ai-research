Kelly Mahler's Interoception Approach

Honestly read for adults. What is genuinely adult-targeted vs. pediatric being applied to adults, what the evidence supports, and how it relates to IFS-style felt-sense work.

Overview

Kelly Mahler's work is the most visible clinical name in occupational-therapy interoception. Her Interoception Curriculum, the Eighth Sensory System book, the activity decks, and a growing set of online courses are taught in special-education classrooms worldwide and recommended widely in OT, autism-clinical, and neurodiversity-affirming circles. If you are an autistic adult with suspected alexithymia and low interoceptive access who is reading about IFS and bumping into the body-noticing wall, sooner or later someone will hand you a Mahler product.

This page is a focused, adult-only read of that catalog. It answers four questions:

  1. What is Mahler's interoception model, precisely?
  2. What in her catalog is genuinely designed for adults — versus pediatric work being recommended for adults?
  3. What does the evidence base actually support, and where are the gaps?
  4. How does it relate to IFS's interoception requirement, and is it a good fit for an alexithymic autistic adult?

The honest answer up front: a small core of her catalog (about two products) is designed for adults from inception; the bulk of it is pediatric/K–12 content marketed as adaptable to adults. There is no peer-reviewed evidence in adult populations. Her clinical framing is neurodiversity-affirming and well-articulated. The empirical case is unproven, especially for alexithymic adults. There are real conceptual reasons it could function as a perceptual front-end for IFS-style work, and real reasons (Aaron 2020; harm risk in trauma populations) to be careful about that move. The page lays out all four.

Confidence overview. Of eighteen substantive claims independently validated, thirteen are confirmed, four are uncertain (the precise adult/pediatric catalog split; CAIA-3 psychometric validation; Mahler's adverse-effects engagement scope; her formal co-PI status), and one was corrected (Mahler does engage harm publicly — but specifically about Interoceptive Exposure Therapy, not the body-scan or Aaron-2020 risk).

Key Takeaways

  • A minority of Mahler's catalog is designed for adults from inception. The majority is pediatric content recommended for adult use.
  • None of her catalog has been tested in adult populations in a published peer-reviewed intervention study. All her published research is with autistic children in school settings.
  • Her three-stage clinical pathway (Notice → Connect → Act) maps loosely onto Garfinkel's three-component model but collapses one dimension — metacognitive awareness — that the empirical literature keeps separate.
  • Conceptually, Mahler's curriculum could scaffold the perceptual front-end of IFS's 6 Fs (Find, Focus, Feel toward), but the goals diverge at Stage 3, and the pathway is not validated as IFS preparation.
  • Aaron 2020's quadratic finding (more interoception is not always better) is the load-bearing risk Mahler's materials do not engage. For alexithymic or "sensory surplus" adults, this matters.

Who Kelly Mahler Is

Kelly Mahler holds an OTD from Misericordia University and is a licensed OTR/L practising out of Hershey, Pennsylvania. She has been an occupational therapist for roughly twenty years (~2002 onward) and is adjunct faculty at Elizabethtown College and Misericordia University. She describes herself as co-principal investigator on several research projects (interoception, self-regulation, trauma, and autism), authors approximately a dozen interoception books and resources, runs international workshops, and won the 2020 American Occupational Therapy Association Emerging and Innovative Practice Award.

Two clarifications matter for an adult reader. First, Mahler is a clinician-educator, not a basic-science researcher. Her published peer-reviewed work is co-authored with practitioner colleagues (Hample, Amspacher, McLaughlin, Anson) and was conducted in special-education school settings. Second, the Eighth Sensory System book carries a foreword by A. D. (Bud) Craig — the neuroscientist whose 2002 and 2009 Nature Reviews Neuroscience papers on the anterior insula are the canonical citations for interoception's neural substrate. The Craig endorsement is real and matters; it does not, however, make Mahler's clinical model itself a peer-reviewed neuroscience claim.

Her Interoception Model

Mahler's framing has three interlocking pieces.

The "eighth sensory system." Mahler positions interoception alongside the seven traditional senses (vision, hearing, smell, taste, touch, vestibular, proprioception) as a discrete sense to assess and train. The framing is clinically useful — it makes interoception a thing you can teach to in an OT lesson plan — and it is consistent with how Bud Craig's neuroscience treats the modality. It is not, however, a strict neuroscientific claim that interoception is structurally identical to the other seven senses. The "eighth sense" framing is best read as a clinician's pedagogical reframe rather than as a neurobiological taxonomy.

The Interoception Awareness Pathway. Her flagship Interoception Curriculum (2019, $159, 635 downloadable pages, 25 lessons) operationalises a three-stage pipeline.

  • Stage 1 — Notice (Lessons 1–16). Discriminate sensations: tense vs relaxed, full vs empty, fast vs slow heart, hot vs cool, hungry vs satiated, tired vs energised. Build a personalised vocabulary for what each state feels like for this person — Mahler explicitly disavows teaching "what hunger feels like" as a universal. Sixteen of twenty-five lessons sit here. This is 64% of the curriculum, and it is precisely the perceptual capacity that alexithymia disrupts.
  • Stage 2 — Connect (Lessons 17–20). Bridge from body signal to feeling word: "for me, this tight chest and shallow breath usually means anxious." This is granularity-building work, parallel to (though uncited in) Lisa Feldman Barrett's emotional-granularity literature.
  • Stage 3 — Act (Lessons 21–25). Use the identified state to choose a regulating response — co-regulation, sensory tools, breath, environmental change. The outcome Mahler names is "body trust": stabilised relationship between perceived signal and effective regulating action.

Where her framework converges with the empirical literature, and where it simplifies. Mahler's clinical pathway maps onto two of the three dimensions in Garfinkel's foundational 2015 Biological Psychology model — accuracy (objective performance), sensibility (self-reported orientation inward), and awareness (metacognitive correspondence between confidence and accuracy). Her Stage 1 captures some of accuracy and sensibility without distinguishing them. Her Stage 2 overlaps with sensibility. Metacognitive awareness — Garfinkel's third dimension — is not measured in Mahler's pathway.

This matters more than it might seem. An alexithymic adult could become more confident they notice signals (Stage 1 self-report improvement) without their confidence actually tracking accurate detection. Garfinkel's framework predicts and measures this dissociation. Mahler's clinical model does not. For someone whose externalised processing already works — who reads their patterns from data, behaviour, written reflection — going through a perception-training curriculum without the metacognitive layer can move them in the wrong direction without anyone in the loop knowing it.

The Adult Content — Inventoried Honestly

You have been told Mahler "does also address adults." That is true. It is also true that the bulk of her catalog and all of her published research are about children. Here is the honest inventory.

Genuinely adult-designed (designed for adults from inception)

  • My Interoception Workbook: A Guide for Adolescents, Teens and Adults (2019, $39, 18 chapters, three sections) — co-authored with Chloe Rothschild and Jarvis Alma, both autistic self-advocates. Crucially, the workbook itself states it is not designed to build interoceptive awareness; it is for "outsmarting and compensating for interoception challenges." Mahler positions it as a precursor to (not substitute for) the Interoception Curriculum. Self-directed use is permitted; it can also be read with a trusted person.
  • On-Demand Course: Interoception Through the Lens of an Autistic Adult — features Chloe Rothschild narrating her interoception journey, with concrete strategies. Marketed to OTs, mental-health providers, "adult service providers," behaviourists, and caregivers. Adult-experience-as-text but professionally framed.

Adult-applicable by population (not adult-designed)

  • On-Demand Course: Interoception and the Non-Speaking Client — non-speaking populations include adults; this is one of the few products explicitly addressing adult clinical work.
  • CAIA-3 (Comprehensive Assessment of Interoceptive Awareness, 3rd edition) — adult version — Mahler's assessment instrument has an adult version. Published peer-reviewed psychometric validation appears thin; an adult who wants a research-validated self-report instrument is better served by the MAIA-2 (Mehling et al., a different, well-validated 37-item measure).

Pediatric-rooted, marketed as adaptable to adults

  • The Interoception Curriculum ($159) — the FAQ states it can be "adapted for preschoolers through adults" via modifications to language and visuals. The lesson plans, worksheets, and visual scaffolds are K–12-flavoured by default. The buyer does the adaptation work themselves. One published adult-self-learner testimonial appears on the product page; no adult RCT, no adult feasibility study, no adult pilot exists in the peer-reviewed literature.
  • Interoception Activity Cards (170 cards) — described as "preschool through adulthood." There is no separate adult deck; the answer to "is there an adult version of the Activity Cards?" is no.
  • Interoception Yoga Cards / Exercise Cards / Big Book of Interoception Games — bundled with the Curriculum master bundle. Pediatric-leaning aesthetic.
  • Interoception: The Eighth Sensory System (2015 book, AAPC Publishing) — autism-framed clinician/educator book. The subtitle names "individuals with autism spectrum and related disorders" without age limitation, but the case studies and exercises are predominantly pediatric.

Pediatric-only

Children's books, toileting course (a 2026 "Toileting 2.0" live course exists; toileting is largely a pediatric-readiness frame), most of the school-based interventions described in her published research.

The honest summary. A minority of Mahler's catalog is designed for adults from inception. The majority is pediatric work being recommended for adult use. Her flagship — The Interoception Curriculum — sits firmly in the pediatric-rooted-with-adaptation-language bucket. None of her catalog has been tested in adult populations in a published peer-reviewed intervention study. The top-right quadrant in the catalog map is empty.

The Mainstream Interoception Research It Draws From

Mahler's clinical model is built on real foundational neuroscience and is consistent with — though it does not formally cite — several streams of empirical work.

Bud Craig's anterior insula model. Craig's Nature Reviews Neuroscience papers (2002 How do you feel?; 2009 How do you feel — now? The anterior insula and human awareness) established the anterior insular cortex as the integrative substrate for interoception, and proposed it as a basis for subjective awareness more broadly. Craig wrote the foreword to Mahler's 2015 book — a public endorsement of the eighth-sense framing.

Sarah Garfinkel's three-component model. The 2015 Biological Psychology paper Knowing your own heart and the 2016 follow-up with Hugo Critchley distinguish accuracy, sensibility, and awareness. All three are empirically dissociable; a person can be high on one without being high on the others. This framework is more analytically rigorous than Mahler's clinical model; it is also harder to operationalise in a school OT classroom, which is probably why her materials don't formally adopt it.

The interoception–alexithymia literature. Shah, Hall, Catmur, and Bird (2016 Cortex, "Alexithymia, not autism, is associated with impaired interoception") establish that the autism–interoception link is mediated by alexithymia, not autism per se. Brewer, Cook, and Bird (2016 Royal Society Open Science) generalise this to a multi-modal interoceptive deficit. This literature is the bedrock for understanding why an alexithymic adult might struggle with body-noticing prompts — and it is not deeply integrated into Mahler's clinical materials.

Aaron, Snodgress, Blain, Park (2020). This is the load-bearing finding for any "interoception training will help your alexithymia" claim. In N=76 young adults, the relationship between alexithymia and interoceptive accuracy was best described as quadratic: both very high and very low interoceptive accuracy correlate with elevated alexithymia. The accompanying brief mindfulness body-scan pilot showed no group difference between intervention and control on outcome measures. Translated: "more interoception is better" is empirically false at both ends of the scale, and a brief body-scan intervention did not produce the predicted improvement. This finding does not appear in Mahler's materials.

Mindfulness-and-alexithymia evidence. Norman et al. (2019) meta-analysis found a small statistically significant TAS-20 reduction with mindfulness interventions — but with notable heterogeneity and explicit warning about harm risk in psychiatric populations. Khalsa et al. (2025) larger meta-analysis on mindfulness and self-reported interoception found a small-to-medium positive effect. Lindahl et al. (2017) and Britton et al. (2021) document meditation-related adverse events, particularly in trauma populations. The picture is: real but modest effects, heterogeneous, with non-trivial harm risk for trauma-history clients.

The Evidence Base — What Mahler-Specific Research Actually Supports

Mahler's published intervention studies are these:

  • Hample, Mahler, Amspacher (2020) — pilot study with autistic children.
  • Mahler et al. (2022) in Occupational Therapy International — N=14 autistic students aged 9–19, one-group pre-post design (not an RCT), self-contained special-education classroom. A scoping review classified this as "moderate risk of bias in outcome measurement."
  • Mahler et al. (2024) in Occupational Therapy in Health Care — feasibility study, special-education classroom.
  • A randomised controlled trial of the Interoception Curriculum with school-aged children is listed as in development on her research page.

Her cross-sectional descriptive paper (Mahler, McLaughlin, Anson 2020 AJOT, "Interoception Across Varying Degrees of Mental Wellness") may include adult participants but is not an intervention trial.

What this means honestly: the evidence base for Mahler's specific program in adult populations — including the user's profile of autistic adult with suspected alexithymia — is zero peer-reviewed intervention studies. The case for adult use is built on (a) face validity of the activities, (b) the broader interoception-training literature (Khalsa, Norman) which has its own caveats, and (c) self-learner testimonials. That is not nothing — but it is not the same as "validated for adults."

This is also not a knock specific to Mahler. It is the standard pattern for clinician-developed curricula in adjacent fields: clinical traction precedes formal validation, often by many years, sometimes forever. The right framing for an adult reader is: clinically used with adults; not researched in adults.

How It Relates to IFS

This is where the page lives in your reading arc. The IFS deep-dive Cat 6 page on alexithymia (linked in Related reading at the bottom) names the IFS interoception problem: three of the six Fs — Find, Focus, Feel toward — lean directly on the perception of internal bodily state. Alexithymia disrupts that channel. The Cat 6 page lands on structural modification (substitute access channels) as the working consensus, with cautious openness to interoceptive capacity-building where it emerges naturally — never imposed.

Mahler's curriculum sits at the second of those moves. Could sustained interoception training prepare a low-interoception adult for IFS-style felt-sense work?

The conceptual fit is real. Mahler's Stage 1 (Notice) directly scaffolds IFS's Find ("where in your body do you notice this?") and Focus (sustain inward attention). Her Stage 2 (Connect body to feeling word) scaffolds Feel toward — the IFS gateway question that asks the client to read their own emotional stance. So her curriculum could function as a perceptual front-end for IFS work. Three caveats matter.

The empirical case is unproven for either program in adults. No published study tests "interoception curriculum prepares adults for IFS." It is a clinically reasonable hypothesis. It is not a validated clinical pathway.

Aaron 2020's quadratic finding applies to Mahler's program too. The "more awareness is better" model that the curriculum implicitly assumes is empirically false at the extremes. An adult who is already over-attending to body signals without being able to parse them — which the IFS Cat 6 page describes as the "sensory surplus" presentation in some autistic adults — could plausibly be made worse by training that adds more signal without adding parsing capacity.

The goals diverge at Stage 3. Mahler's curriculum treats successful interoception as a precursor to self-regulation. IFS treats it as a precursor to parts work — witnessing, unburdening, building Self-leadership. These are not the same therapeutic goal. An adult who has spent fifty hours building Stage 1 discrimination and Stage 2 labelling will arrive at IFS with a useful perceptual capacity, but they will also be coming from a self-regulation frame that is not parts-work-shaped. The capacity transfers; the goal frame doesn't.

The IFS Cat 6 synthesis stands here: structural modification (substitute access channels — written, externalised, cognitive, behavioural) is the working consensus. Mahler's curriculum is one possible adjunct in a "training modality" frame — useful for some adults, contraindicated for others, untested specifically.

Suitability for Autism + Alexithymia

Mahler's framing is explicitly neurodiversity-affirming. The interoception-and-neurodiversity page on her site states that "there is no wrong way to feel; your inner experience is always correct and valid," critiques teaching that anxiety or hunger feels a particular way, uses "undoing before doing" trauma-informed language, and centres autistic self-advocates (Rothschild, Alma). This is real, and it puts her materials in a meaningfully different register from older deficit-focused autism interoception work.

Three caveats matter for an autistic adult reader.

The framing is affirming; the empirical instruments are conventional. The studies use measures like the Caregiver Questionnaire for Interoceptive Awareness (CQIA-2) and the Behavior Rating Inventory of Executive Function (BRIEF-2). These instruments operationalise "improvement" in ways that carry their own framing assumptions. The marketing register and the research register are not perfectly aligned.

The published research lens is pediatric-clinical, not autistic-adult. Mahler centres autistic self-advocates in her authorship and courses; she does not run her published intervention research with autistic adults. That gap matters when you are evaluating fit.

Aaron 2020 risk is unaddressed in her materials. Mahler does engage adverse effects publicly — but specifically about Interoceptive Exposure Therapy, the panic-disorder treatment that intentionally provokes distressing body sensations. That is a different modality with different risks. The body-scan literature on harm in trauma populations (Lindahl, Britton) and the Aaron 2020 quadratic finding are not engaged in her materials. For an alexithymic adult with complex trauma, this is the gap to track. The IFS Cat 6 page treats this risk in depth and recommends polyvagal-informed pacing (Winhall) before any felt-sense work; Mahler's curriculum does not provide an equivalent framework.

Honest Assessment of Fit

Mahler's adult-relevant content is most likely to help when the adult:

  • Has reasonable autonomic regulation (no active complex-trauma flooding/shutdown);
  • Is starting from genuinely low Stage 1 perceptual discrimination and would benefit from systematic structured exposure to body-signal vocabulary;
  • Wants self-regulation as the outcome — not parts work or somatic-emotional processing per se;
  • Can do the adaptation work the curriculum requires (the K–12 visuals and pacing don't auto-translate to adult life);
  • Is comfortable with face-validity-based clinical guidance rather than research-validated protocol.

It is most likely to be a poor fit when the adult:

  • Has active complex trauma where body attention triggers dysregulation (the Aaron 2020 risk + polyvagal pacing concern);
  • Is already over-attending to body signal without parsing capacity ("sensory surplus" presentation; building more signal without parsing capacity may worsen distress);
  • Wants a research-validated adult intervention specifically;
  • Is doing IFS or felt-sense work and looking for a parts-work-aligned scaffold (Mahler's Stage 3 diverges into self-regulation, not parts work);
  • Strongly prefers externalised/cognitive/written processing as the destination rather than the workaround (in which case the IFS Cat 6 substitution channels may serve better).
Specific markers for the autistic-adult, suspected-alexithymia profile (externalised processing already works better than felt-sense; complex-trauma history present but the trauma frame is not what is being explored on this page):
  • My Interoception Workbook is the most defensible adult-targeted purchase. $39, self-directed possible, co-authored with autistic self-advocates, explicitly framed as compensation/advocacy rather than awareness-curriculum. Risk profile is low because it is not asking you to do sustained interoception practice.
  • The Lens-of-an-Autistic-Adult on-demand course is the second most defensible purchase. Experiential exposure to Rothschild's lived account; not a structured curriculum to follow.
  • The Interoception Curriculum itself is the most expensive purchase and the one with the largest gap between marketing claim and adult evidence base. Buying it as a self-learner is possible; one published testimonial supports it; you are doing the adult adaptation work yourself.
  • The CAIA-3 adult version is not recommended over the research-validated MAIA-2 (Mehling et al.) if you want a self-report measure of interoceptive awareness for personal tracking.

Where This Page Lands

Mahler's interoception work is the most clinically articulate, neurodiversity-affirming, accessible body of OT-rooted interoception material in the field. Her framing is real, her authorship of autistic-self-advocate co-authored materials is substantive, and her three-stage pathway operationalises a clinically usable model.

It is also a body of work that is clinically used with adults but not researched in adults, that collapses one of the three dimensions in the empirical interoception literature (metacognitive awareness), and that does not engage the Aaron 2020 quadratic-finding risk that complicates "more awareness is better" claims for alexithymic populations specifically.

For an autistic adult with suspected alexithymia who has just finished the IFS deep dive and is asking whether Mahler's curriculum could prepare them for felt-sense work, the answer is: conceptually plausible, empirically untested, with real risk markers if you have complex trauma or "sensory surplus" presentation, and most defensibly entered through the lowest-cost adult-designed product (the Workbook) rather than the flagship Curriculum. If your externalised processing already works for you, the case for retraining toward felt-sense access is genuinely unclear, and that is the same conclusion the IFS Cat 6 page reaches by a different route.
