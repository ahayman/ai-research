What Are Beta Blockers?

Essential information about beta-adrenergic blocking agents

Beta-blockers (beta-adrenergic blocking agents) are medications that block the effects of adrenaline (epinephrine) and other stress hormones on beta-adrenergic receptors. First developed by Scottish scientist James W. Black in the 1960s, they revolutionized the treatment of cardiovascular disease.[5]

By blocking beta receptors, these drugs reduce heart rate, decrease blood pressure, lower cardiac oxygen demand, and help stabilize heart rhythm. They are FDA-approved for treating hypertension, heart failure, arrhythmias, angina, post-myocardial infarction, migraine prophylaxis, essential tremor, hyperthyroidism, glaucoma, and other conditions.[6]

Three Generations of Beta Blockers

First Generation (Non-Selective)

Block both beta-1 and beta-2 receptors

  • Examples: Propranolol, Nadolol, Timolol, Pindolol, Sotalol
  • Affect heart, lungs, vessels, GI tract, and metabolism
  • Higher risk of bronchospasm - avoid in asthma
  • More CNS and metabolic side effects
Source: PubMed[2]

Second Generation (Cardioselective)

Preferentially block beta-1 receptors

  • Examples: Metoprolol, Atenolol, Bisoprolol, Acebutolol, Esmolol
  • Primary effect on heart - less lung/peripheral effects
  • Safer in mild asthma/COPD (with caution)
  • Better tolerability than first generation
Source: PubMed[2]

Third Generation (Vasodilating)

Beta blockade + vasodilation via alpha-blockade or NO release

  • Examples: Carvedilol, Labetalol, Nebivolol
  • Additional vasodilatory properties
  • No unfavorable metabolic effects
  • Better for diabetics - metabolically neutral
Source: Wiley[7]

Individual Beta Blocker Profiles

Detailed information on each major beta blocker

Metoprolol
Lopressor, Toprol-XL
2nd Generation - Beta-1 Selective

The most prescribed beta blocker in the US. Available as immediate-release (tartrate) and extended-release (succinate). Proven mortality reduction in heart failure and post-MI.[6]

Primary Uses
Hypertension Heart Failure (HFrEF) Post-MI Angina Arrhythmias Migraine Prophylaxis
Benefits
  • Most prescribed - extensive clinical data[1]
  • Proven mortality reduction in heart failure[8]
  • Safer than non-selective agents in asthma/COPD
  • Once-daily dosing with succinate form
  • Inexpensive and widely available
Drawbacks
  • Tartrate requires twice-daily dosing
  • Can cause fatigue, dizziness, cold extremities
  • Must be tapered - do not stop abruptly[9]
  • May cause weight gain
Side Effects
  • Bradycardia, fatigue, dizziness
  • Cold extremities, weight gain
  • Depression, vivid dreams
  • Erectile dysfunction
Sources: StatPearls[6], PMC[8]
Carvedilol
Coreg
3rd Generation - Non-selective + Alpha Blocker

Third-generation beta blocker with dual mechanism: blocks beta-1, beta-2, AND alpha-1 receptors. Provides vasodilation and has antioxidant properties. Preferred for diabetics due to metabolically neutral profile.[8]

Primary Uses
Heart Failure (HFrEF) Post-MI Hypertension
Benefits
  • Dual mechanism provides vasodilation[8]
  • Better metabolic profile - improves insulin sensitivity[10]
  • Lower risk of new-onset type 2 diabetes
  • Antioxidant and antiproliferative effects
  • COMET trial: superior to metoprolol for HF mortality[8]
Drawbacks
  • Requires twice-daily dosing
  • Non-selective - caution in asthma
  • More hypotension due to alpha-blockade
  • Drug interactions (especially with digoxin)
Side Effects
  • Dizziness, fatigue, hypotension
  • Diarrhea, weight gain
  • Bradycardia, hyperglycemia
Sources: PMC[8], JAHA[10]
Propranolol
Inderal
1st Generation - Non-Selective

The first successful beta blocker, developed by James W. Black in the 1960s. Wide range of uses including performance anxiety, tremor, and hyperthyroidism. Crosses blood-brain barrier (helps with anxiety/tremor but causes CNS effects).[5]

Primary Uses
Hypertension Migraine Essential Tremor Performance Anxiety Hyperthyroidism Portal HTN
Benefits
  • First and most studied beta blocker[5]
  • Wide range of approved indications
  • Effective for performance anxiety and tremor[5]
  • Most studied for hyperthyroidism symptoms
  • Used for infantile hemangioma
Drawbacks
  • Non-selective - contraindicated in asthma
  • CNS effects (crosses blood-brain barrier)
  • Can mask hypoglycemia symptoms
  • Multiple daily doses (short half-life: 3-6 hrs)[5]
  • Smoking decreases effectiveness
  • Most severe withdrawal symptoms[11]
Side Effects
  • Bradycardia, fatigue, cold extremities
  • Bronchospasm, GI upset, vivid dreams
  • Depression, short-term memory effects
  • Erectile dysfunction
Sources: StatPearls[5]
Bisoprolol
Zebeta
2nd Generation - Highly Beta-1 Selective

Highly cardioselective beta blocker with once-daily dosing. Shows superior survival benefit in COPD patients with heart failure compared to carvedilol or metoprolol.[12]

Primary Uses
Heart Failure (HFrEF) Hypertension
Benefits
  • Highest beta-1 selectivity among common agents
  • Once-daily dosing
  • Superior survival in HF + COPD patients[12]
  • Evidence-based for heart failure
  • Similar efficacy to carvedilol with better tolerability
Drawbacks
  • Less commonly prescribed in US
  • Limited data for some indications
Side Effects
  • Fatigue, headache, dizziness
  • Cold extremities, GI upset
Sources: Clin Res Cardiol[12]
Nebivolol
Bystolic
3rd Generation - Beta-1 Selective + NO Release

The only beta blocker that stimulates nitric oxide production, providing unique vasodilatory properties. Improves endothelial function and has better tolerability profile (less fatigue, less sexual dysfunction).[13]

Primary Uses
Hypertension Heart Failure (Europe)
Benefits
  • Only BB that stimulates nitric oxide production[13]
  • Improves endothelial function
  • Less fatigue, less sexual dysfunction
  • Does not decrease nocturnal melatonin (better sleep)[7]
  • Beneficial for diabetics, elderly, African-Americans[13]
  • Does not inhibit exercise heart rate at doses <10mg
  • Antioxidant properties
Drawbacks
  • More expensive than older agents
  • Less outcome data than metoprolol/carvedilol for HF
  • Limited availability in some regions
Side Effects
  • Headache, fatigue, dizziness, diarrhea
  • (Generally well-tolerated)
Sources: PMC[13]
Atenolol
Tenormin
2nd Generation - Beta-1 Selective

Cardioselective, water-soluble beta blocker with once-daily dosing. Good for blood pressure control but notably does NOT have proven mortality reduction post-MI like metoprolol.[14]

Primary Uses
Hypertension Angina
Benefits
  • Once-daily dosing
  • Cardioselective
  • Good blood pressure control
  • Water-soluble - fewer CNS effects
Drawbacks
  • NO proven mortality reduction post-MI[15]
  • Requires dose adjustment in renal impairment
  • Inferior to metoprolol for CV outcomes[14]
  • Not recommended first-line for elderly with HTN[16]
Side Effects
  • Fatigue, dizziness, cold extremities
  • Bradycardia, GI upset
Sources: SingleCare[14]
Sotalol
Betapace
1st Gen Non-Selective + Class III Antiarrhythmic

Unique beta blocker with additional Class III antiarrhythmic properties (potassium channel blockade). Used primarily for arrhythmias but carries significant QT prolongation risk - requires in-hospital initiation with ECG monitoring.[4]

Primary Uses
Ventricular Tachycardia Atrial Fibrillation
Benefits
  • Dual beta-blocker and antiarrhythmic effects[4]
  • Effective for ventricular and supraventricular arrhythmias
  • Maintains sinus rhythm in AF
Drawbacks
  • QT prolongation risk (dose-dependent)[4]
  • Torsades de pointes risk: 1% (<320mg) to 5% (>320mg)
  • Requires in-hospital initiation with ECG monitoring
  • Cannot use with other QT-prolonging drugs
  • Contraindicated in long QT syndrome
Side Effects
  • Bradycardia, fatigue, dizziness
  • QT prolongation, torsades de pointes
  • Bronchospasm
Sources: StatPearls[4]
Labetalol
Trandate
3rd Gen Non-Selective + Alpha Blocker

Combined alpha and beta blocker - the drug of choice for pregnancy-induced hypertension and pre-eclampsia. Available in IV form for hypertensive emergencies. Better birth weights compared to atenolol.[17]

Primary Uses
Pregnancy HTN Pre-eclampsia HTN Emergencies Hypertension
Benefits
  • Drug of choice for pregnancy hypertension[17]
  • Rapid IV form for emergencies
  • Better birth weights than atenolol in pregnancy[17]
  • Does not significantly affect HR or cardiac output
  • Lower lipophilicity - less placental/CNS transfer
Drawbacks
  • Non-selective - caution in asthma
  • Postural hypotension (due to alpha-blockade)
  • Requires multiple daily doses orally
  • Alpha:beta ratio varies (oral 1:3, IV 1:7)[18]
Side Effects
  • Dizziness, fatigue, nausea
  • Nasal congestion, scalp tingling
  • Postural hypotension
Sources: PMC[17], StatPearls[18]
Esmolol
Brevibloc
2nd Generation - Beta-1 Selective (IV Only)

Ultra-short acting IV beta blocker for emergency and perioperative use. Rapid onset (2 min), very short half-life (9 min), and effects gone 15-30 min after stopping. Ideal when quick reversibility is needed.[19]

Primary Uses
SVT (Acute) Perioperative HTN Emergency ACS
Benefits
  • Ultra-short acting (half-life 9 min)[19]
  • Rapid onset (2 minutes)
  • Easily titratable
  • Effects reverse rapidly when stopped
  • Metabolism not dependent on liver/kidney
  • Ideal for testing beta-blocker tolerance
Drawbacks
  • IV only - not for chronic use
  • Requires continuous monitoring
  • Can cause injection site reactions
  • Hypotension with rapid boluses
Side Effects
  • Hypotension, bradycardia
  • Injection site reactions, nausea
Sources: StatPearls[19]
Nadolol
Corgard
1st Generation - Non-Selective

Long-acting non-selective beta blocker - the PREFERRED agent for Long QT Syndrome (LQTS) and catecholaminergic polymorphic ventricular tachycardia (CPVT). More effective than propranolol for preventing breakthrough cardiac events.[20]

Primary Uses
Long QT Syndrome CPVT Hypertension Migraine
Benefits
  • Very long half-life - once daily dosing
  • Preferred agent for LQTS and CPVT[20]
  • More efficacious than propranolol for cardiac events
  • Does not cross blood-brain barrier well (fewer CNS effects)
Drawbacks
  • Non-selective - avoid in asthma
  • Requires dose adjustment in renal impairment
  • Can accumulate in kidney disease
Side Effects
  • Fatigue, dizziness, cold extremities
  • Bradycardia, bronchospasm
Sources: Medscape[20]

Side Effects Overview

Common and serious side effects of beta blockers

Very Common

  • Fatigue / Tiredness
  • Bradycardia (slow heart rate)
  • Dizziness / Lightheadedness
  • Cold hands and feet
  • Hypotension (low blood pressure)
Source: Cleveland Clinic[21]

Common

  • Weight gain
  • GI upset (nausea, diarrhea, constipation)
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Vivid dreams / Nightmares
  • Headache
Source: ESC[22]

Less Common

  • Depression
  • Erectile dysfunction
  • Bronchospasm
  • Muscle cramps
  • Blurred vision
  • Rash
Source: StatPearls[6]

Serious (Seek Care)

  • Severe bradycardia
  • Heart block
  • Heart failure exacerbation
  • Severe bronchospasm
  • QT prolongation (sotalol)
  • Torsades de pointes (sotalol)
Source: StatPearls[6]

Withdrawal Syndrome

NEVER stop beta blockers abruptly - always taper gradually

Withdrawal Symptoms

  • Tachycardia (rapid heart rate)[9]
  • Hypertension (elevated blood pressure)
  • Palpitations
  • Sweating
  • Headache
  • Tremor
  • Anxiety / Agitation
  • Chest pain (angina)
  • Nervousness
  • Nausea

Serious Risks

  • Unstable angina[9]
  • Myocardial infarction (heart attack)
  • Sudden death (in CAD patients)
  • Increased postoperative complications

Safe Discontinuation

  • NEVER stop beta blockers abruptly[23]
  • Taper gradually over 1-2 weeks minimum
  • Reduce dose by 25-50% every few days
  • Monitor closely during taper
  • Agents with ISA (pindolol, acebutolol) have milder withdrawal[11]
  • Contact doctor immediately if chest pain or severe symptoms

When to Use Which Beta Blocker

Condition-based recommendations from clinical guidelines

❤ Heart Failure (HFrEF)
Carvedilol Bisoprolol Metoprolol Succinate Nebivolol
Only these have mortality evidence in heart failure. Start low, go slow.
Source: ESC[22]
🩹 Post-MI
Metoprolol Carvedilol Propranolol Timolol
Atenolol has NOT shown mortality benefit post-MI. May discontinue after 1 year if EF 50%.
Source: JACC[15]
😸 Asthma / COPD
Bisoprolol Metoprolol Nebivolol
Cardioselective agents ONLY. Bisoprolol best in HF + COPD. NEVER use non-selective agents.
Source: PMC[24]
🥐 Diabetes
Carvedilol Nebivolol
Metabolically neutral - don't worsen insulin resistance. Monitor for masked hypoglycemia.
Source: JAHA[10]
🤰 Pregnancy Hypertension
Labetalol
Drug of choice. Better birth weights than atenolol. Avoid atenolol in pregnancy.
Source: PMC[17]
🖓 Atrial Fibrillation
Metoprolol Bisoprolol Carvedilol Esmolol (acute)
First-line for rate control. Sotalol for rhythm control if needed.
Source: StatPearls[6]
😬 Performance Anxiety / Tremor
Propranolol
Crosses blood-brain barrier. Effective for physical symptoms. Used by musicians/speakers.
Source: StatPearls[5]
⚡ Long QT Syndrome / CPVT
Nadolol
Preferred agent. More effective than propranolol for preventing breakthrough cardiac events.
Source: Medscape[20]
🔥 Hyperthyroidism
Propranolol
Most studied for thyrotoxicosis. Controls tachycardia and tremor. Inhibits T4T3 at high doses.
Source: StatPearls[5]
🚑 Emergency / IV Use
Esmolol Labetalol IV
Esmolol: ultra-short acting (t=9 min), titratable, quickly reversible.
Source: StatPearls[19]
👁 Glaucoma
Timolol (topical)
Decreases aqueous humor production. Even eye drops can cause systemic effects.
Source: RxList[25]
💉 Perioperative
Bisoprolol Metoprolol Succinate
Start 1 week-30 days pre-surgery. Target HR 60-70, SBP >100. NEVER stop abruptly.
Source: VUMC[26]

Contraindications

Absolute Contraindications
  • Symptomatic Bradycardia (<60 bpm)
    Beta blockers further slow heart rate[27]
  • High-Grade AV Block (without pacemaker)
    Worsens cardiac conduction
  • Sick Sinus Syndrome (without pacemaker)
    Worsens conduction abnormalities
  • Severe/Uncontrolled Asthma
    Bronchospasm risk (especially non-selective agents)[24]
  • Decompensated Heart Failure
    Reduces cardiac output when patient unstable
  • Cardiogenic Shock
    Worsens hemodynamics
Relative Contraindications
  • Mild/Controlled Asthma
    Cardioselective agents may be used cautiously
  • COPD
    Cardioselective agents often tolerated
  • Diabetes
    Not contraindicated but monitor - can mask hypoglycemia[27]
  • Peripheral Vascular Disease
    May worsen claudication
  • Raynaud's Phenomenon
    Cold extremities worsen symptoms
  • Depression
    May exacerbate (lipophilic agents)
Source: BPAC NZ[27]

Drug Interactions

Important interactions to be aware of

Interacting Drug Severity Risk / Effect
Verapamil / Diltiazem HIGH Severe bradycardia, heart block, hypotension[28]
Clonidine HIGH Rebound hypertension on withdrawal[28]
Digoxin HIGH Bradycardia, toxicity (especially with sotalol)[28]
Class I/III Antiarrhythmics HIGH Additive effects, QT prolongation
Thioridazine / Chlorpromazine HIGH Increased drug levels, arrhythmia risk[29]
NSAIDs MODERATE Reduced antihypertensive effect[28]
SSRIs (fluoxetine, paroxetine) MODERATE Increased beta blocker levels[28]
Amiodarone MODERATE Bradycardia, increased metoprolol levels
Alcohol MODERATE Additive hypotension, dizziness, fainting[28]
Pseudoephedrine MODERATE Antagonizes blood pressure lowering[28]
Stimulants (caffeine, amphetamines) MODERATE Antagonizes beta blocker effects
Hawthorn MODERATE Potentiates effect - excessive BP lowering[28]
