Semasiographic vs. Glottographic Systems Writing That Represents Meaning vs. Writing That Represents Speech

Why the distinction defines the entire visual language project

Validation Status

This explanation draws on validated concept research (overall confidence 9/10). All core theoretical claims were confirmed. One minor issue: an assessment about ISOTYPE's limitations is better understood as a paraphrase of scholarly consensus rather than a direct quotation from any single source.

The Fundamental Fork

Every writing system ever created by humans can be placed along a single axis: does it represent meaning directly, or does it encode the sounds of a specific spoken language?

This question, first systematized by Ignace Gelb in his 1952 A Study of Writing, is not a minor taxonomic detail. It defines the entire theoretical framework for any visual language project. A metaphor-based visual language that encodes image schemas and conceptual structures rather than the phonemes of English or Mandarin is, by definition, a semasiographicWriting that represents meaning directly without encoding the sounds of a spoken language; also called thought writing or ideographic writing. system — writing that represents meaning directly, readable by speakers of any language without translation. The question is whether a semasiographic system can achieve the full expressive power of natural language. That has never been accomplished.

The term semasiographic derives from the Greek semasia (meaning) and graphe (writing): writing that speaks directly to the mind without detour through any spoken language. GlottographicWriting that represents elements of a specific spoken language (words, syllables, phonemes); also called phonographic writing., from glotta (tongue), is writing that encodes elements of a specific spoken language — its words, syllables, or phonemes.

What Semasiographic Means

A semasiographic system represents meaning directly without encoding the sounds of any spoken language. Consider the expression:

2 + 3 = 5

A Japanese mathematician, a Brazilian engineer, and a Nigerian accountant all read this expression identically. No translation is needed. The notation is fully compositional — novel expressions can be constructed freely. "2 + 3 = 5" is pure semasiography: it says what it means without passing through any particular language's sound system.

Mathematical notation is the most successful semasiographic system in human history. But it is domain-restrictedThe property of a semasiographic system that limits its expressiveness to a particular subject area, such as mathematics, music, or chemistry.. None of those three readers can use it to say "I'm feeling anxious about tomorrow's meeting."

Other functioning semasiographic systems include:

  • Musical notation: A quarter note on the G line of a treble staff represents the same pitch and duration regardless of the reader's language. Compositional — but restricted to acoustic specification.
  • Chemical formulas: H2O means the same thing in every language. Elements combine into compounds following bonding rules. But the domain is molecular structure.
  • Road signs: The red octagon means STOP internationally (Vienna Convention on Road Signs, 1968). But the vocabulary is fixed and closed.
  • Circuit diagrams: Resistors, capacitors, and transistors are represented by universal symbols. But the domain is electrical systems.

These systems succeed because they share four properties: (1) a restricted domain with clear boundaries, (2) shared context among users, (3) a finite or slowly growing vocabulary, and (4) precise, unambiguous semantics. The question driving the visual language project is whether these properties can be extended beyond domain restriction to general-purpose communication.

What Glottographic Means

A glottographic system represents elements of a specific spoken language. The Latin alphabet is glottographic: the letter sequence "c-a-t" encodes the English phonemes /k/, /ae/, /t/. You can only read it if you know English.

Glottographic systems come in several types:

  • LogographicGlottographic writing where symbols represent words or morphemes. Chinese characters, Sumerian cuneiform, and Egyptian hieroglyphs all have logographic elements.: Each symbol represents a word or morpheme. Chinese characters, Sumerian cuneiform, Egyptian hieroglyphs.
  • SyllabicGlottographic writing where symbols represent syllables; also called syllabary. Examples: Japanese kana, Cherokee syllabary, Linear B.: Each symbol represents a syllable. Japanese kana, the Cherokee syllabary, Linear B.
  • AlphabeticGlottographic writing where symbols represent phonemes; also called segmental writing. Examples: Latin, Greek, Cyrillic, Hangul.: Each symbol represents a phoneme. Latin, Greek, Cyrillic, Hangul.
  • AbjadA consonant-only alphabet with optional vowel marking; also called consonantary. Examples: Arabic, Hebrew.: A consonant-only alphabet with optional vowel marking. Arabic, Hebrew.
  • AbugidaA writing system where consonant-vowel units are the basic symbols, with vowels shown as modifications of the consonant base; also called alphasyllabary. Examples: Devanagari, Ethiopic.: Consonant-vowel units as basic symbols, vowels shown as modifications. Devanagari, Ethiopic.

The key insight: all glottographic systems identify phonetic values. They are inherently tied to a specific spoken language or language family. But a semasiographic symbol — 2, +, =, the red octagon, H2O, a quarter note — can be read in any language.

The Spectrum, Not the Binary

The semasiographic-glottographic distinction is a continuum, not a binary switch. Most real writing systems blend both elements, and the most successful writing systems in history have been mixed.

Chinese: The Master Case

Chinese characters are the most important example of a mixed system, and the most commonly misunderstood. Over 80% of Chinese characters are semantic-phonetic compounds — each containing both a semasiographic element and a glottographic element within a single character.

Consider the character for "mother": ma (妈). It contains two components:

  • Semantic radicalIn Chinese characters, the component that indicates the meaning domain of the character (semasiographic function). Crosses language boundaries.: 女 (nu, "woman/female") — the semasiographic component. It says "this character relates to women/femaleness." A Korean reader familiar with hanja can extract this meaning even though they pronounce it differently.
  • Phonetic radicalIn Chinese characters, the component that hints at pronunciation (glottographic function). Does not cross language boundaries.: 马 (ma, "horse") — the glottographic component. It says "pronounce this something like 'ma.'" This information is useless without knowing Mandarin pronunciation.
Figure 2 — Three decomposition examples showing how semantic-phonetic compounds combine a semasiographic element (semantic radical) with a glottographic element (phonetic radical). This mixed architecture evolved over millennia for good reason.

This mixed architecture evolved because neither component alone is sufficient. The semantic radical provides cross-linguistic transparency. The phonetic radical provides the precision needed for a specific spoken language.

Egyptian Hieroglyphs

Egyptian hieroglyphs combined logographic symbols, phonetic signs, and determinativesA semasiographic element within a glottographic system that specifies meaning domain without contributing to pronunciation. Example: the "walking legs" determinative indicates physical movement. — purely semasiographic markers that specify a meaning domain without contributing to pronunciation. The "walking legs" determinative after a verb indicates physical movement. These function exactly like Chinese semantic radicals.

Pre-Columbian Mesoamerican Writing

Aztec and Maya notation systems blended semasiographic notation (pictographic scenes, calendric symbols) with glottographic elements (phonetic signs representing syllables of Nahuatl or Mayan languages). Research on Teotihuacan notation has analyzed these as occupying a stable middle ground — they were not "evolving toward" phonetic writing but deliberately maintaining both channels.

Modern Emoji

Emoji function as a semasiographic overlay on glottographic text. "I'm going to the beach tomorrow" is glottographic. Adding a beach emoji creates a mixed expression. Emoji are the most widespread semasiographic system in modern use — and also demonstrate the severe limitations of semasiography without grammar.

The Teleological Debate

Gelb argued in 1952 that writing systems naturally evolve from semasiographic origins toward ever more precise phonetic encoding. The Greek alphabet was the "culmination." This is called the teleologicalGelb's now-contested claim that writing systems inevitably evolve from semasiographic to phonographic representation, with the alphabet as the endpoint. view of writing development.

Modern scholarship has largely rejected this claim:

  • Chinese has not evolved toward an alphabet in over 3,000 years. Despite extensive contact with alphabetic systems.
  • Mesoamerican mixed systems were stable, not transitional. They maintained both channels without "evolving" toward pure phonography.
  • Emoji represents movement in the reverse direction. Modern digital communication is adding semasiographic elements to alphabetic text.
  • Mathematical notation has expanded, not contracted. The most powerful semasiographic system continues to grow.
Figure 3 — Gelb's linear evolution model (semasiography toward alphabet) versus the modern understanding of multiple parallel stable states with bidirectional movement.
Why This Matters for Design

If Gelb's teleology were true, designing a semasiographic visual language would be swimming against history. But the modern view — that semasiography, mixed systems, and phonography are all stable, viable architectures — opens the design space.

The Generality Problem

Natural language can express anything that can be thought. Novel propositions, counterfactual conditions, nested hypotheticals, emotional nuance, irony, poetry, metaphor, lies. This capacity is called open-ended expressivenessThe capacity to express anything that can be thought, including novel propositions never before expressed. Depends on productivity, displacement, and metalinguistic capacity., and it depends on several properties that domain-restricted semasiographic systems lack:

  • ProductivityThe ability of a language system to create unlimited novel expressions from finite elements. Requires compositionality and recursion.: Unlimited novel expressions from a finite set of elements.
  • DisplacementThe ability to refer to things not present in the immediate context: past events, future possibilities, hypothetical scenarios, abstract concepts.: Referring to things not present — past, future, hypothetical.
  • Metalinguistic capacity: Talking about the language itself. "I didn't mean that literally."
  • Pragmatic flexibility: Meaning different things with the same expression depending on context.

The Counterfactual Test

A simple test exposes the expressiveness gap. Attempt to express: "If it hadn't rained yesterday, we would have gone hiking."

  • Mathematical notation: impossible. Not in the domain.
  • Road signs: impossible. Not in the domain.
  • Emoji: hopelessly ambiguous.
  • Blissymbolics: possible but extremely cumbersome.
  • Natural language: trivial.

This sentence requires temporal displacement (yesterday), negation (hadn't), conditionality (if), and irrealis mood (would have). Any system that cannot express these is not a general-purpose language.

The Sign Language Counter-Argument

A natural objection: sign languages are visual-spatial communication systems that achieve full expressiveness. Doesn't this prove semasiographic systems can be fully expressive?

The answer requires careful distinction. Sign languages — ASL, BSL, JSL — are indeed full natural languages with complete grammars. But they are not semasiographic systems. They are full natural languages that happen to use the visual-spatial modality instead of the vocal-auditory modality. They have phonology, they are language-specific (ASL and BSL are mutually unintelligible), and they are tied to communities.

What Sign Languages Prove

Not that semasiography can be fully expressive, but something more fundamental: the visual-spatial modality has the bandwidth and structural resources for full linguistic expressiveness. The limitation of semasiographic writing is a design limitation — a problem not yet solved, not a problem that cannot be solved.

The Three Grounding Strategies

There are three fundamentally different ways a visual symbol can acquire meaning:

Strategy 1: Convention

An arbitrary symbol is given meaning by social agreement. The dollar sign ($) means "dollar" because everyone agrees it does. Strength: Can represent anything, including abstract concepts. Weakness: Must be learned. Not cross-culturally transparent.

Strategy 2: Iconicity

A visual resemblance links the symbol to its referent. A picture of a house means "house" because it looks like a house. Strength: Immediately recognizable. Weakness: Fails for abstract concepts. "Justice," "if," "because" have no visual form.

Strategy 3: Experiential Grounding

Image schemas from embodied experience provide the visual vocabulary, and metaphorical extension maps those schemas onto abstract meaning domains. The CONTAINER schema (interior, boundary, exterior) arises from the bodily experience of being in and out of enclosed spaces. Through primary metaphor, it extends: we are "in" trouble, "in" love, "in" a good mood.

Strength: Combines the language-independence of semasiography with the expressiveness potential of natural language. Weakness: The metaphorical mappings must be consistent and learnable. Not all abstract concepts have obvious experiential grounding.

The Hypothesis

Experiential grounding — the third strategy — can bridge the gap between domain-restricted semasiographic success and general-purpose expressiveness. Image schemas are both visual (spatially structured) and abstract (metaphorically extensible). This remains a hypothesis. No one has built and tested such a system.

Programming Languages: The Closest Existing Model

Consider the symbol + in the expression x = a + b. This symbol means addition in C, Python, Java, Ruby, JavaScript, and dozens of other languages. It is partially semasiographic — the operator carries meaning independent of any spoken language.

Programming languages achieve enormous expressiveness through precisely the mechanism that general-purpose semasiographic systems lack: formal grammar and compositionality. A programming language has complete grammar, semantic compositionality, productivity, and even displacement (conditional branches, future events through control flow).

The parallel to visual language design is strong:

  • Programming languages prove formal grammar enables semasiographic expressiveness far beyond pictographic systems.
  • They demonstrate arbitrary conventional symbols become natural to trained users.
  • They show compositionality scales to enormous complexity when grammar is well-designed.
  • They also demonstrate the cost: significant training required, and expressiveness is domain-restricted.

Design Requirements Derived from the Framework

The semasiographic-glottographic distinction yields seven specific design requirements:

  1. Compositionality: Must combine primitives to produce unlimited novel expressions.
  2. Grammar: Must encode relationships — agent, patient, action, time, condition, negation, scope.
  3. Abstractability: Must represent abstract concepts (justice, possibility, irony) not just concrete objects.
  4. Displacement: Must reference past, future, hypothetical, and counterfactual situations.
  5. Productivity: Must create new meanings without requiring new symbols for each concept.
  6. Phonetic bridge: Must handle proper names, borrowed words, and technical terminology. Chinese's mixed architecture evolved this solution over millennia.
  7. Learnability: Must be acquirable in reasonable time. Experiential grounding may provide the key to rapid acquisition.

Edge Cases and Exceptions

Emoji in Legal Contexts

Courts increasingly treat emoji as communicative acts. A Canadian court ruled that a thumbs-up emoji constituted agreement to a contract. This demonstrates that even a limited semasiographic system can acquire pragmatic force in context — but the meaning is always context-dependent and disputable, precisely because emoji lack grammar.

Musical Notation's Boundary

Musical notation is semasiographic for pitch and duration but requires verbal annotation for expression: "Allegro con brio" is glottographic Italian. Music notation demonstrates both the power of domain-restricted semasiography and the point where domain restriction forces a return to glottographic communication.

Where the Proposed Visual Language Sits

The proposed visual language aims to sit near the semasiographic end of the spectrum but not at the extreme. It would be primarily semasiographic — representing meaning through image schemas and conceptual structures — but would include a phonetic bridge subsystem for proper names and borrowed terms, making it a mixed system.

The theoretical grounding comes from three complementary frameworks:

  • NSM's 65 semantic primes: The irreducible atoms of meaning, validated across 30+ languages. These define what must be representable.
  • Image schemas: Pre-linguistic spatial-bodily structures (CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE, BALANCE, UP-DOWN) that provide the visual vocabulary.
  • Conceptual metaphor theory: Systematic mappings extending spatial schemas to abstract domains (HAPPY IS UP, KNOWING IS SEEING, PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS). These bridge the gap between visual and abstract.
