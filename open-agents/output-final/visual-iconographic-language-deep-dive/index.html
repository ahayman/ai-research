What This Deep Dive Is About

Humans have attempted to build visual communication systems independent of phonetics for centuries — from Leibniz's characteristica universalis (1666) through Bliss's Semantography (1949) to the peer-reviewed Icono system published in Frontiers in Psychology in 2023. None has achieved widespread adoption as a general-purpose language. But the theoretical raw materials are richer than ever. Wierzbicka's Natural Semantic Metalanguage has identified 65 semantic primes — irreducible meaning atoms found in every known human language. Lakoff and Johnson's Conceptual Metaphor Theory has mapped how abstract thought is systematically structured by embodied experience. Johnson's image schemas provide a vocabulary of spatial-relational primitives that humans learn before language from physical interaction with the world.

Meanwhile, sign languages prove that full natural languages can operate entirely in the visual-spatial modality. Chinese radical composition demonstrates that a finite set of semantic building blocks can generate an open-ended vocabulary across millennia. And cognitive science reveals that the human brain devotes 30–40% of cortical real estate to vision, with symbols processed more efficiently than their verbal equivalents.

The hard problems are real. No semasiographic system — one that represents meaning directly without encoding speech sounds — has ever achieved full natural-language expressiveness. The phoneme representation problem, compositionality at scale, and the adoption challenge remain unsolved. This deep dive maps the full landscape: what has been tried, what theoretical foundations could ground a new approach, what the cognitive science says, and whether the pieces can actually fit together into something that works — particularly for visual-spatial thinkers whose cognitive architecture may be poorly served by phonetic-verbal systems.

Image Schemas as Visual Building Blocks Core

The theoretical heart of the proposed language. Chinese Radical Composition as Model

The world's longest-running experiment in composable visual meaning-making. 214 Kangxi radicals generate tens of thousands of compound characters, with 80%+ being semantic-phonetic compounds. Proof that a finite set of visual building blocks can serve a civilization for millennia — but also a warning about opacity, phonetic drift, and the learning burden of thousands of symbols.

Open Questions Driving This Research

  • Has anyone attempted to systematically map Wierzbicka's 65 semantic primes to visual representations? What happens when you try to draw IF, BECAUSE, NOT, or VERY?
  • Could the DISL framework (which already formalizes visual representations of image schemas) be extended from a descriptive notation into a productive communication system?
  • What is the actual overlap between Wierzbicka's semantic primes and Johnson's image schemas? Are they complementary inventories or competing frameworks?
  • Has any research tested whether icon recognition for abstract concepts is consistent cross-culturally?
  • What is the minimum viable symbol set for functional communication? (Makaton says ~450 concepts; core vocabulary research says ~300 words; NSM says 65 primes + 50 molecules)
  • What would a digital input method for composable visual symbols look like?
