Image Schemas as Visual Building Blocks The Cognitive Geometry Beneath Language

From embodied experience to visual primitives

The Cognitive Geometry

Before you ever spoke a word, you understood containers. You understood paths. You understood force.

At six months old, you dropped a toy into a cup and fished it out. That act — putting something inside a bounded region, watching it disappear behind a boundary, then retrieving it — was not just play. It was the formation of a cognitive structure that would organize your thought for the rest of your life: the CONTAINER schemaThe structure of interior, boundary, and exterior. Grounded in the body itself (skin as first container), cups, rooms, and all bounded spaces. Extends metaphorically to emotional states, categories, and concepts.. Interior. Boundary. Exterior. Things can be in, out, or at the edge. This structure was in place before you had any word for "in" or "out" or "inside."

This is the fundamental insight of image schemaA recurring spatial-temporal structure arising from pre-linguistic bodily experience. Not a mental image but an abstract spatial pattern that structures conceptual understanding. theory, first articulated by Mark Johnson in The Body in the Mind (1987): the recurring spatial-temporal patterns that arise from pre-linguistic bodily interaction with the world form the cognitive infrastructure on which all later thought — including language — is constructed. These patterns are not mental images (they are too abstract for that) and they are not verbal concepts (they exist before language). They are something more elemental: the spatial geometry of experience itself.

For the project of designing a visual language, image schemas offer something extraordinary. They are simultaneously pre-linguistic (acquired before any spoken language), embodied (arising from universal bodily experience), and inherently spatial (naturally expressible as visual forms). Unlike semantic primes, which include many concepts resistant to visualization (IF, BECAUSE, TRUE), image schemas are the kind of thing you can draw.

Figure 1 — The major image schemas organized into four families (spatial, force, multiplicity, state), each with a canonical visual representation. These pre-linguistic structures form the proposed "visual alphabet."

The Inventory: Patterns the Body Teaches the Mind

Johnson's original inventory identifies at least sixteen recurring schemas. The list has never been closed — different researchers include different schemas, and the boundary between a "basic" schema and a composition of simpler schemas remains debated.

Spatial Schemas

CONTAINER (interior, boundary, exterior) is grounded in the body itself — your skin is the first container you ever know. Every cup, room, box, and building reinforces the pattern. PATHThe structure of starting point, trajectory, and destination. Grounded in every experience of movement from one place to another. Also called SOURCE-PATH-GOAL. (source, trajectory, goal) emerges from every experience of movement. UP-DOWN captures the asymmetry of the vertical axis: up correlates with more, with active, with conscious. NEAR-FAR encodes the significance of distance. CENTER-PERIPHERY distinguishes focal attention from background. CONTACT records surfaces meeting. SURFACE represents the extended plane supporting objects.

Force Schemas

The FORCE schemaThe structure of applied physical influence: compulsion (being pushed), blockage (being stopped), counterforce (pushing back), enablement (path cleared), attraction (being pulled toward), and diversion (being redirected). includes compulsion, blockage, counterforce, enablement, attraction, and diversion. BALANCEEquilibrium around an axis. Grounded in standing upright and perceiving symmetry. represents equilibrium — maintained every time you stand upright. LINKConnection between two entities. Grounded in physical linking (holding hands, chains, bridges) and social bonds. captures connection between entities. SCALE records continuous variation along a dimension.

Multiplicity and State Schemas

PART-WHOLE represents components to a larger entity. SPLITTING and MERGING capture divergence and convergence. CYCLE records recurring paths returning to start, grounded in heartbeat, breathing, day-night. ITERATION captures repetition. FULL-EMPTY marks container capacity. OBJECT establishes that there are bounded, discrete things in the world.

Five properties distinguish image schemas: they are pre-linguistic, embodied, spatial, schematic (abstract patterns, not specific images), and productive (they combine to form more complex structures).

How Infants Build Schemas: Mandler's Perceptual Meaning Analysis

The developmental psychologist Jean Mandler proposed the mechanism by which infants form image schemas from raw sensory experience: Perceptual Meaning AnalysisJean Mandler's proposed mechanism by which infants distill sensory input into schematic spatial structures — the process that creates image schemas. An innate, domain-general attentional mechanism. (PMA) — a data summarization device that reformats rich, continuous sensory input into a sparser, more abstract code.

Figure 2 — The developmental sequence from rich perceptual input through schematic consolidation. Stages 1-4 are pre-linguistic, making image schemas the deepest cross-cultural cognitive universals.

Certain spatial information is innately salient to the infant mind: paths of object motion that change spatial relations, objects going into or out of sight, contingencies between paths (chasing), and the critical distinction between self-moving entities and caused-motion entities (animacy detection, observable in infants as young as three months).

Through repeated exposure, PMA consolidates observations into schemas. "Things going into bounded regions" becomes CONTAINER. "Entity moving from here to there" becomes PATH. "One entity causing another to move" becomes CAUSED MOTION (FORCE + PATH). Infants as young as nine months distinguish animals from vehicles — not by appearance, but by motion pattern.

Why This Matters

If schemas are the first concepts every human constructs — prior to and independent of any spoken language — then they represent the deepest possible level of cross-linguistic, cross-cultural cognitive universality. A visual system built on them would be grounded in cognitive structures every human develops in infancy.

DISL: The Proof That Visual Formalization Works

The most important development for the visual language project is the Diagrammatic Image Schema Language (DISL)A formal visual notation for image schemas published by Hedblom, Neuhaus, and Mossakowski (published online 2024, Volume 25, 2025). Takes informal diagrams and formalizes them into a complete notation with defined symbols and composition rules., published by Maria Hedblom, Fabian Neuhaus, and Till Mossakowski in Spatial Cognition and Computation (published online 2024, in Volume 25, 2025). DISL formalizes informal cognitive linguistics diagrams into a complete visual notation system with defined symbols and explicit composition rules.

Figure 3 — DISL's visual primitives, composition rules, and strip format. Objects as circles (filled=active agent, outlined=passive patient), paths as arrows, regions as rectangles, forces as thick arrows.

DISL defines visual symbols for conceptual primitivesThe simplest spatial-relational elements from which image schemas are composed. In DISL: objects, paths, regions, forces, contacts.: objects as circles (filled for active agents, outlined for passive patients), paths as arrows (solid for actual, dashed for potential motion), regions as bounded rectangles, forces as thick arrows with width encoding magnitude, and contacts as shared boundary points.

The crucial innovation is DISL's composition rules: object inside region = containment, object connected to path arrow = traversal, object at receiving end of force arrow = patient undergoing causal action. These compositions follow the same spatial logic the schemas embody.

For temporal sequences, DISL uses a strip formatA sequence of panels representing a temporally extended narrative in DISL notation, inspired by comic strip conventions — the same cognitive tool humans have used for millennia. inspired by comic strips: panels read left to right, each depicting an image-schematic scene. DISL also includes a machine-readable logical exchange format — exactly what a practical visual language would need.

What DISL Proves vs. What It Does Not

Proves: Image schemas can be given formal, unambiguous visual representations. A small set of primitives generates a wide range of schemas through composition.

Does not yet do: Handle abstract domains (emotions, logic, social relationships). Provide metaphorical extension. Function as a productive communication system. It is a powerful foundation that needs additional layers to become a language.

The Neural Evidence

Neuroimaging studies show that processing metaphorical uses of image schemas activates both sensorimotor regions and abstract reasoning regions. Reading "she's in trouble" (CONTAINER schema used metaphorically) activates spatial processing areas alongside emotional processing areas. This dual activation is consistent with the metaphorical mapping account.

Developmental evidence is equally compelling. Infant looking-time paradigms confirm pre-linguistic sensitivity to containment violations, path continuity violations, and force dynamic events. These sensitivities exist before language, consistent with the claim that image schemas are acquired before words.

The implication: visual symbols activating these schemas should engage deep, pre-linguistic cognitive machinery. The learning curve for schema-based visual primitives should be shallow, because the mind is already primed to recognize containers, paths, and forces.

The Metaphorical Bridge: From Space to Abstract Thought

The most consequential claim of image schema theory is not about space. It is about everything else. Lakoff and Johnson demonstrated in Metaphors We Live By (1980) that abstract thought is systematically structured by metaphorical mappings from embodied experience.

Figure 4 — Four parallel chains from embodied experience through image schema to primary metaphor to abstract expression. The chain suggests visual primitives based on schemas could represent any concept human thought can express.

The chain works like this: during early development, spatial experiences co-occur with non-spatial ones. Piling objects makes piles go higher — more correlates with up. This generates the primary metaphorAn innate conceptual mapping from a sensorimotor source domain to an abstract target domain, arising from experiential correlation during development. Examples: MORE IS UP, PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS, STATES ARE CONTAINERS. MORE IS UP. Key mappings include:

  • PURPOSES ARE DESTINATIONS (PATH schema): "I'm on the path to success." "We've arrived at a conclusion."
  • STATES ARE CONTAINERS (CONTAINER schema): "I'm in trouble." "She's full of joy." "Deep in thought."
  • CAUSES ARE FORCES (FORCE schema): "She was driven to succeed." "He was pushed into a corner."
  • KNOWING IS SEEING (PERCEPTION schema): "I see what you mean." "A clear explanation."
  • INTIMACY IS CLOSENESS (PROXIMITY schema): "Close friends." "A distant relative."
  • AFFECTION IS WARMTH (TEMPERATURE-CONTACT schema): "A warm personality." "A cold response."
  • TIME IS MOTION (PATH schema): "The deadline is coming." "That's behind us."

Joseph Grady's Primary Metaphor Hypothesis (1997) identifies the specific sensorimotor correlations generating these mappings and argues they are universal because the bodily experiences are universal.

The Central Claim

If image schemas ground primary metaphors, and primary metaphors structure abstract thought, then a system that visually represents image schemas could — through metaphorical extension — represent any concept that human thought can express. The visual primitives for CONTAINER, PATH, and FORCE become the building blocks of all meaning.

Schema Composition: The Combinatorial Engine

The expressive power of image schemas resides in their composition. A single CONTAINER schema represents containment. A single PATH schema represents movement. Combine them: "entering" — an entity traversing a path terminating inside a bounded region. Combine FORCE with PATH: "pushing along." Combine all three: "putting something into a box."

With sixteen to twenty base schemas, binary compositions exceed two hundred. Ternary compositions number in the thousands. Add metaphorical extension and the territory becomes enormous.

Consider "I'm stuck in a dead-end job." This activates at least three schemas simultaneously: CONTAINER ("stuck in" — the job as container), PATH ("dead-end" — a path terminating without a goal), FORCE ("stuck" — blocked, unable to move). In a visual composition, all three would be present: a bounded region, a path within it terminating at a wall, and an entity at the blocked point.

Frequent multi-schema compositions should eventually crystallize into single visual units through lexicalizationThe process by which a frequent composition is stored and retrieved as a single unit, improving reading speed at the cost of compositional transparency. Like how the Chinese character for "look" (hand + eye) is now processed holistically. — just as the Chinese character for "look" (hand + eye) is now processed as a single unit.

The Open Schema List Problem

A significant difference from semantic primes: Wierzbicka's NSM defines exactly 65 primes — a closed, enumerated set. The image schema inventory has never been closed. Johnson's original 1987 list included roughly sixteen, but researchers have proposed additions while others argue some schemas are compositions rather than primitives.

This openness is both strength (flexibility) and weakness (users might decompose concepts differently). The practical resolution: define a core set — perhaps twelve to fifteen schemas appearing in virtually every researcher's inventory (CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE, BALANCE, LINK, CENTER-PERIPHERY, PART-WHOLE, UP-DOWN, NEAR-FAR, SCALE, CONTACT, CYCLE, SPLITTING, MERGING) — and treat others as compositional or community-defined extensions.

The Gap: Where Schemas Cannot Reach

Intellectual honesty requires confronting the limits. Image schemas handle spatial relationships directly, physical actions, causal dynamics, and — through well-established metaphor — emotions, goals, knowledge, social relationships, morality, and time.

Figure 5 — The gap between direct schema representation and required expressiveness. Well-established metaphors bridge many abstract domains, but speculative extensions and un-grounded concepts represent genuine unsolved challenges.

But several domains pose genuine challenges:

  • Logical operators (NOT, IF, MAYBE) — proposed groundings (negation as BLOCKAGE, conditionality as BRANCHING PATH) are theoretically motivated but less empirically established.
  • Propositional attitudes ("I believe that she thinks that he knows...") — nested mental state attribution pushes beyond spatial composition.
  • Cultural/institutional concepts (democracy, copyright, mortgage) — depend on social conventions rather than embodied experience.
  • Precise quantification (the difference between "some" and "37%") — demands specificity beyond schematic patterns.

A visual language built on image schemas will likely need a hybrid approach: schema-based symbols for the vast majority of meanings, with conventional symbols for domains that resist spatial grounding.

Common Misconceptions

Image schemas are mental images

Image schemas are schematic, not imagistic. The CONTAINER schema is not a picture of any specific container; it is the abstract structure of inside/outside/boundary. For visual design, symbols must capture the schematic structure — a circle with a dot inside is better than a detailed drawing of a cup.

Image schemas are just spatial vocabulary

The relationship runs in the opposite direction. Image schemas are pre-linguistic cognitive structures; the fact that English uses "in" for containment is a consequence of the CONTAINER schema, not the other way around. Languages express schemas differently, but the schemas appear universal.

DISL already provides a communication-ready visual language

DISL is an analytical notation for researchers. It lacks mechanisms for metaphorical extension, abstract logical operators, cultural vocabulary, or pragmatic flexibility. It is a foundation — a proof of concept — not the building.

From Schemas to Visual Alphabet: Design Implications

  • Small, distinct symbol set: Roughly 16-20 core schemas, represented with circles, arrows, rectangles, thick lines, contact points. Each must be visually distinguishable at reading sizes.
  • Rule-governed composition: Object inside region = containment. Force arrow agent-to-patient = causation. A grammar formalized from DISL's composition rules.
  • Systematic metaphorical extension: Explicit definitions of how CONTAINER means "emotional state" with an emotion indicator, how PATH means "purpose" with a goal indicator.
  • Permitted lexicalization: Frequent compositions eligible to crystallize into single symbols.
  • Honest gap addressing: Where schemas cannot ground meaning, test proposed encodings empirically and fall back to conventional symbols where necessary.

NSM Primes and Image Schemas: Complementary Coverage

A visual language cannot be built on image schemas alone, and it cannot be built on semantic primes alone. The two cover different territory, and their combined coverage exceeds either.

Where they overlap (INSIDE = CONTAINER, MOVE = PATH, ABOVE/BELOW = UP-DOWN, TOUCH = CONTACT), the integration is natural: the NSM prime specifies semantic content, the image schema provides visual form.

Where they diverge, each compensates for the other. Image schemas provide visual representations for spatial-physical concepts that NSM describes verbally. NSM primes provide semantic definitions for abstract concepts that schemas reach only through speculative metaphorical extension.

The integration architecture: for overlap concepts, the image schema IS the visual symbol for the NSM prime. For abstract primes (IF, BECAUSE, TRUE), the visual symbol draws on proposed metaphorical groundings but is explicitly taught as convention. For cultural constructs (democracy, copyright), the system uses compositional definition or community-assigned symbols.

The Convergence

The argument for image schemas as the foundation of a visual language is not that they can represent everything. It is that they represent the largest possible territory with the smallest possible set of primitives, grounded in the deepest possible level of human cognitive universality.

NSM provides the semantic inventory — the list of atomic meanings a language must express. Image schemas provide the visual vocabulary — the spatial forms through which those meanings can be drawn. DISL proves the formalization is possible. Mandler's developmental theory establishes cognitive universality. Grady's primary metaphors provide the extension mechanism. The neural evidence confirms cognitive reality.

The open questions — whether metaphorical extension reaches all the way to logic and cultural constructs, whether visual parsing of schema compositions is fast enough for fluent reading, whether schema-based symbols are recognized consistently across cultures — are empirical questions that a research program can answer.

The Pattern-Seeker's Reward

Image schemas are not an arbitrary organizational choice. They are the patterns the human mind uses to organize all experience — spatial and non-spatial, physical and abstract, personal and cultural. A visual language built on these patterns would be building on the cognitive bedrock of human meaning-making itself.
