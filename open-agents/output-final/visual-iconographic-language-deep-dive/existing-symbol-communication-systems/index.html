Existing Symbol Communication Systems What Works, What Doesn't, and What They Teach Us

Decades of real-world AAC deployment have produced hard-won lessons about visual communication. The failures are as instructive as the successes.

Validation Status

Research validated with overall confidence 8/10. Key corrections: PCS now contains over 79,000 symbols (not 45,000); original creator was Roxie Johnson. All other core claims independently confirmed.

1. Why Study the Predecessors?

Before designing a new visual communication system, there is a necessary discipline: study the ones that already exist. Not for academic completeness, but because these systems represent decades of real-world deployment — decades of discovering what actually happens when humans try to communicate through visual symbols.

Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC)Methods and technologies that supplement or replace speech for people who cannot rely on verbal language, serving populations including individuals with cerebral palsy, autism, intellectual disabilities, aphasia, and ALS. encompasses the methods and technologies that supplement or replace speech for people who cannot rely on verbal language. The symbol systems at the heart of AAC — PECS, PCS, Widgit, Makaton, Blissymbolics, SymbolStix — each embody different design philosophies and, critically, different trade-offs between ease of learning and power of expression.

The Counterintuitive Finding

The accumulated evidence from this field yields one finding that overturns intuition: the most important words for communication are not the ones you can easily draw a picture of.

Figure 1 — The AAC symbol system landscape comparing six major systems across iconicity, compositionality, vocabulary size, linguistic level, and regional dominance.

2. The Six Major Systems

PECS: Teaching Communication, Not Just Symbols

The Picture Exchange Communication System, developed by Andy Bondy and Lori Frost in 1985, is not primarily a symbol system at all. It is a behavioral teaching methodology that can use any pictures or symbols. Its innovation is in the pragmatics of communication — teaching people who do not spontaneously initiate communication to do exactly that.

This distinction is critical and often missed. Most AAC systems focus on the symbols. PECS focuses on the communicative act itself. Before any symbol design matters, the user must learn that communicating is a thing you do, that it produces results, and that you can initiate it.

Figure 2 — The six phases of PECS, from physical exchange (Phase 1) to spontaneous commenting (Phase 6). Communicative function drives vocabulary introduction, not the other way around.

PECSPicture Exchange Communication System -- a behavioral teaching protocol for communication (Bondy & Frost, 1985). Teaches communicative initiative through six phases, from physical exchange to spontaneous commenting. operates through six phases, each building on the last:

  • Phase 1: Physically handing a picture to another person to request something. No verbal prompts. The learner discovers that giving a symbol produces a desired result.
  • Phase 2: Traveling to find the communication book and a partner — building persistence and motivation.
  • Phase 3: Discrimination — choosing the correct picture from several options.
  • Phase 4: Sentence construction using an "I want" starter strip. The first moment anything resembling grammar appears.
  • Phase 5: Responsive communication — answering "What do you want?"
  • Phase 6: Spontaneous commenting: "I see a bird," "I hear music."
Design Principle from PECS

Communicative function should drive vocabulary introduction, not the other way around. Phase 1 can be achieved in hours, not weeks. A visual language that requires months of study before enabling a single communicative act has already failed the PECS test.

PCS: The Market Leader

Picture Communication Symbols (PCS)Tobii Dynavox's pictorial symbol set with over 79,000 symbols. The dominant symbol set in AAC worldwide. Created by Roxie Johnson in the early 1980s., originally developed by Roxie Johnson in the early 1980s and now owned by Tobii Dynavox, is the dominant symbol set in AAC worldwide. With over 79,000 symbols, PCS provides a pictorial symbol for virtually any common word.

The design philosophy is maximum pictorial clarity — make each symbol as immediately recognizable as possible. This works excellently for concrete nouns and simple actions. But it encounters a hard wall when it reaches abstract concepts: how do you draw "if"? How do you draw "because"? PCS handles these through arbitrary symbolic conventions that must be memorized. The result is a system with enormous vocabulary breadth but no compositional depth. Every new concept requires a new, dedicated symbol designed by the publisher.

Widgit: Rules You Can See

Widgit, originally developed as Rebus Symbols and now dominant in UK educational settings, takes a different approach. Where PCS maximizes pictoriality, Widgit maximizes visual consistency. Symbols are designed according to rules: related concepts share visual elements, and consistent conventions signal semantic relationships.

This is the compositionalityThe ability to build new meanings systematically from existing parts. In a fully compositional system, a learner who knows the base elements can compose symbols for concepts they have never encountered. principle in embryonic form. A user who learns that a specific visual element indicates "communication" can partially interpret any unfamiliar symbol containing that element. The ability to infer rather than merely recall.

Makaton: Three Channels at Once

MakatonA multimodal communication program using speech, signs (derived from BSL), and symbols simultaneously. ~450 core concepts organized in 8 stages, with over 11,000 resource vocabulary items., developed by Margaret Walker, Kathy Johnston, and Tony Cornforth in the 1970s, is distinctive for its multimodal approach. It uses three channels simultaneously: speech (full sentences spoken aloud), signs (key words signed using BSL-derived signs), and symbols (pictures reinforcing the message).

The theoretical basis connects directly to Paivio's dual coding theory: information encoded through multiple channels creates stronger, more retrievable memory traces. Makaton essentially implements triple coding. The vocabulary is structured in 8 stages of increasing complexity, starting with the most immediately functional needs: eat, drink, more, stop, yes, no.

Makaton reports over 1 million users worldwide across 40+ countries. Its 450 core concepts map closely to the 200–400 words that AAC research identifies as covering 80% of daily communication.

The Multimodal Lesson

A visual language should not be designed as a standalone, visual-only system. A visual language used alongside speech and gesture will be more effective than any single-channel system. The goal is adding a modality, not replacing existing ones.

Blissymbolics: The Closest Precedent

BlissymbolicsCharles Bliss's compositional visual language created in 1949. Uses approximately 120 base elements to compose over 5,000 authorized symbols. The only AAC symbol system with genuine compositional semantics. stands apart from every other AAC symbol system in one critical respect: it has genuine compositional semantics. Created by Charles K. Bliss in 1949 as "Semantography," it uses approximately 120 base elements to compose over 5,000 authorized symbols.

Figure 3 — How Blissymbolics composes meaning from ~120 base elements. Examples: heart + up = HAPPY (HAPPY IS UP), heart + down = SAD. The same primary metaphors that the visual iconographic language proposes are already present here.

The composition is semantic, not arbitrary. "Feeling" is represented by a heart symbol. "Happy" is heart + upward indicator. "Sad" is heart + downward indicator. "Protection" is enclosure + person. The same primary metaphors the visual iconographic language proposes as its foundation are already present in Blissymbolics, though used ad hoc rather than systematically.

Blissymbolics also has a grammatical indicator system: an inverted V marks actions (verbs), a small square marks things (nouns), a small V marks descriptions (adjectives). This enables the same base elements to function in different grammatical roles — a degree of flexibility no other AAC symbol system achieves.

The Hard Lesson

Blissymbolics is more linguistically powerful than PCS but harder to learn. In the real world of AAC deployment, PCS dominates. Learnability often wins over expressiveness. The market chose easy over powerful.

SymbolStix and Others

SymbolStix provides over 15,000 stick-figure style symbols emphasizing action and context. ARASAAC offers 12,000+ open-source symbols widely used in Spanish-speaking countries. Mulberry Symbols (~3,400) was designed specifically for adult AAC users, addressing the persistent problem that many AAC symbol sets look infantilizing to adults.

The diversity illustrates a broader point: one size does not fit all. Different user populations have different needs, aesthetics, cognitive profiles, and functional requirements.

3. The Core Vocabulary Discovery

The single most important finding from AAC research for any visual language project:

Approximately 200–400 high-frequency words account for 80% of daily communication. And these words are not what most people would guess.
Figure 4 — How 50, 100, 200, and 400 words cover increasing percentages of daily communication. The counterintuitive finding: nouns are fringe vocabulary. The most essential words are verbs, prepositions, and pronouns.

The research is consistent across speakers, ages, environments, and activities:

  • ~50 words cover 40–50% of everything people say
  • ~100 words cover about 60%
  • 200–400 words cover about 80%

Dominated by verbs, adjectives, pronouns, and prepositions -- not nouns. is dominated by verbs, adjectives, pronouns, and prepositions — not nouns. Words like "want," "go," "more," "not," "help," "like," "is," "can," "with" appear at the top of every frequency list. Nouns — the words that are easiest to draw pictures of — are overwhelmingly fringe vocabularyContext-specific, personally relevant words (mostly nouns) that supplement core vocabulary. Non-Negotiable Implication

"Apple" labels one object. "Want" enables a thousand requests: want apple, want drink, want go, want more, want help, want stop. The first 200 symbols must cover the functional words that make sentences possible, even though these are the hardest words to represent visually. Any visual language that starts with easy-to-picture concrete objects will produce users who can label things but cannot communicate.

4. The Fundamental Trade-Off

Across all six systems, a single tension defines the landscape: iconicityHow visually transparent a symbol is -- how easily it can be recognized without training. A picture of a cup is highly iconic; an abstract geometric shape is not. trades off against compositionality.

Figure 5 — Current AAC systems mapped on iconicity vs. compositionality axes. PCS: high iconicity, zero compositionality. Blissymbolics: low iconicity, full compositionality. The visual iconographic language targets the unoccupied zone: high compositionality with moderate iconicity via image-schema grounding.

Iconicity means visual transparency — how easily a symbol can be recognized without training. PCS maximizes this. Compositionality means productive power — the ability to build new meanings from existing parts. Blissymbolics maximizes this.

Current systems cluster along a frontier: more iconic tends to mean less compositional. The visual iconographic language aims for a position that no current system occupies: high compositionality with moderate iconicity. The mechanism: grounding base symbols in image schemas — the pre-linguistic spatial-relational structures (CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE, BALANCE) that arise from universal bodily experience.

Whether this is achievable remains an empirical question. No image-schema-based visual symbol system has been tested. But the existing landscape demonstrates that the trade-off space exists and that no current system occupies the target position. The gap is real.

5. AAC Technology and the Digital Requirement

Modern AAC operates through a layered technology stack: symbol sets (PCS, Widgit, SymbolStix) provide the visual vocabulary; software platforms (Proloquo2Go, TD Snap, Grid 3) present symbols for selection; input methods range from direct touch through switch scanning to eye trackingInput technology detecting eye gaze position, enabling symbol selection without motor action. Used by individuals with severe motor impairments such as ALS. for users with severe motor impairments.

Any new visual language must integrate with this existing ecosystem. Digital-first design is not optional — it is a hard constraint. SVG format for resolution independence, multiple input method support, and speech synthesis bridging for communication with non-users are all practical necessities.

The technology dimension also introduces a speed constraint. Communication through symbol selection is inherently slower than speech — perhaps 10–20 words per minute vs. 125–150 for typical speech. A compositional visual language introduces additional possibilities (compose novel meanings without navigating pre-made symbol screens) but also additional challenges (composition takes time).

6. What People Get Wrong

"Symbol-based communication is not real language." Most AAC symbol systems are less expressive than natural language, but this reflects the design of the systems, not the cognitive capacity of the users. Blissymbolics demonstrates genuine compositional semantics. AAC research consistently shows that users' communicative competence exceeds what their symbol system allows them to express.

"Pictorial symbols are universally understood." Even highly pictorial symbols require learning. A picture of a mailbox means "mail" in countries with a specific mailbox shape and nothing in countries where mail delivery looks different.

"Using symbol communication prevents speech development." Research consistently shows the opposite: AAC use does not inhibit speech development and may facilitate it. Professional organizations (ASHA, RCSLT) have issued position statements against withholding AAC.

"A nonspeaking person has nothing to say." The Raven's Matrices finding demonstrates that nonspeaking autistic individuals may possess rich cognitive lives invisible through the verbal channel.

7. What These Systems Teach

Lesson 1: Start Functional, Not Comprehensive

PECS achieves its first communicative act in hours. Makaton starts with eat, drink, more, stop. The most successful AAC systems get users communicating immediately with a tiny vocabulary, then expand.

Lesson 2: Core Vocabulary First, Always

The 200–400 most communicatively powerful words are verbs, prepositions, pronouns, and adjectives. Not nouns. Design for communicative power, not pictorial ease.

Lesson 3: Compositionality Is the Prize

Blissymbolics is the only AAC symbol system that enables users to express concepts they have never been explicitly taught symbols for. This productive power is what separates a symbol set from a language.

Lesson 4: Multimodal Beats Unimodal

Makaton's triple coding (speech + sign + symbol) demonstrates that multiple channels reinforce each other.

Lesson 5: Grounding Matters

Blissymbolics already uses metaphorical grounding in some compositions (HAPPY IS UP, SAD IS DOWN). But it uses it inconsistently. The visual iconographic language proposes to systematize this.

Lesson 6: The Market Will Choose Easy Over Powerful

PCS dominates not because it is the best-designed system but because it is the easiest to learn. A visual language that cannot compete on learnability will remain a niche tool regardless of its theoretical elegance.

Lesson 7: Bilingual Bridging Is Non-Negotiable

Every AAC system that succeeds includes a mechanism for communication with non-users. A visual language that can only be read by other visual language users is a private code, not a communication system. Speech synthesis bridging is a practical requirement.

8. Implications for the Visual Language Project

Requirements from AAC Evidence
  1. Minimum viable vocabulary: 200–400 concepts covering core vocabulary. Non-negotiable.
  2. Priority order: Verbs and function words first; nouns are expandable fringe vocabulary.
  3. Staged learning: Follow the PECS/Makaton model — immediate functional communication from day one.
  4. Compositional architecture: Blissymbolics proves compositionality is possible; image schemas may make it more learnable.
  5. Digital-first: SVG symbols, input method editors, speech synthesis bridging, AAC platform compatibility.
  6. Multimodal design: Visual language alongside speech and gesture, not replacing them.
  7. Learnability is a survival requirement: If it is harder to learn than PCS without demonstrably greater expressive power, it will not be adopted.
