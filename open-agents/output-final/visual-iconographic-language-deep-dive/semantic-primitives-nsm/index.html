Semantic Primitives and Natural Semantic Metalanguage The Atoms of Meaning

What Leibniz dreamed of and Wierzbicka found

Validation Status

This explanation draws on validated concept research (overall confidence 8/10). The NSM framework, the 65 primes inventory, the development history, and cross-linguistic validation claims were all confirmed. One correction applied: the semantic molecules count was revised — approximately 25 appear truly universal, with ~180 productive molecules catalogued for English alone.

What Is the Ground?

If you want to build a visual language from the ground up, you need to know what the ground is.

What are the irreducible building blocks of human meaning — the concepts that cannot be decomposed into simpler concepts, that every human language encodes, and from which all other meanings can be constructed? This is the question that Leibniz asked in 1666 when he proposed his "alphabet of human thought." It is the question that Anna Wierzbicka has spent over five decades investigating through her Natural Semantic MetalanguageA theory of semantic analysis that defines all meanings through irreducible primes. Developed by Anna Wierzbicka and Cliff Goddard, tested across 30+ languages. (NSM) program. And the answer — 65 semantic primesA concept that cannot be defined in simpler terms and has exact equivalents in all known languages; also called semantic primitive or conceptual atom., claimed to be universal across every known human language — is the most empirically grounded candidate for those atoms of meaning.

For a visual language project, NSM offers two critical gifts:

  1. A principled inventory of what must be visually representable: the 65 primes plus approximately 25 universal semantic moleculesAn intermediate concept defined from primes that serves as a building block for more complex meanings. Examples: HANDS, MOUTH, EAT, WATER..
  2. A method for defining any complex concept entirely in terms of primes: the explicationA reductive paraphrase that defines a concept entirely in terms of primes and molecules, eliminating circularity. technique, which eliminates circularity and produces cross-linguistically translatable definitions.

But NSM also poses a fundamental design challenge. Many primes are inherently abstract — IF, BECAUSE, NOT, VERY, MAYBE — and resist visual representation. Drawing a picture of SOMEONE is straightforward. Drawing a picture of IF is a design problem with no obvious solution.

The NSM Program: History and Development

Developed by Anna Wierzbicka, beginning at Warsaw University and continuing at the Australian National University, with Cliff Goddard at Griffith University as principal collaborator since the 1990s.

  • 1972: Wierzbicka proposes 14 semantic primes in Semantic Primitives
  • 1980s: Expansion and testing across additional languages
  • 1996: Semantics: Primes and Universals establishes 55 primes
  • 2002: Expansion to 60 primes
  • 2014: Current inventory stabilized at 65 primes

The expansion stopped — not because research stopped, but because no new irreducible concepts were found.

The identification method is rigorous: a concept qualifies as a prime if and only if (1) it cannot be defined in simpler terms, (2) it has exact lexical equivalents in all tested languages, and (3) its combinatorial propertiesThe grammatical patterns in which a prime participates (e.g., GOOD combines with SOMETHING as "something good"). are consistent across languages. Primes have been tested across more than 30 typologically diverse languages spanning every major language family.

Key Properties of Primes

  • Each prime has its own lexical embodiment in every tested language — not a gap filled by circumlocution.
  • Many primes have allolexesMultiple surface forms that express the same underlying prime concept. Example: "someone" and "person" in English are allolexes of the same prime. — multiple surface forms expressing the same concept. "Someone" and "person" are allolexes of the same prime.
  • DON'T WANT is a separate prime, not simply NOT + WANT. Many languages have a single, non-decomposable word for refusal. Some apparently compound concepts must be treated as atomic.

Semantic Molecules: The Intermediate Layer

Between the 65 atomic primes and the unlimited complexity of everyday concepts lies an intermediate layer: semantic molecules. These are words defined entirely using primes that serve as building blocks for more complex concepts.

Correction Note

The original research estimated ~50 universal molecules. Validation reveals approximately 25 are truly universal, with a broader inventory of ~60-80 near-universal. For English alone, approximately 180 productive molecules have been catalogued — but most are language-specific.

Categories include: body parts (HANDS, MOUTH, LEGS, EYES), physical activities (EAT, DRINK, SIT, SLEEP), shapes (ROUND, LONG, FLAT), environmental (WATER, FIRE, SUN, GROUND), and social (MAN, WOMAN, CHILD).

Molecules serve as compression — pre-built meaningful chunks that simplify the path from primes to complex meaning. For a visual language, molecules suggest a natural second layer of symbols above the prime inventory.

The Explication Method

The core technology of NSM is the explication — a reductive paraphraseThe method of defining a concept by expressing it in simpler terms, ultimately reaching primes. that captures complete meaning using only primes in canonical sentences. Not a dictionary definition; a decomposition eliminating all circularity.

Figure 2 — Three layers of meaning construction: 65 atomic primes, ~25 universal molecules, and unlimited complex concepts. The traced example shows HAPPY decomposing into its component primes.

Example: "Happy"

X felt something good at that time.
X felt something like this:
    "something good is happening to me"
    "I want this"
    "this is good"
X felt like this because X thought something good.

Every word — X, FELT, SOMETHING, GOOD, TIME, LIKE, HAPPEN, I, WANT, THIS, THINK, BECAUSE — is a semantic prime.

Example: "Angry"

X felt something bad at that time.
X felt something like this:
    "someone did something bad"
    "I don't want this"
    "I want to do something because of this"
X felt like this because X thought something like this.

The structural difference: happy involves GOOD + HAPPEN + WANT, while angry involves BAD + SOMEONE DID + DON'T WANT + WANT TO DO.

Example: "Afraid"

X felt something bad at that time.
X felt something like this:
    "something bad can happen to me"
    "I don't want this"
    "I can't do anything"
X felt like this because X thought something like this.

Angry adds WANT TO DO (agency) while afraid adds CAN'T DO ANYTHING (helplessness) and CAN HAPPEN (possibility). The primes capture these distinctions with precision.

Key Insight for Visual Language

Every NSM explication is a compositional structure built from primes. If each prime can be assigned a visual symbol, then any explication can — in principle — be visually composed. The question is whether the resulting compositions are readable, learnable, and efficient enough for actual communication.

The Visual Representability Challenge

Not all primes are equally amenable to visual representation. The 65 primes fall into three broad zones:

Figure 3 — All 65 primes classified into three zones: directly imageable (~25), metaphorically groundable (~20), and necessarily conventional (~20). The red zone marks the hardest design challenges.

Zone 1: Directly Imageable (~25 primes)

These primes have natural visual forms: spatial primes (ABOVE, BELOW, INSIDE, NEAR, FAR) through spatial arrangement; substantives (I, YOU, PERSON, BODY) through figures; descriptors (BIG, SMALL) through relative size; senses (SEE, HEAR) through eye/ear icons; basic quantity (ONE, TWO) through dots or tallies. These are the easy part — and the least interesting, because any pictographic system can handle them.

Zone 2: Metaphorically Groundable (~20 primes)

These resist direct depiction but can be represented through documented conceptual metaphors:

  • GOOD/BAD through GOOD IS UP / BAD IS DOWN — cross-culturally robust.
  • BEFORE/AFTER through TIME IS A HORIZONTAL LINE — earlier left, later right.
  • FEEL through body metaphor — hand over the heart/chest.
  • SOME, ALL, MUCH through quantity metaphors — partial fill, complete fill, overflow.

Zone 3: Necessarily Conventional (~20 primes)

Highly abstract primes that resist both iconic and metaphorical representation: logical (NOT, MAYBE, CAN, BECAUSE, IF), mental (THINK, KNOW, WANT, DON'T WANT), speech/truth (SAY, WORDS, TRUE), and abstract relations (THE SAME, OTHER, KIND OF, LIKE, VERY, MORE). These are where the hardest design challenges lie — the primes needed for grammar, logic, and complex thought.

Possible strategies: conventional symbols with metaphorical justification (NOT as blocked path, IF as forking path, BECAUSE as force arrow), spatial grammar encoding (position rather than shape), or purely arbitrary but learnable symbols (like mathematical +, -, =).

NSM Primes and Image Schemas: Complementary Inventories

A crucial insight: NSM primes and image schemas are not competing frameworks. They are complementary inventories covering different aspects of the same problem.

Several abstract primes can be metaphorically bridged: BECAUSE maps onto FORCE (causation as force dynamics), WANT maps onto ATTRACTION, NOT maps onto BLOCKAGE, IF maps onto PATH-WITH-BRANCH. These bridges are the most promising avenue for visually representing abstract primes.

The Proposed Architecture

Figure 5 — Proposed four-layer architecture combining image schemas (visual vocabulary) with NSM primes (semantic atoms), molecules (intermediate chunks), and open composition. This is a hypothesis, not a proven design.

Drawing together NSM, image schemas, and historical lessons, a four-layer architecture emerges:

  • Layer 0 — Image Schema Foundation: CONTAINER, PATH, FORCE, BALANCE, UP-DOWN, LINK, SCALE, CYCLE. The raw visual vocabulary.
  • Layer 1 — Semantic Primes: All 65 primes mapped to visual symbols — imageable (~25), metaphor-bridged (~20), conventional (~20).
  • Layer 2 — Semantic Molecules: ~25 universal molecules as compressed building blocks.
  • Layer 3 — Open Composition: Any concept through visual explication using primes and molecules.
Important Caveat

This architecture is a hypothesis. The gap between "any concept can be explicated in primes" (linguistically proven) and "any explication can be visually composed and read" (unproven) is where the critical open questions lie.

Criticisms and Limitations

Are 65 Primes Sufficient?

Some linguists argue 65 is too few. Complex concepts produce very long explications that may lose nuance. A visual language may need to tolerate this: the price of universality is verbosity.

Is Universality Overstated?

While tested in 30+ languages, exact lexical equivalence across all human languages is an extraordinarily strong claim. The NSM program acknowledges "a couple of difficult and/or contested cases."

The Semantic Molecules Problem

Critics argue molecules "do little more than conceal complexity." If they are merely convenient shortcuts, the architecture could potentially be simplified.

English-Centric Presentation

Despite cross-linguistic testing, primes are typically discussed using English exponents. A visual language would sidestep this criticism — visual symbols have no linguistic bias.
