1. The Question That Matters

After eleven categories of research — historical systems, theoretical frameworks, cognitive science, existing AAC deployment, sign language grammar, Chinese radical composition, image schemas, semantic primitives, and the neurological case for autistic visual-spatial processing — the only question that matters is practical.

Can a visual iconographic language actually be built, learned, and used?

The answer is not yes or no. It is a map of what is achievable, what requires research, and what may be impossible. Honest feasibility assessment requires separating these three categories and resisting the temptation to collapse them into a single optimistic or pessimistic verdict.

2. The Theoretical Foundation: Stronger Than You Might Think

The project is not speculation from a single angle. It draws on multiple converging evidence bases:

  • Natural Semantic MetalanguageNSM: 65 empirically validated semantic primes -- concepts that appear universal across all known human languages.   • Image schemaA pre-linguistic spatial-relational structure arising from embodied experience: CONTAINER (inside/outside), PATH (source, trajectory, goal), FORCE (push, pull, resistance), BALANCE, LINK, CYCLE, UP-DOWN. theory provides spatial-relational primitives grounded in pre-linguistic embodied experience.
  • Conceptual Metaphor Theory maps how these primitives systematically structure abstract thought (MORE IS UP, KNOWING IS SEEING, TIME IS MOTION).
  • The Diagrammatic Image Schema Language (DISLDiagrammatic Image Schema Language (2024) -- formalized visual representations of image schema primitives, providing a starting point for symbol design., 2024) has begun formalizing visual representations of image schema primitives.
  • Sign languages prove that full linguistic expressiveness is achievable in the visual-spatial modality — an existence proof.
  • Chinese character composition demonstrates that a finite set of visual building blocks (214 radicals) can generate an open-ended vocabulary.
  • Cognitive science confirms visual symbol memory advantages, VWFA plasticity, and autistic enhanced perceptual processing.

None of these foundations is individually sufficient. Together, they provide a convergent theoretical case stronger than any previous visual language attempt has had.

3. The Three Tiers of Feasibility

Figure 2 — Three tiers of feasibility. Tier 1 (green): achievable now, genuinely valuable on its own. Tier 2 (amber): plausible with 2–5 years of research. Tier 3 (red): may never be fully achievable semasiographically.

Tier 1: Achievable Now

What: Basic functional communication — requests, refusals, object identification, simple actions, basic spatial and temporal relations, emotions, questions, social exchange. Approximately 200–400 concepts covering core vocabularyThe 200-400 highest-frequency words accounting for ~80% of daily communication. Dominated by verbs, pronouns, prepositions -- not nouns..

Why it is achievable: Every capability in Tier 1 has been demonstrated by existing systems. Makaton's 450 core concepts achieve this level. PECS enables basic requesting within days. Blissymbolics' compositional approach works for this range. The difference would be in the grounding mechanism (image schemas and primary metaphors rather than arbitrary symbols).

What Tier 1 Looks Like

A core symbol set of 65–100 base symbols, combinable through spatial composition rules into 200–400+ composed concepts. A user could express: "I want to go home," "The food is too hot," "I feel sad," "Where is the book?" "Thank you," "Not that one."

Tier 2: Requires Research

What: Extended expressiveness — negation of complex propositions, conditional statements, temporal complexity, causation, quantification, relative clauses, modality (can/must/might/should), reported speech.

Why it requires research: Each of these capabilities involves grammatical structures that natural languages encode through word order, morphology, or prosody — mechanisms not directly available in a 2D static visual system. Sign languages achieve all of these, but they exploit temporal sequencing and 3D spatial arrangement.

The central engineering challenge: adapting sign language's spatial grammar principles to 2D static form. The most promising approach uses visual markers for conditional scope, visual negation operators, spatial reference zones, and visual quantifiers. Whether these 2D adaptations preserve the clarity of their 3D sources is the open research question.

Estimated timeline: 2–5 years of dedicated design research, user testing, and iterative refinement.

Tier 3: May Be Impossible

What: Full natural language equivalence — recursive embedding, counterfactual conditionals, precise technical and legal language, literary and poetic expression, humor, irony, sarcasm.

The Hard Truth

No semasiographicWriting that represents meaning directly, without encoding the sounds of a specific spoken language. No semasiographic system has ever achieved full natural language expressiveness. system — no writing that represents meaning directly without encoding spoken language sounds — has ever achieved full natural language expressiveness. Blissymbolics attempted it. Philosophical languages from Wilkins (1668) to Leibniz attempted it. None succeeded.

The design should aim for Tier 1 and Tier 2, not Tier 3. Partial achievement may be possible for some Tier 3 capabilities, but full equivalence with natural language in a 2D semasiographic system is unproven and may be impossible.

Honesty Check

These learnability targets are projections, not empirical data. No image-schema-based visual language has been tested with any learner population. The compositionality hypothesis — that grounded primitives shift the trade-off curve — is theoretically motivated but empirically untested. This is the single most important research gap in the entire project.

5. Compositionality: The Central Engineering Challenge

The visual language's value proposition depends on compositionalityThe ability to build complex meanings from simpler parts according to systematic rules. Without it, the system is just another symbol set requiring dedicated symbols for every concept.. Without it, the system is just another symbol set. With it, a learner who masters 100 base elements can express thousands of concepts.

What compositionality must achieve:

  1. Productive vocabulary: Any concept expressible by composing known elements. "Justice" from BALANCE + evaluation + social-group.
  2. Predictable meaning: A reader encountering an unfamiliar composition can work out what it means from its parts plus the combination rules.
  3. Grammatical marking: Distinguishing statements from questions, assertions from negations, encoding tense/aspect/modality.

Design approaches (synthesized from Chinese, sign language, Blissymbolics, and visual programming):

  • Spatial positioning as syntax: Agent/subject left; patient/object right; modifiers above; modified below; enclosure for scope.
  • Visual operators as grammatical markers: Strike-through for negation; distinctive enclosure for questions; directional flow for tense.
  • Radical-style semantic classification: Shared visual elements identifying semantic domains (emotion, action, spatial, temporal, evaluative).
  • Metaphor-based extension: Abstract concepts through primary metaphor mappings. "Understanding" = SEEING + GRASPING. "Progress" = PATH + forward.

6. The Phonetic Escape Hatch

A purely semasiographic system cannot represent "Tokyo," "Shakespeare," or "paracetamol" without some phonetic escape hatchA subsystem within a visual language that handles phonetic representation for proper names, borrowings, and novel technical terms. Every successful visual system has needed one.. Proper names, place names, borrowed words, and novel technical terms require phonetic representation.

Four design options exist:

  1. Featural phonetic notation (Hangul-inspired): Visual elements encode articulatory features. Language-independent but complex.
  2. IPA-based visual encoding: Cross-linguistically valid but requires phonetic knowledge.
  3. Alphabetic fingerspelling analogue: Simple and fast but privileges European languages.
  4. Phono-semantic hybrid (Chinese-inspired): Both semantic composition and phonetic approximation. Balanced but complex.

A hybrid approach — language-independent featural notation for formal use, shorthand alphabetic borrowing for casual use — may be optimal. The deeper lesson: purity versus pragmatism. Every successful writing system bridges between meaning and sound representation when needed.

7. The Adoption Problem: The Hardest Obstacle

The adoption problemThe chicken-and-egg challenge: nobody learns a language unless others use it, and nobody uses it unless others have learned it. Design quality alone does not determine adoption. — nobody learns a language unless others use it, and nobody uses it unless others have learned it — may be the greatest practical obstacle.

Historical precedents:

  • Esperanto: Well-designed, regular, easy to learn. 100,000–2 million speakers after a century. Has not achieved universal adoption.
  • Blissymbolics: Succeeded in AAC (filled a genuine need). Failed as a universal language.
  • Braille: Succeeded through institutional mandate, community advocacy, and practical necessity. Took decades.
  • Unicode: Succeeded because it solved a real technical problem with institutional support.
Figure 4 — Five-stage incremental adoption pathway. Each stage provides standalone value. The system does not need universal adoption to justify its existence.

The proposed adoption pathway follows five stages, each providing standalone value:

  1. Stage 1 — Personal notation: A thinking tool for a single user. Achievable now.
  2. Stage 2 — Small community: Communication between 2–10 people. Requires a prototype.
  3. Stage 3 — AAC supplement: Additional modality within existing AAC frameworks. Requires clinical validation.
  4. Stage 4 — Educational tool: Schools for communication support or literacy bridging. Requires curriculum.
  5. Stage 5 — Broader modality: Growing community for general-purpose communication. Requires governance.
Key Principle

Each stage provides value on its own. A single user who finds it useful as a thinking tool has already achieved Stage 1. The system does not need to reach Stage 5 to justify its existence.

9. Digital Implementation

The visual language requires a complete technology stack:

  • Symbol format: SVG with semantic metadata. Resolution-independent, renderable on any screen, searchable by meaning and component.
  • Input methods: Component-based selection (like Chinese pinyin input), drawing/gesture input, keyboard mapping, and touch-based composition.
  • Search and indexing: Symbols searchable by meaning, by component, and by semantic domain.
  • Encoding standard: Unicode-compatible encoding (Private Use Area initially, eventual standard proposal).
  • Speech synthesis bridge: Visual compositions translatable to spoken language for communication with non-users.
  • Accessibility: Screen magnification, high contrast, eye-tracking, switch scanning, screen reader compatibility via semantic metadata.

10. Honest Failure Modes

The opacity trapOver time, composed symbols become conventionalized -- their meaning remembered as a unit rather than derived from components. Exactly what happened to Chinese characters over three millennia.: Over time, composed symbols may become conventionalized — meaning remembered as a unit rather than derived from components. This is exactly what happened to Chinese characters. Mitigation: maintain an etymology database, design for ongoing transparency, establish governance (like BCI for Blissymbolics).

The learnability wall: Image-schema grounding may not actually make symbols easier to learn than arbitrary shapes. If so, the learning curve matches Blissymbolics', and the market will choose PCS. There is no mitigation except empirical testing.

The adoption desert: Even a brilliant system may never achieve community scale. A communication system used by one person is a code, not a language. The niche-first strategyStarting with a specific user community where the system provides clear value, then expanding. Every successful communication system (Blissymbolics, Braille, Unicode) started as a niche tool. addresses this, but there is no guarantee.

Cultural mismatch: Image schemas are claimed to be universal, but visual representations of those schemas may carry unintended cultural connotations. Cross-cultural testing is essential.

Cognitive fatigue: Extended visual symbol processing may be more demanding than reading text. Text reading benefits from thousands of years of typographic optimization. A new visual language would start without any of that.

Scope creep: A project beginning with "Tier 1 functional communication" can easily expand to "full natural language replacement" under the excitement of early success. The stage-gate adoption pathway is designed to prevent this.

11. What Next: A Realistic Plan

  1. Design the core symbol set: 65–100 base symbols grounded in image schemas, NSM primes, and core vocabulary research. Primarily a design task, achievable with current knowledge.
  2. Establish composition rules: Spatial positioning syntax, visual grammatical operators, composition templates. Drawing on sign language, Chinese radicals, and Blissymbolics.
  3. Build a digital prototype: SVG rendering, basic input method, searchable dictionary. Web-based for maximum portability.
  4. Test with users: Begin with autistic visual-spatial thinkers. Measure recognition speed, composition accuracy, learning curve, subjective preference. Iterate.
  5. Open-source everything: Symbol set, composition rules, digital tools, documentation. Community governance from the earliest stage.
The Order Matters

A symbol set without composition rules is just another PCS. The composition rules are what make it a language rather than a vocabulary. Testing must validate the compositional system, not just the individual symbols. And open-sourcing must happen early — every successful communication system evolved through community use.

12. Final Assessment

The visual iconographic language is theoretically grounded, practically motivated, and partially feasible.

Theoretically grounded: The convergence of NSM semantic primes, image schema theory, conceptual metaphor theory, sign language existence proofs, Chinese radical composition models, and cognitive science of visual processing provides a richer theoretical foundation than any previous visual language attempt.

Practically motivated: The modality mismatch between autistic visual-spatial cognition and verbal communication infrastructure is real, documented, and underserved.

Partially feasible: Tier 1 is achievable now. Tier 2 is plausible with research. Tier 3 may be impossible. Learnability targets are reasonable but untested. Adoption requires community building, not just design quality.

Historical Perspective

When Charles Bliss published Semantography in 1949, he had no empirical evidence it would work. Twenty-two years later, Shirley McNaughton adapted it for AAC, and it is still used in 33+ countries. When Louis Braille developed his system in 1824, there was no guarantee it would become the standard. It took decades. When the Unicode Consortium began in 1988, encoding all writing systems seemed impossibly ambitious.

The visual iconographic language has a stronger theoretical foundation than any of these had at inception. It has digital implementation advantages none had. It has a well-characterized target population. And it has something none of the universal language projects had: the explicit acknowledgment that it does not need universal adoption to be valuable.

Start small. Start practical. Build incrementally. Test everything. Let the evidence decide what works. And if certain elements do not work, adapt, narrow scope, and deliver what is achievable rather than abandoning the project because perfection was not possible.
