Arabic Morphology Broken Plurals, the Dual, and a Case System Hiding in Plain Sight

Arabic stacks nonconcatenative root-and-pattern morphology on top of fusional inflection — producing an order of magnitude more nominal forms than English

Validation Status

Critical correction applied: sound plurals shown as the statistical majority (74% by type, 61% by token per corpus research), correcting a widespread pedagogical myth about broken plural dominance.

1. The Scale of Complexity

If the root-and-pattern system is Arabic's defining morphological feature, the consequences of that system fill this category: broken pluralsPlurals formed by changing the internal vowel pattern of a word. The root stays; the template transforms. Arabic: jamʕ al-taksiːr, literally "plural of breaking." that shatter words from within, a dual numberA grammatical number for exactly two of something, formed by adding -aːn (nominative) or -ayn (accusative/genitive). Completely regular with no exceptions. that English abandoned a thousand years ago, a case system that marks grammatical role on every noun, and a construct state that chains nouns without connecting words.

The scale difference with English is dramatic. English has approximately 8 inflectional morphemes total: plural -s, possessive 's, third-person -s, past tense -ed, past participle -en, progressive -ing, comparative -er, superlative -est. That is the entire English inflectional system. Arabic has over 31 commonly used broken plural patterns alone — more patterns just for pluralization than English has inflections for everything.

Key Takeaways
  • Broken plurals change the internal vowel pattern of a word (the root stays, the template transforms)
  • Sound plurals (suffix-based) are actually the statistical majority in Arabic (74% by type, 61% by token), correcting a widespread pedagogical myth
  • The dual (-aːn/-ayn) is completely regular with no exceptions — one of Arabic's most elegant features
  • The three-case system (nominative, accusative, genitive) exists in grammar but is mostly dropped in spoken Arabic
  • A single Arabic noun like "kitaːb" can take 12 distinct inflected forms; English "book" has 2
Prerequisites

The root-and-pattern system. Broken plurals are a direct application of nonconcatenative morphology to number marking. Understanding that Arabic weaves vowels between consonant roots is essential.

Phonemic vs. orthographic forms. Arabic case endings are short vowels, often unwritten. The forms discussed here represent the full phonemic system (Modern Standard Arabic with case endings), which is more complex than what most speakers actually pronounce.

2. Broken Plurals: When the Word Shatters From Within

Most languages form plurals by adding something to the outside of a word. English adds -s: book becomes books. Arabic does this too (with "sound" plurals), but Arabic also has a fundamentally different strategy: it changes the internal vowel pattern of the word. The singular form is "broken apart" and reassembled with different vowels.

The root stays. The pattern changes. The word transforms from within.

The 31+ Patterns: Organized Chaos

Arabic grammarians have catalogued over 70 broken plural patterns, but about 31 are commonly used. They organize into two groups:

Plurals of paucity (جمع القلة, for small numbers 3–10):

  • ʔafʕul: ʔashhur "months"
  • ʔafʕaːl: ʔawlaːd "boys"
  • ʔafʕila: ʔasʔila "questions"

Plurals of abundance (جمع الكثرة, for larger numbers or default):

  • fuʕuːl: buyuːt "houses," quluːb "hearts"
  • fiʕaːl: rijaːl "men," jibaːl "mountains"
  • mafaːʕil: madaːris "schools," makaːtib "offices"

Can You Predict the Broken Plural?

The answer: partially, and increasingly well with experience. Regular correspondences exist — the singular pattern often predicts the plural pattern:

  • Singular CaCiːC (faʕiːl) frequently yields plural CuCaCaːʔ (fuʕalaːʔ): kabiːr becomes kubaraːʔ "elders"
  • Singular CaːCiC (faːʕil) frequently yields plural CuCCaːC (fuʕʕaːl): ʕaːmil becomes ʕummaːl "workers"
  • Singular maCCaC (mafʕal) frequently yields plural maCaːCiC (mafaːʕil): maktab becomes makaːtib "offices"

The system is patterned enough to be partially predictable, irregular enough to require some memorization. Broken plurals are genuinely the hardest part of Arabic nominal morphology.

3. Sound Plurals: The Regular Alternative

Sound plurals preserve the base word and add a suffix:

  • Masculine sound plural: -uːn (nominative) / -iːn (accusative/genitive). Used primarily for male humans and participles: مُعَلِّمون muʕallimuːn "teachers (m.)"
  • Feminine sound plural: -aːt. Used for feminine nouns and loanwords: مُعَلِّمات muʕallimaːt "teachers (f.)," tilifizyuːnaːt "televisions"

Correcting a Widespread Myth: Sound Plurals Are the Majority

Myth vs. Reality

A commonly repeated claim in Arabic pedagogy states that broken plurals "dominate" Arabic, with sound plurals being the exception. This is incorrect. Corpus-based research analyzing actual Arabic text reveals:

  • Sound plurals account for 74% of word types and 61% of word tokens
  • Broken plurals account for 26% by type and 39% by token

The traditional claim comes from dictionary sampling bias. Dictionaries disproportionately list uncommon, semantically specific nouns — precisely the category where broken plurals are most frequent. In actual running text, sound plurals are the clear majority.

4. The Dual Number: Two Is Not Just Plural

English has two grammatical numbers: singular (one) and plural (two or more). Arabic has three: singular (one), dual (exactly two), and plural (three or more).

The dual is formed by adding -aːn (nominative) or -ayn (accusative/genitive) to the base form. This is completely regular — no exceptions whatsoever. Every noun, adjective, verb, and pronoun can take dual form.

English comparison: Old English had a dual preserved in pronouns (wit "we two" vs. weː "we"). Modern English lost it entirely by ~1200 CE. Arabic grammatically requires the dual form for exactly two of anything — using the plural for two items is ungrammatical.

The Dual in Everyday Use

The dual is not a literary archaism — it is used in everyday spoken Arabic across all dialects. The dual also interacts with Arabic numerals in a characteristic way: numbers 3–10 use the plural form; numbers 11–99 use the singular; but the number 2 uses the dual, and the numeral "two" is often omitted because the dual form already encodes "two-ness." The grammar itself does the counting.

5. The Case System: Grammatical Role Marked on Every Noun

Arabic has three grammatical cases marked by short vowel endings:

English comparison: English has no case marking on nouns except the possessive 's. English signals grammatical role through word order: "The dog bit the man" vs. "The man bit the dog." Arabic can rearrange noun positions more freely because case endings identify subjects and objects independently of position.

The Practical Reality: Cases Are Mostly Inaudible

In all modern spoken Arabic dialects, short vowel case endings are universally dropped. The case system exists in the grammar but is phonetically absent in everyday speech. Case endings survive in formal registers (news broadcasts, lectures, religious recitation), fully vowelized written texts, and certain fixed phrases. For learners, this means the case system must be understood conceptually but is not required for basic communication.

6. The Construct State: Chaining Nouns Without Connectors

The idaːfaThe construct state (literally "annexation"): Arabic's way of expressing possession and association by placing two nouns in sequence with no connecting word. (إضافة) is Arabic's way of expressing possession by placing two nouns side by side:

English: "the book of the teacher" or "the teacher's book" (requires "of" or "'s")
Arabic: كِتابُ المُعَلِّمِ kitaːbu l-muʕallimi (just two nouns side by side)

Chains extend to multiple nouns: بابُ بَيْتِ المُعَلِّمِ baːbu bayti l-muʕallimi = "the door of the house of the teacher." Three nouns, zero connecting words.

Figure 4 — Two-noun and three-noun idaːfa chains, with labeled components and English comparison showing required connectors.

The idaːfa encodes not just possession but also material (a ring of gold), contents (a cup of coffee), origin (the people of the city), and purpose (a bedroom = "room of sleeping"). Context disambiguates.

7. Gender Marking: The Taʼ Marbuːta and Beyond

Most feminine nouns are marked with ة (taʼ marbuːtaThe feminine ending marker in Arabic script. Most feminine nouns carry it, but some feminine nouns lack it and rare masculine nouns have it.): muʕallim (teacher, m.) becomes muʕallima (teacher, f.). But the system has exceptions in both directions:

  • Feminine without taʼ marbuːta (must be memorized): أُمّ ʔumm "mother," شَمْس shams "sun," أَرْض ʔard "earth"
  • Masculine with taʼ marbuːta (rare): خَليفة khaliːfa "caliph"

8. The Diminutive: Shrinking Words Through Pattern

Even diminutives in Arabic use internal pattern change rather than suffixation. The diminutiveA pattern expressing smallness or endearment. Arabic uses the internal pattern fuʕayl rather than adding a suffix like English -let or -ling. pattern for trilateral nouns is fuʕayl:

  • kitaːb (book) becomes kutayyib (booklet)
  • walad (boy) becomes wulayd (little boy)
  • kalb (dog) becomes kulayb (puppy)

The name حُسَيْن Husayn ("Hussein") is itself a diminutive of حَسَن Hasan — literally "little Hasan" or "dear Hasan."

English comparison: English diminutives are concatenative suffixes: -let (booklet), -ling (duckling), -ette (kitchenette). Arabic diminutives, like Arabic plurals, work from within.

9. One Noun, Many Forms

To see the scale of Arabic nominal morphology, consider كِتاب kitaːb "book" in all its inflected forms:

Singular: kitaːbun, kitaːban, kitaːbin (indefinite, three cases) / al-kitaːbu, al-kitaːba, al-kitaːbi (definite) = 6 forms

Dual: kitaːbaːni, kitaːbayni (indefinite) / al-kitaːbaːni, al-kitaːbayni (definite) = 4 forms

Plural: kutubu, kutuba, kutubin (indefinite, broken plural, three cases) / al-kutubu, al-kutuba, al-kutubi (definite) = 6 forms

That is approximately 12–16 distinct forms from one noun. English "book" has 2 forms: book, books.

10. Edge Cases and Exceptions

DiptotesNouns taking only two case endings instead of three. They lack tanwiːn and use fatha (-a) for both accusative and genitive. (mamnuːʕ min al-sarf): Certain nouns take only two case endings instead of three — they lack tanwiːn and use fatha (-a) for both accusative and genitive. This affects broken plurals of the maCaːCiC pattern, many proper nouns, and comparative adjectives.

The five nouns (أب "father," أخ "brother," حم "father-in-law," فم "mouth," ذو "possessor of"): These show case through long vowels (-uː nominative, -aː accusative, -iː genitive) when in the construct state — relics of an older morphological system.

Non-human plural agreement: This is one of Arabic's most counterintuitive rules. Plurals of non-human nouns are treated as grammatically feminine singular for adjective and verb agreement. كُتُب كَبيرة kutub kabiːra "big books" uses the feminine singular adjective, not a plural form. The books — no matter how many — agree as if they were a single feminine entity.
