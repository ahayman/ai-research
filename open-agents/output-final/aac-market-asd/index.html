01 Executive Summary

The core problem: AAC products are built for motor-speech bypass, but ASD communication struggles are primarily social and pragmatic. Autism is the single largest AAC user population (~19–23M globally needing dedicated AAC), yet the communication struggles most central to ASD — pragmatic/social cognition (Domain 3), receptive comprehension of figurative language (Domain 1), and psychosocial barriers (Domain 6) — are the least addressed by every product on the market. All 14 products excel at motor speech bypass (Domain 2.1), which is only a mild/variable concern for most autistic individuals.

Key Finding 1

Pragmatic/social cognition is the #1 ASD struggle but ranked last in product support. Zero AAC apps address Theory of Mind, emotion recognition, or social judgment. Only TD Snap (via PODD) and Avaz (SCERTS alignment) offer structured pragmatic frameworks.

Key Finding 2

Situational speech loss ("going nonverbal") is unserved. Many verbal autistic people lose speech under stress, sensory overload, or emotional overwhelm. No AAC product is designed as a quick-deploy backup for intermittent speech loss.

Key Finding 3

Figurative language is a concrete, solvable gap. Meta-analytic evidence (PMC5843023) confirms significant comprehension deficits for idioms, sarcasm, and metaphor in ASD. LLM-powered real-time detection and translation is technically feasible today but no product offers it.

Key Finding 4

The ASD AAC market segment is growing fastest. CDC prevalence rose from 1 in 36 to 1 in 31 in one reporting cycle. Market research identifies autism as the fastest-growing application segment in the AAC devices market. Access disparities persist: 84% of white families have AAC access vs. 32% of minority families.

03 ASD Communication Struggles Profile

How autism maps to the 7-domain Communication Struggles Taxonomy

ASD is unique among AAC conditions: the primary struggle is pragmatic/social (Domain 3), not motor or expressive. Most AAC conditions (CP, ALS, apraxia) are defined by inability to physically produce speech. In ASD, the motor system is typically intact or mildly affected. The core communication challenges are social cognition, pragmatic language use, and receptive processing of nonverbal/figurative communication.

ASD-Specific Struggle Details

Pragmatic/Social (PRIMARY)

  • Theory of Mind deficits (core diagnostic feature)
  • Turn-taking: difficulty knowing when to speak/listen
  • Topic maintenance: abrupt shifts to preferred topics
  • Conversational repair: struggles detecting breakdowns
  • Register adaptation: may use overly formal language
  • Social judgment: difficulty with "reading the room"
  • Narrative construction: excessive/insufficient detail

Receptive (Mild but Specific)

  • Figurative language: literal interpretation of idioms, sarcasm, metaphor (meta-analytic evidence, PMC5843023)
  • Prosodic comprehension: atypical processing of tone/emphasis
  • Nonverbal cue reading: may process facial expressions through different cognitive pathways
  • Pragmatic inference: difficulty inferring implicit meaning

Psychosocial (Moderate)

  • Stigma from using AAC devices (attitudes from peers)
  • AAC abandonment driven by social pressure
  • Identity: child-oriented symbols inappropriate for teens/adults
  • The "double empathy problem" reframes social difficulties as reciprocal, not solely autistic deficit

Situational Speech Loss

"Going Nonverbal" — An Unaddressed ASD-Specific Phenomenon Distinct from permanent minimal verbal status. Many autistic individuals who typically speak fluently lose speech under stress, sensory overload, emotional overwhelm, or in unfamiliar environments.

Characteristics: Intermittent expressive failure with preserved receptive abilities. May involve motor planning shutdown, anxiety-mediated speech block, or sensory overload cascading to the speech system.

AAC Implication: Requires systems that can be quickly activated as backup when speech becomes unavailable, then set aside when speech returns. Suggests need for "emergency mode" AAC always accessible on a smartphone.

Current Solutions: None purpose-built. Closest options are Proloquo4Text (iPhone/Apple Watch messaging-style) and Spoken (modern chat interface), but neither is designed for rapid deploy/stow.

ASD Trajectory

ASD is classified as Developmental/Variable. Unlike progressive conditions (ALS, dementia) or sudden-onset (stroke), ASD communication abilities can:

  • Improve significantly with intervention and maturation
  • Fluctuate situationally (speech loss under stress)
  • Remain stable across the lifespan
  • Require different AAC at different life stages

Strong Evidence for ASD

  • Proloquo2Go: Enhanced classroom performance in ASD children (PubMed 30255726). Increased socio-communicative behaviors between minimally speaking autistic children and peers (PMC11253647). Reduced echolalia (ERIC ED577783).
  • LAMP WFL: All 8 children with autism made gains in spontaneous communication. 100% developed social communication through commenting. Parents/teachers reported gains "greater than those achieved in previous interventions."
  • Symbol-based AAC + aided language modeling: Multiple systematic reviews confirm effectiveness for ASD children.
  • PECS → symbol-based SGD: Meta-analysis supports progression pathway for pre-literate ASD.

Limited/Emerging Evidence

  • Proloquo: Limited ASD-specific studies due to recency. Internal data shows 60-button vs 77-button grid produced same language outcomes across 10,000+ users.
  • TD Snap (PODD): PODD has strong clinical evidence for pragmatic organization, but specific TD Snap + ASD studies are limited.
  • Avaz: Tested in 25+ schools with 500 children (India, self-reported). Limited peer-reviewed Western studies.
  • NAO-assisted therapy: Emerging 2025 research on robot-assisted AAC for minimally verbal autistic children.
  • Peer-mediated AAC: 2025 research shows peers can learn responsive interaction strategies to foster AAC use.
Evidence Gap: Most AAC research is on children. Adult ASD AAC evidence is nearly nonexistent. Speaking autistic adults who use AAC report it is "life-changing" (ASHA Perspectives 2021), but no rigorous outcome studies exist for adult ASD AAC use, situational speech loss management, or pragmatic support tools.

School-Home Coordination Breakdown

The school-home transition is the single largest barrier to AAC success for autistic children. Our analysis identifies six primary breakdown points:

  • Vocabulary mismatch: School vocabulary unavailable at home and vice versa
  • Device availability: Devices left at school or restricted from going home
  • Training gap: Parents receive minimal AAC modeling instruction
  • Partner differences: Different adults use different interaction strategies
  • Data silos: No shared view of communication progress across settings
  • Paper-based coordination: Most school-home communication relies on notes in backpacks
Research finding: Generalization across environments is the primary outcome determinant for autistic children's AAC success. The "train and hope" model persists: provide device, hope it gets used across settings.

06 LLM Applications for ASD Communication

How large language models can address ASD-specific communication patterns no current product serves

Research finding: No AAC app combines LLM technology with ASD-specific features. While nascent LLM adoption exists in AAC (Vocable/ChatGPT, Cboard/OpenAI, PRC-Saltillo/Apple Intelligence for editing), none target ASD-specific needs. Autistic communication patterns — literal thinking, monotropism, gestalt language processing, and pragmatic differences — require fundamentally different language technology than general-purpose text prediction.

How Autistic Communication Differs

1
Literal Thinking Autistic individuals frequently process language literally, creating challenges with idioms, metaphors, sarcasm, and indirect speech. Meta-analytic evidence (PMC5843023) confirms significant figurative language comprehension deficits.
2
Monotropism Deep focus on a narrow range of interests, with difficulty processing multiple things simultaneously. LLMs could serve as a "cognitive buffer" handling multi-channel processing that monotropic individuals find challenging.
3
Gestalt Language Processing An estimated 75–80% of verbal autistic individuals engage in echolalia. Increasingly recognized as functional and meaningful, not merely an impairment. No AAC app has built GLP-specific features.
4
Pragmatic Differences Core challenges in turn-taking, topic maintenance, conversational repair, and reading non-verbal cues. The "double empathy problem" reframes this as bidirectional, not solely an autistic deficit.

Ethical Considerations

Autonomy & Authentic Voice

ASHA 2025: AAC users expressed more concern about professionals becoming gatekeepers than technology overreach. Design principle: LLM should amplify the user's voice, not replace it.

The Bias Paradox

CHI 2025 found ChatGPT demonstrates a "bias paradox" — highlighting representation importance while perpetuating mostly negative biases about autistic people and suggesting masking behaviors linked to burnout and depression.

Mitigation: Custom fine-tuning on neurodiversity-affirming language, autism advisory board, regular bias audits, reject deficit framing, never suggest masking by default.

Communication Rate Crisis

AAC users communicate at 8–10 WPM vs. 150–160 WPM for natural speech — a 15–20x gap that compounds every school participation challenge.

Our analysis found LLM-powered rate enhancement is the most technically mature opportunity:

  • SpeakFaster: 29–60% rate improvement with LLM abbreviation expansion
  • 57% fewer motor actions than traditional predictive keyboards
  • Participants reached 20–30 WPM with 6–15 practice dialogs
  • Character-based LLM adaptation (arxiv 2501.10582) solves AAC letter-by-letter mismatch

Research finding: No AAC app detects or adapts to emotional state. Emotional dysregulation is intrinsic to the autistic experience — not a peripheral concern. A 2024 literature review confirmed it is "part of the autism spectrum continuum." Interoception difficulties (the "eighth sense") create a mechanistic link between autism, alexithymia, and dysregulation. Yet every existing AAC app presents a static interface regardless of the user's emotional state.

Interoception: The Mechanistic Link

Interoception — perceiving and interpreting internal bodily signals — is the critical mechanistic link. A 2025 systematic review (31 studies) found:

  • Autistic people often struggle to recognize hunger, pain, temperature, and critically, emotional states
  • "Attenuated precision-weighting of interoceptive prediction errors may disrupt emotional processing loops"
  • Autistic children show less differentiated bodily maps of emotion — uniform sensations across different emotions

Kelly Mahler's Interoception Curriculum: 25-lesson protocol found feasible in school settings and effective for improving interoception in autistic children. 25-week implementation showed gains in affective regulation and homeostatic regulation.

App opportunity: Combine physiological detection with body-map visualization to build interoception skills — transforming the app from "emotion detection" to an "interoception development tool" (neurodiversity-affirming framing).

08 Voice Customization & Identity for ASD

Sensory processing, voice cloning feasibility, identity needs, and school-setting challenges for autistic AAC users

Voice Preferences in ASD

Key Research Findings

1
50–70% of autistic children have sound sensitivity affecting voice perception Loud, sharp, or high-pitched sounds cause distress. Some voices that sound pleasant to neurotypical listeners may be aversive to autistic users. Voice previewing and trial periods are essential.
2
Enhanced pitch perception in ASD (meta-analysis of 22 studies) 464 participants showed small-to-medium positive effect size for pitch discrimination. Autistic users may detect subtle differences in synthetic voice pitch and timbre that neurotypical listeners miss — voices that sound “close enough” may sound noticeably wrong.
3
Some autistic users may prefer predictable/consistent voices over natural-variable ones Social robot research shows autistic children prefer “consistent voice and gestures” in a “structured interaction environment.” The predictability of synthetic speech — stable pitch, consistent pacing — may be a feature, not a bug.
4
3–6x higher gender diversity in autistic population → voice identity is critical Trans and nonbinary people are 3–6x more likely to be autistic. 68% of nonbinary adults using AT report voice-related distress. AAC voices listed as “men’s, women’s, or children’s” with no gender-neutral option. Voice-as-identity is a rights issue.

Voice Cloning Feasibility for ASD

The Challenge: Motor planning difficulties block traditional voice cloning. ~40% of non-verbal autistic children have motor planning difficulties. Apraxia co-occurrence with autism: 63.6% (three-year study). Many cannot produce the 150 sentences Apple Personal Voice requires, or even 50 sentences for Acapela My-Own-Voice.
The Solution: Ultra-low-input voice cloning technology. NeuTTS Air needs only 3–15 seconds of audio. Chatterbox needs just 5 seconds. These thresholds are achievable even for users with limited vocalizations. For those who cannot provide any samples, family voice blending (VocaliD model) creates voices carrying familial vocal characteristics.

Part-time AAC users who have some natural voice are ideal candidates for voice cloning — their AAC voice could match their natural voice, creating a seamless experience when switching between speech and AAC.

Nonspeaking individuals raise profound identity questions: if a child has never spoken, who decides what they should sound like? CommunicationFIRST: “Every person should get a chance to listen to voices and choose their own voice, with that choice being entirely their own.”

Bullying & Stigma

Up to 94% of autistic students experience bullying; 87% weekly, 40% daily. “Robot voice” stigma from AAC devices creates an additional vector for teasing. Natural-sounding, age/gender-matched voices may reduce this vulnerability.

COPPA 2025 & Voiceprints

FTC amended COPPA Rule (effective June 2025; compliance April 2026) classifies voiceprints as biometric data. Requires verifiable parental consent before collection. Prohibits indefinite data retention. Separate consent required for AI training use.

FERPA & Voice Data

Biometric records constitute PII under FERPA. Voice data collected as part of educational services is protected. March 2025: DOE required all state agencies to certify FERPA compliance — unprecedented enforcement.

Illinois BIPA Precedent

$8.75M class action settlement against Google for collecting face/voice models of ~660,000 Illinois students. BIPA explicitly lists “voiceprint” as biometric identifier. 23+ states now have biometric privacy laws.

Apple Personal Voice: Best School Option

On-device processing, encrypted local storage, no cloud data transmission. Most COPPA/FERPA-compliant voice cloning option. iOS 26 reduces requirement from 150 sentences to just 10 phrases (~60s), making it accessible to more autistic users.

Offline Requirement

Many school WiFi networks are unreliable or have content filtering that blocks voice synthesis services. Cloud-dependent voices may change mid-day. Any school voice solution needs robust offline fallback maintaining the student’s voice identity.

Voice Cloning Input Requirements

Minimum audio input needed for voice cloning by platform. Lower is better for autistic users with limited speech production.

09 ASD-Specific Market Gaps

What every product fails to deliver for autistic users

Social Cognition Support

Zero products address this

No AAC app provides Theory of Mind support, emotion recognition aids, perspective-taking prompts, or social cue scaffolding. This is the core ASD diagnostic feature and it's completely unserved.

Figurative Language Translation

Zero products address this

Meta-analytic evidence confirms significant figurative language comprehension deficits in ASD. No app flags idioms, sarcasm, or metaphor and provides literal alternatives. LLM technology makes this feasible today.

Quick-Deploy Situational AAC

Zero products designed for this

Verbal autistic individuals who experience situational speech loss need AAC that activates in seconds from a phone, provides essential communication, then stows when speech returns. No product targets this use case.

Conversational Repair

Passive only — no active support

Some apps have repair phrases ("I didn't mean that"), but no app detects breakdowns or proactively suggests alternative phrasings. ASD users struggle to detect when communication has failed.

Conversational Timing

Universal problem, compounded in ASD

No "I'm composing" signals, quick interjection banks, or turn-taking support. ASD users have additional difficulty with turn-taking norms, making the AAC rate gap (8–15 WPM vs. 150+ WPM speech) especially isolating.

Sensory-Aware Design

Minimal consideration

Poorly customized AAC can cause sensory overwhelm and physical discomfort, a specific ASD abandonment driver. Few products offer low-stimulation modes, reduced animation, or sensory preference profiles.

ASD Abandonment Barriers

Research identifies 5 primary barriers to AAC success in autism (PMC11197385, Frontiers in Psychiatry 2024). Up to 50% of AAC users abandon their devices.

12 Strategic Landscape

Strategic landscape for an ASD-focused AAC product

Competitive Positioning

The AAC market is consolidating into 3 power centers (Dynavox Group, Smartbox/TTMT, PRC-Saltillo), creating opportunity for a differentiated new entrant.

Why ASD-first makes strategic sense: ASD is the largest AAC user population, the fastest-growing market segment, and the most poorly served by current products relative to its specific needs. The features ASD users need most (receptive support, pragmatic coaching, sensory adaptation) are in patent-free zones, technically feasible with current LLM technology, and differentiated from every incumbent. An ASD-first strategy serves the largest market with the deepest unmet needs while building a platform that extends to other conditions.
