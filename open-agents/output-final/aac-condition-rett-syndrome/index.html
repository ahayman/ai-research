Rett Syndrome & AAC

A comprehensive guide to understanding Rett syndrome, its profound impact on communication, and how augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) technologies — especially eye gaze systems — are transforming the lives of individuals with this rare neurological condition.

1 Overview

Rett syndrome is a rare, severe neurodevelopmental disorder that predominantly affects females. First described by Austrian physician Andreas Rett in 1966, the condition was not widely recognized until the 1980s when Swedish researcher Bengt Hagberg and colleagues published a landmark paper bringing it to international attention. In 1999, researchers at Baylor College of Medicine identified mutations in the MECP2 gene (methyl-CpG-binding protein 2) on the X chromosome as the primary genetic cause, a discovery that transformed understanding of the condition.

Children with Rett syndrome typically develop normally for the first 6 to 18 months of life. During this period, they may begin babbling, making eye contact, crawling, and even acquiring a small vocabulary. Then, in a devastating regression phase, they lose many of these hard-won skills. Speech may disappear entirely. Purposeful hand movements are replaced by repetitive stereotypic movements such as hand-wringing, clapping, or washing motions. Breathing irregularities, seizures, scoliosis, and impaired mobility develop over time.

Despite these profound motor and speech difficulties, there is growing scientific evidence that the cognitive abilities of individuals with Rett syndrome have been significantly underestimated for decades. Research using eye-tracking technology has revealed that many individuals with Rett syndrome understand far more than they can express — a finding that has profound implications for communication intervention and the role of AAC.

Key Insight

Rett syndrome is fundamentally a disorder of motor output, not necessarily of cognitive understanding. The inability to speak or use hands purposefully does not mean an inability to think, understand, or feel. This distinction is critical for communication intervention planning.

2 Genetics & Stages

The MECP2 Gene

Approximately 95% of classic Rett syndrome cases are caused by mutations in the MECP2 gene, located on the long arm of the X chromosome (Xq28). The MeCP2 protein plays a crucial role in brain development, particularly in synaptic development, neuronal maturation, and epigenetic regulation. It functions as a transcriptional regulator, binding to methylated DNA and modulating gene expression across the genome. When MECP2 is mutated, the resulting protein dysfunction disrupts normal neuronal development and function.

Over 863 distinct Rett-syndrome-causing MECP2 mutations have been catalogued in scientific literature. The vast majority (over 99%) of cases arise from spontaneous (de novo) mutations rather than inherited ones, and studies have shown that these spontaneous mutations are predominantly of paternal origin — meaning the mutation occurs on the X chromosome inherited from the father.

Why Primarily Females?

Because MECP2 is located on the X chromosome, the condition follows an X-linked dominant inheritance pattern. Females have two X chromosomes, so even when one carries a MECP2 mutation, the other can partially compensate through X-inactivation (the random silencing of one X chromosome in each cell). Males have only one X chromosome; a severe MECP2 mutation typically causes fatal encephalopathy in male infants, which is why classic Rett syndrome occurs almost exclusively in females. Rare cases in males involve mosaic mutations, hypomorphic (milder) mutations, or Klinefelter syndrome (XXY karyotype).

Related MECP2 Disorders

MECP2 mutations exist on a spectrum. While classic Rett syndrome results from loss-of-function mutations, MECP2 duplication syndrome occurs when there is an extra copy of the normal gene, primarily affecting males. This demonstrates that both too little and too much MeCP2 protein are harmful to neurological development. Approximately 75% of atypical Rett syndrome cases are caused by MECP2 mutations, with the remainder linked to mutations in CDKL5 or FOXG1 genes.

The Four Clinical Stages

Classic Rett syndrome is characterized by a progression through four recognized clinical stages, though the timing and severity can vary significantly between individuals depending on the specific MECP2 mutation and other genetic and environmental factors.

A Note on Stage III

Stage III is often called the "plateau" stage and is particularly significant for AAC intervention. During this period, regression stops and many individuals show improved alertness, attention, and social engagement. Eye contact often improves markedly. This is frequently when individuals begin to make the most progress with eye gaze AAC systems, though introduction of AAC should begin as early as possible regardless of stage.

3 Prevalence

Rett syndrome is classified as a rare disease, but it is one of the most common genetic causes of severe intellectual and physical disability in females. Understanding its prevalence is essential for awareness, resource allocation, healthcare planning, and research funding.

A 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis published in Systematic Reviews pooled data from 10 eligible studies covering 9.57 million women and 673 confirmed Rett syndrome cases. The resulting pooled prevalence estimate was 7.1 per 100,000 females (95% CI: 4.8–10.5). All included studies were compatible with a prevalence range of approximately 5 to 10 cases per 100,000 females based on their respective confidence intervals.

Rett syndrome occurs across all racial and ethnic groups worldwide with no known variation in prevalence by ethnicity. Because the vast majority of cases result from de novo mutations, family history is rarely a predictive factor. The condition occurs in roughly 1 in every 10,000 to 15,000 female births, making it less common than conditions like Down syndrome but substantially more common than many ultra-rare genetic conditions.

Prevalence in Context

While Rett syndrome is considered rare, in practical terms this means that in a city of 500,000 people, there are likely 15 to 25 females living with the condition. Globally, hundreds of thousands of individuals and their families are affected. The condition has received FDA-designated Rare Pediatric Disease status, which has facilitated research funding and the development of targeted therapies.

4 How Rett Syndrome Affects Communication

The impact of Rett syndrome on communication is among the most devastating aspects of the condition. Communication is affected through multiple converging mechanisms, creating a complex profile that requires specialized understanding and intervention approaches.

Speech and Language Regression

During the early developmental period (before regression), some children with Rett syndrome acquire a limited vocabulary of up to approximately 20 words, along with babbling and early social communication behaviors. During Stage II regression, which typically occurs between ages 1 and 4, this acquired speech is progressively lost. Most individuals with Rett syndrome will ultimately not develop or retain functional expressive speech.

This regression of previously acquired communication skills is one of the most distressing aspects of Rett syndrome for families. Parents describe watching their child's words, babbling, and social reciprocity fade away over a period of weeks to months. The regression in speech is not merely a delay — it represents a genuine loss of abilities the child had already demonstrated.

Motor Apraxia: The Core Communication Barrier

Perhaps the single most important factor affecting communication in Rett syndrome is severe motor apraxia. Apraxia is a neurological condition in which the brain has difficulty planning and coordinating the complex sequences of muscle movements needed for voluntary actions. In Rett syndrome, apraxia affects virtually every motor system:

  • Speech apraxia (apraxia of speech): The brain cannot reliably plan and execute the precise sequences of tongue, lip, jaw, and vocal cord movements needed to produce speech sounds, even when the muscles themselves are not paralyzed
  • Limb apraxia: Purposeful hand and arm movements become extremely difficult or impossible, replaced by involuntary stereotypic movements such as hand-wringing
  • Gait apraxia: Walking and coordinated lower body movements become impaired
  • Oral apraxia: Difficulties with non-speech mouth movements (chewing, swallowing) also occur
The Apraxia-Cognition Gap

The severity of motor apraxia in Rett syndrome creates what researchers have called a "cognitive-communication gap" — the profound difference between what an individual understands and what they can express through motor output. Because traditional cognitive assessments rely on verbal responses or manual tasks (pointing, manipulating objects), the apraxia has led to decades of systematic underestimation of cognitive abilities in Rett syndrome. This is not a problem of the individual's capacity but of our assessment tools and assumptions.

Loss of Hand Function

The loss of purposeful hand use is a hallmark feature of Rett syndrome. Repetitive stereotypic hand movements — including hand-wringing, hand-washing motions, clapping, tapping, and mouthing — replace the purposeful grasping, reaching, and manipulating that the child may have previously demonstrated. This loss has enormous implications for communication, as it eliminates or severely limits the ability to:

  • Point to objects, pictures, or communication boards
  • Use manual sign language
  • Operate touchscreen devices (tablets, smartphones)
  • Use traditional AAC devices that require hand activation
  • Write, draw, or type
  • Make conventional gestures (waving, thumbs up, etc.)

What Communication Abilities Remain

Despite the severe motor limitations, individuals with Rett syndrome retain and often strengthen several important communication modalities:

👁

Eye Gaze

Eye gaze is often the strongest and most reliable motor skill preserved in Rett syndrome. Many individuals develop highly intentional and communicative eye gaze patterns, making this the primary avenue for AAC access.

😊

Facial Expressions

Emotional expression through facial movements remains intact for many individuals. Smiles, frowns, and expressions of surprise, interest, or distress can be powerful communicative signals when partners learn to recognize and respond to them.

🗣

Vocalizations

While not constituting words, many individuals produce a range of vocalizations — humming, laughing, crying, screaming — that carry communicative intent. These prelinguistic vocalizations can express preference, protest, pleasure, or discomfort.

🧠

Body Language

Muscle tone changes, postural shifts, reaching or leaning toward desired objects, and changes in movement patterns can all serve communicative functions when communication partners are attentive and responsive.

5 Communication Challenges

The communication challenges faced by individuals with Rett syndrome are multilayered and interconnected. Understanding these challenges in detail is essential for selecting appropriate AAC systems and developing effective intervention strategies.

Primary Challenges

1. Loss of Previously Acquired Speech

Unlike conditions where speech never develops, Rett syndrome involves the regression and loss of speech skills that had already begun to emerge. This "loss" trajectory has unique psychological and practical implications. Families grieve the communication that existed, and professionals must account for the fact that receptive language development may have continued internally even as expressive output was lost. The individual may understand far more language than they can produce.

2. Severe Apraxia Affecting All Motor Systems

The whole-body apraxia in Rett syndrome means that virtually every conventional communication method — speech, gesture, signing, typing, pointing — is compromised. Unlike conditions where a single motor system is affected, Rett syndrome disrupts the neural motor planning pathways globally. This necessitates finding a motor access point that is least affected, which for most individuals with Rett syndrome is eye gaze.

3. The Cognitive-Communication Gap

Research has repeatedly demonstrated that individuals with Rett syndrome understand and process more information than their motor limitations allow them to express. A seminal 2012 study from the Albert Einstein College of Medicine used eye-tracking technology to show that children with Rett syndrome exhibited meaningful visual search patterns and could process and prioritize visual information — abilities that were previously unknown because traditional assessments could not measure them.

4. Partner-Dependent Communication

Because most communication from individuals with Rett syndrome is nonverbal and idiosyncratic (unique to the individual), successful communication is highly dependent on having partners who know the individual well, can read subtle cues, and are committed to responding to all communicative attempts. This creates a significant challenge in medical settings, educational environments, and any situation involving unfamiliar communication partners.

5. Assessment Difficulties

Standard speech-language and cognitive assessments were designed for populations with intact motor function. When applied to individuals with Rett syndrome, they systematically produce artificially low results. As one research team noted, "clinicians make mistakes about intellectual impairments" because the assessment tools themselves are biased against individuals with severe motor disorders. Eye-tracking-based assessments are beginning to address this gap but are not yet widely available.

Secondary and Associated Challenges

  • Seizures: Present in up to 80% of individuals, seizures can disrupt attention, alertness, and the ability to engage with AAC systems. Seizure medications may also affect cognitive alertness.
  • Breathing irregularities: Hyperventilation and breath-holding episodes can interfere with sustained attention needed for eye gaze AAC use.
  • Scoliosis and positioning: Progressive scoliosis affects seating and positioning, which directly impacts the ability to maintain consistent eye contact with eye gaze devices.
  • Fatigue: Many individuals experience significant physical and cognitive fatigue, particularly with activities that require sustained concentration like using eye gaze technology.
  • Anxiety and sensory processing: Sensory sensitivities and anxiety can affect an individual's ability to engage with communication systems, particularly in unfamiliar or overwhelming environments.

6 Eye Gaze Technology

Eye gaze technology has emerged as the most important and transformative AAC access method for individuals with Rett syndrome. Because eye movements are often the least affected motor skill in Rett syndrome, eye-tracking systems provide a crucial pathway for communication, learning, and self-expression that would otherwise be impossible.

How Eye Gaze AAC Works

An eye gaze AAC system consists of two core components: an eye-tracking device (hardware) and communication software. The eye-tracking device uses cameras and infrared light to detect and follow the precise position of the user's eyes on the screen. When the user looks at a specific area of the screen for a set duration (called "dwell time"), the system registers that as a selection, similar to clicking a mouse.

The communication software provides a visual interface — typically a grid of symbols, pictures, words, or phrases — that the user navigates with their eyes. When a selection is made, the device speaks the chosen word or phrase aloud through a built-in or connected speaker, giving the individual a voice.

Key Eye Gaze Systems Used in Rett Syndrome

🖥

Tobii Dynavox Devices

The most widely used eye gaze AAC systems in the Rett syndrome community. Devices like the Tobii Dynavox I-Series incorporate built-in Tobii eye-tracking technology with AAC software. Research at the Rett Syndrome Center at Montefiore Medical Center has demonstrated significant success with these devices.

💻

Grid Software Platforms

Software such as Grid 3 (by Smartbox) and Communicator 5 (by Tobii Dynavox) provide customizable communication interfaces optimized for eye gaze access, with dynamic displays that allow navigation through vocabulary hierarchies using only eye movements.

📄

Low-Tech Eye Gaze Frames

Simple eye gaze frames (ETRAN boards) — clear acrylic frames with symbols placed around the edges — provide a no-tech option for eye gaze communication. A partner holds the frame between themselves and the individual, observing which symbol the individual looks at.

🔄

Partner-Assisted Scanning

The communication partner presents options verbally or visually, and the individual uses eye gaze, facial expression, or vocalization to indicate "yes" or "no" for each option. This method requires no technology and can be used anywhere.

Considerations for Eye Gaze in Rett Syndrome

While eye gaze technology is enormously beneficial, there are specific factors that affect its use in Rett syndrome:

  • Calibration challenges: The apraxia in Rett syndrome can make the initial calibration process difficult, as the individual may have trouble fixating consistently on calibration targets. Experienced clinicians use specialized calibration strategies.
  • Dwell time adjustment: The dwell time (how long a user must look at a target to select it) often needs to be carefully adjusted. Too short leads to accidental selections; too long leads to fatigue.
  • Positioning and mounting: Optimal positioning of both the individual and the device is critical. Scoliosis, head control difficulties, and seating needs must all be addressed. Mounting systems that can be attached to wheelchairs, tables, or floor stands are essential.
  • Fatigue management: Eye gaze use can be physically and cognitively fatiguing. Sessions may need to be time-limited, with breaks, especially when the individual is first learning.
  • Strabismus: Some individuals with Rett syndrome have intermittent strabismus (eye misalignment), which can temporarily affect eye-tracking accuracy. Modern eye-tracking systems are increasingly able to accommodate this.
  • Vocabulary organization: The layout and organization of communication vocabulary on the screen must be thoughtfully designed, starting simple and expanding as the individual demonstrates competence. Core vocabulary approaches are recommended.
Research Outcome

A study of families in the Netherlands found that the majority reported progress in their child's skills with longer-term use of eye gaze technology. While challenges exist around setup, training, and ongoing support, families consistently reported that eye gaze AAC opened new doors for communication and connection with their children. One family described how their daughter, using a Tobii Dynavox device, was able to communicate food preferences, choose TV shows, and converse with her therapist for the first time at age 14.

7 How AAC Helps

AAC is not a last resort for individuals with Rett syndrome — it is a primary and essential mode of communication. Because natural speech is not a reliable option for the vast majority of individuals with Rett syndrome, AAC provides the pathway to functional communication, self-determination, social participation, and quality of life.

Domains of Impact

Expressive Communication

The most direct benefit of AAC is giving individuals with Rett syndrome a means to express their thoughts, needs, preferences, and feelings. Research has shown that aided language modeling combined with responsive partner strategies and eye-gaze-controlled devices can significantly increase the rate of expressive communication in both children and adults with Rett syndrome. Three experimental single-case studies published in 2021 demonstrated that adults with Rett syndrome increased their communicative output when provided with eye gaze AAC and trained communication partners using aided language modeling.

Choice-Making and Autonomy

AAC systems allow individuals to make choices about their daily lives — what to eat, what to watch, who to interact with, what activities they prefer. This autonomy, which most people take for granted, is profoundly important for quality of life and psychological well-being. Without AAC, many choices are made for the individual by caregivers based on assumptions or familiar routines.

Social Participation

With access to an AAC system, individuals with Rett syndrome can participate more actively in social interactions with family, peers, therapists, and educators. Eye gaze AAC allows them to comment on activities, ask questions, tell jokes, and express emotions in ways that go beyond the yes/no responses of partner-assisted scanning.

Educational Access

AAC opens doors to educational participation. With eye gaze technology, individuals can answer questions, complete learning activities, demonstrate knowledge, and engage with curriculum content. This is particularly important given the emerging evidence that cognitive abilities in Rett syndrome have been systematically underestimated.

Medical Communication

The ability to communicate about pain, symptoms, and medical concerns is potentially life-saving. A 2023 study highlighted the challenges of medical communication in Rett syndrome, noting that healthcare providers often direct questions to caregivers rather than to the individual with Rett syndrome — even when an eye gaze AAC device is available. The study's title captured this frustration: "Why don't they talk to our daughter?"

AAC Intervention Best Practices for Rett Syndrome

Start Early, Don't Wait

AAC should be introduced as early as possible, even before regression is complete. Early exposure to AAC models provides language input and builds familiarity with communication systems. There is no prerequisite skill level for beginning AAC.

Use Aided Language Modeling (ALM)

Communication partners should model AAC use by pointing to symbols while speaking. This technique — showing the individual how to use the system by using it yourself during natural interactions — has strong evidence support for Rett syndrome specifically.

Employ Responsive Partner Strategies

Partners should provide ample wait time for responses, actively look for all potential communicative actions (eye gaze, facial expression, vocalization, body movement), and respond to any communicative attempt as if it were intentional. The RAACS (Responsive Augmentative and Alternative Communication Style) scale identifies 11 key responsive behaviors.

Provide Multimodal Options

Offer both high-tech (eye gaze device) and low-tech (eye gaze frames, communication boards, partner-assisted scanning) options. No single system works in all situations — the individual may need different modalities for different contexts and energy levels.

Focus on Core Vocabulary

Prioritize high-frequency core words (want, more, stop, go, help, like, not, that) over extensive lists of nouns. Core vocabulary allows for more flexible and generative communication across contexts.

Build a Team

Effective AAC intervention for Rett syndrome requires collaboration among speech-language pathologists, occupational therapists, physical therapists, educators, assistive technology specialists, and family members. Each team member brings essential expertise.

Caregiver Coaching via Telehealth

A 2023 study published in PMC demonstrated that caregiver coaching delivered via telehealth was effective in teaching requesting skills to individuals with Rett syndrome using AAC. This finding is particularly important because many families live in areas with limited access to Rett-specialized AAC professionals. Telehealth coaching can extend the reach of expert support to underserved communities.

8 Presuming Competence

"Children with Rett syndrome who cannot speak or use their hands to communicate were thought to be unable to understand and process information. But our research shows they do exhibit meaningful visual search and can process and prioritize information."

— Researchers, Albert Einstein College of Medicine / Montefiore Medical Center, 2012

The concept of presuming competence is arguably the most important principle in Rett syndrome communication intervention. It means assuming that the individual understands more than they can express, that they have thoughts and preferences worth hearing, and that the failure to communicate is a failure of access and tools rather than a failure of intellect.

Decades of Underestimation

For decades after Rett syndrome was first described, the prevailing clinical view held that individuals with the condition had severe to profound intellectual disability. This conclusion was drawn from standardized IQ tests and developmental assessments that required verbal and motor responses — the very abilities that Rett syndrome impairs. Researchers rarely acknowledged the inherent bias of assessing individuals with severe motor impairments using tools founded on assumptions of verbal and motor competency.

As one team of researchers put it: "Clinicians make mistakes about intellectual impairments — as new Rett syndrome findings show." Initial clinical descriptions in the 1980s and 1990s used standardized IQ tests that indicated severe to profound intellectual impairment, but the bias in these assessments was not recognized or accounted for.

Eye-Tracking Reveals Hidden Abilities

The breakthrough came when researchers began using eye-tracking technology to assess cognition in Rett syndrome without requiring verbal or manual responses. Using eye-gaze technology that tracks eye movements with cameras and reflected infrared light, researchers found that while individuals with Rett syndrome cannot verbally communicate, many are able to understand questions and communicate through their eyes.

Key findings from eye-tracking research include:

  • Individuals with Rett syndrome demonstrate meaningful visual search patterns, indicating active information processing
  • They can distinguish between familiar and novel stimuli, showing recognition memory
  • Many can follow visual narratives and show preferential looking toward relevant story elements
  • Some demonstrate understanding of cause-and-effect relationships through their gaze patterns
  • Language comprehension, as measured by looking at named objects, often exceeds what was predicted by traditional assessments

Why Presuming Competence Matters

The practical implications of presuming competence are enormous:

  • Language exposure: When caregivers presume a child is severely intellectually disabled, they may unconsciously reduce the complexity and quantity of language they use around the child. This creates a self-fulfilling prophecy where the child receives impoverished language input.
  • AAC access: If professionals believe an individual "isn't ready" for AAC or "doesn't understand," they may delay or deny access to communication systems. Presuming competence means providing AAC access early and robustly.
  • Educational opportunities: Presumed incompetence leads to exclusion from meaningful educational experiences. Presumed competence opens doors to age-appropriate content and learning.
  • Social dignity: Speaking to an individual as if they understand — because they very well may — is a matter of basic human dignity and respect.
  • Self-fulfilling prophecy: When we presume competence and provide rich communication environments, individuals are more likely to develop their communication abilities to their fullest potential.
The Danger of Presuming Incompetence

Consider this: an individual with Rett syndrome may spend years — or their entire life — understanding the conversations happening around them, having opinions and preferences, experiencing full emotional lives, yet being unable to express any of it because they were never given a communication system. The cost of presuming incompetence is incalculable. The cost of presuming competence and being wrong is zero — because providing a rich communication environment never harms anyone.

International Communication Guidelines

The international Rett Syndrome Communication Guidelines, developed by a team of experts and endorsed by major Rett syndrome organizations, explicitly emphasize the need to presume competence. The guidelines highlight the need for increased awareness of the potential range of cognitive ability in Rett syndrome and the need to approach assessment in fundamentally different ways that do not rely on motor output. Key recommendations include:

  • Provide access to AAC for all individuals with Rett syndrome, regardless of perceived cognitive level
  • Use eye-gaze-based assessment methods rather than traditional motor-dependent tools
  • Speak to individuals with Rett syndrome using age-appropriate language
  • Include individuals in conversations and decisions about their own lives
  • Train all communication partners in responsive strategies
  • Never use a lack of response as evidence of a lack of understanding

9 Evidence & Research

The research base supporting AAC use in Rett syndrome has grown significantly over the past two decades. While much of the evidence comes from single-case studies and small-group designs (appropriate given the rarity of the condition), the findings are remarkably consistent in demonstrating that individuals with Rett syndrome can learn to use AAC effectively and that their communication abilities exceed previous assumptions.

Key Research Findings

Eye-Tracking and Cognition (2012–present)

Groundbreaking research at the Albert Einstein College of Medicine used eye-tracking to demonstrate that children with Rett syndrome exhibit meaningful visual search, recognition memory, and information processing abilities. Published in 2013, a study by Rose and colleagues in Developmental Medicine & Child Neurology provided some of the first objective evidence of attention and recognition memory capabilities in Rett syndrome, challenging decades of assumptions about cognitive impairment.

Eye Gaze AAC Outcomes (2016–present)

A 2016 study examining families' experiences with eye gaze technology in the Netherlands found that the majority of families reported progress in their child's communication skills with sustained use. The study emphasized that success required adequate professional support, training, and ongoing troubleshooting. A subsequent 2018 study on perceptions of impact found that families and professionals consistently reported positive outcomes in engagement, communication, and quality of life.

Aided Language Modeling Effectiveness (2021)

Three experimental single-case studies published in 2021 in the journal Disability and Rehabilitation: Assistive Technology demonstrated that aided language modeling combined with responsive communication strategies and eye-gaze technology increased the rate of expressive communication in adults with Rett syndrome. This study was significant because it included adult participants, showing that communication intervention can be effective at any age.

Communication Partner Training (2022)

A 2022 case report published in the Journal of Communication Disorders examined a trained communication partner's use of responsive strategies in aided communication with three adults with Rett syndrome. The study found that training partners in specific responsive strategies (attentiveness, confirmation, expansion) led to increased communicative interactions and more successful exchanges.

Telehealth Coaching (2023)

A 2023 study published in PMC demonstrated the effectiveness of caregiver coaching via telehealth for teaching requesting skills to individuals with Rett syndrome using AAC. This finding has important implications for expanding access to specialized AAC intervention in geographically underserved areas.

Comprehensive AAC Review (2024–2025)

A May 2024 case report and narrative review published in BioMed found that AAC technologies — including eye-tracking devices, speech-generating devices, and adapted computers — enhance communication, cognitive development, and quality of life for individuals with Rett syndrome. A 2025 narrative review in PMC further synthesized the evidence on communication abilities, assessment procedures, and intervention approaches, providing the most comprehensive overview to date.

Research Gaps and Future Directions

  • Larger sample sizes: Due to the rarity of Rett syndrome, most studies involve small numbers of participants. Multi-center and international collaborations are needed.
  • Longitudinal studies: Long-term studies tracking AAC use and communication development over years are needed to understand trajectories and optimal intervention timing.
  • Standardized assessment tools: Eye-gaze-based cognitive and communication assessment tools specifically designed for Rett syndrome need further development and validation.
  • Medical communication: More research is needed on how to improve communication in medical settings, where individuals with Rett syndrome are often excluded from their own healthcare discussions.
  • Technology innovation: Advances in eye-tracking accuracy, AI-powered prediction, and more intuitive software interfaces could further improve AAC access.

10 Emerging Treatments

While AAC remains the primary means of communication for individuals with Rett syndrome, the treatment landscape has been undergoing a remarkable transformation. The approval of the first disease-modifying drug and the advancement of gene therapy trials offer hope for the future, though communication support remains essential regardless of medical advances.

Trofinetide (Daybue) — First FDA-Approved Treatment

In March 2023, the FDA approved trofinetide (brand name Daybue) as the first-ever medication specifically approved for Rett syndrome treatment in patients aged 2 years and older. Trofinetide is a synthetic analog of glycine-proline-glutamate (GPE), a naturally occurring growth factor fragment. It works through multiple mechanisms:

  • Reducing neuroinflammation in the brain
  • Preventing certain types of glial cells from becoming overactive
  • Increasing levels of insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1)
  • Supporting neuronal function and synaptic connectivity

Clinical trials demonstrated improvements across multiple domains, and case reports from 2024 described improvements beyond what was captured in clinical efficacy assessments. However, trofinetide manages symptoms rather than curing the underlying genetic condition — meaning AAC support remains critically important.

Gene Therapy Approaches

Because Rett syndrome is caused by a single gene (MECP2), it is a strong candidate for gene therapy. Several approaches are in development:

  • Neurogene's NGN-401: An AAV-based gene therapy delivering a functional copy of MECP2. A Phase 1/2 trial began dosing pediatric patients in late 2023. The high-dose arm was discontinued in November 2024 following a patient death from hyperinflammatory syndrome, but positive interim data from the low-dose cohort were reported in November 2025.
  • Taysha Gene Therapies' TSHA-102: A gene therapy designed to regulate MeCP2 expression to avoid the toxicity of overexpression, addressing a key safety challenge in MECP2 gene therapy (since both too little and too much MeCP2 protein are harmful).
  • ProQR's AX-2402: An RNA editing approach targeting specific MECP2 mutations (beginning with R270X), which corrects the genetic message at the RNA level rather than replacing the gene entirely.
AAC Remains Essential

Even as medical treatments advance, AAC will remain essential for individuals with Rett syndrome for the foreseeable future. Trofinetide improves symptoms but does not restore speech. Gene therapies are still in early clinical trials with uncertain outcomes. And for the hundreds of thousands of individuals currently living with Rett syndrome, effective AAC support today cannot wait for treatments that may be years away. Communication is a fundamental human right, and AAC provides that access now.

11 Key Considerations

For families, clinicians, educators, and anyone working with or caring for individuals with Rett syndrome, the following considerations are essential for successful communication support.

For Families

  • Your child understands more than you may think. Research consistently shows that cognitive abilities in Rett syndrome have been underestimated. Talk to your child, read to them, include them in conversations, and narrate your daily activities as you would with any child.
  • AAC is not giving up on speech. Introducing AAC does not mean abandoning hope for verbal communication. Research shows that AAC use actually supports language development and does not inhibit any speech that might emerge. AAC gives your child a voice while any other communication modality is developing.
  • Seek specialized support. Not all speech-language pathologists have experience with Rett syndrome or eye gaze AAC. Look for professionals who specialize in complex communication needs and have experience with eye gaze technology. Rett syndrome family organizations can help connect you with specialists.
  • Be patient and persistent. Learning to use eye gaze AAC takes time. Progress may be slow at first and may not follow a linear trajectory. Celebrate small successes and maintain consistent exposure and modeling.
  • Connect with other families. The Rett syndrome community is active and supportive. Organizations like the International Rett Syndrome Foundation, Rett Syndrome Research Trust, and regional support groups can provide invaluable peer support, information, and advocacy resources.

For Clinicians

  • Presume competence. Always approach assessment and intervention with the assumption that the individual understands more than they can express. Use assessment methods that minimize reliance on motor output.
  • Address positioning first. Effective eye gaze AAC use depends on optimal positioning. Collaborate with physical and occupational therapists to ensure stable seating, appropriate head support, and correct device placement before working on communication goals.
  • Train all communication partners. The individual's communication success depends as much on the skills of their partners as on their own abilities. Provide explicit training in aided language modeling, responsive strategies, and recognition of communicative behaviors.
  • Provide multimodal access. No single AAC system works in every situation. Equip individuals and families with high-tech, low-tech, and no-tech options for different contexts and energy levels.
  • Consider the whole person. Communication intervention should account for seizures, fatigue, medication effects, sensory needs, and emotional well-being. A holistic approach yields the best outcomes.

For Educators

  • Include, don't exclude. Students with Rett syndrome can and should participate in educational activities when given appropriate AAC access and support. Adapt curriculum materials for eye gaze access rather than simplifying or removing content.
  • Allow adequate response time. Eye gaze communication takes longer than verbal responses. Build in wait time, reduce time pressure, and let the student know you are waiting for their answer.
  • Model AAC use throughout the day. The more the student sees AAC being used naturally by adults and peers, the more they understand its communicative power.
  • Collaborate with the AAC team. Regular communication with the student's SLP, AT specialist, and family ensures consistency in vocabulary, strategies, and expectations.

For Medical Professionals

  • Talk to the patient, not just the caregiver. Direct your questions and explanations to the individual with Rett syndrome, using their AAC system when available. Ask the caregiver for interpretation support if needed, but treat the individual as the primary participant in their own healthcare.
  • Allow time for AAC use in appointments. Medical appointments are often rushed, but meaningful communication through AAC takes time. Schedule longer appointments when possible, and allow the individual to respond using their communication system.
  • Learn to recognize communicative behaviors. Eye gaze, facial expressions, and vocalizations may convey pain, discomfort, or preference. Ask caregivers to help you understand the individual's communication patterns.
Remember

Every individual with Rett syndrome is unique. Communication profiles, abilities, and needs vary widely based on the specific MECP2 mutation, the degree of X-inactivation, environmental factors, and intervention history. There is no one-size-fits-all approach. The best outcomes come from person-centered planning that respects the individual's strengths, preferences, and potential.
