1. Receptive Communication Struggles

Understanding and processing incoming communication -- spoken, written, signed, or nonverbal

5 sub-domains ▶
1.1 Auditory Comprehension Disorders ▶
1.1.1 Acoustic/Phonemic Processing
Inability to discriminate between similar speech sounds (phonemes) or process the acoustic envelope of speech. Ranges from mild difficulty in noise to complete inability to discriminate speech sounds.
AphasiaTBIDown Syndrome
1.1.2 Word-Level Comprehension
Difficulty understanding individual spoken words despite adequate hearing. May include loss of word meaning. In semantic dementia, word meaning degrades progressively, starting with uncommon words.
AphasiaDementiaDown Syndrome
1.1.3 Sentence-Level Comprehension
Difficulty understanding syntactically complex sentences even when individual words are understood. Particularly affected: passive constructions, relative clauses, embedded clauses, multi-step instructions.
AphasiaDementiaTBIDown Syndrome
1.1.4 Discourse-Level Comprehension
Inability to follow extended spoken passages, conversations, or multi-turn discussions. Difficulty extracting main ideas, making cross-sentence inferences, and maintaining comprehension over time.
TBIDementiaMSAphasia
1.2 Linguistic Comprehension Disorders ▶
1.2.1 Semantic Comprehension Deficits
Loss or impairment of word meaning, category relationships, and conceptual knowledge. In semantic dementia, low-frequency words are lost first, with everyday words preserved longest.
DementiaAphasiaDown Syndrome
1.2.2 Syntactic Comprehension Deficits
Difficulty understanding sentence structure, word order, and grammatical relationships. AAC output and communication partner language should use simple, active-voice structures.
AphasiaDementiaDown Syndrome
1.2.3 Morphological Comprehension Deficits
Difficulty interpreting grammatical morphemes -- tense markers, plurals, possessives, comparatives. Morphological difficulty is the single most reliable clinical marker of Developmental Language Disorder.
AphasiaDown Syndrome
1.3 Social/Pragmatic Reception Disorders ▶
1.3.1 Prosodic Interpretation (Tone of Voice)
Inability to interpret emotional content, sarcasm, questioning, or emphasis from vocal prosody. Called "receptive aprosodia." The person hears words correctly but misses the "how."
ASDTBIParkinson
1.3.2 Nonverbal Interpretation
Difficulty reading body language, facial expressions, eye gaze direction, posture, and communicative gestures. People with this deficit may not notice partner's confusion, boredom, or attempts to change topic.
ASDTBIDementia
1.3.3 Contextual/Pragmatic Interpretation
Difficulty understanding conversational implicature, reading social dynamics, and interpreting communicative intent beyond literal meaning. Includes indirect speech acts and shared knowledge assumptions.
ASDTBI
1.3.4 Figurative Language Comprehension
Difficulty understanding metaphor, idiom, sarcasm, irony, humor, simile, and other non-literal language. Meta-analytic evidence confirms significant deficits in ASD; literal interpretations dominate.
ASDTBI
1.4 Reading and Written Comprehension ▶
1.4.1 Acquired Reading Disorders (Alexia)
Loss of previously acquired reading ability. Subtypes include surface alexia, deep alexia, phonological alexia, and pure alexia. Text-based AAC systems may be partially or fully inaccessible.
AphasiaTBIDementia
1.4.2 Developmental Literacy Limitations
Limited reading/writing skills never fully developed due to developmental conditions, limited educational access from motor disabilities, or co-occurring learning disabilities.
CPDown SyndromeAngelmanRettASD
1.4.3 Symbol/Picture Comprehension
Difficulty understanding graphic symbols in AAC systems. Symbols range from transparent (photo) to opaque (abstract). Adding visual supports may actually increase cognitive demands.
DementiaAngelman
1.5 Temporal Processing and Speed ▶
1.5.1 Millisecond-Level Temporal Processing
Deficient processing of rapid acoustic changes in speech, affecting ability to resolve the temporal fine structure of spoken language.
Aphasia
1.5.2 Processing Speed / Delayed Processing
Generalized slowing of cognitive processing. TBI patients 1.54x slower than healthy controls. In MS, processing speed is the most common cognitive deficit and predicts impairment 10 years later.
TBIMSParkinsonDementiaDown Syndrome
1.5.3 Information Overload
Inability to process multiple simultaneous information streams or cope with rapid information delivery. Environmental distractions worsen performance.
TBIMSDementia

2. Expressive Communication Struggles

All forms of producing outgoing communication -- speech, writing, gesture, AAC-generated output

5 sub-domains ▶
2.1 Speech Motor Execution Disorders ▶
2.1.1 Dysarthria (Motor Execution Failure)
Motor speech disorder caused by weakness, spasticity, or incoordination of speech muscles. Seven clinically recognized types based on the Mayo Clinic/Darley-Aronson-Brown classification, each with distinct lesion sites, features, and associated conditions.
ALSCPMSParkinsonTBI
2.1.1a Flaccid Dysarthria
Lesion: Lower motor neurons (cranial nerves, neuromuscular junction, muscles)
Features: Breathy voice, short phrases, hypernasality, imprecise articulation, nasal emission, audible inspiration
Conditions: ALS (LMN component), Myasthenia gravis, Guillain-Barre, brainstem stroke, SMA (bulbar involvement)
2.1.1b Spastic Dysarthria
Lesion: Bilateral upper motor neurons
Features: Strained-strangled voice, monotonicity, slow rate, imprecise consonants, hypernasality, short phrases
Conditions: Cerebral palsy (spastic type), bilateral stroke, ALS (UMN component), primary lateral sclerosis
2.1.1c Ataxic Dysarthria
Lesion: Cerebellum or cerebellar circuits
Features: Irregular articulatory errors, equal and excessive stress ("scanning speech"), inappropriate pitch/loudness variation, imprecise consonants, prolonged phonemes
Conditions: MS (characteristic scanning speech), cerebellar stroke, Friedreich's ataxia
2.1.1d Hypokinetic Dysarthria
Lesion: Basal ganglia (substantia nigra)
Features: Reduced loudness (monoloudness), rapid speech rate (festination), rushes of speech, sound repetitions, monotone pitch, imprecise articulation
Conditions: Parkinson's disease (affects up to 89-90%), progressive supranuclear palsy
Unique feature: The "scaling problem" -- patients internally perceive they are speaking louder/clearer than they actually are
2.1.1e Hyperkinetic Dysarthria
Lesion: Basal ganglia; involuntary movements
Features: Harsh voice, variable loudness/rate, voice stoppages, involuntary voice breaks, strained voice, distorted vowels, irregular articulatory breakdowns
Conditions: Huntington's disease, dystonia, Tourette's, tardive dyskinesia, some cerebral palsy (dyskinetic type)
2.1.1f Unilateral Upper Motor Neuron (UUMN) Dysarthria
Lesion: Unilateral UMN damage
Features: Generally milder; imprecise articulation, harsh voice, slow rate, primarily one-sided
Conditions: Unilateral stroke
2.1.1g Mixed Dysarthria
Lesion: Multiple motor systems
Features: Combination of two or more types; most common mixed pattern is flaccid-spastic
Conditions: ALS (flaccid + spastic -- the hallmark), MS (variable combinations), TBI, Wilson's disease
2.1.2 Apraxia of Speech (Motor Planning Failure)
A motor planning disorder where the brain cannot sequence movements for speech despite adequate muscle strength. Acquired (stroke, TBI) or childhood (CAS -- 1-2 per 1,000 children). Muscles are not weak; the motor plan is disordered.
AphasiaCASASDDown SyndromeRettAngelman
2.1.3 Structural Speech Impairment
Speech impairment from physical removal of or damage to speech structures. Laryngectomy: complete voice loss (98% impact). Glossectomy: 95% impact for total removal. Often sudden and permanent.
Cancer
2.1.4 Respiratory-Phonatory Interaction
Inadequate breath support for speech. Weakened respiratory muscles reduce voice volume, utterance length, and speech endurance. May require ventilator use that interferes with speech.
ALSCPMD/SMAParkinsonMS
2.1.5 Prosodic Impairment
Abnormal rhythm, stress, intonation, and pacing of speech. Parkinson's: monopitch/monoloudness. MS: scanning speech. Listeners perceive flat prosody as disinterest even when the speaker is engaged.
ParkinsonMSCASASDCP
2.1.6 Fluency Disorders
Stuttering (repetitions, prolongations, blocks) and cluttering (rapid irregular rate). Includes Parkinson's festinating speech (rapid bursts) and stuttering-like disfluencies in Down Syndrome.
Down SyndromeParkinsonTBI
2.2 Language Formulation Disorders ▶
2.2.1 Word Retrieval / Anomia
Inability to access and produce known words. Universal across all aphasia types. Compensatory patterns include circumlocutions, semantic and phonemic paraphasias. 50-80% of dementia patients experience word-finding difficulty.
AphasiaDementiaTBIMSParkinson
2.2.2 Sentence Construction Disorders
Agrammatism (telegraphic speech, omitting function words) in Broca's aphasia. Paragrammatism (grammatical distortion, "confused sentence monsters") in Wernicke's aphasia. Simplified syntax in dementia and Down Syndrome.
AphasiaDementiaDown SyndromeASD
2.2.3 Vocabulary/Semantic Access
Reduced active vocabulary even when passive vocabulary may be intact. ASD: may have extensive vocabulary but inability to use it spontaneously. Down Syndrome: ~300-400 words at age 6 vs. several thousand typical.
AphasiaDementiaASDDown Syndrome
2.2.4 Discourse Organization
Difficulty organizing extended speech into coherent narrative. TBI: rambling, disorganized, tangential speech -- the hallmark cognitive-communication pattern. ASD: difficulty with narrative construction.
TBIASDDementia
2.3 Written/Typed Expression Disorders ▶
2.3.1 Agraphia (Acquired Writing Impairment)
Cognitive agraphia (language-based), motor agraphia (fine motor), and visuospatial agraphia. Writing almost always affected in aphasia, often more severely than speech.
AphasiaTBI
2.3.2 Micrographia
Handwriting becomes progressively smaller and illegible, eliminating writing as a communication fallback. Affects 63% of Parkinson's patients.
Parkinson
2.3.3 Motor Writing Barriers
Physical inability to write or type due to weakness, paralysis, tremor, or incoordination. Complete paralysis in Locked-in syndrome; loss of hand function in Rett syndrome.
ALSCPMD/SMALocked-inRett
2.4 Nonverbal Expression Disorders ▶
2.4.1 Gesture Production Impairment
Limb apraxia: impaired ability to perform skilled gestures. Often co-occurs with aphasia. Rett: loss of purposeful hand use replaced by stereotypic movements. Parkinson's: gestures become smaller and less frequent.
AphasiaASDRettParkinsonALSCPAngelman
2.4.2 Facial Expression Impairment
Facial masking (hypomimia): up to 70% of Parkinson's patients. Partners misinterpret facial masking as disinterest, depression, or unfriendliness. ASD: atypical expressions that don't match neurotypical expectations.
ParkinsonALSLocked-inASDCP
2.4.3 Reduced Body Language
Diminished use of posture, head movements, and body positioning for communicative intent. Parkinson's stooped posture conveys disinterest; progressive motor loss in ALS/MD/SMA.
ParkinsonCPALSMD/SMALocked-in
2.5 Emotional and Identity Expression Barriers ▶
2.5.1 Emotional Conveyance Through AAC
Difficulty conveying emotion through AAC output. Synthetic speech historically lacked prosodic variation. Emerging: "Expressive Tones" features (anger, excitement, sadness, sarcasm, whispering).
ALSCPAphasiaParkinsonLocked-inASD
2.5.2 Humor, Sarcasm, and Timing
Humor requires precise timing, conflicting with 8-15 WPM AAC output. A joke delivered 30 seconds late loses its effect. Users often prefer full-auto AI for humor because timely participation matters more than perfect wording.
ALSCPAphasiaLocked-inASD
2.5.3 Personal Identity and Voice
Voice is a core component of personal identity. Using a synthetic voice that doesn't match one's age, gender, or cultural background is alienating. Voice banking and personal voice synthesis are critical for acceptance.
ALSCancerAphasiaParkinson
2.5.4 Pseudobulbar Affect
Involuntary laughing or crying unrelated to actual emotional state. Creates profound misunderstanding in social contexts.
ALSMSTBIAphasia

3. Interactive/Pragmatic Struggles

Social use of language -- how communication is used in context rather than its linguistic form

4 sub-domains ▶
3.1 Conversational Management ▶
3.1.1 Turn-Taking
Difficulty alternating speaker/listener roles and timing contributions. AAC users communicate at 8-15 WPM vs. 150+ WPM for speech, making natural turn-taking extremely difficult.
ASDTBIDementia
3.1.2 Topic Initiation, Maintenance, and Shifting
Difficulty starting conversations, staying on topic, and transitioning smoothly. ASD: abrupt shifts to preferred topics. TBI: tangential speech. Dementia: frequent tangential shifts and repetition.
ASDTBIDementia
3.1.3 Conversational Repair
Difficulty identifying and resolving communication breakdowns -- detecting when misunderstood, requesting clarification, rephrasing. Partners often take ownership of repair, reinforcing passivity.
ASDTBICASCP
3.2 Social Cognition ▶
3.2.1 Theory of Mind / Perspective-Taking
Difficulty understanding others' beliefs, desires, intentions, and knowledge states. ASD core feature -- though the "double empathy problem" reframes this as reciprocal, not solely an autistic deficit.
ASDTBIDementia
3.2.2 Emotion Recognition
Difficulty recognizing and interpreting emotions in others through facial expressions, vocal tone, and body language.
TBIASDDementia
3.2.3 Social Judgment and Behavior
Inappropriate social behavior, disinhibited comments. TBI social cognition deficits are the "most challenging aspect of long-term recovery." Frontotemporal dementia: loss of conversational filters.
TBIDementiaASD
3.3 Register and Context Adaptation ▶
3.3.1 Formality/Style Shifting
Difficulty adapting language formality to context. ASD: may use overly formal language in casual settings. Pre-programmed AAC messages may not match required formality. Inability to use slang or code-switching limits social belonging.
ASDTBI
3.3.2 Audience Awareness
Difficulty adjusting communication based on the knowledge, age, relationship, and needs of the listener.
ASDTBIDementia
3.4 Discourse and Narrative Skills ▶
3.4.1 Narrative Construction
Difficulty organizing events into coherent stories with appropriate structure, temporal sequencing, and cohesion. AAC narrative interventions target storytelling precursors.
ASDTBI
3.4.2 Presupposition Management
Difficulty estimating shared knowledge between speaker and listener, leading to either over- or under-specification of information.
ASDTBI

4. Physical/Motor Access Barriers

Physical ability to access communication tools, independent of language or cognitive ability

6 sub-domains ▶
4.1 Gross Motor Barriers ▶
Gross Motor Access
Impaired trunk/head control, reaching, pointing, and body positioning. Solutions include adaptive seating, wheelchair-mounted devices, and alternative access sites (chin, head, foot, sip-and-puff).
CPMD/SMAALSLocked-inRett
4.2 Fine Motor Barriers ▶
Fine Motor Access
Inability to perform precise touch, button pressing, or keyboard use. Parkinson's: bradykinesia and tremor cause extra key presses. Solutions include keyguards, enlarged targets, and alternative input methods.
CPParkinsonMSMD/SMAALSAngelman
4.3 Oral-Motor Barriers ▶
Oral-Motor Control
Impaired control of tongue, lips, jaw, and velum. CP: hypertonia pushes tongue to palate. Down Syndrome: hypotonia, small oral cavity, relatively large tongue. CAS: muscles capable but motor plan disordered. Oral motor difficulty in 98% of Down Syndrome.
CPDown SyndromeCASRettAngelmanALSParkinsonCancer
4.4 Respiratory Barriers ▶
Respiratory Support for Speech
Inadequate breath support directly limits voice volume, utterance length, and endurance. Ventilator interaction: speaking valves may not be tolerated, NIV masks interfere, ventilator cycles don't synchronize with speech breathing.
ALSMD/SMACPParkinsonMS
4.5 Visual-Motor Barriers (Eye Gaze) ▶
Eye Gaze Access
Challenges with eye gaze as communication access -- the last-resort method for many with severe physical disabilities. Calibration difficulties, reduced accuracy in CP, involuntary movements, fatigue, and strabismus. Rett: eye gaze is often the strongest preserved motor skill.
CPALSRettLocked-inMD/SMATBI
4.6 Physical Fatigue as Access Barrier ▶
Communication Fatigue (Physical)
Communication effort exceeds available energy. MS: 80% experience fatigue, speech degrades throughout day. MD/SMA: start clearly but become increasingly unintelligible. Multimodal communication essential -- switch between access methods based on energy.
CPMSALSMD/SMA

5. Cognitive-Communication Barriers

Cognitive deficits that disrupt communication, rather than primary language or motor impairment

5 sub-domains ▶
5.1 Attention Deficits ▶
Attention-Based Communication Barriers
Five types (Sohlberg & Mateer): focused, sustained, selective, alternating, and divided attention. Divided attention critical for AAC use (formulating message + navigating device + monitoring conversation). TBI: 60%+ of moderate-to-severe cases up to 10 years post-injury.
TBIMSDementiaParkinsonRett
5.2 Memory Deficits ▶
Working Memory
Critical for AAC: must maintain target concepts while navigating pages, remembering paths, locating symbols, and inhibiting distractors. Failure to account for working memory demands creates unintentional AAC barriers.
TBIAphasiaDementiaMSDown Syndrome
Long-Term Memory
Affects vocabulary knowledge, word-form associations, learned conversational scripts, and AAC device navigation procedures. Drives repetitive questioning and losing thread in dementia.
DementiaTBI
Prospective Memory
Remembering to communicate at the right time ("I need to tell my teacher about..."). Impaired ability to initiate communication at appropriate times.
TBI
5.3 Executive Function Deficits ▶
Executive Function Barriers
Planning, organizing, self-monitoring, inhibition, initiation, and cognitive flexibility. TBI: 48% moderate-severe, 55% chronic severe. Hidden AAC burden: simplifying communication through AAC may require MORE executive function.
TBIDementiaMSASDParkinson
5.4 Processing Speed ▶
Cognitive Processing Speed
Generalized cognitive slowing affecting comprehension, formulation, and response generation. Real-time conversation feels overwhelming; conversation moves on before response is ready; group settings with rapid turn-taking are especially difficult.
TBIMSParkinsonDementiaDown Syndrome
5.5 Learning and Generalization ▶
Learning and Generalization Barriers
Difficulty learning new AAC systems, vocabulary, and navigation patterns and generalizing across contexts. Down Syndrome: needs ~20x more repetitions. Angelman: consistent repetition required. Dementia: declining ability to learn new systems.
AngelmanDown SyndromeDementiaTBI

6. Psychosocial Communication Barriers

Emotional, social, and psychological dimensions -- often underestimated in their impact

6 sub-domains ▶
6.1 Communication Confidence and Anxiety ▶
Confidence-Competence Cycle
Failed communication attempts lead to reduced confidence, fewer attempts, less practice, reduced competence, and further reduced confidence. 33% of caregivers cite lack of training, contributing to partner anxiety.
CASCPAphasiaParkinsonASDALS
6.2 Identity and Self-Expression ▶
Voice and Identity Loss
Voice is a key part of identity. Using a synthetic voice that doesn't match one's age, gender, or cultural background is alienating. Particularly acute for adults who previously had full speech. Voice banking and customization are critical.
ALSCancerParkinsonAphasia
6.3 Stigma and Device Acceptance ▶
Device Stigma
Carrying an AAC device signals "incapability" to some. Fear of being teased. Higher device abandonment when stigma is present. Students may avoid using devices in class. However, peers often show curiosity and acceptance.
ASDCPDown SyndromeAngelmanRett
6.4 Social Participation and Inclusion ▶
Participation Barriers
Lack of AAC access has "devastating effects" -- jeopardizing outcomes in education, employment, healthcare, and community activities. Puts people at risk for adverse medical events, social isolation, and maltreatment.
ASDCPAphasiaALSLocked-inDementiaTBI
6.5 Communication Partner Dependency ▶
Partner Dependency
Reliance on familiar partners who interpret speech, present choices, or dominate conversations. Individuals are at risk for learned helplessness. Adults dominate interactions while AAC users take passive roles.
RettLocked-inCPAngelman
6.6 Psychosocial Impact of Communication Loss ▶
Depression, Isolation, and Role Loss
Aphasia depression: 31-70% (vs. 8% general population). 90%+ report feeling isolated. Head/neck cancer: 20% mild, 13% moderate, 20% severe depression. Vicious cycle: communication difficulty leads to social withdrawal, fewer opportunities, slower recovery, more frustration.
AphasiaParkinsonMSCancerALSTBI

7. Rate and Efficiency Barriers

Inherent limitations of augmented communication that affect all AAC users regardless of condition

4 sub-domains ▶
7.1 Communication Speed Gap ▶
10-20x Rate Gap
AAC users produce 8-15 WPM vs. natural speakers at 120-180 WPM. Best current technology: 30.4 WPM with LLM-based prediction -- still 5-10x slower than speech. Fundamental constraint on all AAC-mediated communication.
ALSCPLocked-inAphasiaASDRettAngelmanMD/SMA
7.2 Conversational Timing ▶
Missed Conversational Opportunities
AAC users regularly miss opportunities to participate in fast-paced conversation. Humor, interjections, and timely responses are particularly affected. Group settings with multiple threads compound the problem.
ALSCPLocked-inAphasiaASDRettAngelmanMD/SMA
7.3 Communication Fatigue ▶
Cognitive and Physical Exhaustion
Both physical and cognitive effort of using AAC leads to fatigue, shorter messages, reduced participation, and withdrawal. Users must contemplate thought, translate from restricted vocabulary, navigate interface, and do it at conversational speed.
ALSCPMSMD/SMALocked-in
7.4 Context Switching and Topic Lag ▶
Out-of-Context Responses
While composing a response, other participants change the topic. Result: AAC users produce out-of-context responses. Interesting finding: aided communicators may initiate more in multi-party settings, having more preparation time when others talk.
ALSCPLocked-inAphasiaASDRettAngelmanMD/SMA

Additional Communication Struggles

Clinically relevant struggle types that fall outside the 15 core conditions mapped in the taxonomy above. Included for completeness.

Selective Mutism

Prevalence: 0.7-2% of children | DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria

Anxiety-based communication disorder where individuals cannot speak in certain social situations despite having normal speech capability in other contexts. Does not fit neatly into motor/language/cognitive framework -- fundamentally anxiety-driven. Represents a situational expressive failure with intact receptive, linguistic, and motor systems.

AAC relevance: Some individuals use AAC as a bridge intervention in anxiety-provoking settings (school, medical appointments). Text-based or typed AAC may be less stigmatizing than symbol-based systems.

Situational Speech Loss in ASD ("Going Nonverbal")

Intermittent | Stress/Overload Triggered

Distinct from permanent minimal verbal status. Some autistic individuals who typically speak fluently lose the ability to produce speech under stress, sensory overload, emotional overwhelm, or in unfamiliar environments. A transient, situation-dependent phenomenon sometimes called "autistic burnout" speech loss.

AAC relevance: Requires AAC systems that can be quickly activated as backup when speech becomes unavailable, then set aside when speech returns. Suggests need for "emergency mode" AAC always accessible on smartphone.

Hearing Loss / Deafness with Comorbid Disabilities

40-50% of deaf/HoH have additional disabilities

When hearing loss co-occurs with intellectual disability, motor impairment, or ASD, traditional deaf communication methods (sign language, lip reading) may be insufficient. Approximately 40-50% of deaf/hard-of-hearing individuals have additional disabilities that may require AAC beyond sign language.

AAC relevance: Requires multimodal systems that don't depend on auditory feedback. Visual and tactile AAC approaches essential.

Multilingual/Bilingual Communication Barriers

Growing Clinical Concern

Many AAC users live in multilingual households or communities but are forced into English-only (or single-language) AAC systems. This limits communication with family members, cultural participation, and identity expression.

AAC relevance: Only a few AAC apps support true bilingual code-switching (Proloquo2Go, TD Snap). Most apps offer limited language options, and virtually none support lesser-resourced languages.

Spasmodic Dysphonia

Neurological Voice Disorder | Rare

Neurological voice disorder causing involuntary spasms of the laryngeal muscles, resulting in voice breaks, strained/strangled quality, or breathy voice. In severe cases, can make speech nearly impossible.

AAC relevance: Rare, but severe cases may require AAC. Primarily treated with botulinum toxin injections rather than AAC.
