AAC Condition Profile

Muscular Dystrophy & Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA)

A comprehensive guide to understanding how progressive neuromuscular conditions affect communication and how Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) provides a lifeline for self-expression as motor function changes over time.

Overview

Muscular Dystrophy (MD) and Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA) are two groups of genetic conditions that cause progressive muscle weakness and wasting. While they arise from different genetic mechanisms and affect different parts of the neuromuscular system, both can profoundly impact an individual's ability to communicate verbally as the conditions progress. Understanding these conditions in the context of Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) is essential for clinicians, families, and individuals living with these diagnoses.

Muscular Dystrophy refers to a group of over 30 genetic diseases characterized by progressive weakness and degeneration of the skeletal muscles that control movement. The most common and severe form, Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD), is caused by mutations in the DMD gene on the X chromosome, which encodes the protein dystrophin. Without functional dystrophin, muscle fibers are fragile and easily damaged, leading to progressive muscle loss. The various forms of MD differ in which muscles are primarily affected, the age of onset, the rate of progression, and the pattern of inheritance.

Spinal Muscular Atrophy is characterized by the progressive degeneration and loss of motor neurons in the spinal cord and brainstem. This results in symmetric muscle weakness that is typically more pronounced in proximal muscles (those closer to the trunk). SMA is most commonly caused by deletions or mutations in the survival motor neuron 1 (SMN1) gene. Unlike muscular dystrophy, where the muscle itself is the primary site of pathology, in SMA it is the nerve cells controlling the muscles that degenerate.

Both conditions share a critical feature relevant to AAC: they are progressive. As motor function declines, the muscles involved in speech production -- including those controlling the tongue, lips, jaw, soft palate, and respiratory system -- can become affected, making verbal communication increasingly difficult or impossible. Yet in most cases, cognitive and intellectual abilities remain fully intact, creating an urgent need for robust AAC solutions that preserve the individual's ability to communicate their thoughts, needs, and desires.

Types of Muscular Dystrophy

There are more than 30 recognized forms of muscular dystrophy, each caused by different genetic mutations and affecting different muscle groups. The following are the most common types, with particular attention to their potential impact on communication.

Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD)

Duchenne is the most common and severe form of muscular dystrophy. It is caused by frame-shift mutations in the DMD gene on the X chromosome, resulting in an almost complete absence of the dystrophin protein. Because it is X-linked recessive, it occurs almost exclusively in males, though female carriers can occasionally show mild symptoms.

  • Onset: Symptoms typically appear between ages 2 and 3
  • Progression: Rapid; most boys lose the ability to walk by age 9-13
  • Communication impact: Dysarthria (disordered speech) prevalence rises from approximately 8% in the early ambulatory stage to 92% in the late non-ambulatory stage
  • Life expectancy: Typically into the mid-20s to early 30s with modern care
  • Prevalence: 1 in 3,500 to 1 in 5,000 male births
Critical Communication Statistic

In DMD, speech intelligibility problems affect approximately 39% of patients in the early ambulatory stage, rising to 92% in the late non-ambulatory stage. This 10-year longitudinal progression underscores the need for early AAC planning.

Becker Muscular Dystrophy (BMD)

Becker muscular dystrophy is caused by in-frame mutations in the same DMD gene, resulting in a partially functional but reduced amount of dystrophin. It is essentially a milder form of Duchenne.

  • Onset: Usually around age 8, though it can range from childhood to early adulthood
  • Progression: Slower than DMD; walking ability often preserved until at least age 15, and many patients remain ambulatory into adulthood
  • Communication impact: Less severe than DMD but can still develop dysarthria in later stages, particularly as respiratory function declines
  • Life expectancy: Often into the 40s or beyond
  • Prevalence: 1 in 16,700 to 1 in 18,500 male births

Myotonic Dystrophy (DM)

Myotonic dystrophy is the most common form of adult-onset muscular dystrophy. It is unique among the muscular dystrophies because in addition to progressive muscle weakness, it also causes myotonia -- the inability to relax muscles after contraction. There are two types: DM1 (Steinert disease) and DM2 (proximal myotonic myopathy).

  • Onset: Typically between ages 20-30 (adult-onset), but a congenital form can present at birth
  • Key feature: Myotonia makes it difficult to relax muscles -- for example, difficulty releasing a grip after a handshake
  • Communication impact: Facial and neck muscles are often affected first, causing speech abnormalities including nasal speech, reduced facial expression, and articulatory difficulties
  • Cognitive considerations: Unlike most other MDs, myotonic dystrophy can also affect cognitive function, particularly in the congenital and childhood-onset forms

Facioscapulohumeral Muscular Dystrophy (FSHD)

FSHD is the third most common type of muscular dystrophy. As the name suggests, it primarily affects the muscles of the face (facio-), shoulder blades (scapulo-), and upper arms (humeral).

  • Onset: Typically before age 20
  • Communication impact: Facial muscle weakness can significantly impact articulation, facial expression, and the ability to produce clear speech. Difficulty closing eyes fully, smiling, and puckering lips are common
  • Prevalence: About 4 in 100,000 people in the US
  • Progression: Highly variable; some individuals are mildly affected while others develop significant disability

Limb-Girdle Muscular Dystrophy (LGMD)

LGMD is actually a group of at least a dozen distinct disorders that primarily affect the muscles of the hips and shoulders (the "limb girdle" areas).

  • Onset: Usually adolescence or early adulthood, though some forms begin in childhood
  • Communication impact: Speech is less commonly affected initially, but respiratory muscle weakness in advanced stages can reduce breath support for speech
  • Prevalence: Approximately 2 in 100,000 people in the US

Types of Spinal Muscular Atrophy

SMA is classified into types based on the age of onset and the highest motor milestone achieved. The severity is strongly correlated with the number of copies of the SMN2 backup gene: fewer copies generally means more severe disease.

Type 0 (Prenatal Onset)

The most severe and rarest form, with onset before birth. Affected infants show severe weakness and respiratory failure at birth. Survival is typically measured in weeks. These children have only one copy of SMN2.

Type I -- Werdnig-Hoffmann Disease

Type I is the most common form, accounting for approximately 58% of new SMA diagnoses. It is also the most severe form compatible with postnatal survival.

  • Onset: Birth to 6 months
  • Motor milestones: Never able to sit independently
  • SMN2 copies: Usually 1-2
  • Communication impact: Severe oral motor weakness and flaccid dysarthria, often progressing to complete anarthria (inability to produce speech). Children with SMA1 demonstrate near-total reliance on alternative communication methods
  • Prognosis without treatment: Median survival of 7 months; 95% mortality by 18 months
  • Key note: With modern treatments (Zolgensma, Spinraza), outcomes are dramatically improving
Preserved Intelligence in SMA Type I

Research consistently shows that cognitive function and morphosyntactic comprehension are well preserved in children with SMA1. In fact, studies have found that children with SMA1 demonstrate average intelligence significantly higher than healthy controls, likely reflecting compensatory cognitive development. This makes robust AAC absolutely essential -- these are intellectually capable children trapped in bodies that cannot express their thoughts.

Type II -- Dubowitz Disease

An intermediate form that represents about 29% of SMA diagnoses.

  • Onset: 6-18 months
  • Motor milestones: Can sit independently but never stand or walk unaided
  • SMN2 copies: Usually 3
  • Communication impact: Speech is typically present but may be affected by respiratory weakness, reduced volume, and fatigue. Some individuals develop progressive dysarthria
  • Prognosis: Survival into adulthood is common with appropriate respiratory support

Type III -- Kugelberg-Welander Disease

A milder form accounting for about 13% of SMA diagnoses.

  • Onset: After 18 months (typically 2-17 years)
  • Motor milestones: Can walk independently, though this ability may be lost later
  • SMN2 copies: Usually 3-4
  • Communication impact: Speech is generally well preserved, though some individuals may develop mild voice changes related to respiratory muscle weakness in later years
  • Prognosis: Normal or near-normal life expectancy

Type IV (Adult-Onset)

The mildest form, with onset in adulthood (typically after age 30).

  • Onset: Adulthood (mean onset mid-30s)
  • SMN2 copies: Usually 4 or more
  • Communication impact: Speech is rarely significantly affected
  • Prognosis: Normal life expectancy; mild motor symptoms

Genetics & Prevalence

Muscular Dystrophy Genetics

The different forms of muscular dystrophy are caused by mutations in various genes, each encoding a protein essential for muscle structure or function. The inheritance patterns vary by type:

  • X-linked recessive (Duchenne, Becker): Mutations in the DMD gene on the X chromosome. Because males have only one X chromosome, a single defective copy causes disease. About two-thirds of cases are inherited from the mother; one-third result from new (de novo) mutations.
  • Autosomal dominant (Myotonic DM, FSHD): Only one copy of the altered gene is needed to cause disease. Each child of an affected parent has a 50% chance of inheriting the condition.
  • Autosomal recessive (many LGMD subtypes): Two copies of the altered gene are needed. Both parents are typically unaffected carriers.

SMA Genetics

The genetics of SMA center on the survival motor neuron (SMN) genes. Humans have two nearly identical copies of this gene: SMN1 and SMN2. SMA is caused by homozygous deletion or mutation of the SMN1 gene, which is the primary producer of full-length SMN protein. The SMN2 gene serves as a backup but produces mostly a truncated, less functional version of the protein.

The SMN2 Copy Number Effect

The number of SMN2 gene copies a person has is the primary modifier of SMA severity. More SMN2 copies mean more (though still reduced) functional SMN protein is produced, resulting in milder disease. This is why SMA Type I patients typically have 1-2 copies while Type IV patients have 4 or more. This understanding has been crucial for developing gene therapies.

SMA follows an autosomal recessive inheritance pattern. Both males and females are equally affected, and two mutated copies of the gene (one from each parent) are necessary to cause the condition. The carrier frequency is remarkably high: approximately 1 in 50 people worldwide carry one mutated copy, with some variation by ethnicity (approximately 1 in 35 in Caucasian populations to 1 in 66 in African American populations).

How These Conditions Affect Communication

Communication is a complex motor act. Producing speech requires the coordinated function of respiratory muscles (to provide airflow), the larynx (to generate voice), and the oral articulators (tongue, lips, jaw, and soft palate to shape sounds into words). Both MD and SMA can disrupt each of these systems, though through different mechanisms.

Respiratory Weakness

Both MD and SMA cause progressive weakening of the respiratory muscles, including the diaphragm and intercostal muscles. This directly impacts speech production because adequate breath support is the foundation of voice. As respiratory capacity declines:

  • Utterance length shortens (fewer words per breath)
  • Voice volume decreases, making it harder to be heard
  • Speech rate may slow as the individual pauses more frequently to breathe
  • Voice quality becomes breathy or strained
  • Sustained phonation becomes difficult

Oropharyngeal Muscle Weakness

In Duchenne muscular dystrophy, the absent or defective dystrophin protein causes progressive weakness of oropharyngeal muscles -- those crucial for speech production. The tongue is particularly affected, with tongue hypertrophy (pseudo-hypertrophy due to replacement of muscle tissue with fat and connective tissue) being one of the most commonly reported findings. This results in:

  • Imprecise articulation of consonants
  • Difficulty with rapid alternating speech movements
  • Nasal speech (hypernasality) due to soft palate weakness
  • Reduced facial expression, affecting non-verbal communication

Bulbar Involvement in SMA

In SMA, particularly Types I and II, the motor neurons controlling the bulbar muscles (those of the face, tongue, and throat) degenerate. This causes:

  • Flaccid dysarthria -- speech that sounds weak, breathy, and imprecise
  • Fasciculations (muscle twitches) of the tongue
  • Swallowing difficulties (dysphagia) that often coexist with speech problems
  • In severe cases, complete anarthria (inability to produce any speech)

Fatigue

Muscle fatigue is a hallmark of both conditions and significantly impacts communication. Even when speech is still possible, sustained conversation is exhausting. Individuals may:

  • Start conversations clearly but become increasingly unintelligible
  • Need long rest periods between speaking efforts
  • Reserve their limited energy for essential communications
  • Withdraw from social interaction due to communication fatigue
The Fatigue Factor

Communication fatigue is often underestimated. People with neuromuscular conditions frequently report that the effort of speaking is one of their greatest daily challenges. AAC can serve as an energy-conservation strategy even for individuals who retain some speech ability, allowing them to use their limited energy for the conversations that matter most while relying on AAC for routine communication.

Communication Challenges

The communication challenges faced by individuals with MD and SMA are multifaceted, extending well beyond the physical ability to produce speech. Understanding the full scope of these challenges is essential for effective AAC intervention.

Progressive Speech Deterioration

Unlike conditions where communication ability is stable, the progressive nature of MD and SMA means that speech abilities continually change -- almost always declining. This creates unique psychological and practical challenges:

  • Anticipatory grief: Individuals may mourn the anticipated loss of their voice before it occurs
  • Identity concerns: Voice is deeply tied to personal identity; losing it can feel like losing a part of oneself
  • Adaptation fatigue: Constantly needing to learn new communication strategies as abilities change
  • Timing dilemmas: When is the right time to transition to AAC? Too early may feel premature; too late may mean critical learning opportunities are missed

Physical Access Barriers

The same muscle weakness that affects speech also affects the ability to use many AAC devices and systems:

  • Reduced hand and finger dexterity for touchscreen use
  • Limited range of motion for pointing to symbols or letters
  • Head control difficulties affecting head-tracking systems
  • Postural instability requiring specialized positioning for device access
  • Progressive loss of reliable access sites as motor function declines

Respiratory-Communication Interaction

Many individuals with advanced MD or SMA use non-invasive ventilation (NIV) or tracheostomy ventilation. This creates additional communication challenges:

  • Speaking valves on tracheostomy tubes may not be tolerated
  • NIV masks can interfere with speech and facial expression
  • Ventilator cycles may not synchronize with natural speech breathing patterns
  • The noise of ventilation equipment can mask already-quiet speech

Social and Emotional Impact

Communication difficulties have far-reaching effects on quality of life and social participation:

  • Social isolation and withdrawal from peer interactions
  • Difficulty participating in education and employment
  • Challenges in expressing pain, needs, and medical concerns to healthcare providers
  • Frustration and depression related to communication barriers
  • Dependence on familiar communication partners who can interpret unintelligible speech

Cognitive Abilities & Intelligence

One of the most critical points for AAC provision is understanding the cognitive profile of individuals with these conditions. The widespread misconception that physical disability equates to cognitive impairment has historically led to under-provision of robust AAC systems.

SMA: Preserved and Often Superior Cognition

Research has consistently demonstrated that cognitive function is well preserved across all forms of SMA. A landmark finding from multiple studies shows that children with SMA Type I demonstrate average intelligence that is significantly higher than healthy age-matched controls. This includes:

  • Well-preserved morphosyntactic comprehension (understanding of grammar and sentence structure)
  • Strong receptive language skills
  • Normal or above-normal non-verbal reasoning abilities
  • Good memory and learning capacity

The theory behind this finding is that reduced physical exploration of the environment may lead to compensatory development of cognitive and observational skills. Regardless of the mechanism, the implication is clear: these individuals need AAC systems that support full, generative language -- not simplified systems with limited vocabulary.

Presuming Competence

The principle of "presuming competence" is paramount in AAC provision for individuals with MD and SMA. Research supports providing the most robust, language-rich AAC system that is physically accessible, rather than starting with simplified systems. Children with SMA have repeatedly demonstrated cognitive abilities that exceed expectations when given appropriate communication tools.

Muscular Dystrophy: A More Complex Picture

The cognitive profile in muscular dystrophy varies by type:

  • DMD: About one-third of boys with Duchenne have some degree of intellectual disability or learning differences, related to the role of dystrophin in brain development. However, two-thirds have normal or near-normal cognition. Importantly, even those with learning differences still benefit enormously from AAC.
  • BMD: Cognitive function is typically less affected than in DMD.
  • Myotonic dystrophy: Can affect cognition, particularly in congenital and childhood-onset forms. Executive function, processing speed, and visual-spatial skills may be impacted.
  • FSHD and LGMD: Cognition is typically unaffected.

The key takeaway is that for the majority of individuals with these conditions, cognitive abilities are well preserved and often underestimated. AAC planning should always begin with the assumption of full cognitive competence, adjusting as needed based on individual assessment rather than diagnosis alone.

Progressive AAC Planning

Because both MD and SMA are progressive conditions, AAC planning must be proactive rather than reactive. The goal is to have communication supports in place before they are desperately needed, allowing time for learning, customization, and emotional adjustment.

The Stage-Based Approach

Best practice in progressive neuromuscular disease follows a stage-based approach to AAC intervention, anticipating future needs while addressing current ones.

Stage 1: Early / Pre-symptomatic

Speech status: Normal or near-normal intelligibility.
AAC actions: Education about future AAC options. Voice banking while speech is clear and strong. Introduction to low-tech backup systems. Baseline speech assessment. Build AAC awareness and positive attitudes toward AAC use.

Stage 2: Early Communication Changes

Speech status: Mild dysarthria, reduced volume, some fatigue with extended conversation. Speaking rate may drop below 125 words per minute.
AAC actions: Formal AAC referral (recommended when speaking rate <125 wpm or intelligibility <90%). Voice amplification devices. Begin trialing AAC systems for supplemental use. Explore access methods while motor function allows for learning.

Stage 3: Moderate Communication Difficulty

Speech status: Moderate dysarthria, speech intelligible primarily to familiar partners, significant fatigue.
AAC actions: AAC becomes a primary communication tool for unfamiliar partners. Ongoing access method optimization. Multimodal communication (speech + AAC). Social scripts and pre-stored messages for common situations.

Stage 4: Severe Communication Limitation

Speech status: Severe dysarthria or anarthria, speech unintelligible to most listeners.
AAC actions: AAC becomes the primary communication method. Eye-gaze or other alternative access methods may be needed. Voice-output devices with the person's banked voice. Emergency communication protocols established. Full integration of AAC into medical care plans.

Stage 5: End-of-Life Communication

Speech status: No functional speech, possibly limited movement.
AAC actions: Simplified, high-reliability AAC systems. Yes/no communication methods. Partner-assisted scanning. Advance care communication documented. Emotional expression support.

The Voice Banking Window

Voice banking -- the process of recording a person's natural speech to create a synthetic voice that sounds like them -- should be initiated as early as possible, ideally while speech is still clear and strong. For individuals with DMD, this often means the early ambulatory stage (childhood). For SMA, the timing depends on the type: children with SMA Type I may never develop sufficient speech for traditional voice banking, but newer AI-based voice creation technologies are expanding options. The banked voice can later be used with speech-generating devices, preserving a piece of the person's identity.

Key Principles of Progressive AAC Planning

  • Start early: Introduce AAC concepts before they are urgently needed
  • Plan ahead: Always consider what the next stage will require and prepare for it
  • Maintain multimodal communication: AAC should supplement, not replace, natural speech for as long as speech remains functional
  • Preserve access: Identify and preserve the most reliable motor access site for AAC use
  • Build skills progressively: Each stage builds on skills learned in the previous stage
  • Include the individual: The person using AAC should be central to all planning decisions
  • Coordinate with the medical team: AAC planning should be integrated into overall care coordination, including respiratory management and physical therapy

Access Methods

As motor function changes, the methods used to operate AAC devices must adapt. Selection of the appropriate access method is one of the most critical decisions in AAC provision for people with progressive neuromuscular conditions.

Direct Selection

Direct selection (pointing directly to a target) is the fastest and most efficient access method. For individuals who retain some hand or finger movement:

  • Touchscreen: Standard tablets and dedicated AAC devices with touchscreens. May require keyguards, styluses, or adjusted sensitivity settings as motor control decreases.
  • Adapted keyboards: Larger keys, reduced force requirements, alternative layouts
  • Head pointing: Using a head-mounted pointer or reflective dot with camera tracking
  • Mouth stick / chin pointer: For individuals with good head control but limited hand function

Eye Gaze / Eye Tracking

Eye-gaze technology has become a transformative access method for people with severe motor impairments. Because eye muscles are among the last to be affected by both MD and SMA, eye tracking provides a reliable, long-term access solution.

  • Uses infrared cameras to track pupil position
  • Allows users to select targets on screen simply by looking at them
  • Dwell time (looking at a target for a set duration) or blink activation can trigger selections
  • Modern systems are increasingly accurate and affordable
  • Can control full computer environments, not just AAC software
Eye Gaze as the Long-Term Access Solution

For many individuals with severe MD or SMA, eye-gaze technology becomes their primary means of interacting with the world. It enables not only communication through AAC but also environmental control (operating lights, TV, calling for help), computer use (email, social media, education), and creative expression (writing, art). The technology has advanced significantly in recent years, with systems becoming more accurate, more portable, and more integrated with mainstream technology.

Switch Access

Switch scanning involves using one or more switches to navigate through options on an AAC device. While slower than direct selection, it requires very little motor ability and can be activated by almost any reliable movement:

  • Hand/finger switches: Activated by pressing with minimal force
  • Head switches: Positioned near the head for side-to-side movement
  • Proximity/gesture switches: Activated by movement near the switch without touching it (particularly useful for individuals with very limited strength)
  • Sip-and-puff switches: Activated by breathing in or out through a straw
  • Muscle-based (EMG) switches: Detect tiny muscle movements that may not produce visible motion

Emerging Technologies

New access technologies are continuously being developed:

  • Brain-computer interfaces (BCI): Experimental systems that detect neural signals to control AAC devices
  • AI-powered prediction: Machine learning algorithms that predict intended messages from partial inputs, dramatically increasing communication speed
  • Adaptive interfaces: Systems using fuzzy logic and machine learning that automatically adjust to the user's changing motor abilities
  • Noncontact gesture recognition: Camera-based systems that detect subtle movements without requiring physical contact with a switch. A recent case study documented improved communication speed and social function in a young adult with advanced DMD using noncontact gesture-based switch access.

How AAC Helps

AAC is not merely a substitute for speech -- it is a comprehensive communication system that can dramatically improve quality of life, social participation, independence, and psychological well-being for individuals with MD and SMA.

Maintaining Communication Across the Lifespan

For individuals with progressive conditions, AAC provides continuity of communication even as physical abilities change. A well-planned AAC system evolves with the individual, ensuring they are never left without a voice.

Supporting Full Participation

  • Education: AAC enables participation in classroom discussions, answering questions, writing assignments, and interacting with peers. For children with SMA who have strong cognitive abilities, robust AAC is essential for academic achievement.
  • Employment: Many adults with milder forms of MD or SMA maintain careers. AAC supports workplace communication, teleconferencing, and professional interactions.
  • Social life: AAC enables social media participation, text messaging, phone calls (through text-to-speech), and in-person conversations with both familiar and unfamiliar partners.
  • Healthcare: The ability to communicate symptoms, preferences, and concerns to medical providers is critical, especially for individuals with complex medical needs.
  • Self-advocacy: AAC empowers individuals to express their needs, make choices, and advocate for themselves in all areas of life.

Energy Conservation

Using AAC for some or all communication allows individuals to conserve their limited physical energy. Rather than exhausting themselves trying to speak, they can use AAC for routine communications and save their speech energy for situations where they prefer to use their natural voice.

Emergency Communication

AAC systems can include pre-programmed emergency messages such as "I need help," "I'm having trouble breathing," or "Call 911." This is particularly important for individuals who may experience respiratory crises and need to communicate urgently when their voice is too weak to call for help.

Emotional Expression and Relationships

Perhaps most importantly, AAC allows individuals to express love, humor, frustration, creativity, and all the full range of human emotions. It supports the deep personal connections that are essential to quality of life:

  • Telling jokes and stories
  • Expressing affection to family and friends
  • Sharing opinions and perspectives
  • Creating art and writing
  • Participating in religious or cultural practices
AAC Success in Practice

Research and clinical experience consistently show that individuals with neuromuscular conditions who receive timely AAC intervention report significantly higher quality of life, greater social participation, and better psychological outcomes compared to those without adequate communication support. The key factors for success include early introduction, appropriate access methods, robust language systems, and ongoing support and training for both the user and their communication partners.

Types of AAC Used

A comprehensive AAC system for someone with MD or SMA typically includes multiple tools and strategies, forming a "communication toolkit" that can be deployed in different situations and at different stages of disease progression.

No-Tech and Low-Tech AAC

These are always important as backup communication methods and may be the primary method in some situations:

  • Eye gaze boards: Clear acrylic boards with letters, words, or symbols that the user looks at while a partner follows their gaze
  • Communication boards: Paper or laminated boards with symbols, pictures, or text arranged in grids
  • Alphabet boards: For spelling out messages letter by letter
  • Partner-assisted scanning: A communication partner points to options while the user signals (e.g., blink, slight nod) to indicate selection
  • Yes/No signals: Agreed-upon signals for basic yes/no communication (eye blinks, slight head movements, thumbs up)

Mid-Tech AAC

  • Simple speech-generating devices: Single or multi-message devices that play pre-recorded messages at the press of a button or switch
  • Voice amplifiers: Portable devices that amplify quiet speech, often the first AAC tool introduced when voice begins to weaken
  • Text-to-speech apps: Simple applications that convert typed text to spoken output

High-Tech AAC

These are comprehensive communication systems that offer the most flexibility and power:

  • Dedicated speech-generating devices (SGDs): Purpose-built AAC devices from manufacturers like Tobii Dynavox, PRC-Saltillo, and others. Often include integrated eye-tracking and environmental control.
  • Tablet-based AAC apps: Applications such as Proloquo2Go, TD Snap, TouchChat, LAMP Words for Life, and others running on iPads or Android tablets. More portable and less conspicuous than dedicated devices.
  • Computer-based systems: Full computer access with AAC software, enabling communication, education, employment, and entertainment from one device.
  • Eye-tracking integrated systems: Devices with built-in eye-tracking cameras that allow complete hands-free operation.

Voice Banking and Voice Generation

  • Traditional voice banking: Recording extensive speech samples (typically 800+ utterances) that are synthesized into a personalized text-to-speech voice
  • Message banking: Recording specific phrases in the person's natural voice for playback (e.g., "I love you," "Go team!")
  • AI-powered voice creation: Newer technologies that can create a personalized synthetic voice from smaller speech samples, or even create a voice for someone who has never had functional speech based on family members' voices
  • Double-dip voice synthesis: Using a combination of voice banking and AI to create more natural-sounding output
The Multimodal Approach

Best practice recommends a multimodal communication approach -- using a combination of whatever methods work best in each situation. This might mean using natural speech with familiar partners, a voice amplifier in noisy environments, an AAC app for extended conversations, an eye-gaze system for computer access, and a simple eye-gaze board as an emergency backup. No single tool meets every communication need.

New Treatments & Changing AAC Needs

The treatment landscape for both MD and SMA has been transformed by groundbreaking therapies, particularly in the area of gene therapy. These advances are changing the natural history of these conditions and, consequently, the communication profiles and AAC needs of affected individuals.

SMA Gene Therapy Revolution

Onasemnogene abeparvovec (Zolgensma)

Zolgensma is a one-time gene therapy that delivers a functional copy of the SMN1 gene. Approved for children under 2 years of age with SMA, it has dramatically changed outcomes:

  • In clinical trials, 92% of the higher-dose cohort could take food orally and speak, compared to near-zero in untreated SMA Type I
  • Many treated children achieve motor milestones previously considered impossible for their SMA type
  • Long-term data (up to 5 years) from the RESTORE registry shows sustained benefits with no unexpected late-onset safety concerns

Nusinersen (Spinraza)

Spinraza is an antisense oligonucleotide that modifies SMN2 gene splicing to produce more functional SMN protein. It requires repeated intrathecal injections (four loading doses in the first two months, then maintenance doses every four months for life):

  • Demonstrated improvement in motor function across SMA types
  • Research shows cognitive improvements in addition to motor gains
  • First disease-modifying therapy approved for SMA (2016)

Risdiplam (Evrysdi)

An oral SMN2 splicing modifier taken daily at home. Approved for patients 2 months and older with all SMA types. Its oral administration makes it the most accessible of the three treatments.

Impact on AAC Needs

These treatments are creating a new generation of individuals with SMA whose trajectories are fundamentally different from the pre-treatment era. Children with SMA Type I who previously would never have spoken are now developing speech. However, this does not eliminate the need for AAC -- it changes its role. Treated children may develop speech that is functional but impaired, requiring AAC as a supplement. They may have periods of illness or fatigue where AAC becomes essential. And the long-term trajectory of treated SMA is still being studied, meaning proactive AAC planning remains critical.

Muscular Dystrophy Treatment Advances

While treatment advances for MD have not been as dramatic as those for SMA, significant progress is being made:

  • Exon-skipping therapies: Drugs like eteplirsen (Exondys 51), golodirsen (Vyondys 53), and casimersen (Amondys 45) allow the cellular machinery to "skip" over specific mutated exons in the dystrophin gene, producing a shorter but partially functional protein
  • Corticosteroids: Remain the standard of care for DMD, significantly extending ambulation and delaying respiratory decline
  • Gene therapy trials: Multiple clinical trials are investigating micro-dystrophin gene therapy for DMD
  • Cardiac therapies: Improved management of cardiomyopathy is extending life expectancy

Implications for AAC Practice

The evolving treatment landscape has several important implications for AAC practice:

  • Longer life spans: As treatments extend survival, individuals need AAC systems that support communication over a longer period and across more life stages
  • Changed trajectories: The traditional progression models may not apply to treated individuals, requiring more individualized AAC planning
  • Combination therapy: Research suggests combination approaches (e.g., gene therapy plus ongoing treatment) may be most effective, creating even more variable outcomes
  • Newborn screening impact: With SMA now on newborn screening panels in all 50 US states, pre-symptomatic treatment is becoming the norm, fundamentally changing who needs AAC and when
  • AAC as a bridge: For some treated individuals, AAC may serve as a temporary bridge during treatment response periods rather than a permanent primary communication method

Evidence & Research

The evidence base for AAC use in neuromuscular conditions has grown substantially, though much of the research has focused on ALS (amyotrophic lateral sclerosis), with findings applied to MD and SMA populations.

Key Research Findings

  • Cognitive preservation: Multiple studies (PMC7856807, PMC11261103) confirm that cognitive function and language comprehension are well preserved in SMA, supporting the provision of robust AAC systems
  • DMD dysarthria progression: A 10-year longitudinal study documented the progression of dysarthria in DMD from 8% in the early ambulatory stage to 92% in the late non-ambulatory stage, emphasizing the need for proactive planning
  • Eye-gaze effectiveness: Research demonstrates that eye-gaze technology provides effective communication access for individuals with severe motor impairments, with improving accuracy and speed as technology advances
  • Gene therapy speech outcomes: Clinical trial data shows that 92% of children receiving high-dose Zolgensma developed the ability to speak, a milestone previously unattainable for most SMA Type I patients
  • Adaptive AAC systems: Emerging research on fuzzy logic and machine learning-based AAC systems shows promise for automatically adapting to users' changing motor abilities
  • Quality of life impact: Studies consistently show that effective communication support is one of the strongest predictors of quality of life in neuromuscular disease

Research Gaps

Several important areas need more research:

  • Long-term AAC outcomes specifically for MD and SMA populations (most literature focuses on ALS)
  • Optimal timing for AAC introduction across different condition types
  • AAC needs of the "new" population of treated SMA individuals
  • Effectiveness of different access methods as motor function changes over time
  • Psychosocial impact of proactive versus reactive AAC provision
  • Cultural and linguistic considerations in AAC provision for these populations
The Shifting Evidence Landscape

The rapid pace of treatment advances means that historical research on communication outcomes in MD and SMA may not reflect current realities. Research conducted before the availability of Zolgensma, Spinraza, and Evrysdi describes a natural history that is now being fundamentally altered. Clinicians must balance historical evidence with emerging clinical observations in this rapidly evolving field.

Key Considerations

Providing effective AAC for individuals with MD and SMA requires attention to several important factors that are often overlooked.

Funding and Insurance

High-tech AAC devices and eye-gaze systems can be expensive. In the US, speech-generating devices are covered by Medicare and most insurance plans as durable medical equipment, but the process of obtaining funding can be lengthy and complex. Key considerations include:

  • Documentation requirements (evaluation reports, trials, medical necessity letters)
  • The distinction between "dedicated" SGDs (which are more readily funded) and consumer devices with AAC apps
  • Funding for device maintenance, replacement, and upgrades as needs change
  • Insurance denials and the appeals process
  • State-specific Medicaid waiver programs that may provide additional support

Mounting and Positioning

For individuals who use wheelchairs (common in both MD and SMA), proper mounting of AAC devices is essential:

  • Devices must be securely mounted to the wheelchair and positioned for optimal access
  • Mounting must accommodate changes in seating and positioning over time
  • Eye-gaze systems require specific distances and angles for accurate tracking
  • The device should not interfere with other medical equipment (ventilators, feeding systems)

Training and Support

AAC is only effective if the user and their communication partners know how to use it. Ongoing training should include:

  • The AAC user (skills building, vocabulary customization, rate enhancement strategies)
  • Family members (modeling AAC use, creating communication opportunities, avoiding communication breakdown)
  • Educators (integrating AAC into classroom activities and curriculum)
  • Healthcare providers (understanding how to communicate with AAC users, allowing adequate time)
  • Peers (fostering natural and inclusive interactions)

Emotional and Psychological Support

The transition from natural speech to AAC use is a significant emotional experience, especially for individuals who once spoke fluently. Support should address:

  • Grief related to loss of natural voice
  • Identity and self-concept changes
  • Frustration with the slower pace of AAC compared to speech
  • Social stigma or reluctance to use AAC in public
  • The psychological benefit of preserving one's own voice through voice banking

Coordination of Care

Individuals with MD and SMA typically have complex medical needs involving multiple specialists. AAC planning must be integrated into the broader care plan:

  • Speech-language pathologists specializing in AAC
  • Occupational therapists for seating, positioning, and access
  • Physical therapists for motor function assessment
  • Pulmonologists for respiratory management
  • Neurologists for disease management
  • Assistive technology specialists
  • Rehabilitation engineers for custom solutions
  • Social workers for funding and support coordination
The Importance of Backup Systems

Technology fails. Batteries die. Devices break. For individuals who depend on AAC for all communication, having a reliable backup system is not optional -- it is a safety issue. Every AAC user should have at minimum a low-tech backup communication method (such as an eye-gaze board or partner-assisted scanning protocol) that does not depend on electricity or technology. This backup should be readily accessible at all times, and communication partners should be trained in its use.

Cultural and Linguistic Considerations

AAC systems must be culturally and linguistically appropriate. For individuals from non-English-speaking backgrounds, this includes:

  • AAC vocabulary in the user's preferred language(s)
  • Culturally relevant symbols and vocabulary
  • Text-to-speech voices available in appropriate languages and dialects
  • Understanding of cultural attitudes toward disability and assistive technology
  • Bilingual or multilingual AAC support for multilingual individuals

Transition Planning

As individuals with MD and SMA move through life stages, their AAC needs change. Important transition points include:

  • Early intervention to school: Ensuring AAC is integrated into the educational plan
  • School to adult services: Navigating the shift from educational to vocational/medical funding
  • Home to hospital: Ensuring AAC is available and understood during hospitalizations
  • Changes in living situation: Training new caregivers in AAC use
