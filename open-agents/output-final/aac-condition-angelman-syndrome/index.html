Overview

Angelman Syndrome (AS) is a rare neuro-genetic disorder that profoundly affects the nervous system, causing severe developmental delays, intellectual disability, and -- most relevantly to this guide -- a profound communication disorder that makes speech extremely difficult or impossible for the vast majority of individuals. First described by British pediatrician Dr. Harry Angelman in 1965, the syndrome is caused by the loss of function of the UBE3A gene on chromosome 15, specifically the maternal copy.

Despite the significant challenges AS presents, individuals with this condition are often described as having a uniquely happy and sociable demeanor, characterized by frequent smiling, laughter, and an apparent love of social interaction. This warm, engaging personality often masks the deep frustration that comes with being unable to communicate effectively through speech. Most people with Angelman Syndrome understand far more than they can express verbally -- a gap that makes Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) not just helpful but essential.

AAC encompasses a wide spectrum of tools and strategies, from simple picture boards and sign language to sophisticated speech-generating devices and eye-gaze technology. For individuals with Angelman Syndrome, AAC represents a lifeline -- a way to bridge the gap between what they understand and what they can express, enabling them to make choices, share their feelings, build relationships, and participate more fully in the world around them.

✭ Key Insight

Angelman Syndrome is one of the conditions where AAC is most critical. Up to 85% of individuals with AS do not use natural speech, yet they demonstrate strong social motivation and receptive language skills that far exceed their expressive abilities. AAC provides the essential bridge between understanding and expression.

Genetics & Characteristics

The UBE3A Gene

Angelman Syndrome is caused by the loss of function of the UBE3A gene, located on chromosome 15 (specifically the 15q11.2-q13 region). The UBE3A gene provides instructions for making an enzyme called ubiquitin protein ligase E3A, which plays a critical role in the normal development and function of the nervous system. This enzyme is involved in tagging damaged or unnecessary proteins for degradation within cells, a process essential for proper neuronal function.

What makes Angelman Syndrome genetically unique is the concept of genomic imprinting. In most tissues of the body, both copies of the UBE3A gene (one inherited from each parent) are active. However, in the brain, only the maternal copy (inherited from the mother) is typically active -- the paternal copy is naturally silenced through imprinting. This means that if the maternal copy is missing, mutated, or otherwise non-functional, the brain has no active UBE3A gene, leading to Angelman Syndrome.

Genetic Subtypes

There are four primary genetic mechanisms that cause Angelman Syndrome, each with somewhat different clinical profiles:

Genetic Mechanism Frequency Clinical Severity
Deletion -- a segment of maternal chromosome 15 containing UBE3A is deleted ~70% Typically the most severe form; earlier and more frequent seizures; more pronounced speech impairment
UBE3A Mutation -- a specific variant in the maternal UBE3A gene ~10-20% Variable severity; some individuals may develop limited speech
Paternal Uniparental Disomy (UPD) -- two paternal copies, no maternal copy ~2-5% Generally milder; better speech development; fewer seizures; higher BMI
Imprinting Defect -- the imprinting control region is disrupted ~2-5% Generally milder; greater communicative abilities possible
ℹ Genotype-Phenotype Relationship

The genetic subtype significantly influences communication potential. Individuals with non-deletion forms of Angelman Syndrome (mutations, UPD, or imprinting defects) typically have greater communicative abilities, including a higher likelihood of developing some spoken words. This distinction is important for setting AAC goals and selecting appropriate communication systems.

Core Clinical Characteristics

Angelman Syndrome is characterized by a constellation of clinical features that affect multiple systems:

🧠

Developmental Delay

Moderate to severe intellectual disability is universal, typically becoming apparent by 6-12 months of age. Developmental milestones are significantly delayed across all domains.

⚡

Seizures / Epilepsy

Epilepsy affects approximately 80-90% of individuals with AS, typically beginning between ages 1-3. Seizures are more frequent and severe in deletion subtypes (91%) vs. non-deletion (61%).

🚶

Movement & Balance

Ataxia (unsteady, jerky movements), tremulousness of limbs, and wide-based gait are hallmark features. Fine motor difficulties significantly impact the ability to use AAC devices.

😊

Happy Demeanor

Frequent laughter and smiling, an excitable personality, and a fascination with water and shiny objects. This sociable disposition makes individuals with AS eager communicators.

💤

Sleep Disturbances

Sleep problems affect approximately 69% of individuals, including difficulty with both onset and maintenance. Poor sleep can significantly impact daytime communication and learning.

🗣

Speech Impairment

Severe speech impairment is a cardinal feature, with 71-90% of individuals never developing functional speech. Those who do speak are typically limited to 2-15 words.

Angelman Syndrome affects all ethnic groups equally, with no reported geographic or racial predominance. However, diagnosis rates may vary significantly between countries due to differences in access to genetic testing and awareness among healthcare providers. ⚠ Diagnostic Challenges

Angelman Syndrome is frequently misdiagnosed or diagnosed late because early symptoms overlap with other conditions such as autism spectrum disorder, cerebral palsy, or non-specific developmental delay. The characteristic features become more distinctive with age, but early identification is crucial for implementing AAC and communication interventions as soon as possible.

How Angelman Syndrome Affects Communication

Profound communication disorder is one of the four defining diagnostic features of Angelman Syndrome, alongside developmental delay, movement/balance disorders, and the characteristic behavioral profile. The impact on communication is multifaceted, affecting both the physical ability to produce speech and the cognitive processes involved in language formulation.

The Nature of the Speech Impairment

The severe speech impairment in Angelman Syndrome is believed to be related to oral-motor dyspraxia (also called speech apraxia) -- a neurological condition that affects the ability to plan and coordinate the complex muscle movements needed for speech. This is not simply a matter of "not knowing" language; rather, the brain struggles to send the correct signals to the muscles of the mouth, tongue, lips, and jaw that produce speech sounds.

Between 71% and 90% of individuals with Angelman Syndrome never develop functional speech, and among those who do produce some spoken words, their vocabulary is typically limited to 2 to 15 words. Even these few words may be difficult for unfamiliar listeners to understand. This means that the vast majority of people with AS cannot meet their communication needs through speech alone, making alternative communication methods essential.

What Remains Intact

Despite the severe speech impairment, individuals with Angelman Syndrome retain several important communication abilities:

  • Social motivation -- People with AS are highly social, eager to interact, and motivated to communicate. Their characteristic happy demeanor includes a genuine desire for social connection.
  • Nonverbal communication -- Facial expressions, eye gaze, body language, gestures, physical manipulation of others (e.g., leading someone by the hand), and vocalizations are all used purposefully.
  • Receptive language -- Understanding of spoken language is significantly stronger than the ability to produce it. Many individuals comprehend far more than they can express.
  • Intentional communication -- People with AS communicate with clear intent, including making requests, refusing activities, expressing emotions, and seeking social interaction.
✭ Critical Understanding

The inability to speak does not equal the inability to communicate or understand. Individuals with Angelman Syndrome are skilled communicators who use a wide repertoire of nonverbal strategies. The challenge is providing them with tools and systems that allow them to communicate with the same richness and specificity that speech would provide.

Communication Functions

Research has identified patterns in how individuals with AS use communication. Requesting (asking for things or actions) is the best-developed and most widely used communication function. In contrast, commenting (making observations about the world) and imitation are areas of comparative weakness. This has implications for AAC intervention, as systems and strategies need to support not just basic requests but also the development of broader communication functions like commenting, asking questions, and social interaction.

The Receptive-Expressive Gap

One of the most important -- and most frequently misunderstood -- aspects of Angelman Syndrome is the dramatic gap between receptive language (what a person understands) and expressive language (what a person can say or communicate outwardly). This gap is far wider in AS than in most other developmental conditions, and it has profound implications for how we approach communication support.

Understanding the Gap

Research consistently shows that the highest receptive communication abilities in AS are commonly described as comparable to those of two-year-old typically developing children (approximately 24-month equivalent), while expressive speech skills commonly do not exceed those of 12-month-olds. However, it is crucial to note that these developmental equivalence scores are approximations and may significantly underestimate actual comprehension, particularly because standard language assessments typically require verbal responses that individuals with AS cannot provide.

Recent neuroimaging research from 2022 provides compelling evidence that the language comprehension abilities of individuals with Angelman Syndrome may be greater than previously thought. Studies have shown that unlike children with limited speech due to other neurodevelopmental disorders, participants with Angelman Syndrome differentiated known words from novel pseudowords using neural processes comparable to those of typically developing children with similar vocabulary size. This suggests that the underlying language processing machinery in the brain is more intact than the severe speech impairment would suggest.

ℹ Why This Matters for AAC

The receptive-expressive gap means that individuals with AS are likely understanding much of what is said around them but have extremely limited ways to respond. This is why AAC must be introduced as early as possible -- not because the child doesn't communicate, but because they deserve access to a richer, more specific vocabulary than gestures and vocalizations alone can provide. The AAC system should be designed to match the person's comprehension level, not their expressive speech level.

Genetic Subtype Influences

The size of the receptive-expressive gap varies by genetic subtype. Individuals with non-deletion forms of Angelman Syndrome (UBE3A mutations, UPD, or imprinting defects) typically demonstrate greater communicative abilities overall, including:

  • Higher likelihood of developing some functional spoken words
  • Better receptive language scores
  • More diverse use of communicative forms (symbolic and nonsymbolic)
  • Greater success with high-tech AAC devices

However, even individuals with the deletion subtype (the most common and typically most severe form) demonstrate clear receptive language abilities and can benefit significantly from AAC intervention.

Communication Challenges

Implementing effective communication strategies for individuals with Angelman Syndrome requires understanding the specific and interconnected challenges they face. These challenges go beyond simply not being able to speak -- they involve motor, cognitive, sensory, and behavioral factors that all influence communication.

Motor Challenges

The motor difficulties associated with Angelman Syndrome significantly impact access to AAC systems:

  • Ataxia -- Unsteady, jerky movements make precise pointing and selection on AAC devices difficult. Many individuals have a wide-based, unsteady gait.
  • Oral-motor dyspraxia -- Difficulty coordinating the mouth muscles needed for speech sounds, even when the individual knows what they want to say.
  • Fine motor impairment -- Difficulty with precise hand movements affects the ability to point to small targets on communication boards or touchscreens.
  • Tremor -- Hand and limb tremulousness can reduce accuracy when selecting items on a communication device.

Cognitive Challenges

  • Attention and focus -- Short attention spans and high distractibility can make sustained communication difficult, particularly with complex AAC systems.
  • Abstract reasoning -- Understanding symbolic representations (pictures representing words or concepts) requires cognitive skills that develop at different rates in AS.
  • Memory and learning -- While individuals with AS can and do learn, the rate of learning is typically slower, requiring consistent repetition and practice with AAC systems.
  • Executive function -- Planning multi-step communication sequences (navigating through pages on an AAC device) can be challenging.

Behavioral Challenges

  • Hyperactivity and restlessness -- Many individuals with AS, particularly children, are highly active and may have difficulty sitting still long enough to engage with an AAC device.
  • Mouthing behaviors -- A tendency to put objects in their mouths, including communication devices, can damage equipment and interrupt communication.
  • Fascination with water and textures -- Devices need to be protected from water exposure.
  • Frustration -- The gap between understanding and expression can lead to frustration, which may manifest as challenging behaviors, particularly before effective AAC is established.

Environmental and Systemic Challenges

  • Limited AAC-specific training -- Many speech-language pathologists and educators may have limited experience with Angelman Syndrome specifically, leading to communication systems that do not adequately account for the unique AS profile.
  • Presumption of competence -- Due to the severity of the speech impairment and co-occurring intellectual disability, there is a risk that communication partners underestimate what individuals with AS can understand and learn.
  • Consistency across settings -- For AAC to be effective, it must be used consistently across home, school, therapy, and community settings, which requires training and buy-in from all communication partners.
  • Funding and access -- High-tech AAC devices can be expensive, and navigating insurance and funding pathways can be difficult for families.
⚠ Common Pitfall

One of the most significant barriers to communication success for individuals with AS is the underestimation of their potential. Because they cannot speak and have intellectual disability, there is a tendency to assume they cannot learn to use AAC or that they do not have anything meaningful to communicate. Research and clinical experience consistently demonstrate that this assumption is wrong. With appropriate support, individuals with AS can learn to use AAC systems and expand their communicative repertoire throughout their lives.

How AAC Helps

Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) is the primary approach to addressing the profound communication needs of individuals with Angelman Syndrome. AAC does not replace natural communication methods like gestures and vocalizations; instead, it augments them, providing additional tools that allow for more specific, varied, and independent communication.

The Total Communication Approach

Best practice for Angelman Syndrome calls for a total communication approach (also called multimodal communication), meaning that all forms of communication are valued and encouraged simultaneously. An individual might use:

  • Facial expressions and eye gaze to show preferences and emotions
  • Gestures and pointing to indicate choices
  • Physical manipulation (e.g., leading someone by the hand) to make requests
  • Vocalizations (non-speech sounds) to express feelings
  • Signs or key word signs for frequently used concepts
  • A low-tech communication book or board for broader vocabulary
  • A high-tech speech-generating device for the most specific communication

The goal is never to restrict someone to a single method but to provide a comprehensive toolkit that allows them to communicate as effectively as possible in all situations.

What AAC Provides

🎤

A Voice

Speech-generating devices give individuals with AS an audible voice, allowing them to be heard by others without requiring the listener to be physically close or familiar with their nonverbal cues.

📚

Expanded Vocabulary

While gestures and facial expressions are powerful, they are limited in specificity. AAC systems can provide access to hundreds or thousands of words and concepts, allowing for more precise communication.

👥

Independence

AAC reduces reliance on familiar communication partners who have learned to interpret the individual's nonverbal signals, enabling communication with unfamiliar people in the community.

🎓

Language Development

Exposure to and use of AAC systems supports the ongoing development of language skills, including vocabulary growth, concept development, and understanding of language structure.

The Language Immersion Model

One of the most important principles in AAC implementation for Angelman Syndrome is the concept of language immersion. Just as children learn spoken language by being surrounded by people who speak to them, individuals with AS learn AAC by being surrounded by people who model AAC use. This approach, sometimes called "Aided Language Stimulation" or "Modeling," involves communication partners using the AAC system themselves while talking to the individual.

"Learning to use an alternative communication system (AAC), where we touch a photo or symbol to express a word, requires the same kind of immersion in the language that it takes to learn a second language."

-- Angelman Syndrome Foundation

The PODD (Pragmatic Organization Dynamic Display) system is particularly well-suited to this approach because it promotes a language immersion model where there is no pressure on the language-learner to reproduce specific words at a specific rate. The primary requirement is that adults are consistently modeling the system for the language-learner, showing them how it works through real, meaningful communication.

The Model, Encourage, Respond Framework

Research supported by the Angelman Syndrome Foundation has developed the "Model, Encourage, Respond" framework for AAC implementation:

  • Model -- Communication partners consistently demonstrate how to use the AAC system during natural interactions throughout the day.
  • Encourage -- Create opportunities and motivation for the individual to use their AAC system, without demanding performance.
  • Respond -- Provide immediate, meaningful responses to all communication attempts, whether they come through AAC, gestures, vocalizations, or any other method.
✓ Evidence of Effectiveness

Research shows that approximately 60% of individuals with Angelman Syndrome accept their most advanced AAC device. The key factors associated with acceptance include the individual's ability to communicate more effectively and purposefully, alongside contingent responses from communication partners -- meaning that when the person uses the device, they get an immediate and meaningful response. This underscores the importance of responsive communication partners in AAC success.

Types of AAC Used

AAC for Angelman Syndrome spans a wide continuum from no-tech and low-tech solutions to sophisticated high-tech devices. Most individuals benefit from having access to multiple types of AAC (a multimodal approach), using different tools in different situations depending on their needs and the context.

Access Methods

How an individual physically interacts with their AAC system is just as important as the system itself. Several access methods may be appropriate for individuals with Angelman Syndrome:

Direct Selection (Finger Pointing)

The most intuitive method -- the individual touches or points to the desired symbol. For individuals with AS who have sufficient motor control, this is preferred. Physical keyguards (overlays with holes cut out for each symbol) can improve accuracy by providing tactile boundaries.

Whole-Hand or Fist Selection

For individuals who cannot isolate a single finger, selecting with the whole hand or a closed fist may work with larger targets and appropriate spacing between symbols.

Stylus or Pointing Aids

A stylus, head pointer, or pointing splint can extend reach and improve accuracy for those with poor finger isolation or tremor.

Partner-Assisted Scanning

The communication partner points to symbols or reads options aloud, and the individual indicates "yes" or "no" (through any reliable signal). This is often much easier than direct selection for people with AS and is strongly recommended as a starting approach.

Eye Gaze

Technology tracks where the user is looking to make selections. Can be useful for some individuals with AS, but is very tiring and requires significant practice. Partner-assisted scanning may be preferable for many.

ℹ Device Selection Tip

When selecting AAC technology for someone with Angelman Syndrome, consider the individual's motor abilities, attention span, and environments where the device will be used. Many families find that a combination approach works best: a low-tech PODD book that is always available (doesn't need charging, survives water exposure) paired with a high-tech speech-generating device for situations where speech output is important. The AAC system can be worn on a harness, mounted on a wheelchair, or carried in a protective case.

Evidence & Research

The evidence base supporting AAC intervention for Angelman Syndrome has grown significantly in recent years, though researchers note that additional large-scale studies are still needed. Here is a summary of the key research findings.

Systematic Review Findings

A systematic review of AAC intervention for people with Angelman Syndrome (published in Current Developmental Disorders Reports, 2020) identified nine studies investigating the impact of various AAC modes on individuals under 18 with AS. The review concluded:

  • All nine studies reported positive benefits in enhancing and supporting communication development.
  • AAC intervention is a viable approach for enhancing the communication skills of people with Angelman Syndrome.
  • Both low-tech and high-tech AAC approaches showed benefits.
  • The quality and quantity of evidence remains limited, with most studies being single-case or small-group designs.
  • Additional research with larger samples is needed to strengthen the evidence base.

Scoping Review: Communication in AS (Pearson et al., 2019)

A landmark scoping review published in Developmental Medicine & Child Neurology examined the breadth of research on communication in Angelman Syndrome and found:

  • Individuals with AS are multimodal communicators who use a wide variety of methods including nonsymbolic communication, symbolic communication, and AAC.
  • The most commonly used forms of communication are nonsymbolic: non-speech vocalizations, physical manipulations of others, and gestures.
  • Receptive communication skills are more advanced than expressive abilities, though both are significantly delayed.
  • The use of communicative forms differs between genetic aetiologies, with non-deletion subtypes showing greater abilities.

Neural Processing of Language (2022)

Groundbreaking neuroimaging research published in Brain and Language (2022) demonstrated that individuals with Angelman Syndrome process spoken words using neural mechanisms that are more typical than previously assumed. Specifically, participants with AS differentiated known words from novel pseudowords using brain processes comparable to those of typically developing children with similar vocabulary size. This finding suggests that the underlying language comprehension system in AS is more intact than the severe speech impairment would suggest, reinforcing the importance of providing robust AAC systems that match individuals' true comprehension abilities.

Parent Perceptions of AAC Use

A study on parents' perceptions of communication patterns and AAC effectiveness (published in 2014) found that:

  • Nonsymbolic methods of communication proved to be very important to individuals with AS.
  • Electronic AAC devices were valued, although to a lesser extent than natural communication methods.
  • Despite inconsistent access to best practices, individuals with AS demonstrated the ability to use AAC systems successfully, including high-tech devices.
  • Communication partner training and responsive interaction styles were identified as key factors in success.

Expressive Communication Skills Survey

Research published in the American Journal of Speech-Language Pathology surveyed expressive communication skills in children with Angelman Syndrome and found that requesting was the best-developed communication function, while commenting and imitation were areas of relative weakness. This has practical implications for AAC programming, suggesting that intervention should build on the strength of requesting while also explicitly teaching commenting and social interaction skills.

✭ Research Summary

The research evidence consistently supports three key conclusions: (1) individuals with AS can learn to use AAC effectively, (2) a multimodal approach combining low-tech and high-tech methods is optimal, and (3) communication partner behavior -- particularly consistent modeling and responsive interaction -- is one of the strongest predictors of AAC success. The evidence base, while growing, still needs larger controlled studies to further strengthen these findings.

Key Considerations

Successfully implementing AAC for individuals with Angelman Syndrome requires careful consideration of several factors that are specific to this population.

Start Early and Presume Competence

AAC should be introduced as early as possible -- ideally as soon as the diagnosis is made or suspected, and certainly not delayed while "waiting to see if speech develops." The evidence is clear that AAC does not inhibit speech development; if anything, it supports it. Organizations like AngelmanUK strongly advocate for AAC within a total communication environment from the earliest age.

Presuming competence means assuming that the individual can learn and has something meaningful to communicate, even when their current behavior may not suggest it. Given the receptive-expressive gap in AS, the person almost certainly understands more than they can currently express. The AAC system should be designed to grow with the individual, not limited to what they can demonstrate today.

Incorporate AAC into Daily Routines

The most effective way to make AAC meaningful is to embed it in daily routines and activities. Mealtimes, getting dressed, bath time, play, and outings all provide natural opportunities for communication. When parts of the day are familiar and expected, these become ideal times to introduce and practice AAC.

Address Motor Access Carefully

Given the significant motor challenges in Angelman Syndrome, careful attention must be paid to how the individual accesses their AAC system. Key considerations include:

  • Symbol size and spacing -- Larger symbols with more space between them can improve accuracy for individuals with ataxia and tremor.
  • Keyguards -- Physical overlays with cutouts for each symbol can dramatically improve targeting accuracy on touchscreen devices.
  • Device positioning -- The device should be stable, at an appropriate height and angle, and positioned to minimize the impact of motor difficulties.
  • Alternative access -- If direct selection is too difficult, partner-assisted scanning is often a very effective alternative that removes the motor barrier entirely.
  • Seating and positioning -- Proper postural support can improve upper extremity function and AAC access.

Train All Communication Partners

AAC is only as effective as the people surrounding the individual. All communication partners -- parents, siblings, teachers, therapists, aides, extended family, and community members -- need to understand and use the system. This includes:

  • Learning to model AAC use during natural interactions
  • Understanding how to respond to all communication attempts, including imprecise ones
  • Knowing how to set up and maintain the AAC device or system
  • Developing patience -- AAC learning is a long-term process, not an overnight solution
  • Maintaining consistency across all environments (home, school, community)

Plan for the Lifespan

Angelman Syndrome is a lifelong condition, and communication needs evolve over time. AAC planning should consider:

  • Vocabulary growth -- The system should be expandable as the individual's communication needs become more complex.
  • Changing contexts -- As individuals with AS transition from school to adult settings, their communication needs and environments will change.
  • Technology updates -- Devices wear out and software evolves; transition plans should be in place.
  • Increasing independence -- As individuals grow, the goal is to maximize their ability to communicate independently in their community.

Manage Seizures and Medication Effects

Given that 80-90% of individuals with AS have epilepsy, it is important to consider the impact of both seizures and anti-epileptic medications on communication and learning. Some medications may cause drowsiness or cognitive slowing that affects AAC use. Communication teams should coordinate closely with neurologists and be prepared to adjust expectations and strategies around medication changes or periods of increased seizure activity.

✓ Best Practice Checklist

For families and professionals implementing AAC for someone with Angelman Syndrome:

  • Begin AAC as early as possible -- do not wait for speech to develop
  • Use a total (multimodal) communication approach
  • Model AAC use consistently in daily routines
  • Presume competence -- provide vocabulary that matches understanding, not speech
  • Address motor access with appropriate adaptations
  • Train all communication partners
  • Celebrate all communication, regardless of the method used
  • Provide both low-tech (always available) and high-tech (speech output) options
  • Coordinate with the full clinical team (neurology, PT, OT, SLP)
  • Plan for growth, transitions, and lifelong communication needs

Emerging Therapies

While AAC remains the primary and most immediately impactful approach to communication for individuals with Angelman Syndrome, there are significant developments in medical research that may, in the future, change the landscape of AS treatment and its effects on communication.

Gene Therapy and Genetic Treatments

Because Angelman Syndrome is caused by a single gene (UBE3A), it is a strong candidate for genetic-based therapies. Several approaches are in development:

Antisense Oligonucleotides (ASOs)

GTX-102 (apazunersen), developed by Ultragenyx, is the furthest along in clinical development. This antisense oligonucleotide works by "unsilencing" the paternal copy of UBE3A -- essentially reactivating the backup copy of the gene that is normally silenced in the brain. The Phase 3 ASPIRE study has completed enrollment with approximately 129 participants ages 4-17 with genetically confirmed full maternal UBE3A gene deletion. GTX-102 has been granted Breakthrough Therapy Designation, Orphan Drug Designation, Rare Pediatric Disease Designation, and Fast Track Designation from the FDA.

Gene Replacement Therapy

MavriX Bio's MVX-220 has received FDA Fast Track Designation, with a Phase 1/2 clinical trial (ASCEND-AS) planned for approximately 12 patients with AS aged 4-50 years. Separately, UC Davis researchers are testing a stem cell gene therapy approach that aims to replace the missing UBE3A protein with a functional one.

Small Molecule Therapies

Texas A&M researchers have discovered a drug candidate that has entered human clinical trials in 2025, representing another approach to treating the underlying genetic cause of AS.

ℹ Implications for Communication

It is important to note that even if genetic therapies prove successful, they are unlikely to eliminate the need for AAC entirely, particularly for individuals who receive treatment after early childhood. The degree to which restoring UBE3A protein function would improve speech and language abilities is still unknown. AAC should never be delayed while waiting for future therapies. Current communication needs must be addressed now, and AAC skills will remain valuable regardless of future medical advances.

Communication Research

Active areas of communication research for AS include:

  • Development of standardized communication assessment tools specifically validated for Angelman Syndrome
  • Investigation of optimal AAC system features for the unique motor and cognitive profile of AS
  • Studies on the long-term outcomes of early AAC intervention in AS
  • Research on communication partner training programs and their impact on communication development
  • Exploration of how emerging technologies (brain-computer interfaces, AI-powered AAC) might benefit individuals with AS in the future
