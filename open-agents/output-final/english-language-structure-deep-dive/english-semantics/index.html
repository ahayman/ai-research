English Semantics How English Encodes Meaning

A vocabulary built from three civilizations, where polysemy radiates from physical cores through conceptual metaphor, and the grammar itself carries meaning

A Vocabulary Built From Three Civilizations

The English vocabulary is not merely large — it is architecturally unique. Where most languages have one primary source for their core vocabulary, English draws from three: Germanic (Anglo-Saxon and Old Norse), French (post-1066 Norman Conquest), and Latin-Greek (Renaissance scholarly borrowing). These three layers did not replace each other. They accumulated, creating a lexicon where nearly every common concept has two or three words available at different formality levels, each carrying distinct connotations, collocational preferences, and social signals.

The Oxford English Dictionary's Second Edition (1989) recorded approximately 171,476 words in current use, plus about 47,000 obsolete words. The current OED has expanded substantially to over 520,779 entries with 888,251 defined senses. But the sheer size matters less than the internal structure: English vocabulary is simultaneously redundant (multiple words for the same concept) and precise (each variant signals a specific registerLevel of formality in language use. English's three-layer lexicon provides systematic register choices: ask (casual), question (neutral-formal), interrogate (formal-technical).). This is not a bug but a feature — and it is the three-layer system that makes it work.

Key Takeaways
  • The three-layer lexicon (Germanic / French / Latin-Greek) provides register-calibrated alternatives for virtually every concept
  • Polysemy is not random — word senses radiate from concrete physical cores through systematic metaphorical extension
  • Conceptual metaphor maps concrete experience onto abstract domains: UNDERSTANDING IS SEEING, TIME IS MONEY, ARGUMENT IS WAR
  • Semantic prosody is invisible grammar — "cause" collocates with negative events ~90% of the time
  • Constructions carry meaning independently of the words that fill them — the grammar itself is a semantic resource

The Three-Layer Register System

Figure 1 — The three-layer lexicon pyramid. Germanic words dominate by frequency (~26% of headwords but the overwhelming majority of tokens); French (~29%) and Latin-Greek (~35%) provide formal and technical alternatives. Register triplets emerge at every level.

Layer 1 — Germanic

Germanic provides the everyday, concrete, emotionally direct vocabulary. These words are typically short (one or two syllables), bear initial stress, and cover the fundamental domains of human experience: body parts (head, hand, heart, gut), basic actions (eat, drink, walk, sleep, run), core emotions (love, hate, fear, anger), kinship (mother, father, brother, sister), and nature (sun, moon, water, earth, fire). By dictionary headwords, Germanic words account for roughly 26% of English vocabulary. But by token frequency — how often words actually appear in text — Germanic words dominate overwhelmingly. The 100 most frequent English words are almost entirely Germanic: the, of, and, to, a, in, is, it, you, that.

Layer 2 — French

French supplies the formal, social, and institutional vocabulary. These words entered English primarily after the Norman Conquest of 1066, when French became the language of the ruling class, the courts, and the church for roughly 300 years. French-origin words tend to be two or three syllables, carry an air of formality or elegance, and dominate specific domains: law (justice, court, judge, jury, prison, crime), government (parliament, authority, sovereign, minister), cuisine (beef, pork, veal, sauce, roast), fashion (garment, costume, tailor), and art (beauty, design, portrait). By headword count, French contributes roughly 29% of English vocabulary.

Layer 3 — Latin and Greek

Latin and Greek furnish the technical, abstract, and scientific vocabulary. These words entered English primarily during the Renaissance (16th–17th centuries), when English began replacing Latin as the language of scholarship and needed technical terms to fill the gap. Latin-Greek words tend to be long (three or more syllables), carry stress-shifting suffixes, and dominate academic and professional domains: medicine (cardiac, gastric, renal, pulmonary), science (hypothesis, phenomenon, taxonomy), philosophy (epistemology, ontology, teleology), and law (jurisprudence, litigation, adjudication). Latin contributes roughly 29% and Greek about 6% of headwords.

These are not perfect synonyms. Each carries different register connotations: "begin" is neutral, "commence" is ceremonial, "initiate" is technical. A skilled English speaker shifts between layers fluidly depending on context, and the Latinate-to-Germanic ratio in a text is a reliable quantitative measure of its formality level. Child-directed speech: overwhelmingly Germanic. Casual conversation: mostly Germanic. Academic writing: heavily Latinate. Legal prose: extremely Latinate.

The Animal-Meat Split

The Norman Table

The most famous illustration of the three-layer system: English-speaking peasants tended the animals using Germanic words (cow, pig, sheep, calf, deer), while French-speaking Norman nobility ate the meat using French words (beef, pork, mutton, veal, venison). The social divide of 1066 is still encoded in the English dinner table.

Polysemy: Radiating Meaning From Physical Cores

Figure 2 — The radial category structure of "head." A core physical meaning at the center, with metaphorical and metonymic extensions radiating outward through systematic mapping patterns.

English words do not have fixed, single meanings. The most common words are massively polysemousHaving multiple related meanings organized around a central prototype. The word "run" has 645 defined senses in the OED. — they carry dozens or hundreds of related senses organized around a central prototype. The word "run" holds the current OED record with 645 defined senses for the verb alone, surpassing "set," which held the record in the OED's 1989 Second Edition with 430+ senses. "Get" has 150+ senses. "Take" has 120+. "Turn" has 100+.

A reliable pattern emerges: short, frequent, Germanic-origin words tend to be the most polysemous, while long, rare, Latinate words tend to be monosemous or oligosemous (having one or few meanings). This is not accidental. High-frequency words serve as semantic workhorses, extending into new domains through metaphorical and metonymic mapping rather than being replaced by specialized terms.

Radial Categories

The senses of a polysemous word are not randomly scattered. They form a radial categoryA polysemous word's senses organized around a core (usually the most concrete or spatial) with extensions radiating outward through metaphor and metonymy. — a structure where a core (usually the most concrete or spatial) meaning sits at the center, and extended meanings radiate outward through systematic mapping. Consider "head":

  • Core: body part (the head on a person)
  • Metonymy (part for whole): person ("counting heads," "per head," "$10 a head")
  • Metaphor (topmost): top/front position ("head of the table," "head of the line")
  • Metaphor (leader): "head of department," "head of state," "figurehead"
  • Metaphor (top layer): foam ("head on a pint")
  • Metaphor (origin/source): "headwaters," "fountainhead"
  • Metaphor (crisis point): "come to a head" (like a boil swelling)
  • Metonymy (location of brain): intellect ("use your head," "good head for numbers")

All extensions connect to the prototype through shared schematic structure: the head is the topmost, leading, most important part of the body. This is not a quirk of "head" — it is how polysemy is organized throughout English. The pattern is systematic enough to be predictive.

Conceptual Metaphor: The Engine of Abstract Meaning

Figure 3 — Eight major conceptual metaphor mappings in English. Each maps a concrete source domain onto an abstract target domain. The mapping is always unidirectional: concrete to abstract, never the reverse.

Behind the polysemy of individual words lies a deeper system: conceptual metaphorA systematic mapping from a concrete source domain to an abstract target domain. Not a poetic device but the primary mechanism for generating abstract vocabulary.. Lakoff and Johnson (1980) demonstrated that English (and human language generally) systematically maps concrete, physical, spatial experience onto abstract domains. These mappings are not poetic flourishes — they are the primary mechanism through which English generates abstract vocabulary.

Core Metaphor Mappings

UNDERSTANDING IS SEEING: "I see what you mean," "a clear explanation," "shed light on," "in view of," "a bright student," "a blind spot." The entire vocabulary of intellectual comprehension is borrowed from vision.

TIME IS MONEY: "Spend time," "waste time," "save time," "invest time," "budget your time," "a costly delay." English treats time as a quantifiable economic resource.

ARGUMENT IS WAR: "Attack a position," "defend a thesis," "shoot down an argument," "a weak point," "under fire." English speakers conceptualize intellectual debate through the vocabulary of combat.

MORE IS UP / GOOD IS UP: "Prices are rising," "high numbers," "her spirits soared," "he fell into depression," "top-notch," "feeling down," "low quality." Vertical space maps onto both quantity and evaluation.

LIFE IS A JOURNEY: "At a crossroads," "a dead end," "making progress," "going in circles," "moving forward," "a milestone."

The Directionality Principle

The mapping is always unidirectional: concrete to abstract, never the reverse. We say "I see your point" (mapping vision onto understanding), but we never say "I understand that sunset" to mean we saw it. Abstract thought is structured by physical experience, not the other way around.

Body-to-Mind Mapping

The body-to-mind mapping is especially pervasive: physical perception maps onto intellectual perception ("I see" = I understand, "That sounds right" = seems correct, "I feel that..." = I believe), physical force maps onto social force ("pressure someone," "push an agenda," "pull strings"), and physical movement maps onto abstract progress ("move forward," "take steps," "make strides," "fall behind").

Collocations and Semantic Prosody: The Invisible Grammar

Figure 4 — Semantic prosody visualized for "cause," "commit," and "provide." The evaluative aura of each verb is determined by the company it habitually keeps — invisible grammar that governs word choice through statistical association.

English words do not combine freely. They have collocationalA habitual word combination that native speakers internalize from exposure: "strong tea" (not "*powerful tea"), "heavy rain" (not "*strong rain"). preferences — habitual co-occurrence patterns that native speakers internalize from exposure but that follow no explicit rule. "Strong tea" is natural; "*powerful tea" is not. "Heavy rain" is normal; "*strong rain" is wrong. "Make a decision" is standard; "*do a decision" is unacceptable.

The partial explanation: "strong" collocates with qualities and intensities (tea, coffee, wind, accent, feelings, argument), while "powerful" collocates with capacities for action (engine, computer, speech, weapon, nation, emotion). But many collocational restrictions are purely conventional — historical usage patterns that have crystallized into norms that must be acquired through exposure rather than learned through rules.

Semantic Prosody

More subtle than collocation is semantic prosodyThe evaluative aura a word acquires from the company it habitually keeps. "Cause" has negative prosody (cause damage, cause problems) making "cause happiness" feel slightly wrong. — the evaluative aura that a word acquires from the company it habitually keeps. The verb "cause" collocates with negative events approximately 90% of the time: cause damage, cause problems, cause suffering, cause death, cause delays. This overwhelming negative association means that even grammatically correct sentences like "The policy caused happiness" feel slightly wrong — the negative prosody of "cause" clashes with the positive outcome. A more natural phrasing would use "brought happiness" or "created happiness."

"Commit" has even stronger negative prosody: commit murder, commit fraud, commit crime, commit suicide, commit atrocities. The verb is almost exclusively associated with morally negative actions, making "*commit kindness" sound jarring. By contrast, "provide" has positive/neutral prosody: provide support, provide assistance, provide evidence, provide resources.

Semantic prosody is invisible grammar — it governs word choice through associative patterns rather than explicit rules. Native speakers "feel" it without being able to articulate it. It is one of the last things second-language learners master, because it requires massive exposure to internalize the statistical patterns.

Constructional Meaning: The Grammar Itself Carries Meaning

Figure 5 — Four English constructions that carry meaning independently of their words: the ditransitive (transfer), caused-motion (X moves Y), resultative (X causes Y to become Z), and way-construction (motion via manner). Based on Goldberg (1995).

Perhaps the most counter-intuitive aspect of English semantics is that syntactic constructionsA form-meaning pairing at the syntactic level. The ditransitive pattern [S V O1 O2] means "X causes Y to receive Z" regardless of which verb fills it. carry meaning independently of the words that fill them. This is the core claim of Construction Grammar (Goldberg, 1995), and it is demonstrated most dramatically by the ditransitive construction.

The Ditransitive Construction

The ditransitive pattern [Subject V Object1 Object2] means "X causes Y to receive Z." When filled with the verb "give" — "She gave him the book" — the transfer meaning seems to come from the verb. But when filled with "bake" — "She baked him a cake" — the transfer meaning cannot come from the verb, because "bake" does not inherently mean "give." The construction itself imposes the transfer interpretation. Even more striking: "She denied him the promotion" uses the construction to express negative transfer — the pattern forces a transfer frame even when the verb negates the transfer.

Caused-Motion and Resultative Constructions

The caused-motion construction [Subject V Object PP-direction] means "X causes Y to move to Z." It can turn intransitive verbs into transitive ones: "She sneezed the napkin off the table." "Sneeze" is normally intransitive — you cannot "*sneeze something." But the caused-motion construction imposes its own meaning, creating a reading where the sneeze caused the napkin to move off the table.

The resultative construction [Subject V Object AdjP] means "X causes Y to become Z": "She hammered the metal flat," "She painted the wall red," "She drank herself silly."

The way-construction [Subject V possessive way PP] creates a motion reading from virtually any verb: "She elbowed her way through the crowd," "He talked his way into the job."

The Pattern-Learner Implication

Learning English grammar is not just learning which words go where. It is learning that the grammatical patterns themselves are meaningful. The ditransitive pattern means "transfer" regardless of which verb fills it. The caused-motion pattern means "X moves Y" regardless of whether the verb normally allows an object. The grammar is a meaning system, not just a word-ordering system.

The Latinate word typically has a narrower, more precise, or more abstract meaning: "understand" covers general comprehension; "comprehend" implies thorough, complete understanding. "Eat" is any ingestion; "consume" is systematic or complete ingestion (and extends to non-food: "consumed by fire").

Legal Doublets

A special historical artifact: pairs of Germanic and French terms used together in English law to ensure comprehension by both language communities under Norman-English bilingual conditions: "aid and abet," "cease and desist," "will and testament," "null and void," "goods and chattels," "breaking and entering." Some even form triplets: "right, title, and interest" / "give, devise, and bequeath."

Sense Relations: The Network of Meaning

English vocabulary is organized into structured networks through systematic sense relations:

Synonymy (same or similar meaning) is rarely exact in English. Near-synonyms differ in register (begin/commence), connotation (slender/thin/skinny), collocation (strong tea/*powerful tea), or scope (broad/wide). The three-layer system provides register-differentiated near-synonyms as a core feature.

Antonymy (opposite meaning) comes in four types: gradable (hot/cold — allow degrees), complementary (dead/alive — binary), relational/converse (buy/sell, teacher/student — imply each other), and directional (up/down, in/out).

HyponymyIS-A relationship creating hierarchical taxonomies: animal > mammal > dog > poodle. (IS-A relationship) creates hierarchical taxonomies: animal > mammal > dog > poodle.

MeronymyPART-OF relationship mapping compositional structure: finger is part of hand, wheel is part of car. (PART-OF relationship) maps compositional structure: finger is part of hand, wheel is part of car.

These relations are not isolated facts but organizing principles: they create the structure within which English vocabulary is mentally stored and accessed.

The Pattern Summary

The Integrated System
  1. The three-layer lexicon provides register-calibrated alternatives for every concept (Germanic / French / Latin-Greek)
  2. Polysemy and conceptual metaphor systematically extend concrete meanings into abstract domains through predictable mappings
  3. Constructions carry meaning beyond the sum of their words, making the grammar itself a semantic resource
  4. Collocational restrictions and semantic prosody form invisible grammar governing word choice through associative patterns
  5. Sense relations (synonymy, antonymy, hyponymy, meronymy) create the structural network within which vocabulary is organized

English semantics is not a formless sea of vocabulary. It is a structured system — architecturally rich, historically layered, and for the pattern-oriented learner, deeply satisfying to analyze.
