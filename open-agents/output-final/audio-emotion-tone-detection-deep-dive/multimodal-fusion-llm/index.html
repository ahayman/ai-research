Multimodal Fusion & LLM Integration

How to combine audio-based emotion signals with LLM text analysis to create emotion-enriched transcriptions. The most directly relevant architecture for transcription apps.

Introduction #

If you are building a transcription app that already has access to both the audio stream and the resulting text, you are in an ideal position to add emotion and tone awareness. Multimodal fusion is the practice of combining signals from multiple modalities—in this case, audio features (pitch, energy, speaking rate) and text content (word choice, syntax, semantics)—to produce a richer, more accurate understanding of the speaker's emotional state.

This page covers the full landscape: from academic fusion architectures to practical prompt engineering patterns you can implement today. The field has advanced rapidly in 2024–2025, with LLM-based approaches making it possible to achieve strong multimodal emotion recognition without modifying the LLM architecture at all—just through clever prompt construction.

Why this matters for your transcription app

A transcription app naturally sits at the intersection of audio and text. You already have both modalities. The question is not whether to combine them, but how. This page gives you the practical blueprints to do exactly that, from the simplest approach (inject audio feature descriptions into LLM prompts) to the most sophisticated (train cross-modal transformers).

Why Multimodal Beats Single-Modality #

Research consistently demonstrates that combining audio and text modalities significantly outperforms either modality alone. The improvements range from 2% to 23% depending on the specific task, dataset, and fusion strategy used.

Consider the sentence "That's just great." Text-only analysis might classify this as positive. But the audio might reveal a flat, low-energy delivery with falling intonation—classic sarcasm indicators. Only a multimodal system catches this discrepancy.

Text Alone

Captures what is said. Misses tone, sarcasm, hesitation, emphasis. Good baseline but blind to paralinguistic cues.

Accuracy range
60–78% on emotional text benchmarks

Audio Alone

Captures how it's said. Misses semantic context. Can confuse excitement with anger (similar acoustic profiles).

Accuracy range
65–80% on speech emotion benchmarks

Audio + Text Combined

Captures both dimensions. Resolves ambiguity through complementary signals. Handles sarcasm, irony, and mixed emotions.

Accuracy range
78–93%+ on multimodal benchmarks

The largest gains come when modalities provide complementary information. Audio adds the most value when text is emotionally ambiguous, and text adds the most value when audio quality is poor or the speaker is monotone.

Key Takeaway: The Three-Tier Approach #

Practical recommendation

For a transcription app, you don't need to implement complex neural fusion from day one. Here is a phased approach, from simplest to most sophisticated:

1

Tier 1: LLM Prompt Injection (Days to implement)

Extract basic audio features (pitch, energy, speaking rate) per utterance. Convert them to natural language descriptions. Inject into LLM prompts alongside the transcribed text. This is the SpeechCueLLM approach and requires zero model training.

2

Tier 2: Parallel Classification + LLM Fusion (Weeks to implement)

Run a dedicated audio emotion classifier (e.g., emotion2vec or a fine-tuned HuBERT model) in parallel with text transcription. Feed both the emotion predictions and the text to an LLM for post-hoc fusion and refinement—the EmoJudge approach.

3

Tier 3: Trained Cross-Modal Transformer (Months to implement)

Build a MemoCMT/WavFusion-style architecture with cross-modal attention between audio and text encoders. Highest accuracy but requires training data, GPU resources, and ML engineering expertise.

Early Fusion (Feature-Level) #

Early fusion concatenates features from both modalities into a single vector before any classification happens. Audio features (MFCCs, pitch contours, energy) are combined with text embeddings (from BERT, RoBERTa, or similar) and fed to a unified classifier.

  • When to use: When you have well-aligned, time-synchronized audio and text data and want to capture fine-grained cross-modal interactions.
  • Pros: Can learn subtle interactions between how something sounds and what words are used. Single model to optimize.
  • Cons: Struggles with heterogeneous feature distributions (audio features and text embeddings live in very different spaces). Requires careful temporal alignment. Fails gracefully is difficult—needs both modalities present.
  • Practical complexity: Moderate. Requires feature engineering and alignment.

Late Fusion (Decision-Level) #

Late fusion runs separate models for each modality and combines their predictions. An audio emotion classifier produces one prediction; a text sentiment model produces another; they are merged via voting, averaging, or a learned weighting function.

  • When to use: Production systems, rapid prototyping, or when you want modularity and graceful degradation (system still works if one modality is unavailable).
  • Pros: Each model can be optimized independently. Easy to add/remove modalities. Robust to missing data. Simplest to implement and debug.
  • Cons: Misses cross-modal dependencies. Cannot capture cases where the combination of audio and text conveys meaning that neither conveys alone (e.g., sarcasm).
  • Practical complexity: Low. Just run two models and combine outputs.
  • Example: EmoJudge uses late fusion with logistic regression, then refines with an LLM post-hoc step.

Hybrid Fusion (Multi-Stage Interaction) #

Hybrid fusion allows modalities to interact at intermediate stages through mechanisms like cross-modal attentionA mechanism where features from one modality (e.g., audio) serve as queries to attend to features from another modality (e.g., text), learning which aspects of each modality are most relevant to the other., adaptive weighting, and dynamic gating. This captures both local and global cross-modal dependencies.

  • When to use: When maximum accuracy matters and you have the engineering resources to train and maintain a custom model.
  • Pros: Highest accuracy. Captures nuanced interactions between modalities. Can learn modality-specific and shared representations simultaneously.
  • Cons: Most complex to implement. Requires training data and compute. Harder to debug and interpret.
  • Examples: MemoCMT (cross-modal transformer), WavFusion (gated cross-modal attention), EGMF (expert-guided with dynamic gating).

Key Research Models #

The following models represent the state of the art in multimodal emotion recognition. Understanding their architectures will inform your design decisions, even if you don't implement them directly.

MemoCMT: Cross-Modal Transformer #

MemoCMT — Scientific Reports (Nature), February 2025

Open Source
HuBERT (audio) BERT (text) 81.33% UW-Acc IEMOCAP 91.93% UW-Acc ESD

MemoCMT uses a cross-modal transformer (CMT) to fuse audio and text representations. HuBERT extracts audio features while BERT processes text. The CMT applies cross-attention to align and integrate both modalities, then uses aggregation techniques (CLS, MEAN, MIN, MAX) to produce a final representation.

The MIN aggregation variant achieves the highest accuracy, improving over unimodal baselines by 5.70–17.83%. The model is open source on GitHub.

Key insight: Cross-modal attention lets the model learn which parts of the audio are most relevant to which parts of the text—for instance, a rising pitch on a specific word can change its emotional interpretation.

WavFusion: Gated Cross-Modal Attention #

WavFusion — Published December 2024

SOTA on IEMOCAP & MELD
wav2vec 2.0 (audio) RoBERTa (text)

WavFusion builds on wav2vec 2.0 for audio and RoBERTa for text. Its key innovation is a gated cross-modal attention mechanism that uses parallel cross-attention pathways followed by a dynamic gating network.

The gate performs context-aware, channel-wise filtering: it adaptively suppresses irrelevant information from one modality while amplifying salient signals from the other. This prevents the common problem of one modality dominating or introducing noise.

Key insight: Not all audio features are equally useful at all times. The gating mechanism learns to trust audio signals more when audio quality is high and the text is ambiguous, and vice versa.

Emotion-LLaMA: LLM-Native Emotion #

Emotion-LLaMA — NeurIPS 2024

Trimodal LLM
HuBERT (audio) EVA + MAE + VideoMAE (visual) LLaMA2-chat 7B

Emotion-LLaMA represents the frontier of LLM-native multimodal emotion recognition. It processes audio (via HuBERT), visual (via EVA/MAE/VideoMAE), and text inputs, projecting all features into a shared 4096-dimensional space before feeding them to a LLaMA2-chat model.

The model uses LoRA for parameter-efficient tuning and performs multi-task learning: simultaneously recognizing emotions and generating emotional reasoning (explaining why it classified a given emotion).

Key insight: By embedding emotion features directly into the LLM's token space, the model can both classify emotions and generate natural language explanations—moving beyond simple labels to nuanced descriptions.

EGMF: Expert-Guided Multimodal Fusion #

EGMF Framework — Published January 2025

Cross-Lingual
3 Expert Networks Hierarchical Dynamic Gating LLM Integration via LoRA

EGMF introduces three specialized expert networks, each handling a different aspect of emotional signals:

  1. Fine-grained local expert: Captures subtle emotional nuances within short segments (a tremor in the voice, a particular word emphasis).
  2. Semantic correlation expert: Models cross-modal relationships (how word choice interacts with vocal delivery).
  3. Global context expert: Handles long-range dependencies (emotional arc across a conversation).

These experts are adaptively integrated through hierarchical dynamic gating. The fused representations are then injected into an LLM via pseudo token injection and prompt-based conditioning, enabling both classification and open-vocabulary emotion generation through a single framework.

Key insight: Different emotional signals operate at different scales (word-level, utterance-level, conversation-level). Separating experts by scale then adaptively combining them outperforms one-size-fits-all approaches.

SpeechCueLLM: Audio Features as Text Prompts #

SpeechCueLLM is the approach most directly applicable to a transcription app. It translates audio characteristics into natural language descriptions and includes them in the LLM prompt, enabling multimodal analysis without any architectural changes to the LLM.

How It Works

  1. Extract five key audio features: average volume, volume variation, average pitch, pitch variation, speaking rate
  2. Convert each to a natural language description (e.g., "Speaker has above-average pitch with high variation")
  3. Construct a four-part prompt: Instruction + Context + Speech Descriptions + Question
  4. Feed to any LLM (no fine-tuning required, though fine-tuning improves results)
Why this matters for your app

SpeechCueLLM is the lowest-friction path to multimodal emotion detection. If your transcription app already calls an LLM for any post-processing (summarization, formatting, etc.), you can add emotion awareness by simply enriching your prompts with audio feature descriptions. The marginal engineering effort is minimal.

On IEMOCAP and MELD benchmarks, SpeechCueLLM outperforms models that require structural modifications to the LLM, demonstrating that clever prompt engineering can rival architectural changes.

Feature-to-Text Conversion: The distilRoBERTa Approach #

This approach takes the SpeechCueLLM idea further: instead of just describing audio features in prompts, it uses rule-based systems to convert all non-verbal audio cues into structured text, then fine-tunes a language model (distilRoBERTa) on the combined textual representation.

Features Converted to Text

  • Loudness: "Speaker volume is 85th percentile, with sudden increase at segment 3"
  • Spectral flux: "High spectral variability indicating emotional instability"
  • MFCCs: "Vocal tract configuration suggests tension in the upper register"
  • Pitch stability: "Pitch varies by ±40Hz around 220Hz baseline"
  • Emphasis patterns: "Stress on words 2 and 5 of the utterance"

This approach achieved 93.18% accuracy on RAVDESS and 93.69% on BAUM-1, performing on par with state-of-the-art multimodal architectures that use dedicated audio encoders.

Key insight

The textualization principle is powerful: any modality can be converted to text for LLM consumption. This means you can leverage the full reasoning capabilities of LLMs without building custom multimodal architectures. The rule-based conversion acts as a bridge between the audio world and the text world.

EmoJudge: LLM Post-Hoc Refinement #

EmoJudge, presented at Interspeech 2025, takes a different angle: instead of fusing modalities at the input level, it runs independent audio and text classifiers first, then uses an LLM to adjudicate when predictions conflict.

Architecture

  • Audio path: WavLM-Large + attentive pooling + residual networks
  • Text path: RoBERTa-Large for linguistic analysis
  • Late fusion: Logistic regression combines initial predictions
  • LLM refinement: Transcripts + speaker indicators + audio descriptions are fed to an LLM for post-hoc correction of conflicting predictions
When to use EmoJudge-style refinement

This approach is ideal when you already have separate audio and text emotion classifiers and want to add a "smart arbitrator" that resolves disagreements. The LLM acts as a judge, considering the full context (transcript, speaker identity, audio characteristics) to make a final determination.

VowelPrompt: Linguistically-Grounded Prosody #

VowelPrompt (2025) takes a more linguistically grounded approach. Instead of extracting generic audio features, it focuses specifically on vowel-level prosodic cues—the sounds that carry the most emotional information in speech.

  • Extracts pitch, energy, and duration descriptors from time-aligned vowel segments
  • Converts prosodic features to natural language descriptions
  • Uses a two-stage adaptation: supervised fine-tuning (SFT) + reinforcement learning with verifiable reward (RLVR)
  • Outperforms SOTA methods across zero-shot, fine-tuned, cross-domain, and cross-linguistic conditions
  • Generates interpretable explanations grounded in both semantics and prosody

The key linguistic insight: vowels function as the primary carriers of intonation contours and emotional coloring. They have sustained voicing and spectral stability, making them ideal targets for prosodic analysis.

Open-Vocabulary Emotion Recognition #

Traditional emotion recognition classifies into fixed categories (happy, sad, angry, neutral, etc.). Open-vocabulary emotion recognition treats the task as generative: produce flexible, descriptive natural language that captures the full spectrum of emotional states.

The MER2025 challenge (theme: "When Affective Computing Meets Large Language Models") is driving this shift. Instead of classifying "angry," the system might output:

"The speaker exhibits controlled frustration—careful word choice suggests they are
trying to remain professional, but elevated pitch and compressed dynamic range
on key words reveal underlying irritation. The emotion is layered: frustrated
on the surface, with a deeper sense of disappointment."
For your transcription app

Open-vocabulary emotion recognition is a natural fit. Instead of appending a simple emotion label to each transcription segment, you can generate nuanced emotional context that actually helps the reader understand the speaker's state. LLMs are inherently suited to this generative approach.

Processing Pipeline Breakdown

1

Audio Input & Segmentation

Capture audio stream from microphone or load from file. Use Voice Activity Detection (VAD) to identify speech segments and silence. Segment audio by utterance boundaries (natural pauses >300ms).

2

Parallel Processing (Transcription + Audio Analysis)

Run ASR (Whisper, Deepgram) to get timestamped text with word-level alignments. Simultaneously extract audio features per utterance: fundamental frequency (F0), RMS energy, speaking rate (words/second), pause duration. Optionally run a lightweight SER model for initial emotion predictions.

3

Temporal Alignment

Use ASR-provided timestamps to align audio features with text segments. Compute per-segment statistics: mean pitch, pitch range, mean energy, energy variation, words per second. Calculate relative metrics: compare each segment to the speaker's running average.

4

Feature-to-Text Conversion

Convert numeric audio features to natural language descriptions using rule-based templates. Example: pitch=280Hz, speaker_avg=200Hz, variation=high becomes "Speaker's pitch is significantly elevated (+40% above their baseline) with high variability, suggesting emotional arousal."

5

LLM Fusion & Output

Assemble prompt with system instructions, transcribed text, and audio feature descriptions. Send to LLM (GPT-4, Claude). Parse structured output: per-segment emotion labels, confidence scores, and natural language emotional context. Optionally generate a conversation-level emotion summary.

Temporal Alignment Strategies #

Aligning audio features with text segments is critical for accurate fusion. Here are the practical options, from simplest to most precise:

Strategy Granularity Complexity Accuracy Requirements
Utterance-level Sentence / turn Low Good VAD + utterance boundary detection
Word-level (ASR timestamps) Per word Medium High ASR with word-level timestamps (Whisper, Deepgram)
Forced alignment Per phoneme High Highest Forced alignment tool (Montreal, gentle)
Sliding window Fixed time window Low Moderate None (just divide audio into equal segments)
Recommendation

For most transcription apps, utterance-level alignment is the best starting point. Most ASR engines already provide sentence-level timestamps, and emotion features aggregated over a full utterance are sufficient for meaningful analysis. Move to word-level alignment only if you need to identify exactly which words triggered an emotional shift.

Advanced Prompt Strategies #

1. Relative vs. Absolute Audio Features

Always provide audio features relative to the speaker's baseline. "Pitch is 250Hz" is meaningless without context. "Pitch is 25% above this speaker's average" is immediately informative. Maintain running averages per speaker and compute deviations for each segment.

2. Audio-Text Disagreement Detection

Explicitly instruct the LLM to flag when audio and text signals conflict. This is where the most interesting emotional insights lie: sarcasm (positive words + negative audio), emotional masking (calm text + agitated audio), and social performance (enthusiastic text + flat audio).

3. Emotional Arc Tracking

Include the emotional history of the conversation in the prompt. This allows the LLM to detect trends (escalating frustration, gradual warming) and identify turning points. A sudden emotional shift is more significant if it breaks a pattern.

4. Chain-of-Thought for Emotion

For more accurate analysis, ask the LLM to reason step by step:

Think through the emotion analysis step by step:

Step 1: What emotion does the TEXT content suggest? Why?
Step 2: What emotion do the VOCAL features suggest? Why?
Step 3: Do they agree or disagree? If they disagree, what
  might explain the discrepancy?
Step 4: Considering the conversation context, what is the most
  likely true emotional state?
Step 5: Assign your final labels and confidence scores.

5. Batch Processing for Efficiency

Rather than analyzing one segment at a time, batch multiple segments in a single prompt. This reduces LLM calls and provides natural context. Process 5–10 segments at once for optimal balance of context and latency.

Cross-Modal Attention Mechanisms #

For those interested in building more sophisticated fusion systems (Tier 3), understanding cross-modal attention is essential. This is the mechanism that enables models like MemoCMT and WavFusion to learn which parts of the audio are most relevant to which parts of the text.

Text tokens serve as queries, attending to audio features (keys/values). The attention weights learn which audio moments are most relevant to each text token. For instance, the pitch spike on "really" and the emphasis on "don't" receive high attention weights.

How Cross-Modal Attention Works

  1. Queries from text: Each text token's embedding becomes a query vector, representing "what emotional information am I looking for?"
  2. Keys & Values from audio: Each audio frame's features become key-value pairs, representing "what emotional information do I carry?"
  3. Attention weights: The dot product of queries and keys produces weights that indicate which audio frames are most relevant to each text token.
  4. Weighted sum: Audio values are weighted and summed to produce a fused representation for each text token, enriched with the most relevant audio information.

Dynamic Modality Weighting

Advanced systems like WavFusion add a gating mechanism on top of cross-modal attention. A Modality Importance Estimator predicts dynamic, instance-level confidence weights for each modality at each timestep. This allows the system to:

  • Trust audio more when it carries strong emotional signals (crying, laughing, shouting)
  • Trust text more when audio quality is poor or the speaker is monotone
  • Suppress noisy or unreliable signals automatically
  • Adapt per-utterance rather than using fixed weights

Glossary #

Term Definition
ASRAutomatic Speech Recognition. Converting spoken audio into text (e.g., Whisper, Deepgram).
Cross-modal attentionMechanism where features from one modality (e.g., audio) attend to features from another modality (e.g., text) to learn relevant correspondences.
DiarizationSpeaker diarization identifies "who spoke when" in a multi-speaker audio stream.
Early fusionCombining raw features from multiple modalities before classification into a single feature vector.
F0 / Fundamental frequencyThe lowest frequency of a periodic waveform. In speech, perceived as pitch. Measured in Hertz (Hz).
Feature-to-textConverting numeric audio features into natural language descriptions for LLM consumption.
HuBERTHidden-Unit BERT. A self-supervised speech representation model used as an audio encoder in many SER systems.
Hybrid fusionCombining early and late fusion with intermediate cross-modal interactions at multiple processing stages.
Late fusionRunning separate classifiers per modality and combining their predictions through voting or learned weighting.
LoRALow-Rank Adaptation. A parameter-efficient fine-tuning method that trains small adapter matrices instead of all model parameters.
MFCCsMel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients. Features representing the short-term power spectrum of audio, widely used in speech processing.
Multimodal fusionCombining information from multiple sensory modalities (e.g., audio + text) to improve analysis accuracy.
OV-MEROpen-Vocabulary Multimodal Emotion Recognition. Generating free-text emotion descriptions instead of classifying into fixed categories.
RMSRoot Mean Square. A measure of the average power/loudness of an audio signal.
SERSpeech Emotion Recognition. The task of detecting emotional states from spoken audio.
VADVoice Activity Detection. Identifying which parts of an audio stream contain speech vs. silence.
wav2vec 2.0A self-supervised speech representation model by Meta. Learns speech representations from unlabeled audio.
