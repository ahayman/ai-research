Acoustic Feature Engineering & Signal Processing

How raw audio waveforms are transformed into emotion-revealing numerical representations through mathematical signal processing, perceptual modeling, and carefully engineered feature extraction.

Foundation Layer MFCCs • Prosody • Spectral • Voice Quality ~15 min read

🎧 Overview

Every audio emotion detection system begins here: converting a continuous pressure wave captured by a microphone into a structured set of numbers that reveal the speaker's emotional state. This process, called acoustic feature engineering, is the single most important step in the entire pipeline. No amount of model sophistication can recover emotion information that was lost during feature extraction.

The human voice encodes emotion through dozens of simultaneously varying acoustic properties: the pitch rises and falls, the voice trembles or stabilizes, the speaking pace quickens or slows, the timbre brightens or darkens. Feature engineering is the art and science of capturing these variations as measurable quantities.

💡 Why This Matters

All downstream components in the emotion detection pipeline (ML classifiers, deep learning models, commercial APIs) ultimately depend on the quality of acoustic features. Understanding these features is essential for debugging, improving accuracy, choosing the right model architecture, and building practical real-time systems on platforms like iOS.

Pre-emphasis

The human voice concentrates most of its energy in lower frequencies (below 1 kHz). Higher frequencies, which carry critical information for distinguishing consonants and detecting emotion-related timbral changes, are naturally weaker. Pre-emphasis applies a simple first-order high-pass filter to boost these higher frequencies.

Pre-emphasis filter y[n] = x[n] - α · x[n-1],   where α = 0.95 to 0.97

This is computationally trivial (a single subtraction per sample) but significantly improves the signal-to-noise ratio for high-frequency components and produces a flatter spectral envelope, making downstream analysis more numerically stable.

Framing & Windowing

Speech is a non-stationary signal: its properties change over time as the speaker articulates different sounds. However, over very short time intervals (20-30 milliseconds), the vocal tract shape remains approximately constant, making the signal quasi-stationary A signal that changes over time but can be considered approximately constant (stationary) within short time windows, enabling frequency-domain analysis techniques that assume stationarity. . This motivates splitting the audio into short overlapping frames.

Frame
25ms windows at 16kHz = 400 samples per frame
Overlap
10ms hop = 60% overlap between consecutive frames
Window
Hamming window tapers edges to zero, reducing spectral leakage
Result
100 frames/sec, each ready for FFT analysis
Hamming Window Function w[n] = 0.54 - 0.46 · cos(2πn / (N-1)),   0 ≤ n ≤ N-1

Without windowing, the abrupt boundaries of each frame create high-frequency artifacts in the frequency domain (spectral leakage). The Hamming window smoothly tapers the frame edges to zero, producing a cleaner spectrum at the cost of slightly reduced frequency resolution.

Normalization

After features are extracted, normalization removes unwanted variability caused by recording conditions, microphone characteristics, background noise, and speaker volume differences.

Method Formula Purpose
Mean Normalization x' = x - mean(x) Remove DC offset / channel bias
Z-score (CMVN) x' = (x - mean) / std Standardize mean and variance
Min-Max x' = (x - min) / (max - min) Scale to [0, 1] range
Per-utterance CMN x' = x - utterance_mean Reduce speaker/channel effects within an utterance
⚠️ Normalization Pitfall

In real-time systems, you cannot compute utterance-level statistics (mean, std) until the utterance is complete. Solutions include using a running mean/variance estimate updated with each new frame, or normalizing against a speaker-specific baseline captured during calibration.

🎼 Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs)

MFCCs are the single most important and widely used feature in speech and audio processing. They model the human auditory system's frequency perception to create a compact representation of the vocal tract shape, which encodes both the identity and emotional state of the speaker.

The Mel Scale

Human pitch perception is not linear: we can easily distinguish between 200 Hz and 300 Hz, but 5200 Hz and 5300 Hz sound nearly identical. The mel scale A perceptual scale of pitches where equal distances correspond to equal perceived pitch intervals. Below ~1000 Hz it is approximately linear; above ~1000 Hz it is approximately logarithmic. Named from the word "melody." transforms frequency to match this nonlinear perception.

Hz to Mel Conversion (O'Shaughnessy, 1987) m = 2595 · log₁₀(1 + f/700)
Mel to Hz (Inverse) f = 700 · (10m/2595 - 1)

Key property: below ~1000 Hz, the mel scale is approximately linear (equal spacing in Hz = equal spacing in mels). Above ~1000 Hz, it becomes approximately logarithmic (each doubling of frequency corresponds to a roughly equal perceptual increase). This mirrors the cochlea's tonotopic organization.

Step-by-Step Breakdown

1. Pre-emphasis, Framing, Windowing (covered above): Prepare overlapping, windowed frames.

2. FFT (Fast Fourier Transform): Convert each time-domain frame to its frequency representation. The result is a complex-valued spectrum; we take the squared magnitude to get the power spectrum.

3. Mel Filterbank: Apply 26-40 overlapping triangular bandpass filters whose center frequencies are equally spaced on the mel scale. Each filter sums the energy in its frequency band, producing one number per filter. Filters are narrower at low frequencies (higher resolution where human perception is sharper) and wider at high frequencies.

4. Log Compression: Take the natural logarithm of each filterbank energy. This models human loudness perception (Weber-Fechner law: perceived loudness is proportional to log of intensity).

5. DCT (Discrete Cosine Transform): Apply the DCT Discrete Cosine Transform: A Fourier-related transform that uses only cosine functions. It decorrelates the mel-log energies and compacts most of the information into the first few coefficients, providing dimensionality reduction. to the log mel energies. This decorrelates the features (adjacent mel bands are correlated because of filter overlap) and compacts the information: the first few DCT coefficients capture most of the variance. We keep only 12-13 coefficients, discarding coefficient 0 (which represents overall energy and is often replaced by log energy).

💡 Why MFCCs Capture Emotion

The vocal tract changes shape with emotional state: muscle tension increases during anger, the larynx shifts position during sadness, breathing patterns change during fear. MFCCs capture the spectral envelope (which is determined by vocal tract shape), so they naturally encode these emotion-driven changes. The mel scale ensures we emphasize the frequency ranges most important for human perception of speech and emotion.

Pseudocode 
function extractMFCC(signal, sampleRate=16000):
    // Step 1: Pre-emphasis
    emphasized = [signal[0]]
    for n in 1..length(signal):
        emphasized[n] = signal[n] - 0.97 * signal[n-1]

    // Step 2: Framing (25ms frame, 10ms hop)
    frameSize  = round(0.025 * sampleRate)   // 400 samples
    hopSize    = round(0.010 * sampleRate)   // 160 samples
    frames     = splitIntoFrames(emphasized, frameSize, hopSize)

    // Step 3: Windowing
    window = hammingWindow(frameSize)
    for each frame in frames:
        frame = frame * window

    // Step 4: FFT + Power Spectrum
    nfft = nextPowerOf2(frameSize)           // 512
    for each frame in frames:
        spectrum   = FFT(frame, nfft)
        powerSpec  = |spectrum|^2 / nfft

    // Step 5: Mel Filterbank (26 filters)
    melFilters = createMelFilterbank(26, nfft, sampleRate)
    for each powerSpec:
        melEnergy = melFilters * powerSpec   // matrix multiply

    // Step 6: Log compression
    logMelEnergy = log(melEnergy + 1e-10)    // epsilon for stability

    // Step 7: DCT and keep 12 coefficients
    mfcc = DCT(logMelEnergy)[1:13]           // skip c0

    return mfcc  // shape: (numFrames, 12)

Delta and Delta-Delta (Acceleration) Features

Static MFCCs capture a snapshot of the spectrum at each frame. But emotion is encoded not just in what the voice sounds like at any instant, but how it changes over time. Delta features capture this temporal dynamics.

Delta (Velocity) Coefficient dt = ∑n=1..N n · (ct+n - ct-n) / (2 · ∑n=1..N n²)

Where dt is the delta at frame t, c are the static MFCCs, and N is typically 2.

Delta-delta (acceleration) coefficients apply the same formula to the delta coefficients, capturing the rate of change of the rate of change.

Feature TypeCountWhat It CapturesExample
Static MFCCs13Spectral shape at each instantVocal tract configuration during a vowel
Delta (velocity)13How spectrum is changingTransition from one sound to the next
Delta-delta (acceleration)13How rate of change is changingAbrupt vs. gradual timbral shifts
Total39Standard MFCC feature vector per frame
✅ Practical Tip

For emotion recognition, delta and delta-delta features are especially important. Emotional speech is characterized by dynamic patterns (pitch swoops, energy bursts, abrupt pauses) that static features miss. Including deltas typically improves emotion classification accuracy by 5-15%.

📈 Prosodic Features

Prosody The patterns of rhythm, stress, and intonation in speech. Prosodic features are "suprasegmental" because they span multiple speech segments (syllables, words) rather than individual phonemes. They are the primary acoustic carrier of emotional information. refers to the musical qualities of speech: its melody (pitch), loudness, timing, and rhythm. These suprasegmental features are the primary acoustic carriers of emotional information and are among the first cues humans use to detect a speaker's emotional state.

Fundamental Frequency (F0 / Pitch)

F0 is the rate at which the vocal folds vibrate, perceived as pitch. It is the single most studied feature in speech emotion recognition.

PropertyDetails
Typical range80-250 Hz (male), 150-350 Hz (female)
Extraction methodsAutocorrelation, YIN, RAPT, SWIPE, pYIN
Statistics usedMean, median, std dev, min, max, range, slope, contour shape
Perceptual unitSemitones (logarithmic) — better models perceived pitch changes

F0 and Emotion

  • Anger Higher mean F0, wider range, faster pitch changes
  • Happiness Higher mean F0, wider range (acoustically similar to anger)
  • Sadness Lower mean F0, narrowest range (~90 Hz mean range), flatter contour
  • Fear Higher mean F0, high variability, irregular contour
  • Surprise Highest F0 values overall, sharp upward contours
  • Neutral Mid-range F0, moderate variability
⚠️ Challenge: Anger vs. Happiness

Anger and happiness share many F0 characteristics (both are high-arousal emotions with elevated pitch and energy). Separating them requires additional features like spectral tilt, voice quality measures, and speaking rate patterns.

Energy & Intensity

Energy (or intensity) measures the loudness of the speech signal. It is computed as the root-mean-square (RMS) amplitude or the sum of squared magnitudes within each frame.

RMS Energy E = √( (1/N) · ∑n=0..N-1 x[n]² )

High-arousal emotions (anger, happiness, fear) tend to produce higher overall energy and greater energy variability. Sadness and boredom are characterized by lower energy and flatter energy contours. Energy in specific frequency bands (e.g., above 500 Hz) can further distinguish emotions; anger produces disproportionately more high-frequency energy.

Speaking Rate & Rhythm

Speaking rate is measured as syllables or phonemes per unit time. Rhythm captures the patterns of stressed and unstressed syllables, duration variation, and regularity.

  • High-arousal emotions (anger, fear, happiness): Faster speaking rate, shorter pauses, more rhythmic energy
  • Low-arousal emotions (sadness, boredom): Slower rate, longer pauses, less rhythmic variation
  • Measurement: Voice Activity Detection (VAD) + syllable nuclei detection

🌈 Spectral Features

Spectral features describe how energy is distributed across frequencies at each moment. They complement MFCCs by providing interpretable, low-dimensional measures of the voice's timbral qualities.

Spectral Centroid

The "center of gravity" of the spectrum. Higher values indicate a brighter sound. Anger and excitement produce higher centroids; sadness is lower.

SC = ∑(f · |X(f)|) / ∑|X(f)|

Spectral Flux

Rate of change of the spectrum between frames. High flux indicates rapid timbral changes common in agitated or emotionally intense speech.

SF = ∑(|Xt(k)| - |Xt-1(k)|)²

Spectral Rolloff

The frequency below which 85-95% of total spectral energy lies. Indicates where energy is concentrated; higher rolloff in energetic, bright emotions.

Spectral Bandwidth

Width of the spectrum around the centroid. Tonal sounds have narrow bandwidth; noisy or emotionally aroused speech has wider bandwidth.

Zero Crossing Rate

How often the signal crosses zero. A simple time-domain proxy for spectral content: higher ZCR correlates with more high-frequency energy and noisier speech.

Spectral Slope / Tilt

Slope of the spectrum from low to high frequencies. Differentiates breathy (steep slope) from pressed (flat slope) voice quality. Helps separate anger from happiness.

🔤 Formant Frequencies

Formants Resonance frequencies of the vocal tract. They appear as peaks in the spectral envelope and are determined by the shape, length, and configuration of the mouth, throat, and nasal cavities. Numbered F1 (lowest) through F4+ (highest). are the resonance frequencies of the vocal tract, appearing as prominent peaks in the spectral envelope. They are determined by the physical shape and configuration of the mouth, throat, and nasal cavities.

FormantFrequency RangeControlled ByEmotion Relevance
F1 250-900 Hz Jaw openness / tongue height Tends to rise under stress and anger due to increased jaw opening
F2 700-2500 Hz Tongue front-back position Shifts with articulation changes under emotional arousal
F3 1500-3500 Hz Lip rounding, rhoticity Less studied for emotion but contributes to speaker's timbre
F4 3000-4500 Hz Larynx height, vocal tract length Shifts when larynx raises (tension, stress) or lowers (relaxation)

Formant extraction typically uses Linear Predictive Coding (LPC) A method that models the vocal tract as an all-pole digital filter. By fitting an autoregressive model to the speech signal, LPC estimates the spectral envelope from which formant frequencies can be identified as peaks. , which models the vocal tract as an all-pole digital filter. The LPC coefficients define the spectral envelope, and formants are identified as the peaks (poles) of this envelope.

💡 Formant Bandwidth

The bandwidth of each formant is also emotionally informative. Narrower formant bandwidths (sharper peaks) are associated with tense, pressed voice quality (anger, stress), while wider bandwidths indicate breathier, more relaxed voice quality (calm, sadness).

🗪 Voice Quality Measures

Voice quality features measure micro-perturbations in the periodicity and amplitude of the vocal fold vibration. These subtle variations are invisible on a spectrogram but carry significant emotional information.

Jitter (Frequency Perturbation)

Jitter measures the cycle-to-cycle variation in fundamental frequency period length. A perfectly periodic voice would have zero jitter; real voices always show some perturbation, which increases under stress, fatigue, or emotional intensity.

Local Jitter Jitter(%) = (1/(N-1)) · ∑|Ti - Ti+1| / ((1/N) · ∑ Ti) × 100

Where Ti is the length of the i-th pitch period. Normal range: < 1% for healthy adult voices.

Shimmer (Amplitude Perturbation)

Shimmer measures cycle-to-cycle variation in peak amplitude. Higher shimmer indicates breathier, less stable voice production.

Local Shimmer Shimmer(%) = (1/(N-1)) · ∑|Ai - Ai+1| / ((1/N) · ∑ Ai) × 100

Normal range: < 3-5% for healthy voices. High shimmer often correlates with low HNR.

Harmonics-to-Noise Ratio (HNR)

HNR Harmonics-to-Noise Ratio: The ratio of periodic (harmonic) energy to aperiodic (noise) energy in the voice signal, measured in decibels. Higher HNR indicates a cleaner, more periodic voice; lower HNR indicates breathiness, hoarseness, or vocal strain. measures the ratio of periodic (harmonic) energy to aperiodic (noise) energy in the voice signal.

MeasureWhat It DetectsNormal RangeEmotion Pattern
Jitter Pitch period irregularity < 1% Increases with stress, anger, fear, crying
Shimmer Amplitude irregularity < 3-5% Increases with breathiness, emotional intensity
HNR Harmonic clarity 15-20+ dB Decreases with stress, sadness, crying (breathier voice)
CPP Cepstral peak prominence Varies More robust measure of voice quality across recording conditions
⚠️ Limitations of Voice Quality Measures

Jitter, shimmer, and HNR require accurate pitch tracking, which can be unreliable in noisy environments or during highly emotional speech (where the voice itself becomes irregular). These features are also less stable in spontaneous speech compared to controlled laboratory recordings, producing greater variability in real-world applications.

⏲ Temporal Features

Temporal features capture the timing structure of speech: when the speaker speaks, when they pause, and how these patterns relate to emotional state.

Pause Patterns

  • Pause duration: Mean, median, and maximum silence duration between speech segments
  • Pause frequency: Number of pauses per minute of speech
  • Pause regularity: Variance in pause duration (regular vs. erratic pausing)

Emotion Correlates

  • Sadness More pauses, longer pauses, higher pause-to-speech ratio
  • Happiness Fewer pauses, shorter pauses, more continuous speech
  • Anger Fewer pauses, shorter inter-word silence, forceful onsets
  • Fear Variable pause patterns, hesitations, filled pauses ("um", "uh")

Speech-to-Silence Ratio

The ratio of voiced time to total recording time. Active, high-arousal emotions produce more speech and less silence; passive emotions produce more silence. Depression severity has been shown to correlate with shorter, less variable pauses.

Voice Activity Detection (VAD)

VAD segments audio into speech and silence regions. Methods range from simple energy thresholds to sophisticated ML-based detectors. The quality of VAD directly affects the accuracy of temporal features.

💡 Key Insight: Arousal vs. Valence

Acoustic features are much better at discriminating arousal (activation level: calm vs. excited) than valence (positive vs. negative). Pitch, energy, and speaking rate strongly correlate with arousal. Valence is harder to detect acoustically because anger (negative, high arousal) and happiness (positive, high arousal) share similar acoustic profiles. Separating valence often requires additional features like voice quality, spectral tilt, or linguistic content.

Architecture Key Points

  • Ring buffer: Continuously stores incoming audio samples; frames are extracted from the buffer without copying the entire signal
  • Parallel extraction: Once the FFT is computed, MFCCs, spectral features, and pitch can be extracted in parallel from the same power spectrum
  • Feature buffer: Accumulates ~2 seconds of frame-level features before computing utterance-level statistics
  • Online normalization: Running mean and variance are updated with each new frame (exponential moving average) rather than waiting for the complete utterance
  • Pipeline parallelism: CPU processes the current frame while the ML model (on GPU/ANE) processes the previous feature vector
Pseudocode 
class RealtimeFeatureExtractor:
    ringBuffer:    CircularBuffer(capacity: 16000 * 5)  // 5 sec
    frameSize:     400    // 25ms at 16kHz
    hopSize:       160    // 10ms hop
    melFilters:    MelFilterbank(nFilters: 40, nFFT: 512, sr: 16000)
    featureBuffer: RingBuffer(capacity: 200)  // ~2 sec of frames
    runningMean:   zeros(39)
    runningVar:    ones(39)
    alpha:         0.995  // EMA smoothing factor

    function onAudioReceived(samples: [Float]):
        ringBuffer.append(samples)

        while ringBuffer.availableFrames >= frameSize:
            frame = ringBuffer.nextFrame(frameSize, hopSize)

            // Pre-emphasis
            frame = preEmphasize(frame, coeff: 0.97)

            // Window + FFT (vDSP accelerated)
            frame = frame * hammingWindow
            spectrum = vDSP.fft(frame, n: 512)
            powerSpec = magnitude_squared(spectrum)

            // MFCC path
            melEnergies = melFilters.apply(powerSpec)
            logMel = log(melEnergies + 1e-10)
            mfcc = dct(logMel)[1:13]

            // Spectral features (from same power spectrum)
            centroid = spectralCentroid(powerSpec)
            flux = spectralFlux(powerSpec, previousSpec)
            rolloff = spectralRolloff(powerSpec, threshold: 0.85)

            // Pitch (from original frame)
            f0 = yinPitchTrack(frame, sr: 16000)

            // Combine and normalize
            features = concat(mfcc, [centroid, flux, rolloff, f0])
            features = onlineNormalize(features)

            featureBuffer.append(features)

        // Every ~500ms: compute statistics and classify
        if featureBuffer.isFull:
            stats = computeFunctionals(featureBuffer)
            emotion = model.predict(stats)
            emit(emotion)
 iOS Performance Notes

On Apple Silicon, vDSP FFT operations complete in microseconds for 512-point transforms. A complete MFCC extraction pipeline for a single frame takes approximately 50-100 microseconds on an A14 chip, leaving abundant headroom for 100 frames/second processing. The key bottleneck is pitch tracking (YIN algorithm), which requires a longer analysis window (~50ms minimum) and more computation.

Swift Pseudocode 
import Accelerate
import AVFoundation

class AcousticFeatureExtractor {
    let fftSetup: vDSP.FFT<DSPSplitComplex>
    let melFilterbank: [[Float]]  // Pre-computed mel filter matrix
    let frameSize = 400           // 25ms at 16kHz
    let fftSize = 512             // Next power of 2
    let numMelFilters = 40
    let numMFCCs = 13

    func extractFeatures(frame: [Float]) -> [Float] {
        // 1. Apply Hamming window
        var windowed = [Float](repeating: 0, count: frameSize)
        vDSP.multiply(frame, hammingWindow, result: &windowed)

        // 2. FFT -> Power Spectrum
        var powerSpectrum = performFFT(windowed)

        // 3. Mel filterbank (matrix multiply)
        var melEnergies = [Float](repeating: 0, count: numMelFilters)
        for i in 0..<numMelFilters {
            vDSP.dot(melFilterbank[i], powerSpectrum, result: &melEnergies[i])
        }

        // 4. Log compression
        var logMel = [Float](repeating: 0, count: numMelFilters)
        vForce.log(melEnergies.map { max($0, 1e-10) }, result: &logMel)

        // 5. DCT -> MFCCs
        let mfccs = performDCT(logMel)[1..<numMFCCs+1]

        // 6. Spectral features (from same power spectrum)
        let centroid = computeSpectralCentroid(powerSpectrum)
        let rolloff  = computeSpectralRolloff(powerSpectrum)

        return Array(mfccs) + [centroid, rolloff]
    }
}
 Practical Recommendations for iOS
  • Use 16kHz sampling rate: Sufficient for speech (8kHz bandwidth), reduces computation by 2.75x vs. 44.1kHz
  • Use 40 mel filters with 13 MFCCs as baseline; add F0 and energy if needed
  • Pre-compute all filter matrices at initialization; they are constant for a given sample rate
  • Use vDSP exclusively for signal processing; avoid manual loops over audio buffers
  • Process on a background thread; use DispatchQueue with .userInitiated QoS
  • Consider Core ML with an AudioFeaturePrint model for end-to-end feature extraction trained for emotion
  • Battery: The Accelerate framework's hardware acceleration reduces inference time ~30x compared to naive implementations, keeping energy use minimal

📖 Glossary

TermDefinition
MFCCMel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficient. A compact representation of the spectral envelope based on human auditory perception, obtained by applying the DCT to the log of mel-scaled filterbank energies.
Mel ScaleA perceptual scale of pitch where equal mel intervals sound equally spaced to humans. Linear below ~1000 Hz, logarithmic above.
CepstrumThe "spectrum of the spectrum." Obtained by taking the inverse Fourier transform (or DCT) of the log magnitude spectrum. Separates source (vocal fold vibration) from filter (vocal tract shape).
FFTFast Fourier Transform. An efficient algorithm to compute the Discrete Fourier Transform, converting time-domain signals to frequency-domain representations.
DCTDiscrete Cosine Transform. A Fourier-related transform using only cosine functions. Decorrelates features and compacts information into fewer coefficients.
F0Fundamental Frequency. The lowest frequency of vocal fold vibration, perceived as pitch. Measured in Hz or semitones.
FormantA resonance frequency of the vocal tract, appearing as a peak in the spectral envelope. F1-F4 are numbered from lowest to highest.
JitterCycle-to-cycle variation in the fundamental frequency period. Measures pitch instability; increases with vocal stress.
ShimmerCycle-to-cycle variation in amplitude. Measures loudness instability; associated with breathiness and vocal fatigue.
HNRHarmonics-to-Noise Ratio. The ratio of periodic (harmonic) to aperiodic (noise) energy in the voice, measured in dB. Lower HNR = breathier/hoarser voice.
LPCLinear Predictive Coding. A method that models the vocal tract as an all-pole filter, used to estimate formant frequencies from the spectral envelope.
Spectral CentroidThe "center of mass" of the frequency spectrum, calculated as the weighted average of frequencies present. Indicates the perceived brightness of a sound.
Spectral FluxThe rate of change of the spectral envelope between consecutive frames. High flux indicates rapid timbral changes.
Spectral RolloffThe frequency below which a specified percentage (typically 85-95%) of the total spectral energy is contained.
Pre-emphasisA first-order high-pass filter applied to boost high frequencies and flatten the spectral envelope before feature extraction.
CMVNCepstral Mean and Variance Normalization. A technique to normalize features by removing the mean and standardizing the variance, reducing speaker and channel effects.
eGeMAPSExtended Geneva Minimalistic Acoustic Parameter Set. A standardized set of 88 acoustic features designed for emotion and affect research.
VADVoice Activity Detection. The process of identifying segments of audio that contain speech vs. silence or background noise.
ArousalThe activation/energy dimension of emotion (calm to excited). Strongly correlated with pitch, energy, and speaking rate.
ValenceThe positive-negative dimension of emotion (unpleasant to pleasant). Harder to detect from acoustic features alone; often requires linguistic content.
vDSPApple's Digital Signal Processing library within the Accelerate framework. Provides hardware-optimized routines for FFT, vector math, and signal processing.
